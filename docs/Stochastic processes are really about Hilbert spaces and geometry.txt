What good does it do to know you live in a Hilbert space?

The single most concrete payoff is this: the inner product in H_X is the covariance.

Specifically,

    〈Xₛ, Xₜ〉_{L²(Ω)} = 𝔼[Xₛ X̄ₜ] = K(s,t).

That one identification means every geometric fact in the Hilbert space
is simultaneously a statistical fact about the process.

Orthogonality equals uncorrelatedness.
The angle between two elements equals the correlation coefficient.
The distance ‖Xₜ − Y‖_{L²} is the mean square error between Xₜ and Y.

You get all of Euclidean geometric intuition, now working in infinite
dimensions over random variables.


Projection = Best Linear Prediction

The most operationally important consequence is that orthogonal projection
onto a closed subspace gives you the best linear predictor.

If you want to predict Xₜ₊ₕ from the past
span̄{Xₛ : s ≤ t} =: H_X⁻(t),
the answer is just π_{H_X⁻(t)} Xₜ₊ₕ.

No probability theory beyond second-order structure is needed.
It is a theorem of Hilbert space geometry that the projection is the
unique closest point.

The mean square prediction error is

    ‖Xₜ₊ₕ − π_{H_X⁻(t)} Xₜ₊ₕ‖²,

which is the squared distance from Xₜ₊ₕ to that subspace.

The entire Wiener filter theory is an instance of this.


Stone's Theorem Becomes Available for Free

Because the shift Uᵗ is a unitary operator on a Hilbert space, the entire
spectral theory of unitary groups — Stone's theorem, the projection-valued
measure, and the spectral integral — applies immediately.

You do not construct the Cramér representation by hand.
It drops out as a consequence of having a unitary group on a Hilbert space.

The spectral factorization of the process is exactly the diagonalization
of the shift operator, the infinite-dimensional analog of diagonalizing
a unitary matrix, except that here diagonalization requires the PVM E
instead of eigenvectors, since the shift generically has no eigenvectors
in H_X.


Wold Factorization

The nested subspaces H_X⁻(t) are all closed in H_X, and their intersection

    ⋂ₜ H_X⁻(t)

is again a closed subspace.

The Hilbert space geometry forces that intersection to be either trivial
or to carry a deterministic component.

The Wold factorization — splitting the process into a purely
nondeterministic part and a deterministic part — is a direct consequence
of this subspace geometry and the fact that the innovation subspaces

    H_X⁻(t) ⊖ H_X⁻(t−ε)

are pairwise orthogonal.

None of that requires anything beyond the Hilbert space axioms applied
to the covariance structure.


Completeness

Finally, and most foundationally: you know the space closes.

Any Cauchy sequence of second-order random variables in H_X converges to
something still in H_X.

Without that, infinite linear combinations, limits of approximations,
and spectral integrals would have nowhere to land.

The whole apparatus — Cramér representation, Wiener filter, Wold
factorization — depends on being able to take limits freely and land
inside the same space.

Hilbert space is precisely the structure that guarantees that.
