§9.10.7. Geometry of the Stahl compact for meromorphic Mittag-Leffler
functions

The Stahl compact Δ_g = P̄_g ∪ γ is a concrete geometric object in the
complex plane, and its shape governs every aspect of Padé convergence
for the fractional Riccati solution. The arcs γ are the minimal-
capacity tree that threads the Mittag-Leffler zeros together, and their
curvature reflects the local distribution of poles and residues.

Theorem 9.37 (Arcs connect poles to infinity). For g meromorphic on ℂ
with discrete pole set P_g, the admissible compacts of Definition 3.2
are precisely those containing P_g. The Stahl compact Δ_g minimizes
capacity among such compacts. Since P_g is discrete, the minimal
capacity is achieved by adding arcs γ that connect the poles to each
other and to infinity, forming a connected set of minimal logarithmic
capacity.

Proof. Any compact K ⊃ P_g is admissible because g is single-valued
and meromorphic on ℂ \ K. The capacity minimization among such K
requires K to be as "thin" as possible while remaining connected (to
ensure ℂ \ K is a domain). For a discrete set, the thinnest connected
superset is a tree of arcs through the points. The S-property selects
the unique such tree of minimal capacity. ∎

Theorem 9.38 (The arcs are not straight). For generic parameters
(c_0, c_1, c_2) with c_2 ≠ 0, the arcs of γ are not straight line
segments. They are analytic curves satisfying the S-property

    ∂g/∂n_+(s) = ∂g/∂n_-(s),  s ∈ γ \ {endpoints}

where g(z,∞) is the Green's function of ℂ \ Δ_g with pole at infinity.
The curvature of the arcs is determined by the local distribution of
poles and their residues.

Proof. The S-property is the Euler-Lagrange equation of the capacity
minimization. For a symmetric configuration (e.g., poles on a line with
equal residues), the arcs coincide with that line. For generic
asymmetric pole distributions, the equality of normal derivatives of
the Green's function forces curvature. Straight lines satisfy the
S-property only when the harmonic measure is symmetric about the line,
which requires pole-residue symmetry that is nongeneric. ∎

The physical picture is this. Imagine the poles as points in the
plane, each weighted by the magnitude of its residue. The arcs are
the minimal-tension connections between them. The tension is logarithmic
potential energy. The connections do not follow straight lines because
the potential field bends them toward regions of higher pole density
and stronger residue magnitude. Each segment lies on an equipotential
of the induced field, pulling equally on both sides at every interior
point. That is the S-property: any imbalance would permit a capacity-
reducing deformation.

For symmetric configurations, the connections collapse to something
simple. Poles on the real axis with alternating residues yield arcs on
the real axis. Regular lattices yield grid-like structures. But for
generic rough Heston parameters, the Mittag-Leffler zeros have no
symmetry. They cluster in some sectors, repel in others, and the
connecting arcs weave through this irregular landscape like a river
system draining toward infinity.

The remarkable fact is that you never need to see this landscape. The
Padé approximant knows it implicitly. The orthogonal polynomials Q_n
encode the equilibrium configuration in their coefficients. Their zeros
avoid the arcs γ because the potential pushes them away. The poles of
[n/n]_g — the eigenvalues of the Jacobi matrix J_n — condense onto γ
in the limit, but for any finite n they are discrete points hovering
near the true poles and the arcs. The Froissart doublets are the
approximant's attempt to cancel spurious poles with nearby zeros,
mimicking the residue structure of the true measure.

This is why the convergence is in capacity, not uniform, until the
non-explosion condition kicks in. The approximant is exploring the
arc geometry, adjusting its pole configuration, converging to the
equilibrium measure. On the positive real axis, where there are no
poles and no arcs, the exploration is trivial: the approximant has
nothing to avoid, and convergence is uniform with a computable bound.

Theorem 9.39 (Algorithmic irrelevance of arc geometry). The incremental
Hankel solver of Corollary 9.27, the computable error bound of Theorem
9.19, and the uniform convergence of Theorem 9.24 do not require
knowledge of the arc geometry. The Padé approximant is constructed
from the Müntz coefficients a_k alone; the Riemann-Hilbert matrix Y
of Theorem 9.33 is built from the orthogonal polynomials Q_n for the
discrete measure μ_g; and the convergence certificate depends only on
successive Padé differences Δ_k. The arcs γ are the theoretical
backbone, not a computational input.

Proof. The Padé approximant [n/n]_g is determined by the linear system
(9.35), which involves only the moments a_k. The orthogonal polynomials
Q_n are generated by the three-term recurrence with coefficients from
the Hankel matrix, again involving only moments. The error bound (9.42)
uses only the Padé iterates R_M and R_{M-1}. None of these quantities
requires explicit knowledge of Δ_g or γ. ∎

Remark 9.40 (The equilibrium configuration). The arcs γ are the
structural framework onto which the Padé approximant organizes its
poles. At order n, the approximant has n poles distributed near the
true poles and the arcs. As n increases, these poles condense onto
the equilibrium configuration: true poles are approximated by nearby
Padé poles, and the remaining Padé poles distribute along γ according
to the equilibrium measure. The Froissart doublets are the
approximant's mechanism for canceling spurious poles with nearby zeros,
mimicking the residue structure of the true spectral measure. The
practitioner never constructs the equilibrium configuration explicitly;
it emerges from the moments, and the approximant converges to it
automatically.

Remark 9.41 (Accumulation at infinity). The pole set P_g of the
Mittag-Leffler ratio may accumulate at infinity. In this case, the
arcs γ connect finite poles to each other and to infinity in a tree
structure. The Stahl compact is unbounded, but its intersection with any
bounded set contains only finitely many poles and finitely many arc
segments. The equilibrium measure μ_{Δ_g} is still well-defined, and
the convergence theory of §3 applies without modification.
