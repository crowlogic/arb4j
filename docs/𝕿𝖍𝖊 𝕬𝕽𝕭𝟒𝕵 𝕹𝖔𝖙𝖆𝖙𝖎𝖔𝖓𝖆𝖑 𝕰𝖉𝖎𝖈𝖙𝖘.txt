              𝕿𝖍𝖊 𝕬𝕽𝕭𝟒𝕵 𝕹𝖔𝖙𝖆𝖙𝖎𝖔𝖓𝖆𝖑 𝕰𝖉𝖎𝖈𝖙𝖘

 Governing all expressions written, compiled, rendered, or
 communicated in the context of the ARB4J project, binding
 upon all contributors — natural or artificial.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐈. 𝐓𝐡𝐞 𝐈𝐧𝐭𝐞𝐠𝐫𝐚𝐧𝐝 𝐏𝐫𝐞𝐜𝐞𝐝𝐞𝐬 𝐭𝐡𝐞 𝐃𝐢𝐟𝐟𝐞𝐫𝐞𝐧𝐭𝐢𝐚𝐥

 The differential shall appear after the integrand, never
 before it. ∫ f(x) dx is the sole acceptable form. The
 abomination ∫ dx f(x) shall not appear in code,
 documentation, issues, comments, rendered output, or
 communication of any kind. Any source using differential‐first
 notation shall be silently corrected upon citation.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐈𝐈. 𝐍𝐨𝐭𝐚𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝐒𝐡𝐚𝐥𝐥 𝐏𝐚𝐫𝐬𝐞 𝐔𝐧𝐚𝐦𝐛𝐢𝐠𝐮𝐨𝐮𝐬𝐥𝐲

 Every mathematical expression shall admit exactly one parse
 under a consistent, context‐free grammar. If a notation
 requires lookahead, backtracking, or contextual disambiguation
 to determine the scope of an operator, it is defective and
 shall be replaced with a notation that does not.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐈𝐈𝐈. 𝐒𝐮𝐛‐𝐄𝐱𝐩𝐫𝐞𝐬𝐬𝐢𝐨𝐧𝐬 𝐒𝐡𝐚𝐥𝐥 𝐁𝐞 𝐖𝐞𝐥𝐥‐𝐅𝐨𝐫𝐦𝐞𝐝 𝐢𝐧 𝐈𝐬𝐨𝐥𝐚𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧

 No sub‐expression shall change its meaning when the
 surrounding context is removed. If ∫ₐᵇ dx means b − a in
 isolation, it means b − a everywhere. Notational conventions
 that violate compositionality are forbidden.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐈𝐕. 𝐒𝐜𝐨𝐩𝐞 𝐒𝐡𝐚𝐥𝐥 𝐁𝐞 𝐃𝐞𝐥𝐢𝐦𝐢𝐭𝐞𝐝, 𝐍𝐨𝐭 𝐈𝐦𝐩𝐥𝐢𝐞𝐝

 Operator scope shall be established by explicit delimiters —
 parentheses, braces, brackets, or equivalent syntactic
 markers. Reliance on positional convention or "the reader
 will figure it out" is not engineering; it is negligence.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐕. 𝐇𝐢𝐬𝐭𝐨𝐫𝐢𝐜𝐚𝐥 𝐏𝐞𝐝𝐢𝐠𝐫𝐞𝐞 𝐈𝐬 𝐍𝐨𝐭 𝐎𝐩𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧𝐚𝐥

 Leibniz, Euler, Riemann, and Lebesgue wrote ∫ f(x) dx. Three
 centuries of rigorous mathematics chose this form. Mid‐20th‐
 century shortcuts from applied physics do not override the
 foundational canon.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐕𝐈. 𝐂𝐨𝐧𝐯𝐞𝐧𝐢𝐞𝐧𝐜𝐞 𝐈𝐬 𝐍𝐨𝐭 𝐚 𝐉𝐮𝐬𝐭𝐢𝐟𝐢𝐜𝐚𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧

 "It is easier to typeset" or "it pairs the integral with its
 variable in nested integrals" are admissions that the
 surrounding expression is poorly structured. Factor out
 sub‐expressions, use delimiters, or define named functions.
 Do not mutilate notation to compensate for sprawling inline
 expressions.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐕𝐈𝐈. 𝐓𝐡𝐞 𝐂𝐨𝐦𝐩𝐢𝐥𝐞𝐫 𝐈𝐬 𝐭𝐡𝐞 𝐀𝐫𝐛𝐢𝐭𝐞𝐫

 If the expression compiler can parse it into a unique AST
 without special‐case rules, the notation is acceptable. If it
 requires ad‐hoc heuristics, ambiguity resolution, or semantic
 lookahead, the notation is rejected. The grammar decides;
 convention does not override grammar.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐕𝐈𝐈𝐈. 𝐄𝐱𝐩𝐫𝐞𝐬𝐬𝐢𝐨𝐧𝐬 𝐒𝐡𝐚𝐥𝐥 𝐁𝐞 𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐝𝐮𝐜𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧‐𝐂𝐨𝐦𝐩𝐥𝐞𝐭𝐞

 No stub, placeholder, pseudo‐expression, or "left as an
 exercise" fragment shall appear in any artifact. Every
 expression is either fully specified and compilable or it
 does not exist yet.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐈𝐗. 𝐂𝐨𝐫𝐫𝐞𝐜𝐭𝐞𝐝 𝐎𝐧𝐜𝐞, 𝐂𝐨𝐫𝐫𝐞𝐜𝐭𝐞𝐝 𝐄𝐯𝐞𝐫𝐲𝐰𝐡𝐞𝐫𝐞

 When a notational error is identified and corrected, that
 correction propagates retroactively across all project
 artifacts. There is no grandfather clause for bad notation.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐗. 𝐍𝐨 𝐀𝐦𝐛𝐢𝐠𝐮𝐢𝐭𝐲 𝐒𝐡𝐚𝐥𝐥 𝐁𝐞 𝐏𝐚𝐩𝐞𝐫𝐞𝐝 𝐎𝐯𝐞𝐫

 If two contributors disagree on how to read an expression,
 the expression is wrong — not the contributors. Rewrite it
 until the disagreement is impossible.
