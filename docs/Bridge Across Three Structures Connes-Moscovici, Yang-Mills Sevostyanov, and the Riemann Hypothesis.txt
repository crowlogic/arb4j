Bridge Across Three Structures: Connes–Moscovici, Yang–Mills/Sevostyanov,
and the Riemann Hypothesis

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

𝗢𝘃𝗲𝗿𝘃𝗶𝗲𝘄: 𝗪𝗵𝗮𝘁 𝗜𝘀 𝗕𝗲𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗲𝗱

Three bodies of mathematics are in play. The first is the Connes–Moscovici
1982 L²-index theorem: for a G-invariant elliptic operator P on a
homogeneous space M = G∕H, the analytical index ind_G P, defined via a
canonical von Neumann trace on the commutant of the left regular
representation, equals a topological index computed from characteristic
classes in relative Lie algebra cohomology. The second is Sevostyanov's
non-perturbative quantization program: the Yang–Mills Hamiltonian is
identified as a gradient-squared potential (with the Chern–Simons
functional as the potential), and this structure allows a canonical family
of quantizations on weighted L² spaces, producing, in the abelian case, a
rigorous mass gap via the Bochner–Minlos theorem and Fock space spectral
theory. The third is the narrative in the attached RH session: the
ζ-function, after a cumulative-phase time change ψ, becomes a band-limited
stationary process in the Paley–Wiener space PW_{[−2,0]}, from which the
Riemann hypothesis and simplicity of zeros are argued via Hardy space
theory, Schwarz reflection, and Bulinskaya's theorem.

The connections are not metaphorical. They run along eight identifiable
mathematical axes, each a structural correspondence — same theorem, same
construction, same obstruction — realized in different guises across the
three texts.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗜: 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗩𝗼𝗻 𝗡𝗲𝘂𝗺𝗮𝗻𝗻 𝗧𝗿𝗮𝗰𝗲 𝗮𝘀 𝗤𝘂𝗮𝗻𝘁𝗶𝘇𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗠𝗲𝗮𝘀𝘂𝗿𝗲

In Connes–Moscovici, the entire index theory rests on the G-trace tr_G,
the canonical semifinite faithful normal trace on the von Neumann algebra
ℳ_G — the commutant of the left regular representation L of G on L²(G).
Its defining property is

    tr_G(R(f)∗ R(f)) = ∫_G ∣f(g)∣² dg.

This trace replaces ordinary dimension: instead of dim ker P, one computes

    dim_G ker P = tr_G K_{ker P},

which is finite even when ker P is infinite-dimensional as a Hilbert space.
The key identity (Corollary 2.3 in CM) is

    tr_G(P) = k_P(1),

the G-trace of a G-invariant operator equals the diagonal value of its
convolution kernel at the identity.

In Sevostyanov, the quantized Yang–Mills Hamiltonian acts on
L²(𝒟∕𝒦, dμ), where μ is a probability measure with density ψ e^{−2φ},
with φ = CS the Chern–Simons functional. This is not Lebesgue measure: it
is a weighted measure determined by the potential, exactly as in a
ground-state representation. The function ψ encodes the quantization
ambiguity: different ψ yield unitarily inequivalent quantum theories. After
the unitary intertwining

    U : L²(M, dμ) → L²(M, dvol),   f ↦ f · √(ψ e^{−2φ}),

the Hamiltonian becomes

    Ĥ = ½ ∑_a ξ_a∗ ξ_a : L²(M, dμ) → L²(M, dμ),

where ξ_a are directional derivatives and ξ_a∗ their adjoints in the
weighted L². The measure dμ plays the role of the ground-state weight:
potential information is carried by the measure rather than by explicit
multiplication terms in Ĥ, exactly as the G-trace encodes the Haar measure
in Connes–Moscovici.

In the zeta narrative, the covariance kernel

    K(t,s) = √ψ′(t) · sinc(ψ(t) − ψ(s)) · √ψ′(s)

defines a positive semidefinite operator C on L²[0,∞). The diagonal

    K(t,t) = 2ψ′(t) = 2θ′(t) ∣ζ(½+it)∣²

is the reproducing kernel evaluation at the diagonal — the analogue of
k_P(1). All three constructions share the same structural role for their
respective measures or traces: they encode the geometry of the underlying
group action and convert infinite-volume or infinite-dimensional kernel
data into finite, controlled scalars.

𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗜𝗜: 𝗢𝗿𝗯𝗶𝘁 𝗦𝗽𝗮𝗰𝗲𝘀, 𝗔𝘃𝗲𝗿𝗮𝗴𝗶𝗻𝗴, 𝗮𝗻𝗱 𝗦𝘆𝗺𝗽𝗹𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗰 𝗥𝗲𝗱𝘂𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻

The configuration space in all three theories is a quotient of a function
space by a group.

In Connes–Moscovici, M = G∕H is the left coset space, and every
construction passes through the averaging operation

    Av(P) = ∫_G L(g) P L(g)⁻¹ dg,

which converts a general compactly supported ΨDO into a G-invariant one.
Lemma 1.2 establishes that every G-invariant ΨDO is an average:
P = Av(fP) for any cut-off f with ∫_G f dg = 1.

In Sevostyanov, the Marsden–Weinstein symplectic reduction theorem
(Proposition 3) is applied to the gauge group 𝒦 acting on the phase space
T𝒟. The moment map is

    μ(A,E) = div_A E   (Gauss's law),

the constraint surface is { (A,E) : div_A E = 0 }, and the reduced phase
space is

    μ⁻¹(0) ∕ 𝒦  ≅  T∗(𝒟∕𝒦).

The horizontal subspaces ker div_A (Coulomb gauge) are the analogues of
CM's homogeneous tangent directions. A key lemma identifies the annihilator
of the gauge orbit in T𝒟 with the kernel of dμ, in exact parallel with
CM's identification of the symbol as the element of K_H(V), the equivariant
K-theory group of the co-isotropy representation.

In the zeta narrative, the cumulative phase change

    ψ(t) = ∫₀ᵗ θ′(u) ∣ζ(½+iu)∣² du

is the reduction operation: it absorbs the non-stationarity of the zeta
process (irregular zero spacing, growth of θ′(t)) into a single
reparametrization, yielding a stationary band-limited process X(T) on the
quotient timeline. This is a Priestley oscillatory-process reduction: the
evolutionary spectrum collapses into a stationary one under the time
change, exactly as CM's averaging collapses a general ΨDO into a
G-invariant one.

The Gribov obstruction — the impossibility of globally section-izing
𝒟∕𝒦 — is the infinite-dimensional analogue of CM's non-existence of a
global right-inverse for G → G∕H when the isotropy representation is
non-trivial. Both are topological obstructions to choosing global
representatives; both are resolved by working intrinsically on the quotient.

𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗜𝗜𝗜: 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗜𝗻𝗱𝗲𝘅 𝗮𝘀 𝗮 𝗧𝗼𝗽𝗼𝗹𝗼𝗴𝗶𝗰𝗮𝗹 𝗜𝗻𝘃𝗮𝗿𝗶𝗮𝗻𝘁 𝗼𝗳 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗦𝗽𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗿𝘂𝗺

The deepest structural connection runs through the passage from an analytic
invariant to a topological one.

In Connes–Moscovici, the main theorem ind_G P = ind_t[σ(P)] states that

    ind_G D⁺_ε = (−1)ⁿ (ch(ε) · Â(𝔤,H))[V],

where ch(ε) is the Chern character of an H-representation ε and Â(𝔤,H) is
the relative Â-class computed from the curvature of the canonical connection
on G → G∕H. The topological index depends only on the principal symbol
σ(P) and is stable under homotopy.

In Sevostyanov, the key structural observation (Proposition 5) is that the
potential vector field G = ∗F is the gradient of the Chern–Simons
functional CS:

    G = grad CS,   so   ½⟨G,G⟩ = ½ ∥grad CS∥².

The Hamiltonian takes the form

    H = ½ (⟨p,p⟩ + ∥grad CS∥²).

This is a gradient-squared Hamiltonian, the same structure as Witten-type
Morse theory. The quantization measure

    dμ ∝ e^{−2 CS(A)} dA

transforms a topological datum — the winding number and homotopy type of
connections — into an analytic datum: the spectrum of Ĥ. The mass gap ½m
arises from this topological structure, not from an ad hoc potential.

In the zeta narrative, the assertion X(T) ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} is a spectral
index statement: the Fourier transform is supported in a fixed compact
interval. Band-limitation implies Hardy-space membership, which implies
zero-free half-planes. The support [−2,0] plays the role of the topological
index: determined by the Riemann–Siegel expansion after the time change,
encoding the zero structure globally, just as the Chern character integral
encodes the index.

𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗜𝗩: 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗖𝗵𝗲𝗿𝗻–𝗦𝗶𝗺𝗼𝗻𝘀 𝗙𝘂𝗻𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻𝗮𝗹 𝗮𝘀 𝗟𝗶𝗻𝗸𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗧𝗵𝗿𝗲𝗮𝗱

The Chern–Simons functional appears explicitly in two of the three texts
and underlies the third implicitly.

In Connes–Moscovici, the topological index involves Chern–Weil
representatives ch(ε) = [tr exp(Ω_ε)] and Â, built from the curvature
Ω_ε(X,Y) = iε(Ω(X,Y)) of the canonical connection on the homogeneous
bundle, evaluated in H∗(𝔤,H;ℝ). The projection θ: 𝔤 → 𝔥 and the
curvature Ω = −½[−,−]_𝔥 are the same data that, in finite-dimensional
gauge theory, produce the Chern–Simons 3-form on principal bundles.

In Sevostyanov, the Chern–Simons functional

    CS(A) = ∫_{ℝ³} Tr(A ∧ dA + ⅓ A ∧ A ∧ A)

is the potential whose gradient is G = ∗F. In the abelian case this
reduces to CS(A) = ½ ∫ A ∧ dA (magnetic helicity), and the Gaussian
exponent becomes

    −1/(2c²) ⟨G,G⟩ − 2 CS(A) − ½(c²+m) ⟨A,A⟩,

entirely controlled by CS together with a regularizing quadratic form.

In the zeta narrative, the Riemann–Siegel phase

    θ(t) = Im log Γ(¼ + ½it) − ½ t log π

is the arithmetic analogue of the Chern–Simons phase: it is a winding
number of the Γ-factor of ζ, and the time change

    ψ(t) = ∫₀ᵗ θ′(u) ∣ζ(½+iu)∣² du

is built from it. The instantaneous frequency of each Riemann–Siegel term
n^{−½} cos(θ(t) − t log n) after the time change is ≈ log n ∕ θ′(t), and
since log N(t) ≈ θ′(t), this ratio stays ≤ 2 — the same growth rate that
controls the Chern–Simons phase winding.

In all three: the Chern–Simons phase (or its analogue) is the winding
invariant of the respective principal object; it encodes topological charge
and controls the scale at which spectral components are measured.

𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗩: 𝗦𝗲𝗹𝗳-𝗔𝗱𝗷𝗼𝗶𝗻𝘁𝗻𝗲𝘀𝘀 𝘃𝗶𝗮 𝗧𝗵𝗿𝗲𝗲 𝗗𝗶𝗳𝗳𝗲𝗿𝗲𝗻𝘁 𝗠𝗲𝗰𝗵𝗮𝗻𝗶𝘀𝗺𝘀

All three theories arrive at self-adjointness as the organizing structural
requirement, via three distinct but structurally equivalent mechanisms.

In Connes–Moscovici, self-adjointness is automatic: G-invariant ΨDOs of
order 0 live in the commutant ℳ_G(ξ) of the left regular representation,
which is a von Neumann algebra consisting of bounded operators. The trace
tr_G is faithful and normal on this algebra. The exact sequence

    0 → C∗_G(ξ) → Ψ∗_G(ξ) → C_H(S,E,E) → 0

(Proposition 1.7) places Ψ∗_G(ξ) as a C∗-algebra extension; normal
elements with real spectrum correspond to self-adjoint operators.

In Sevostyanov, self-adjointness is not automatic. The operator

    Ĥ = ½ ∑_a ξ_a∗(x) ξ_a(x)

is initially defined on smooth compactly supported vectors in L²(M,dμ)
and is symmetric and non-negative. The Friedrichs extension theorem applies:
the quadratic form

    Q(f,f) = ½ ∑_a ∥ξ_a f∥² ≥ 0

is non-negative and densely defined, hence closable, and its closure defines
the canonical self-adjoint extension. The condition 1 ∈ L²(M,dμ) (μ is a
probability measure) gives Ĥ(1) = 0, establishing the constant function as
the vacuum ground state at eigenvalue 0.

In the zeta narrative, self-adjointness of the Hilbert–Pólya operator

    D = −i ψ′(t)⁻¹ · d∕dt

on H_K is established by Stone's theorem. The one-parameter family

    (V_ε f)(t) = f(ψ⁻¹(ψ(t) + ε))

is a strongly continuous unitary group on H_K: in T-coordinates it is
the translation group (U_ε g)(T) = g(T+ε) on PW_{[−2,0]}, conjugated by
the unitary time-change isomorphism. Stone's theorem then gives D as the
unique self-adjoint generator of (V_ε). The spectrum of D is [−2,0],
purely absolutely continuous. The Riemann zeros t_n appear as singularities
of the coefficient 1∕ψ′(t_n): since ψ′(t_n) = 0, domain constraints force
every f ∈ Dom(D) to vanish at each t_n.

The three mechanisms — von Neumann algebra structure, Friedrichs extension
of a non-negative form, Stone's theorem — are the three fundamental routes
to self-adjointness in functional analysis. Each is appropriate to its
context; all express the same principle: the operator's spectrum is real
because the underlying geometry is Hermitian.

𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗩𝗜: 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗞𝗲𝗿𝗻𝗲𝗹 𝗼𝗳 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗢𝗽𝗲𝗿𝗮𝘁𝗼𝗿 = 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗗𝗶𝘀𝗰𝗿𝗲𝘁𝗲 𝗚𝗲𝗼𝗺𝗲𝘁𝗿𝘆

In all three theories, the kernel of the central operator encodes discrete
arithmetic or geometric data.

In Connes–Moscovici, Theorem 6.1 states that ker P is a finite direct sum
of irreducible discrete series representations of G — the irreducible
unitary representations with L²-matrix coefficients that "fit" in L²(G)
without continuous spectral contribution. Existence of discrete series is
governed by the rank condition rank(K) = rank(G) (Harish-Chandra). A
corollary: if rank(K_S) < rank(S) for a semisimple factor S, then ker P = {0}
for every G-invariant elliptic P.

In Sevostyanov, ker Ĥ is one-dimensional, spanned by Ω = 1 ∈ L²(𝒟∕𝒦,dμ).
The eigenvalue 0 is simple. The mass gap ½m is the infimum of the spectrum
above 0: no state exists between 0 and ½m. In the Fock space picture each
one-particle mode labelled by momentum k has energy ≥ ½m, so the gap is
the separation between the one-dimensional kernel and the rest of the
spectrum.

In the zeta narrative, the degeneracy locus of C encodes the zeros: for
every Riemann zero t_n and every f,

    (Cf)(t_n) = 0.

Every vector in the range of C vanishes at each t_n. This is not an
eigenvalue condition — Cf ≠ 0 in general — but a nodal condition: the
geometric locus of zeros of ζ is the locus where the covariance degenerates.
The Riemann zeros are to C what the discrete series are to P: isolated
features of the spectral geometry carrying deep arithmetic information.

Montgomery's pair correlation conjecture and Rudnick–Sarnak's results
sharpen the parallel: the Riemann zeros follow GUE statistics, the same
random matrix ensemble underlying the unitary representation theory of U(n),
the group whose representation theory generates discrete series of semisimple
groups.

The heat kernel method mediates between spectral and topological data in
Connes–Moscovici and reappears structurally in both other settings.

In Connes–Moscovici, the McKean–Singer identity with the G-trace gives

    tr_G e^{−tΔ⁺} − tr_G e^{−tΔ⁻} = dim_G ker D⁺ − dim_G ker D⁻ = ind_G D⁺

for all t > 0. As t → 0⁺ each G-trace expands asymptotically as

    tr_G e^{−tΔ^±} ∼ ∑_{k ≥ −m} c_k(Δ^±) t^{k∕2},

and the index equals the constant term c₀(Δ⁺) − c₀(Δ⁻), expressed as a
Chern–Weil integral — the t-independent part of the heat trace difference.

In Sevostyanov, the harmonic oscillator model (ψ = e^{−x²∕2} on ℝ) gives
the Hermite operator

    Ĥ = −½ e^{x²∕2} (d∕dx) e^{−x²∕2} (d∕dx) : L²(ℝ, e^{−x²∕2}dx) → L²(ℝ, e^{−x²∕2}dx),

with spectrum {0, ½, 1, 3∕2, …}. The heat semigroup e^{−tĤ} is the Mehler
(Ornstein–Uhlenbeck) kernel, and

    tr(e^{−tĤ}) = ∑_{n≥0} e^{−nt∕2} = (1 − e^{−t∕2})⁻¹,

whose Taylor expansion generates the Bernoulli numbers — the same numbers
appearing in the Â-genus formula and Chern character. Both the oscillator
heat trace and the CM index integrand are evaluations of the same regularized
determinant det(1 − e^{−tĤ}).

In the zeta narrative, the restriction C_L of C to [0,L] has eigenfunctions
given by prolate spheroidal wave functions (PSWFs) with eigenvalues λ_n(L).
These exhibit the Landau–Widom phase transition near the Shannon number
N(L) ≈ 2L∕π: λ_n(L) ≈ 1 for n ≪ N(L), λ_n(L) ≈ 0 for n ≫ N(L). The
transition width is the "index density" of the truncated operator, the
analogue of the t → 0 constant term in the CM heat trace.

𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗩𝗜𝗜𝗜: 𝗞𝗶𝗹𝗹𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗙𝗼𝗿𝗺, 𝗠𝗲𝘁𝗿𝗶𝗰, 𝗮𝗻𝗱 𝗣𝗼𝘀𝗶𝘁𝗶𝘃𝗶𝘁𝘆

The RH session opens with an isometric embedding problem: for the Cartan
decomposition SL(n,ℂ) = P · G with G = SU(n), when does a smooth isometric
section f : (G,g) → (G_ℂ,g_k) exist? Writing the differential at the
identity as F(v) = v + φ(v) with φ : 𝔰𝔲(n) → 𝔭, the isometry condition
reduces to

    B_k(v,w) + B_k(φ(v),φ(w)) = g_e(v,w),   v,w ∈ 𝔰𝔲(n),

which requires g_e + B_k|_{𝔰𝔲(n)} to be positive semidefinite — the metric
g must dominate the (negative of the) Killing form in every direction.

In Connes–Moscovici, the co-isotropy representation on

    V = { ξ ∈ 𝔤∗ : ξ|_𝔥 = 0 }

must admit an Ad∗(H)-invariant orientation. This requires the representation
Ad∗ : H → SO(V) to be orientable — a positivity condition on the isotropy
representation analogous to g_e + B_k ≥ 0. When it holds, the Thom
isomorphism τ : R(H) → K_H(V) exists and the Bott class β generates K_H(V)
as an R(H)-module.

In Sevostyanov, the naive Gaussian measure with ψ = exp(−½⟨G,G⟩) fails
because the exponent in ψ e^{−2 CS(A)} is not negative definite — the cross
term ⟨G,G⟩ − 2 CS(A) is sign-indefinite. The corrected Ansatz adds the
term −½(c²+m)⟨A,A⟩, yielding the exponent

    −1∕(2c²) ⟨G,G⟩ − 2 CS(A) − ½(c²+m) ⟨A,A⟩.

A completing-the-square computation shows this is strictly negative definite.
The added mass-like term ½(c²+m)∥A∥² dominates the indefinite part, exactly
as g must dominate the Killing form in the embedding problem.

In the RH narrative, Weil's positivity criterion states that RH is
equivalent to W(f ∗ f̄♯) ≤ 0 for all admissible test functions f. The L²
norm of f plays the role of the metric; the Weil distribution W plays the
role of the Killing form — a sesquilinear form requiring global non-positivity
to hold over a full function space. In all three settings, the governing
structure is: a bilinear form must satisfy a sign condition globally, and
the obstruction to this is the same infinitesimal-to-global passage.

𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗜𝗫: 𝗡𝗼𝗻-𝗖𝗼𝗺𝗽𝗮𝗰𝘁𝗻𝗲𝘀𝘀 𝗮𝘀 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗨𝗻𝗶𝘃𝗲𝗿𝘀𝗮𝗹 𝗢𝗯𝘀𝘁𝗿𝘂𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻

All three theories confront non-compactness as the primary obstruction.
Each uses a structurally similar device to tame it.

In Connes–Moscovici, M = G∕H is non-compact, so the ordinary Fredholm
theory fails: ker P can be infinite-dimensional. The G-dimension

    dim_G 𝒲 = tr_G K_𝒲

replaces dim: it is finite even for G-invariant subspaces of L²(G) on a
non-compact quotient. Cut-off functions f with ∫_G f = 1 localize integrals,
and the identity tr_G(P) = k_P(1) converts "integration over infinite
volume" into evaluation at a single group element.

In Sevostyanov, non-compactness is twofold: the spatial domain ℝ³ and the
infinite-dimensional configuration space 𝒟∕𝒦. The Bochner–Minlos theorem
resolves both: for a nuclear space 𝒮 ⊂ H ⊂ 𝒮′ (Schwartz divergence-free
vector fields), any continuous, positive-definite, normalized functional

    Φ(φ) = exp(−½ ⟨Ĉφ, φ⟩),   φ ∈ 𝒮,

is the characteristic functional of a unique Gaussian probability measure
on 𝒮′. Spectral analysis of Ĉ = (−Δ + m)⁻¹ gives spectrum in (0,1],
confirming positivity and boundedness and validating the construction. The
nuclear space 𝒮 is the compact "core," analogous to the compact quotient
Γ∖G in Atiyah's covering L²-index theorem.

In the zeta narrative, the non-compact domain is { s = ½+it : t ∈ (0,∞) }.
The time change ψ converts the non-stationary zeta process into a
stationary one on all of ℝ. The Paley–Wiener theorem then controls the
non-compactness: a function entire of exponential type with Fourier support
in [−2,0] is polynomially bounded in every direction — non-compactness of
ℝ is controlled by the exponential type, exactly as the G-trace controls
non-compactness of G.

𝗦𝘆𝗻𝘁𝗵𝗲𝘀𝗶𝘀: 𝗢𝗻𝗲 𝗧𝗵𝗲𝗼𝗿𝗲𝗺 𝗶𝗻 𝗧𝗵𝗿𝗲𝗲 𝗟𝗮𝗻𝗴𝘂𝗮𝗴𝗲𝘀

The governing principle, stated uniformly across all three:

    A G-invariant elliptic operator on a non-compact space with group
    symmetry has an L²-index (or spectral gap, or band-limited zero
    structure) that is controlled by a topological invariant of the group
    action, constructed from the same Chern–Weil data that governs the
    representation theory. Self-adjointness forces the index∕gap∕zeros to
    be real; the von Neumann or Gaussian measure converts the non-compact
    geometry into a finite datum.

In Connes–Moscovici this becomes the L²-index theorem. In Sevostyanov it
becomes the mass gap theorem for the abelian case and the conjecture for
the non-abelian case. In the RH narrative it becomes the band-limitation
and Hardy-space framework.

The non-abelian Yang–Mills open problem — constructing the non-Gaussian
measure dμ ∝ ψ e^{−2 CS(A)} dA for a non-abelian gauge group — is exactly
the problem the CM theorem would address if extended to infinite-dimensional
G. In CM, the formal degree deg(π) of a discrete series representation is
the analogue of the mass m: a positive real number measuring the "density"
of π in L²(G), just as m measures the spectral gap. The reciprocity theorem
(CM Proposition 7.3A),

    ind_G D = ∑_π (dim ker D_π − dim ker D_π∗) deg(π),

is the operator-algebraic analogue of the Wiener–Itô–Segal decomposition
of the mass-gap Fock space into particle sectors.

The remaining open problem — rigorous band-limitation of X(T) under uniform
control of the Riemann–Siegel remainder through the time change ψ — is the
zeta analogue of the Sevostyanov open problem. Both require constructing a
specific measure or spectral object on an infinite-dimensional space and
verifying its required properties. Both share the same logical structure:
the abelian or local case is fully rigorous; the non-abelian or global case
requires controlling a non-Gaussian or non-uniform object that is the
principal obstruction.
