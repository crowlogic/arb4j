\title{The Fractional Riccati--M\"untz--Pad\'{e} Theorem\\[2pt]
\large An Absolutely Convergent, Quadrature-Free, Closed-Form
European Call and Put Price under Rough Heston via Pad\'{e}
Resummation of the Cumulant Generating Function and a
Schwinger--Hermite Residue Series}
\author{Stephen Crowley}
\date{May 2026}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We give a self-contained construction of European call and put prices in
the rough Heston model that is closed form, absolutely convergent for every
admissible parameter set, and free of Fourier quadrature, cosine
truncation, and oscillatory integrands. The construction proceeds in four
movements. First, the cumulant generating function (cgf) of the log-return,
governed by a fractional Riccati equation, is expanded on the M\"untz
lattice $\{T^{k\mu+1}\}$ by a Tau recurrence and re-cast as a power series
$T\,\widetilde\Phi(T^{\mu};v)$ in $z=T^{\mu}$, with polynomial-in-$v$
coefficients $d_k(v)$. Second, $\widetilde\Phi$ is resummed by its diagonal
$[M/M]$ Pad\'{e} approximant, whose denominator is the degree-$M$ orthogonal
polynomial of the Hankel functional generated by $\{d_k(v)\}$ and is
produced by the Chebyshev three-term recurrence in $O(M^2)$ ring operations,
with no Hankel system solved. The resulting characteristic exponent
$\Phi_M(\cdot,T)$ is rational in the Fourier variable, has a strictly
negative quadratic symbol at infinity (hence Gaussian decay of $\phi_M$ on
the real axis), and the conjugate-symmetric normalisations
$\phi_M(0,T)=\phi_M(-i,T)=1$ that enforce the martingale property. Third,
the call and put are written by Parseval pairing against the bilateral
Laplace transform of the payoff; the integrand is identical for the two
options and the contours differ only by their abscissa, so put--call parity
emerges exactly as the residue difference at $w\in\{0,1\}$. Fourth, the jump
part of $\phi_M$ is expanded multinomially and integrated against a
Schwinger--Gauss--erfc kernel whose every derivative is closed form through
a two-term polynomial recurrence and the Hermite functions; the price is an
absolutely convergent multi-index series with the explicit majorant
$\prod_j e^{|A_j|/\rho}<\infty$. The pole-free limit reproduces the
Black--Scholes formula exactly, fixing all normalisations. The only special
function used is $\erfc$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and exegesis}\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{The object and the obstruction}

The rough Heston model of El~Euch and Rosenbaum~\cite{ElEuchRosenbaum}
replaces the square-root variance of classical Heston by a fractional
(Volterra) square-root process of Hurst index $H\in(0,\tfrac12)$. Writing
$X_T=\log(S_T/S_0)-rT$ for the discounted log-return, its characteristic
function $\phi_T(v)=\E[e^{ivX_T}]=\exp\Phi(v,T)$ is determined not by an
ordinary Riccati ordinary differential equation, as in classical Heston,
but by a \emph{fractional} Riccati equation of order $\mu=H+\tfrac12\in
(\tfrac12,1)$, whose solution has no elementary closed form. This is the
central computational obstruction: there is no finite formula for
$\Phi(v,T)$, only a fractional power series in $T^{\mu}$.

Three families of remedies are in use. (i) Direct numerical solution of the
fractional Riccati on a time grid, followed by Fourier inversion; this is
accurate but couples a Volterra solve to every Fourier node and every
maturity. (ii) Rational (Pad\'e) approximation of the cgf in the maturity
variable, in the spirit of Gatheral and Radoi\v{c}i\'c~\cite{GatheralRadoicic},
which sidesteps the Volterra solve but still terminates in a Fourier
quadrature for the price. (iii) Polynomial or moment expansions of the
density, which fail to converge for the heavy (exponential) tails of
stochastic-volatility models: the Gram--Charlier/Edgeworth series in Hermite
\emph{polynomials} requires the density to decay faster than $e^{-x^2/4}$,
a condition violated by every model with finite critical moments. The
present paper belongs to family (ii) but removes its final quadrature
entirely, and it borrows the analytic vocabulary of family (iii) --- the
Hermite \emph{functions} $H_{n}(z)e^{-z^2}$ --- in a manner that converges
\emph{unconditionally}, because the series we build is the Taylor series of
an entire exponential rather than an orthogonal expansion of a density.

\subsection{Why resum the generator, not the solution}

The fractional Riccati \eqref{eq:riccati} produces a Puiseux series
$h(v,t)=\sum_{k\ge1}a_k(v)t^{k\mu}$ whose coefficients obey a quadratic
convolution recurrence (Theorem~\ref{thm:tau}). One could Pad\'e-resum
$h$ and then integrate. It is both cheaper and analytically cleaner to
resum the \emph{cgf} directly. The cgf is a single fractional integral of a
quadratic functional of $h$ (equation \eqref{eq:Phi}), so its M\"untz
coefficients $d_k(v)$ are explicit linear combinations of the $a_k(v)$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:cgf}); the diagonal Pad\'e of $\widetilde\Phi$ then has a
denominator that is exactly the orthogonal polynomial of the Hankel
functional $\Lc[w^k]=d_k(v)$, and the entire approximant is generated by the
Chebyshev recurrence (Theorem~\ref{thm:cheb}) in $O(M^2)$ scalar operations
without ever forming or inverting a Hankel matrix. Resummation of the
generator also preserves, exactly and at every order $M$, the two algebraic
normalisations $\Phi_M(0,T)=\Phi_M(-i,T)=0$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:norm}) that
encode $\phi_M(0)=1$ (probability) and $\phi_M(-i)=1$ (martingale); these
are precisely the identities from which put--call parity will follow.

\subsection{Why the price is then elementary}

A rational cgf means $\phi_M(v)=\exp\Phi_M(v)$ is the exponential of a
rational function. Euclidean division separates its quadratic part at
infinity from a strictly proper remainder, giving the partial-fraction
representation \eqref{eq:PFE}:
\[
\Phi_M(u,T)=-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2u^2-i\mu_T u
+\sum_{j=1}^{2M}\frac{A_j(T)}{u-u_j(T)} .
\]
The quadratic term is a Gaussian factor in the Fourier variable; the proper
term is a finite product of exponentials of simple poles. After the change
of variable that turns the option integral into a vertical-contour integral
(Section~\ref{sec:transform}), the Gaussian factor pairs with each simple
pole through one elementary contour integral --- the
\emph{Schwinger--Gauss--erfc identity} (Lemma~\ref{lem:E}) --- whose value
is $\tfrac12 e^{g}\erfc(z)$ plus an indicator term. Expanding the product of
pole exponentials multinomially (Lemma~\ref{lem:cauchy}) and applying the
identity term by term yields the price as an absolutely convergent series
in $\erfc$ and its derivatives, the latter being Hermite functions
(Lemma~\ref{lem:dE}). No quadrature node, no truncation interval, no
contour deformation is left in the final formula.

\subsection{What ``optimal and elegant'' means here, and how it is certified}

We take the optimal solution to be the one that (a) is closed form in a
single classical special function, (b) converges absolutely for every
admissible parameter set with an a~priori majorant, (c) treats call and put
as one object with parity built in, and (d) reduces, in the limit of no
jumps, to the Black--Scholes formula with no residual constant. Property (d)
is the sharpest certificate of correctness for the normalisations, and we
prove it as Corollary~\ref{cor:BS}: setting all residues $A_j=0$ in
\eqref{eq:Cprice} returns $S_0N(d_1)-Ke^{-rT}N(d_2)$ exactly, which is what
forces the prefactor $Ke^{-rT}$, the centring $\xi=\tilde k+\mu_T$, and the
pole image $\hat u_j=iu_j$.

\subsection{Scope of the proofs}

Every elementary statement is proved in complete detail, with all
intermediate equations displayed. Three results are used as cited external
inputs because their proofs are book-length or paper-length and orthogonal
to our construction: Stahl's theorem on the convergence of diagonal Pad\'e
approximants to functions with branch points~\cite{Stahl}, the existence
and analyticity of the rough Heston characteristic
function~\cite{ElEuchRosenbaum}, and the Parseval/Lewis contour
representation of European option values~\cite{Lewis,Widder}. Their
hypotheses are verified at the point of use. Crucially, the absolute
convergence of the price series at fixed $M$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:absconv}),
which is the assertion ``convergent for all valid parameter sets,'' uses
none of the three: it rests only on the Gaussian factor and the elementary
majorant.

\section{Notation, hypotheses, and external inputs}\label{sec:standing}

\subsection{Model and transforms}

The rough Heston parameters are the Hurst index $H\in(0,\tfrac12)$, mean
reversion $\lambda>0$, level $\theta\ge0$, vol-of-vol $\nu>0$, correlation
$\rho\in[-1,0]$, initial variance $V_0\ge0$ (with $\theta,V_0$ not both
zero), and rate $r\ge0$. Set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mu-def}
\mu:=H+\tfrac12\in(\tfrac12,1).
\end{equation}
The discounted log-return is $X_T:=\log(S_T/S_0)-rT$, the characteristic
function is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cf-def}
\phi_T(v):=\E\!\left[e^{ivX_T}\right]=\exp\Phi(v,T),\qquad v\in\C,
\end{equation}
and $p(x)$ denotes the (risk-neutral) density of $X_T$ when it exists. The
moneyness constant is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ktilde}
\tilde k:=\log(K/S_0)-rT,\qquad\text{so}\qquad
K=S_0\,e^{rT+\tilde k},\qquad e^{\tilde k}=\tfrac{K}{S_0}\,e^{-rT}.
\end{equation}
The bilateral Laplace transform of a function $f$ is
$\hat f(w):=\int_\R f(x)e^{-wx}\,dx$, and the moment transform of $X_T$ is
$\E[e^{wX_T}]=\phi_T(-iw)=\exp\Phi(-iw,T)$ wherever it is finite.

\subsection{Standing hypotheses}

The following are assumed from Section~\ref{sec:struct} onward and are
discharged or shown standard where indicated.

\begin{assumption}[Quasi-definiteness]\label{A1}
For the fixed argument $v$, the Hankel determinants
$H_n(v):=\det\bigl(d_{i+j}(v)\bigr)_{0\le i,j<n}$ are nonzero for
$1\le n\le M$. \textup{(Generic; failure is a measure-zero event in
parameter space and is detected at run time by $h_k=0$ in
Theorem~\ref{thm:cheb}.)}
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Negative quadratic symbol]\label{A2}
The polynomial part at infinity, in the Fourier variable $u$, of the
rational exponent $\Phi_M(\cdot,T)$ is quadratic with
$\sigma_T^2:=-2\cdot[\text{coefficient of }u^2\text{ in }\Phi_M]>0$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Poles off the axis, in the lower half-plane]\label{A3}
The $2M$ poles $u_j(T)$ of $\Phi_M(\cdot,T)$ satisfy $\Imag u_j(T)<0$, and
a contour abscissa exists at unit separation: for the call, some
$c\in(1,p^*)$, and for the put, some $c_P\in(-p^*,0)$, where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pstar}
p^*:=\min_{1\le j\le 2M}\bigl|\Imag u_j(T)\bigr|,
\end{equation}
with the running separation $\rho:=\min_j|c-\Imag u_j|\wedge\min_j
|\Imag u_j|\ge1$. \textup{(Pole location is proved for large $M$ in
Proposition~\ref{prop:strip}.)}
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Critical moment above one]\label{A4}
$p^*>1$; equivalently $\E[S_T^{1+\epsilon}]<\infty$ for some $\epsilon>0$.
\textup{(Standard for rough Heston below the moment-explosion time; it is
the right-tail condition placing the call contour at $c\in(1,p^*)$.)}
\end{assumption}

\subsection{External inputs}

\begin{description}
\item[(E1) Parseval/Lewis representation~\cite{Widder,Lewis}.] If $f$ and
$p$ have overlapping vertical/horizontal strips of analyticity for their
bilateral Laplace and Fourier transforms, the pairing $\int_\R f(x)p(x)\,dx$
equals the contour integral obtained by substituting the inverse transform
of $f$ and exchanging integrals; absolute integrability on the contour
(furnished by Lemma~\ref{lem:decay} and the payoff transforms of
Section~\ref{sec:transform}) justifies the exchange.
\item[(E2) Rough Heston cf~\cite{ElEuchRosenbaum}.] The function
$\Phi(\cdot,T)$ of \eqref{eq:Phi}--\eqref{eq:riccati} is finite and analytic
on the closed upper half-plane $\overline{\{\Imag v\ge0\}}$ minus
$\{0,-i\}$; on $\R$ the Volterra fixed point is bounded. Used only in
Proposition~\ref{prop:strip}.
\item[(E3) Stahl's theorem~\cite{Stahl}.] Diagonal Pad\'e approximants of a
function holomorphic at the origin with a finite branch set converge in
capacity, geometrically, off the minimal-capacity cut. Used only in
Theorem~\ref{thm:stahl} for the rate of $C_M\to C$ as $M\to\infty$; it is
not used for the fixed-$M$ convergence of Theorem~\ref{thm:absconv}.
\end{description}

The classical special-function facts used are the Beta--Gamma identity
$B(\alpha,\beta)=\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)/\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)$, the
Gaussian integral $\int_\R e^{-At^2+iBt}\,dt=\sqrt{\pi/A}\,e^{-B^2/(4A)}$
for $\Real A>0$, the definition $\erfc(z)=\tfrac{2}{\sqrt\pi}\int_z^\infty
e^{-u^2}\,du$, the CDF relation $N(x)=\tfrac12\erfc(-x/\sqrt2)$, and the
Hermite identity proved in Lemma~\ref{lem:hermite}; see~\cite{Olver}.

\section{Fractional calculus and the M\"untz lattice}\label{sec:fc}

\begin{definition}[Riemann--Liouville operators]\label{def:RL}
For $\alpha>0$ and $f$ locally integrable on $(0,\infty)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:RL-def}
I^{\alpha}f(t):=\frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\int_0^t(t-s)^{\alpha-1}f(s)\,ds,
\qquad
\Ico f(t):=\frac{1}{\Gamma(1-\mu)}\int_0^t(t-s)^{-\mu}f(s)\,ds .
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The single computational fact we need about $I^{\alpha}$ is its action on
powers.

\begin{lemma}[Power rule]\label{lem:power}
For $s>-1$ and $\alpha>0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:power}
I^{\alpha}t^{s}=\frac{\Gamma(s+1)}{\Gamma(s+\alpha+1)}\,t^{s+\alpha}.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Definition~\ref{def:RL},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:power-1}
I^{\alpha}t^{s}=\frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\int_0^t(t-\sigma)^{\alpha-1}
\sigma^{s}\,d\sigma .
\end{equation}
Substitute $\sigma=t\tau$, $d\sigma=t\,d\tau$, $\tau\in[0,1]$, and factor
$(t-t\tau)^{\alpha-1}=t^{\alpha-1}(1-\tau)^{\alpha-1}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:power-2}
I^{\alpha}t^{s}
=\frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\int_0^1 t^{\alpha-1}(1-\tau)^{\alpha-1}\,
t^{s}\tau^{s}\,t\,d\tau
=\frac{t^{s+\alpha}}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\int_0^1(1-\tau)^{\alpha-1}\tau^{s}
\,d\tau .
\end{equation}
The integral converges at $\tau=0$ because $s>-1$ and at $\tau=1$ because
$\alpha>0$, and equals the Beta function $B(\alpha,s+1)$. The Beta--Gamma
identity gives $B(\alpha,s+1)=\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(s+1)/\Gamma(s+\alpha+1)$,
so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:power-3}
I^{\alpha}t^{s}=\frac{t^{s+\alpha}}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\cdot
\frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(s+1)}{\Gamma(s+\alpha+1)}
=\frac{\Gamma(s+1)}{\Gamma(s+\alpha+1)}\,t^{s+\alpha},
\end{equation}
the factor $\Gamma(\alpha)$ cancelling.
\end{proof}

Define the lattice ratios
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-def}
\gamma_k:=\frac{\Gamma((k-1)\mu+1)}{\Gamma(k\mu+1)},\qquad k\ge1,
\qquad\text{so that}\qquad
\Imu t^{(k-1)\mu}=\gamma_k\,t^{k\mu}
\end{equation}
by \eqref{eq:power} with $s=(k-1)\mu$, $\alpha=\mu$. Writing $z=t^{\mu}$,
the lattice $\{t^{k\mu}\}_{k\ge0}=\{z^k\}_{k\ge0}$ is mapped to itself by
$\Imu$ and is closed under multiplication; hence all formal manipulations
below reduce to ordinary power-series algebra in $z$.

\section{The M\"untz--Tau recurrence}\label{sec:tau}

\begin{theorem}[M\"untz--Tau recurrence]\label{thm:tau}
The unique formal solution $y(t)=\sum_{k\ge1}a_kt^{k\mu}$ of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tau-ivp}
y=\Imu\!\left[c_0+c_1y+c_2y^2\right],\qquad y(0)=0,
\end{equation}
has coefficients
\begin{align}
a_1&=\frac{c_0}{\Gamma(\mu+1)},\label{eq:a1}\\
a_k&=\gamma_k\!\left[\,c_1\,a_{k-1}+c_2\sum_{j=1}^{k-2}a_j\,a_{k-1-j}\right],
\qquad k\ge2.\label{eq:ak}
\end{align}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Insert the ansatz $y=\sum_{m\ge1}a_mt^{m\mu}$. The Cauchy product gives the
square
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tau-square}
y^2=\Bigl(\sum_{m\ge1}a_mt^{m\mu}\Bigr)^{\!2}
=\sum_{m\ge2}\Bigl(\sum_{j=1}^{m-1}a_j a_{m-j}\Bigr)t^{m\mu}.
\end{equation}
Apply $\Imu$ to each constituent of the bracket in \eqref{eq:tau-ivp}, using
\eqref{eq:power} and \eqref{eq:gamma-def}:
\begin{align}
\Imu c_0&=\frac{c_0}{\Gamma(\mu+1)}\,t^{\mu},\label{eq:tau-c0}\\
\Imu(c_1 y)&=c_1\sum_{m\ge1}a_m\,\gamma_{m+1}\,t^{(m+1)\mu},
\label{eq:tau-c1}\\
\Imu(c_2 y^2)&=c_2\sum_{m\ge2}\Bigl(\sum_{j=1}^{m-1}a_j a_{m-j}\Bigr)
\gamma_{m+1}\,t^{(m+1)\mu}.\label{eq:tau-c2}
\end{align}
Now match the coefficient of $t^{k\mu}$ on the two sides of
\eqref{eq:tau-ivp}. For $k=1$ only \eqref{eq:tau-c0} contributes, giving
$a_1=c_0/\Gamma(\mu+1)$, which is \eqref{eq:a1}. For $k\ge2$ the linear term
\eqref{eq:tau-c1} contributes through $m=k-1$ (so that $(m+1)\mu=k\mu$),
yielding $c_1 a_{k-1}\gamma_k$, and the quadratic term \eqref{eq:tau-c2}
contributes through $m=k-1$, yielding
$c_2\gamma_k\sum_{j=1}^{k-2}a_j a_{k-1-j}$. Summing,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tau-match}
a_k=\gamma_k\Bigl[c_1 a_{k-1}+c_2\sum_{j=1}^{k-2}a_j a_{k-1-j}\Bigr],
\end{equation}
which is \eqref{eq:ak}. Since $a_k$ is determined by $a_1,\dots,a_{k-1}$,
the solution is unique.
\end{proof}

\section{The rough Heston cumulant lattice}\label{sec:cgf}

The rough Heston cgf is, by~\cite{ElEuchRosenbaum},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phi}
\Phi(v,T)=\lambda\theta\!\int_0^T h(v,s)\,ds+V_0\,\Ico h(v,\cdot)(T),
\end{equation}
where $h$ solves the fractional Riccati
\begin{equation}\label{eq:riccati}
h=\Imu\!\left[P(v)+Q(v)\,h+R\,h^2\right],\qquad
\begin{aligned}
P(v)&=-\tfrac12\bigl(v^2+iv\bigr),\\
Q(v)&=\lambda\bigl(iv\rho\nu-1\bigr),\\
R&=\tfrac12\lambda^2\nu^2 .
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The exegesis of \eqref{eq:riccati}: $\Imu$ is the fractional analogue of
time integration that the Volterra square-root variance induces; $P$ is the
diffusive symbol of the log-price (the source of the eventual Gaussian part
of $\phi_M$); $Q$ carries the mean reversion and the leverage $\rho$; $R$ is
the vol-of-vol curvature. Classical Heston is the boundary case $\mu=1$.

\begin{lemma}[Riccati Puiseux coefficients]\label{lem:h}
The unique formal solution of \eqref{eq:riccati} is
$h(v,t)=\sum_{k\ge1}a_k(v)t^{k\mu}$ with $\{a_k\}$ given by
Theorem~\ref{thm:tau} at $(c_0,c_1,c_2)=(P(v),Q(v),R)$. Each $a_k\in\C[v]$
satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-deg}
\deg a_k\le k+1,\qquad a_k(0)=a_k(-i)=0 .
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The representation is Theorem~\ref{thm:tau}. We prove \eqref{eq:h-deg} by
strong induction. Base case:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-base}
a_1(v)=\frac{P(v)}{\Gamma(\mu+1)}=-\frac{v^2+iv}{2\Gamma(\mu+1)}
=-\frac{v(v+i)}{2\Gamma(\mu+1)},
\end{equation}
of degree $2=1+1$, vanishing at $v=0$ and $v=-i$. Inductive step: assume
$\deg a_j\le j+1$ and $a_j(0)=a_j(-i)=0$ for all $1\le j\le k-1$. In
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-rec}
a_k(v)=\gamma_k\Bigl[Q(v)\,a_{k-1}(v)
+R\sum_{j=1}^{k-2}a_j(v)\,a_{k-1-j}(v)\Bigr],
\end{equation}
the term $Q a_{k-1}$ has degree at most $\deg Q+\deg a_{k-1}\le 1+k=k+1$,
and each product $a_j a_{k-1-j}$ has degree at most
$(j+1)+(k-1-j+1)=k+1$. Every summand on the right of \eqref{eq:h-rec}
contains a factor $a_m$ with $m\le k-1$, which by hypothesis vanishes at
$v=0$ and $v=-i$; therefore $a_k(0)=a_k(-i)=0$. Hence $\deg a_k\le k+1$ and
the root conditions persist.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[cgf as a M\"untz-lattice power series]\label{thm:cgf}
With $a_0:=0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:uw-def}
u_k=\begin{cases}0,&k=0,\\[2pt]\dfrac{\lambda\theta}{k\mu+1},&k\ge1,
\end{cases}
\qquad
w_k=\begin{cases}V_0\,\Gamma(\mu+1),&k=0,\\[4pt]
V_0\,\dfrac{\Gamma((k+1)\mu+1)}{\Gamma(k\mu+2)},&k\ge1,\end{cases}
\end{equation}
define the lattice coefficients
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dk}
\boxed{\,d_k(v):=u_k\,a_k(v)+w_k\,a_{k+1}(v)\ \in\ \C[v],\qquad
\deg d_k\le k+2,\ \ d_k(0)=d_k(-i)=0.\,}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phi-series}
\Phi(v,T)=\sum_{k\ge0}d_k(v)\,T^{k\mu+1}=T\,\widetilde\Phi(T^{\mu};v),
\qquad
\widetilde\Phi(z;v):=\sum_{k\ge0}d_k(v)\,z^{k}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Insert Lemma~\ref{lem:h} into \eqref{eq:Phi}. The first term integrates by
$\int_0^T s^{j\mu}\,ds=T^{j\mu+1}/(j\mu+1)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cgf-1}
\lambda\theta\!\int_0^T h(v,s)\,ds
=\lambda\theta\sum_{j\ge1}a_j(v)\int_0^T s^{j\mu}\,ds
=\sum_{j\ge1}\frac{\lambda\theta}{j\mu+1}\,a_j(v)\,T^{j\mu+1}
=\sum_{k\ge1}u_k\,a_k(v)\,T^{k\mu+1}.
\end{equation}
The second term uses Lemma~\ref{lem:power} with $\alpha=1-\mu$, $s=j\mu$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cgf-2a}
\Ico t^{j\mu}=\frac{\Gamma(j\mu+1)}{\Gamma(j\mu+1+(1-\mu))}\,
t^{j\mu+1-\mu}=\frac{\Gamma(j\mu+1)}{\Gamma((j-1)\mu+2)}\,t^{(j-1)\mu+1},
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cgf-2b}
V_0\,\Ico h(v,\cdot)(T)
=V_0\sum_{j\ge1}\frac{\Gamma(j\mu+1)}{\Gamma((j-1)\mu+2)}\,a_j(v)\,
T^{(j-1)\mu+1}
\overset{j=k+1}{=}\sum_{k\ge0}w_k\,a_{k+1}(v)\,T^{k\mu+1},
\end{equation}
where the reindexing $j=k+1$ produces
$w_k=V_0\Gamma((k+1)\mu+1)/\Gamma(k\mu+2)$ for $k\ge0$, and at $k=0$,
$w_0=V_0\Gamma(\mu+1)/\Gamma(2)=V_0\Gamma(\mu+1)$ since $\Gamma(2)=1$, in
agreement with \eqref{eq:uw-def}. Adding \eqref{eq:cgf-1} and
\eqref{eq:cgf-2b}, the coefficient of $T^{k\mu+1}$ is
$u_k a_k(v)+w_k a_{k+1}(v)=d_k(v)$, which is \eqref{eq:dk}; the degree bound
is $\deg d_k\le\max(\deg a_k,\deg a_{k+1})\le\max(k+1,k+2)=k+2$, and the root
conditions follow from Lemma~\ref{lem:h}. Factoring $T$ gives
\eqref{eq:Phi-series}.
\end{proof}

\section{Orthogonal polynomials of the cgf functional}\label{sec:op}

Fix $v$. Define the linear functional $\Lc:\C[w]\to\C$ on the monomial basis
by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:L-def}
\Lc[w^{k}]:=d_k(v),\qquad k\ge0,
\end{equation}
extended linearly. Under Assumption~\ref{A1} we construct its orthogonal
polynomials, whose reversal will be the Pad\'e denominator.

\begin{lemma}[Determinant form of the orthogonal polynomials]\label{lem:exist}
For $0\le n\le M$ there is a unique monic polynomial $p_n$, $\deg p_n=n$,
with $\Lc[w^{k}p_n]=0$ for $0\le k<n$. With $H_n=\det(d_{i+j})_{0\le i,j<n}$
and $H_0:=1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:OPdet}
p_0=1,\qquad
p_n(w)=\frac{1}{H_n}\det\!
\begin{pmatrix}
d_0&d_1&\cdots&d_n\\
d_1&d_2&\cdots&d_{n+1}\\
\vdots&\vdots&&\vdots\\
d_{n-1}&d_n&\cdots&d_{2n-1}\\
1&w&\cdots&w^{n}
\end{pmatrix}
\quad(n\ge1),
\qquad
h_n:=\Lc[w^np_n]=\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}\neq0 .
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Expand the determinant in \eqref{eq:OPdet} along its last row
$(1,w,\dots,w^n)$. The cofactor of $w^n$ is the determinant of the matrix
with last row and last column deleted, namely $H_n$, so the leading
coefficient of $p_n$ is $H_n/H_n=1$: $p_n$ is monic of degree $n$. For
$0\le k<n$ apply $\Lc[w^k\,\cdot\,]$ inside the determinant, which replaces
the last row by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:OP-row}
\Lc\!\left[w^k(1,w,\dots,w^n)\right]
=(\Lc[w^k],\Lc[w^{k+1}],\dots,\Lc[w^{k+n}])
=(d_k,d_{k+1},\dots,d_{k+n}).
\end{equation}
For $k\le n-1$ this coincides with row $k+1$ of the matrix (the rows indexed
$0,\dots,n-1$ are $(d_i,d_{i+1},\dots,d_{i+n})$); the determinant then has
two identical rows and vanishes, so $\Lc[w^kp_n]=0$. For $k=n$ the last row
becomes $(d_n,d_{n+1},\dots,d_{2n})$ and the determinant is exactly
$H_{n+1}$, whence $h_n=\Lc[w^np_n]=H_{n+1}/H_n$, nonzero by
Assumption~\ref{A1}. Uniqueness: if $p_n,p_n'$ are two monic solutions then
$q:=p_n-p_n'$ has degree $<n$ and $\Lc[w^kq]=0$ for $0\le k<n$; writing
$q=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}b_kw^k$ and pairing with $w^\ell$ gives the homogeneous
linear system $\sum_k b_k d_{k+\ell}=0$, $0\le\ell\le n-1$, whose coefficient
matrix $(d_{k+\ell})_{0\le k,\ell<n}$ has determinant $H_n\neq0$; hence all
$b_k=0$ and $q\equiv0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Three-term recurrence]\label{thm:3term}
There exist scalars $\alpha_n,\beta_n$ with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:3term}
p_{n+1}(w)=(w-\alpha_n)p_n(w)-\beta_n p_{n-1}(w),\qquad p_0=1,\ p_{-1}=0,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ab-def}
\alpha_n=\frac{\Lc[w\,p_n^2]}{h_n}\ \ (n\ge0),\qquad
\beta_n=\frac{h_n}{h_{n-1}}\ \ (n\ge1),\qquad \beta_0:=0 .
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $w p_n$ is monic of degree $n+1$, expand it in the orthogonal basis:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:3t-expand}
w p_n=p_{n+1}+\sum_{\ell=0}^{n}c_\ell\,p_\ell .
\end{equation}
Apply $\Lc[\,\cdot\,p_m]$ and use orthogonality $\Lc[p_\ell p_m]=h_\ell
\delta_{\ell m}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:3t-pair}
\Lc[w p_n p_m]=c_m h_m,\qquad 0\le m\le n .
\end{equation}
But $\Lc[w p_n p_m]=\Lc[p_n\,(w p_m)]$, and $w p_m$ has degree $m+1$; for
$m+1<n$, i.e.\ $m\le n-2$, this is $\Lc$ of $p_n$ against a polynomial of
degree $<n$, hence $0$ by orthogonality, so $c_m=0$. Thus
\eqref{eq:3t-expand} reduces to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:3t-reduced}
w p_n=p_{n+1}+c_n p_n+c_{n-1}p_{n-1}.
\end{equation}
Set $\alpha_n:=c_n$ and $\beta_n:=c_{n-1}$. From \eqref{eq:3t-pair} at $m=n$,
$\alpha_n=\Lc[w p_n^2]/h_n$, the first part of \eqref{eq:ab-def}. For
$\beta_n$, use $w p_{n-1}=p_n+(\text{lower order})$ (monicity), so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:3t-beta}
\Lc[w p_n p_{n-1}]=\Lc[p_n\,(w p_{n-1})]
=\Lc[p_n\,(p_n+\text{lower})]=\Lc[p_n^2]=h_n,
\end{equation}
while \eqref{eq:3t-pair} at $m=n-1$ gives $\Lc[wp_np_{n-1}]=\beta_n h_{n-1}$;
equating, $\beta_n=h_n/h_{n-1}$. Rearranging \eqref{eq:3t-reduced} yields
\eqref{eq:3term}, with $\beta_0:=0$ since $p_{-1}:=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Chebyshev recurrence; $O(M^2)$, no matrix solve]\label{thm:cheb}
Let $\sigma_{k,l}:=\Lc[p_k\,w^{l}]$ for $k\ge-1$, $l\ge0$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-init}
\sigma_{-1,l}=0,\qquad\sigma_{0,l}=d_l,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-rec}
\sigma_{k+1,l}=\sigma_{k,l+1}-\alpha_k\,\sigma_{k,l}-\beta_k\,\sigma_{k-1,l},
\end{equation}
and the coefficients are extracted by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-extract0}
\alpha_0=\frac{d_1}{d_0},\qquad\beta_0=0,\qquad h_0=d_0,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-extractk}
\alpha_k=\frac{\sigma_{k,k+1}}{\sigma_{k,k}}
-\frac{\sigma_{k-1,k}}{\sigma_{k-1,k-1}},\qquad
\beta_k=\frac{\sigma_{k,k}}{\sigma_{k-1,k-1}},\qquad
h_k=\sigma_{k,k}\quad(k\ge1).
\end{equation}
The total cost is $O(M^2)$ ring operations on $\{d_l\}_{l=0}^{2M-1}$, with
no Hankel matrix formed or inverted, and the arithmetic is exact in any
fixed precision.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The initial values are $\sigma_{0,l}=\Lc[p_0w^l]=\Lc[w^l]=d_l$ and
$\sigma_{-1,l}=0$ from $p_{-1}=0$. Multiply the recurrence
\eqref{eq:3term} by $w^l$ and apply $\Lc$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-pf1}
\Lc[p_{k+1}w^l]=\Lc[p_k w^{l+1}]-\alpha_k\Lc[p_k w^l]
-\beta_k\Lc[p_{k-1}w^l],
\end{equation}
which is \eqref{eq:cheb-rec}. Orthogonality gives $\sigma_{k,l}=0$ for
$l<<k$, and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-pf2}
h_k=\Lc[p_k w^k]=\Lc[p_k\,(p_k+\text{lower order})]=\Lc[p_k^2]=\sigma_{k,k},
\end{equation}
using monicity $w^k=p_k+\text{lower}$. Set $l=k$ in \eqref{eq:cheb-rec} and
use $\sigma_{k+1,k}=0$ (since $k<<k+1$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-pf3}
0=\sigma_{k,k+1}-\alpha_k\sigma_{k,k}-\beta_k\sigma_{k-1,k}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\alpha_k=\frac{\sigma_{k,k+1}}{\sigma_{k,k}}
-\beta_k\frac{\sigma_{k-1,k}}{\sigma_{k,k}} .
\end{equation}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:3term}, $\beta_k=h_k/h_{k-1}=\sigma_{k,k}/
\sigma_{k-1,k-1}$, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-pf4}
\beta_k\frac{\sigma_{k-1,k}}{\sigma_{k,k}}
=\frac{\sigma_{k,k}}{\sigma_{k-1,k-1}}\cdot\frac{\sigma_{k-1,k}}{\sigma_{k,k}}
=\frac{\sigma_{k-1,k}}{\sigma_{k-1,k-1}},
\end{equation}
which substituted into \eqref{eq:cheb-pf3} gives the stated $\alpha_k$ in
\eqref{eq:cheb-extractk}. The $k=0$ values come from $\alpha_0=\Lc[wp_0^2]/
h_0=\Lc[w]/\Lc[1]=d_1/d_0$ and $h_0=d_0$. Finally, each entry
$\sigma_{k,l}$ with $0\le k\le M$ is computed by one application of
\eqref{eq:cheb-rec} (two multiplications and two subtractions); the
triangular array $\{\sigma_{k,l}:0\le k\le M,\ k\le l\le 2M-k\}$ has
$O(M^2)$ entries, so the cost is $O(M^2)$, and no linear system is solved.
\end{proof}

\section{Pad\'e resummation}\label{sec:pade}

The Pad\'e denominator is the reversal of $p_M$, and the matching condition
is exactly the orthogonality relations of Section~\ref{sec:op}.

\begin{theorem}[Pad\'e matching from the reversed orthogonal polynomial]
\label{thm:match}
Write $p_M(w)=\sum_{i=0}^M\pi_i w^i$ with $\pi_M=1$, and set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:QP-def}
Q_M(z):=z^M p_M(1/z)=\sum_{i=0}^M\pi_i z^{M-i}=\sum_{j=0}^M q_j z^{j},
\quad q_j:=\pi_{M-j},\ q_0=1,
\end{equation}
and let $P_M(z):=\bigl[Q_M(z)\widetilde\Phi(z;v)\bigr]_{\deg\le M-1}$ be the
truncation to degrees $\le M-1$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:match}
Q_M(z)\,\widetilde\Phi(z;v)-P_M(z)=h_M\,z^{2M}+O(z^{2M+1}),
\end{equation}
so $P_M/Q_M$ is the diagonal $[M/M]$ Pad\'e approximant of $\widetilde\Phi$,
$Q_M(0)=1$, $\deg P_M\le M-1$, $\deg Q_M\le M$, and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:PhiM-def}
\Phi_M(v,T):=T\,\frac{P_M(z;v)}{Q_M(z;v)}\bigg|_{z=T^{\mu}},\qquad
\phi_M(v,T):=\exp\Phi_M(v,T)
\end{equation}
is such that $\Phi_M(\cdot,T)$ is rational in $v$ at fixed $T$, with a finite
number of poles in $v$ (the $v$--degree of the denominator of $Q_M$ is
$O(M^2)$ generically, so the number of poles is $O(M^2)$).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The coefficient of $z^n$ in the Cauchy product $Q_M\widetilde\Phi$ is
$\sum_{i=0}^M q_i d_{n-i}$ with the convention $d_{<<0}:=0$. For
$M\le n\le 2M-1$ write $n=M+\ell$ with $0\le\ell\le M-1$; then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:match-pf1}
[z^{M+\ell}]\bigl(Q_M\widetilde\Phi\bigr)
=\sum_{i=0}^M q_i\,d_{M+\ell-i}
=\sum_{i=0}^M\pi_{M-i}\,d_{M+\ell-i}
\overset{i'=M-i}{=}\sum_{i'=0}^M\pi_{i'}\,d_{i'+\ell}
=\Lc\!\Bigl[\Bigl(\sum_{i'}\pi_{i'}w^{i'}\Bigr)w^{\ell}\Bigr]
=\Lc[p_M\,w^{\ell}]=0
\end{equation}
by orthogonality, since $\ell<M$. Thus the coefficients of
$z^{M},\dots,z^{2M-1}$ in $Q_M\widetilde\Phi$ vanish. Subtracting the
truncation $P_M$, which alters only degrees $\le M-1$, leaves
$Q_M\widetilde\Phi-P_M=O(z^{2M})$. Its $z^{2M}$ coefficient is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:match-pf2}
[z^{2M}]\bigl(Q_M\widetilde\Phi\bigr)
=\sum_{i=0}^M\pi_{M-i}\,d_{2M-i}
=\sum_{i'=0}^M\pi_{i'}\,d_{M+i'}
=\Lc[p_M\,w^{M}]=h_M\neq0,
\end{equation}
which is \eqref{eq:match}. Since $Q_M(0)=q_0=\pi_M=1$ and the degree bounds
hold, $P_M/Q_M$ is the diagonal Pad\'e approximant, unique under
Assumption~\ref{A1}.

Each $d_k\in\C[v]$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:cgf}) with $\deg d_k\le k+2$. The
coefficients $\pi_i$ are ratios of Hankel determinants built from
$\{d_0,\dots,d_{2M-1}\}$. The Hankel determinant $H_n$ has $v$--degree at
most $\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\max_{j}\deg(d_{i+j})\le\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(i+n+1)
=O(n^2)$. Hence the coefficients of $p_M$ are rational functions in $v$
whose denominator degree is $O(M^2)$. After reversal, $Q_M(z;v)$ is a
polynomial in $z$ with coefficients that are rational in $v$ with
denominator degree $O(M^2)$; the numerator $P_M$ inherits the same
denominator. Therefore $\Phi_M(\cdot,T)$ is a ratio of two polynomials in
$v$ whose denominator has degree $O(M^2)$, giving at most $O(M^2)$ poles in
$v$. The approximant $\phi_M=\exp\Phi_M$ is the exponential of a rational
function and is \emph{not} itself rational.
\end{proof}

\section{Convergence in the Pad\'e order}\label{sec:conv}

\begin{theorem}[Stahl rate]\label{thm:stahl}
Let $K(v)$ be the minimal-capacity (Stahl) compact of
$\widetilde\Phi(\cdot;v)$ and $\mathsf g(z,K(v))$ the Green function of
$\C\setminus K(v)$ with pole at infinity. Then, by \textup{(E3)},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stahl}
\bigl|\widetilde\Phi(z;v)-P_M(z)/Q_M(z)\bigr|^{1/(2M)}
\xrightarrow[M\to\infty]{}e^{-\mathsf g(z,K(v))}
\end{equation}
in capacity on $\C\setminus K(v)$, locally uniformly off $K(v)$; in
particular $\Phi_M(v,T)\to\Phi(v,T)$ for each $T$ with $T^{\mu}\notin K(v)$,
including $T$ beyond the Puiseux radius of $\widetilde\Phi(\cdot;v)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:cgf}, $\widetilde\Phi(\cdot;v)$ is holomorphic at the
origin, and by \textup{(E2)} the rough Heston cgf is analytic except at its
critical-moment branch points, so $\widetilde\Phi(\cdot;v)$ has a finite
branch set. Stahl's theorem \textup{(E3)}~\cite{Stahl} applies verbatim and
gives \eqref{eq:stahl} with the indicated geometric rate. Evaluating at
$z=T^{\mu}$ off $K(v)$ yields $\Phi_M(v,T)\to\Phi(v,T)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[A-posteriori error bound]\label{thm:apost}
Let $\Delta_n:=\Phi_n-\Phi_{n-1}$ and
$q_M:=\sup_{n\ge M}|\Delta_{n+1}|/|\Delta_n|$. If $q_M<<1$ then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:apost}
\bigl|\Phi(v,T)-\Phi_M(v,T)\bigr|\le\frac{|\Delta_M|}{1-q_M}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:stahl} the telescoping series
$\Phi-\Phi_M=\sum_{n>M}\Delta_n$ converges. From the definition of $q_M$,
$|\Delta_{M+j}|\le q_M^{\,j}|\Delta_M|$ for $j\ge1$ by induction. Hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:apost-pf}
|\Phi-\Phi_M|\le\sum_{j\ge1}|\Delta_{M+j}|
\le|\Delta_M|\sum_{j\ge1}q_M^{\,j}
=|\Delta_M|\,\frac{q_M}{1-q_M}\le\frac{|\Delta_M|}{1-q_M}.\qedhere
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\section{Structure of the rational exponent}\label{sec:struct}

\begin{lemma}[Normalisations and conjugate symmetry]\label{lem:norm}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:norm}
\Phi_M(0,T)=\Phi_M(-i,T)=0,\qquad
\phi_M(0,T)=\phi_M(-i,T)=1,\qquad
\phi_M(-\bar v,T)=\overline{\phi_M(v,T)} .
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:cgf}, $d_k(0)=d_k(-i)=0$ for all $k$, so
$\widetilde\Phi(z;0)\equiv\widetilde\Phi(z;-i)\equiv0$ as power series in
$z$. The Pad\'e data $(\pi_i,P_M,Q_M)$ are rational in the $d_k$ with
$Q_M(0)=1\neq0$; therefore $P_M(z;0)\equiv P_M(z;-i)\equiv0$, whence
$\Phi_M(0,T)=\Phi_M(-i,T)=0$ and $\phi_M=e^0=1$ there. The first identity is
the probability normalisation $\E[1]=1$; the second is the martingale
condition $\E[e^{X_T}]=\phi_M(-i)=1$. For the symmetry, observe
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sym-PQ}
P(-\bar v)=-\tfrac12\bigl((-\bar v)^2+i(-\bar v)\bigr)
=-\tfrac12\bigl(\bar v^2-i\bar v\bigr)=\overline{-\tfrac12(v^2+iv)}
=\overline{P(v)},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sym-Q}
Q(-\bar v)=\lambda\bigl(i(-\bar v)\rho\nu-1\bigr)
=\lambda\bigl(\overline{iv\rho\nu}-1\bigr)=\overline{Q(v)}\qquad
(\lambda,\rho,\nu\in\R),
\end{equation}
and $\overline R=R$. The recurrences \eqref{eq:a1}--\eqref{eq:ak} and
\eqref{eq:dk} have real rational coefficients $\gamma_k,u_k,w_k$, so by
induction $a_k(-\bar v)=\overline{a_k(v)}$ and
$d_k(-\bar v)=\overline{d_k(v)}$; rational operations preserve conjugation,
giving $\Phi_M(-\bar v,T)=\overline{\Phi_M(v,T)}$ and likewise for
$\phi_M=\exp\Phi_M$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Pole location in the lower half-plane]\label{prop:strip}
For $M$ large the $2M$ poles of $\Phi_M(\cdot,T)$ satisfy $\Imag u_j(T)<0$;
consequently the abscissae required in Assumption~\ref{A3} exist.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \textup{(E2)} the cgf $\Phi(\cdot,T)$ is finite and analytic on
$\overline{\{\Imag v\ge0\}}\setminus\{0,-i\}$, hence pole-free there.
Theorem~\ref{thm:stahl} gives $\Phi_M\to\Phi$ locally uniformly off
$K(v)$, equivalently $1/\Phi_M\to1/\Phi$ on compact subsets of
$\overline{\{\Imag v\ge0\}}\setminus\{0,-i\}$ where $\Phi\neq0$. The poles
of $\Phi_M$ are the zeros of $1/\Phi_M$; by Hurwitz's theorem they cannot
accumulate at points where the limit $1/\Phi$ is analytic and nonzero, so
for $M$ large no pole lies in the open upper half-plane. A real pole
$\Imag u_j=0$ is excluded because the Volterra fixed point of
\eqref{eq:riccati} is bounded on $\R$ by \textup{(E2)}, so $\Phi$ is finite
on $\R\setminus\{0\}$ and the same Hurwitz argument applies along $\R$.
Hence $\Imag u_j<<0$ for all $j$. Finitely many poles, all at strictly
negative imaginary part, leave a positive gap to the lines $\Real w=c$ and
$\Real w=c_P$, so admissible abscissae exist; under \ref{A4}, $c\in(1,p^*)$
is available.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Partial fractions and Gaussian decay]\label{lem:decay}
Under \ref{A2}--\ref{A3} the rational exponent admits the decomposition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:PFE}
\boxed{\;\Phi_M(u,T)=-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2u^2-i\mu_T u
+\sum_{j=1}^{2M}\frac{A_j(T)}{u-u_j(T)},
\qquad \sigma_T^2>0,\ \mu_T\in\R,\ \Imag u_j<<0,\;}
\end{equation}
the polynomial part being the part at infinity and the remainder strictly
proper with simple poles. For real $u$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gauss-decay}
|\phi_M(u,T)|\le e^{C_T}\,e^{-\sigma_T^2u^2/2},\qquad
C_T:=\sum_{j=1}^{2M}\frac{|A_j(T)|}{|\Imag u_j(T)|}<\infty .
\end{equation}
Moreover the normalisations of Lemma~\ref{lem:norm} read
\begin{equation}\label{eq:norm-constraints}
\sum_{j}\frac{A_j}{-u_j}=0,\qquad
\tfrac12\sigma_T^2-\mu_T+\sum_j\frac{A_j}{-i-u_j}=0 .
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Euclidean division of the rational function $\Phi_M(\cdot,T)$ in $u$
separates its unique polynomial part at infinity from a strictly proper
remainder. By \ref{A2} the polynomial part is quadratic with $u^2$-coefficient
$-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2<<0$; the imaginary linear coefficient is named $-i\mu_T$
with $\mu_T\in\R$, consistent with the reality structure of
Lemma~\ref{lem:norm} ($\Phi_M(-\bar u)=\overline{\Phi_M(u)}$ forces the
polynomial part to have real $u^2$- and purely imaginary $u$-coefficients).
The $2M$ poles are simple (generic; the proper remainder then expands in
simple fractions) and off the real axis (Proposition~\ref{prop:strip}),
giving \eqref{eq:PFE}. Taking real parts at real $u$:
$\Real(-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2u^2)=-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2u^2$,
$\Real(-i\mu_T u)=0$, and since
$|u-u_j|^2=(u-\Real u_j)^2+(\Imag u_j)^2\ge(\Imag u_j)^2$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decay-pf}
\Bigl|\Real\sum_j\frac{A_j}{u-u_j}\Bigr|
\le\sum_j\frac{|A_j|}{|u-u_j|}
\le\sum_j\frac{|A_j|}{|\Imag u_j|}=C_T<\infty .
\end{equation}
Hence $\Real\Phi_M(u)\le C_T-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2u^2$ and
$|\phi_M(u)|=e^{\Real\Phi_M(u)}\le e^{C_T}e^{-\sigma_T^2u^2/2}$. Evaluating
\eqref{eq:PFE} at $u=0$ and $u=-i$ with $\Phi_M(0)=\Phi_M(-i)=0$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:norm}) gives \eqref{eq:norm-constraints}.
\end{proof}

\section{Transform pairs and the option integrals from first principles}
\label{sec:transform}

We derive, from the definition of the option value, the vertical-contour
integrals for both the call and the put. The two share one integrand; the
difference is solely the contour abscissa, and put--call parity will be the
residue difference between them.

\begin{lemma}[Bilateral Laplace transforms of the payoffs]\label{lem:payoff}
Let $g_C(x):=(S_0e^{rT+x}-K)^{+}$ and $g_P(x):=(K-S_0e^{rT+x})^{+}$. Then
$g_C(x)=S_0e^{rT}(e^x-e^{\tilde k})^{+}$, $g_P(x)=S_0e^{rT}(e^{\tilde k}-e^x)^{+}$,
and with $\hat g(w):=\int_\R g(x)e^{-wx}\,dx$,
\begin{align}
\hat g_C(w)&=S_0e^{rT}\,\frac{e^{(1-w)\tilde k}}{w(w-1)},
&&\Real w>1,\label{eq:gChat}\\
\hat g_P(w)&=S_0e^{rT}\,\frac{e^{(1-w)\tilde k}}{w(w-1)},
&&\Real w<<0.\label{eq:gPhat}
\end{align}
The two transforms are the same meromorphic function, valid on disjoint
half-planes.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The identity $g_C(x)=S_0e^{rT}(e^x-e^{\tilde k})^{+}$ follows from
$K=S_0e^{rT+\tilde k}$ in \eqref{eq:ktilde}; $g_C$ is supported on
$x>\tilde k$. For $\Real w>1$,
\begin{align}
\int_{\tilde k}^\infty(e^x-e^{\tilde k})e^{-wx}\,dx
&=\int_{\tilde k}^\infty e^{(1-w)x}\,dx
-e^{\tilde k}\int_{\tilde k}^\infty e^{-wx}\,dx\notag\\
&=\Bigl[\tfrac{e^{(1-w)x}}{1-w}\Bigr]_{\tilde k}^\infty
-e^{\tilde k}\Bigl[\tfrac{e^{-wx}}{-w}\Bigr]_{\tilde k}^\infty
=\frac{e^{(1-w)\tilde k}}{w-1}-e^{\tilde k}\frac{e^{-w\tilde k}}{w},
\label{eq:gC-pf}
\end{align}
the first integral converging because $\Real(1-w)<0$ and the second because
$\Real w>0$. Since $e^{\tilde k}e^{-w\tilde k}=e^{(1-w)\tilde k}$ and
$\tfrac1{w-1}-\tfrac1w=\tfrac1{w(w-1)}$, multiplying by $S_0e^{rT}$ gives
\eqref{eq:gChat}. For the put, $g_P$ is supported on $x<\tilde k$ and for
$\Real w<<0$ both $\int_{-\infty}^{\tilde k}e^{(1-w)x}\,dx$ and
$\int_{-\infty}^{\tilde k}e^{-wx}\,dx$ converge (the lower limit $-\infty$
is now the active one, requiring $\Real(1-w)>0$ and $\Real(-w)>0$),
producing the same algebraic expression with the same sign pattern after the
orientation of the improper limits is accounted for; this is
\eqref{eq:gPhat}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Lewis contour representations]\label{thm:lewis}
Under \ref{A2}--\ref{A4} the discounted European call and put satisfy, with
$c\in(1,p^*)$ and $c_P\in(-p^*,0)$,
\begin{align}
C_M(K,T)&=\frac{e^{-rT}}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
\phi_M(-iw,T)\,\hat g_C(w)\,dw
=\frac{K e^{-rT}}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
\frac{\phi_M(-iw,T)\,e^{-w\tilde k}}{w(w-1)}\,dw,
\label{eq:lewis-call}\\
\Pi_M(K,T)&=\frac{K e^{-rT}}{2\pi i}\int_{c_P-i\infty}^{c_P+i\infty}
\frac{\phi_M(-iw,T)\,e^{-w\tilde k}}{w(w-1)}\,dw,
\label{eq:lewis-put}
\end{align}
where $\Pi_M$ is the put. The integrands are identical; only the abscissa
differs.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The discounted call is $C_M=e^{-rT}\E[g_C(X_T)]=e^{-rT}\int_\R g_C(x)p(x)
\,dx$. By Mellin/Parseval for the bilateral Laplace transform \textup{(E1)},
for a vertical line $\Real w=c$ inside the intersection of the strip
$\Real w>1$ (where $\hat g_C$ is analytic, Lemma~\ref{lem:payoff}) and the
strip where $\E[e^{wX_T}]=\phi_M(-iw)$ is finite,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lewis-pf1}
\int_\R g_C(x)p(x)\,dx
=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\hat g_C(w)\,
\E[e^{wX_T}]\,dw
=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\hat g_C(w)\,\phi_M(-iw,T)
\,dw .
\end{equation}
Writing $u=-iw$ (so $\Imag u=-\Real w$), Lemma~\ref{lem:decay} gives
$|\phi_M(-iw)|=|\phi_M(u)|\le e^{C_T}e^{-\sigma_T^2(\Real w)^2/2}\cdot
(\text{bounded along the line})$ on $\Real w=c$, by Cauchy-extension of the
real-axis bound into the strip $0<\Imag u<p^*$ where there is no pole
(Proposition~\ref{prop:strip}, \ref{A4}); together with
$|\hat g_C(w)|=S_0e^{rT}e^{(1-c)\tilde k}|w(w-1)|^{-1}=O(|\Imag w|^{-2})$
the integrand is absolutely integrable, validating \eqref{eq:lewis-pf1}.
Now substitute \eqref{eq:gChat} and multiply by $e^{-rT}$:
\[
C_M(K,T)=\frac{e^{-rT}}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
S_0e^{rT}\frac{e^{(1-w)\tilde k}}{w(w-1)}\phi_M(-iw,T)\,dw.
\]
Since $S_0e^{rT}e^{(1-w)\tilde k}=S_0e^{rT}e^{\tilde k}e^{-w\tilde k}$
and $S_0e^{rT}e^{\tilde k}=K$ by \eqref{eq:ktilde}, this simplifies to
\eqref{eq:lewis-call}. The put is identical with $\hat g_P$
in place of $\hat g_C$ and the line $\Real w=c_P<<0$ inside the common strip;
since $\hat g_P=\hat g_C$ as meromorphic functions (Lemma~\ref{lem:payoff}),
the integrand is the same and only the abscissa changes, giving
\eqref{eq:lewis-put}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Put--call parity as a contour shift]\label{rem:parity}
The call and put integrands in \eqref{eq:lewis-call}--\eqref{eq:lewis-put}
coincide; the difference $C_M-\Pi_M$ is the integral over the closed contour
between $\Real w=c_P$ and $\Real w=c$, which encloses the two kernel poles
$w=0$ and $w=1$ and no $u_j$-images. By the residue theorem,
\[
C_M-\Pi_M=2\pi i\cdot\frac{K e^{-rT}}{2\pi i}
\Bigl[\Res_{w=1}+\Res_{w=0}\Bigr]
\frac{\phi_M(-iw)e^{-w\tilde k}}{w(w-1)} .
\]
At $w=1$, $\phi_M(-i)=1$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:norm}) and the simple-pole residue is
\[
\Res_{w=1}\frac{e^{-w\tilde k}}{w(w-1)}
=\lim_{w\to1}(w-1)\frac{e^{-w\tilde k}}{w(w-1)}
=\frac{e^{-\tilde k}}{1}.
\]
Since $e^{-\tilde k}=\frac{S_0}{K}e^{rT}$ by \eqref{eq:ktilde}, this residue
contributes $K e^{-rT}\cdot\frac{S_0}{K}e^{rT}=S_0$.
At $w=0$, $\phi_M(0)=1$ and the residue is
\[
\Res_{w=0}\frac{e^{-w\tilde k}}{w(w-1)}
=\lim_{w\to0}w\frac{e^{-w\tilde k}}{w(w-1)}
=\frac{1}{-1}=-1,
\]
contributing $-K e^{-rT}$. Assembling,
$C_M-\Pi_M=S_0-K e^{-rT}$,
exactly put--call parity. The normalisations $\phi_M(0)=\phi_M(-i)=1$ are
therefore precisely what make parity hold at every order $M$.
\end{remark}

\section{The Schwinger--Gauss--erfc kernel}\label{sec:kernel}

Fix an admissible abscissa $c$ (call) or $c_P$ (put). Substitute $u=-iw$ in
\eqref{eq:PFE}; the images of the poles and residues are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rotate-data}
\hat u_j:=i u_j\quad(\Real\hat u_j=-\Imag u_j>0),\qquad B_j:=i A_j,
\end{equation}
and we set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:xi-g}
\xi:=\tilde k+\mu_T,\qquad g(w):=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2 w^2-w\xi,\qquad
z_w:=\frac{\xi-\sigma_T^2 w}{\sigma_T\sqrt2}.
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Rotation of the exponent]\label{lem:rotate}
With the data \eqref{eq:rotate-data}--\eqref{eq:xi-g},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rotate}
\phi_M(-iw,T)\,e^{-w\tilde k}
=e^{g(w)}\prod_{j=1}^{2M}\exp\!\Bigl(\frac{B_j}{\,w-\hat u_j\,}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Substitute $u=-iw$ in \eqref{eq:PFE}. The quadratic term:
$-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2(-iw)^2=-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2(-1)w^2=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2w^2$.
The linear term: $-i\mu_T(-iw)=i^2\mu_T w=-\mu_T w$. The pole terms: since
$-iw-u_j=-i(w-iu_j)=-i(w-\hat u_j)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rotate-pf}
\frac{A_j}{-iw-u_j}=\frac{A_j}{-i(w-\hat u_j)}
=\frac{A_j}{-i}\cdot\frac{1}{w-\hat u_j}
=iA_j\,\frac{1}{w-\hat u_j}=\frac{B_j}{w-\hat u_j},
\end{equation}
using $1/(-i)=i$. Therefore
$\Phi_M(-iw)=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2w^2-\mu_T w+\sum_jB_j/(w-\hat u_j)$, and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rotate-pf2}
\phi_M(-iw)e^{-w\tilde k}
=\exp\!\Bigl(\tfrac12\sigma_T^2w^2-\mu_T w-w\tilde k\Bigr)
\prod_j\exp\!\Bigl(\tfrac{B_j}{w-\hat u_j}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
Since $-\mu_T w-w\tilde k=-w(\tilde k+\mu_T)=-w\xi$, the prefactor exponent
is $\tfrac12\sigma_T^2w^2-w\xi=g(w)$, giving \eqref{eq:rotate}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Schwinger--Gauss--erfc identity]\label{lem:E}
For $\sigma_T^2>0$ and $\Real w_*\neq c$ define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Edef}
\boxed{\;\mathcal E(w_*):=\tfrac12\,e^{g(w_*)}\erfc(z_{w_*})
-\mathbf1_{\{\Real w_*>c\}}\,e^{g(w_*)}.\;}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-ident}
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{e^{g(w)}}{w-w_*}\,dw
=\mathcal E(w_*).
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Case $\Real w_*<<c$.} On $w=c+it$, $\Real(w-w_*)=c-\Real w_*>0$, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-pf1}
\frac{1}{w-w_*}=\int_0^\infty e^{-(w-w_*)s}\,ds\quad(\text{absolutely}),
\qquad
I:=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{e^{g(w)}}{w-w_*}\,dw
=\int_0^\infty e^{w_*s}J(s)\,ds,
\end{equation}
with $J(s):=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}e^{g(w)-ws}\,dw$.
The exchange of $\int_0^\infty ds$ with the contour integral is licensed by
the finite Tonelli bound
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-tonelli}
\int_0^\infty\!\!\int_\R
e^{\sigma_T^2(c^2-t^2)/2-c\xi}\,e^{(\Real w_*-c)s}\,dt\,ds
=\sqrt{\tfrac{2\pi}{\sigma_T^2}}\;e^{\sigma_T^2 c^2/2-c\xi}\;
\frac{1}{c-\Real w_*}<\infty .
\end{equation}
\emph{Evaluation of $J(s)$.} The exponent
$g(w)-ws=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2w^2-(\xi+s)w$ is completed to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-Jcompl}
\tfrac12\sigma_T^2w^2-(\xi+s)w
=\frac{\sigma_T^2}{2}\Bigl(w-\frac{\xi+s}{\sigma_T^2}\Bigr)^{\!2}
-\frac{(\xi+s)^2}{2\sigma_T^2}.
\end{equation}
On $w=c+it$ set $b:=c-\tfrac{\xi+s}{\sigma_T^2}\in\R$; then $dw=i\,dt$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-Jint}
\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
e^{\frac{\sigma_T^2}{2}(w-\frac{\xi+s}{\sigma_T^2})^2}dw
=i\!\int_\R e^{\frac{\sigma_T^2}{2}(b+it)^2}\,dt
=i\,e^{\sigma_T^2 b^2/2}\!\int_\R e^{-\sigma_T^2t^2/2+i\sigma_T^2 b t}\,dt
=i\,e^{\sigma_T^2 b^2/2}\sqrt{\tfrac{2\pi}{\sigma_T^2}}\,e^{-\sigma_T^2 b^2/2}
=\frac{i\sqrt{2\pi}}{\sigma_T},
\end{equation}
using $\int_\R e^{-At^2+iBt}\,dt=\sqrt{\pi/A}\,e^{-B^2/(4A)}$ with
$A=\sigma_T^2/2$, $B=\sigma_T^2 b$. Hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-J}
J(s)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\cdot\frac{i\sqrt{2\pi}}{\sigma_T}\,
e^{-(\xi+s)^2/(2\sigma_T^2)}
=\frac{1}{\sigma_T\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-(\xi+s)^2/(2\sigma_T^2)}.
\end{equation}
\emph{Evaluation of $I$.} Complete the square in $s$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-scompl}
w_*s-\frac{(\xi+s)^2}{2\sigma_T^2}
=-\frac{1}{2\sigma_T^2}\bigl(s-(\sigma_T^2w_*-\xi)\bigr)^2+g(w_*),
\qquad
\frac{(\sigma_T^2w_*-\xi)^2-\xi^2}{2\sigma_T^2}
=\frac{\sigma_T^2w_*^2}{2}-w_*\xi=g(w_*).
\end{equation}
Substitute $u=(s-(\sigma_T^2w_*-\xi))/(\sigma_T\sqrt2)$, so that $s=0$ maps
to $u=(\xi-\sigma_T^2w_*)/(\sigma_T\sqrt2)=z_{w_*}$ and $ds=\sigma_T\sqrt2\,
du$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-Ifinal}
I=\frac{e^{g(w_*)}}{\sigma_T\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_0^\infty
e^{-(s-(\sigma_T^2w_*-\xi))^2/(2\sigma_T^2)}\,ds
=\frac{e^{g(w_*)}}{\sqrt\pi}\int_{z_{w_*}}^\infty e^{-u^2}\,du
=\tfrac12\,e^{g(w_*)}\erfc(z_{w_*}),
\end{equation}
by $\erfc(z)=\tfrac{2}{\sqrt\pi}\int_z^\infty e^{-u^2}\,du$. The indicator in
\eqref{eq:Edef} is $0$ for $\Real w_*<<c$, so $I=\mathcal E(w_*)$.
\emph{Case $\Real w_*>c$.} Shift the contour rightward to
$\Real w=\Real w_*+1$. The horizontal closing segments at $\Imag w=\pm R$
vanish as $R\to\infty$ because
$|e^{g(w)}|=e^{\sigma_T^2((\Real w)^2-(\Imag w)^2)/2-\xi\Real w}\to0$. The
only enclosed singularity is the simple pole at $w=w_*$ with residue
$e^{g(w_*)}$, swept clockwise, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-shift}
I=I'-e^{g(w_*)},\qquad
I':=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{\Real w=\Real w_*+1}\frac{e^{g(w)}}{w-w_*}\,dw
=\tfrac12 e^{g(w_*)}\erfc(z_{w_*}),
\end{equation}
the last equality by Case~1 applied to the shifted line. Hence
$I=\tfrac12 e^{g(w_*)}\erfc(z_{w_*})-e^{g(w_*)}=\mathcal E(w_*)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Hermite differentiation identity]\label{lem:hermite}
With the physicists' Hermite polynomials $H_n$ defined by
$\partial_z^n e^{-z^2}=(-1)^n H_n(z)e^{-z^2}$, one has for $j\ge1$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hermite}
\frac{d^{j}}{dz^{j}}\erfc(z)=-\frac{2}{\sqrt\pi}(-1)^{j-1}H_{j-1}(z)\,
e^{-z^2}.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By the fundamental theorem of calculus,
$\erfc'(z)=\tfrac{2}{\sqrt\pi}\,\partial_z\!\int_z^\infty e^{-u^2}\,du
=-\tfrac{2}{\sqrt\pi}e^{-z^2}$. For $j\ge1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hermite-pf}
\erfc^{(j)}(z)=\partial_z^{\,j-1}\erfc'(z)
=-\frac{2}{\sqrt\pi}\partial_z^{\,j-1}e^{-z^2}
=-\frac{2}{\sqrt\pi}(-1)^{j-1}H_{j-1}(z)e^{-z^2},
\end{equation}
by the definition of $H_{j-1}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Closed-form derivatives of $\mathcal E$]\label{lem:dE}
Let $P_0\equiv1$ and $P_{k+1}(w):=P_k'(w)+(\sigma_T^2w-\xi)P_k(w)$, so that
$\partial_w^{k}e^{g(w)}=e^{g(w)}P_k(w)$. Then for $m\ge1$ and
$\Real w_*\neq c$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dE-int}
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{e^{g(w)}}{(w-w_*)^{m}}\,dw
=\frac{1}{(m-1)!}\,\partial_{w_*}^{m-1}\mathcal E(w_*),
\end{equation}
and, with the parameter-free Gaussian cancellation
$e^{g(w)}e^{-z_w^2}=e^{-\xi^2/(2\sigma_T^2)}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dE-explicit}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\partial_w^{\,m-1}\mathcal E(w)
&=\tfrac12\,e^{g(w)}\,P_{m-1}(w)\,\erfc(z_w)\\
&\quad+\frac{e^{-\xi^2/(2\sigma_T^2)}}{\sqrt\pi}
\sum_{j=1}^{m-1}\binom{m-1}{j}\Bigl(\tfrac{\sigma_T}{\sqrt2}\Bigr)^{\!j}
P_{m-1-j}(w)\,H_{j-1}(z_w)\\
&\quad-\mathbf1_{\{\Real w>c\}}\,e^{g(w)}\,P_{m-1}(w).
\end{aligned}}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The recurrence $\partial_w(e^gP_k)=e^g(P_k'+g'P_k)$ with $g'(w)=\sigma_T^2w
-\xi$ gives $\partial_w^k e^g=e^gP_k$ by induction. Since
$\partial_{w_*}^{m-1}(w-w_*)^{-1}=(m-1)!\,(w-w_*)^{-m}$ and the Gaussian
$|e^g|$ dominates every $w_*$-derivative of $(w-w_*)^{-1}$ uniformly on the
contour for $\{|\Real w_*-c|\ge\epsilon\}$, differentiating
\eqref{eq:E-ident} under the integral sign $m-1$ times yields
\eqref{eq:dE-int}.

For \eqref{eq:dE-explicit}, apply the Leibniz rule to $\tfrac12 e^{g(w)}
\erfc(z_w)$. The argument $z_w=(\xi-\sigma_T^2w)/(\sigma_T\sqrt2)$ is affine
with $dz_w/dw=-\sigma_T/\sqrt2$, so by the chain rule
$\partial_w^{j}\erfc(z_w)=(-\sigma_T/\sqrt2)^{j}\erfc^{(j)}(z_w)$. The $j=0$
term of Leibniz is $\tfrac12\,e^gP_{m-1}\erfc(z_w)$. For $1\le j\le m-1$,
using $\partial_w^{m-1-j}e^g=e^gP_{m-1-j}$ and Lemma~\ref{lem:hermite},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dE-term}
\begin{aligned}
\tfrac12\binom{m-1}{j}\bigl(\partial_w^{m-1-j}e^g\bigr)
\bigl(\partial_w^{j}\erfc(z_w)\bigr)
&=\tfrac12\binom{m-1}{j}e^gP_{m-1-j}
\Bigl(-\tfrac{\sigma_T}{\sqrt2}\Bigr)^{\!j}\\
&\quad\times\Bigl(-\tfrac{2}{\sqrt\pi}\Bigr)(-1)^{j-1}H_{j-1}(z_w)e^{-z_w^2}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The scalar prefactor simplifies: with
$\bigl(-\tfrac{\sigma_T}{\sqrt2}\bigr)^{j}=(-1)^j\bigl(\tfrac{\sigma_T}
{\sqrt2}\bigr)^{j}$ the three sign factors give
$(-1)^j\cdot(-1)\cdot(-1)^{j-1}=(-1)^{2j}=1$, while $\tfrac12\cdot
\tfrac{2}{\sqrt\pi}=\tfrac1{\sqrt\pi}$, so the constant is
$\tfrac1{\sqrt\pi}\bigl(\tfrac{\sigma_T}{\sqrt2}\bigr)^{j}\binom{m-1}{j}$.
The Gaussian factor is parameter-free: with $g(w)=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2w^2-w\xi$
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dE-cancel}
z_w^2=\frac{(\xi-\sigma_T^2w)^2}{2\sigma_T^2}
=\frac{\xi^2}{2\sigma_T^2}-w\xi+\tfrac12\sigma_T^2w^2,
\qquad
g(w)-z_w^2=-\frac{\xi^2}{2\sigma_T^2},
\end{equation}
so $e^{g(w)}e^{-z_w^2}=e^{-\xi^2/(2\sigma_T^2)}$, independent of $w$. This
produces the displayed sum. Finally the indicator term differentiates as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dE-ind}
\partial_w^{m-1}\bigl(\mathbf1_{\{\Real w>c\}}e^g\bigr)
=\mathbf1_{\{\Real w>c\}}\,e^g\,P_{m-1},
\end{equation}
the indicator being locally constant where $\Real w\neq c$, which completes
\eqref{eq:dE-explicit}.
\end{proof}

\section{Multinomial expansion and the closed-form price}\label{sec:price}

\begin{lemma}[Cauchy product of pole exponentials]\label{lem:cauchy}
On $\{w:\min_j|w-\hat u_j|\ge\rho>0\}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cauchy}
\prod_{j=1}^{2M}\exp\!\Bigl(\frac{B_j}{w-\hat u_j}\Bigr)
=\sum_{\mathbf n\in\Z_{\ge0}^{2M}}\prod_{j=1}^{2M}
\frac{B_j^{\,n_j}}{n_j!\,(w-\hat u_j)^{n_j}},
\end{equation}
absolutely and uniformly, with $\sum_{\mathbf n}\bigl|\prod_j B_j^{n_j}/
(n_j!(w-\hat u_j)^{n_j})\bigr|\le\prod_{j=1}^{2M}e^{|B_j|/\rho}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Each factor satisfies $|B_j/(w-\hat u_j)|\le|B_j|/\rho$, hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cauchy-pf}
\sum_{n_j\ge0}\frac{|B_j/(w-\hat u_j)|^{n_j}}{n_j!}\le e^{|B_j|/\rho},
\qquad
\sum_{n_j\ge0}\frac{(B_j/(w-\hat u_j))^{n_j}}{n_j!}
=\exp\!\Bigl(\frac{B_j}{w-\hat u_j}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
The Cauchy product of $2M$ absolutely convergent series converges absolutely
to the product of their sums, and the product of the majorants is
$\prod_j e^{|B_j|/\rho}<\infty$, uniform on the stated set.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Heaviside cover-up for repeated poles]\label{lem:heav}
For pairwise distinct $w_0,\dots,w_r$ and $n_\ell\ge1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heav}
\frac{1}{\prod_{\ell=0}^r(w-w_\ell)^{n_\ell}}
=\sum_{\ell=0}^r\sum_{m=1}^{n_\ell}\frac{R_{\ell,m}}{(w-w_\ell)^{m}},
\qquad
R_{\ell,m}=\frac{1}{(n_\ell-m)!}\,\partial_w^{\,n_\ell-m}
\Bigl[\prod_{j\neq\ell}(w-w_j)^{-n_j}\Bigr]_{w=w_\ell}.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $F(w)=(w-w_\ell)^{-n_\ell}G_\ell(w)$ with
$G_\ell(w)=\prod_{j\neq\ell}(w-w_j)^{-n_j}$, holomorphic and nonzero at
$w_\ell$. Its Taylor expansion $G_\ell(w)=\sum_{p\ge0}
\frac{G_\ell^{(p)}(w_\ell)}{p!}(w-w_\ell)^p$ multiplied by
$(w-w_\ell)^{-n_\ell}$ has, as the coefficient of $(w-w_\ell)^{-m}$
($1\le m\le n_\ell$), the value $G_\ell^{(n_\ell-m)}(w_\ell)/(n_\ell-m)!
=R_{\ell,m}$. Summing the principal parts over all poles reconstructs $F$:
the difference is entire and tends to $0$ at infinity, hence vanishes by
Liouville.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Absolutely convergent closed-form call and put]\label{thm:price}
Under \ref{A1}--\ref{A4}, with $S(\mathbf n)=\{j:n_j\ge1\}$,
\begin{align}
R^{(\delta,\mathbf n)}_\delta&=\!\!\prod_{j\in S(\mathbf n)}\!\!
\frac{1}{(\delta-\hat u_j)^{n_j}},\label{eq:Rdelta}\\
R^{(\delta,\mathbf n)}_{\hat u_\ell,m}&=\frac{1}{(n_\ell-m)!}\,
\partial_w^{\,n_\ell-m}\!\Bigl[\frac{1}{(w-\delta)\!\!\!
\prod\limits_{j\in S(\mathbf n)\setminus\{\ell\}}\!\!\!(w-\hat u_j)^{n_j}}
\Bigr]_{w=\hat u_\ell},\label{eq:Rlm}\\
\Psi^{(\delta)}_{\mathbf n}&=R^{(\delta,\mathbf n)}_\delta\,
\mathcal E(\delta)
+\sum_{\ell\in S(\mathbf n)}\sum_{m=1}^{n_\ell}
R^{(\delta,\mathbf n)}_{\hat u_\ell,m}\,\frac{1}{(m-1)!}\,
\partial_w^{\,m-1}\mathcal E(w)\big|_{w=\hat u_\ell}.\label{eq:Psi}
\end{align}
Then the European call and put on $S_T=S_0e^{x_T+rT}$ with $x_T$
characterised by $\phi_M$ of \eqref{eq:PFE} are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Cprice}
\boxed{\;C_M(K,T)=K e^{-rT}\sum_{\mathbf n\in\Z_{\ge0}^{2M}}
\Bigl(\prod_{j=1}^{2M}\frac{B_j^{\,n_j}}{n_j!}\Bigr)
\bigl[\Psi^{(1)}_{\mathbf n}-\Psi^{(0)}_{\mathbf n}\bigr],\qquad B_j=iA_j\;}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Pprice}
\boxed{\;\Pi_M(K,T)=C_M(K,T)-\bigl(S_0-K e^{-rT}\bigr)\;}
\end{equation}
where in \eqref{eq:Cprice} the contour data ($\mathcal E$, $\partial^{m-1}
\mathcal E$) use the call abscissa $c\in(1,p^*)$, and \eqref{eq:Pprice} is
put--call parity.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{(1)} By Theorem~\ref{thm:lewis},
$C_M=\frac{K e^{-rT}}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\phi_M(-iw)e^{-w\tilde k}
[w(w-1)]^{-1}dw$.
\textbf{(2)} Lemma~\ref{lem:rotate} rewrites the integrand as
$e^{g(w)}\prod_j\exp(B_j/(w-\hat u_j))\,[w(w-1)]^{-1}$.
\textbf{(3)} The kernel splits by $1/[w(w-1)]=1/(w-1)-1/w$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:heav} on $\{0,1\}$), so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:price-pf3}
C_M=\frac{K e^{-rT}}{2\pi i}\bigl[\mathcal J^{(1)}-\mathcal J^{(0)}\bigr],
\qquad
\mathcal J^{(\delta)}=\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
\frac{e^{g(w)}\prod_j\exp(B_j/(w-\hat u_j))}{w-\delta}\,dw,\ \delta\in\{0,1\}.
\end{equation}
\textbf{(4)} On $\Real w=c$, $\rho\ge1$ by \ref{A3}; Lemma~\ref{lem:cauchy}
expands the product, and the absolute majorant (finite by the Gaussian
factor; Theorem~\ref{thm:absconv}) licenses term-by-term integration
(Fubini).
\textbf{(5)} For each $\mathbf n$ the denominator is $(w-\delta)
\prod_{j\in S(\mathbf n)}(w-\hat u_j)^{n_j}$ with all poles distinct
($\delta\in\{0,1\}$ off the line $\Real w=c$, and $\Real\hat u_j=-\Imag u_j$
separated from the line by \ref{A3}). Lemma~\ref{lem:heav} produces
\eqref{eq:Rdelta}--\eqref{eq:Rlm}; integrating the simple pole at $\delta$
by Lemma~\ref{lem:E} and the order-$m$ pole at $\hat u_\ell$ by
Lemma~\ref{lem:dE},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:price-pf5}
\frac{\mathcal J^{(\delta)}}{2\pi i}
=\sum_{\mathbf n}\Bigl(\prod_j\frac{B_j^{n_j}}{n_j!}\Bigr)
\Psi^{(\delta)}_{\mathbf n}.
\end{equation}
\textbf{(6)} Substituting into \eqref{eq:price-pf3} gives \eqref{eq:Cprice}.
The put \eqref{eq:Pprice} is Remark~\ref{rem:parity}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Black--Scholes limit; normalisation certificate]\label{cor:BS}
If all $A_j=0$ (no jump part) and $\sigma_T^2,\mu_T$ are the Black--Scholes
variance and the martingale-adjusted mean, then $\Phi_M(u,T)=-\tfrac12
\sigma_T^2u^2-i\mu_T u$ with $\mu_T=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2$ (from
\eqref{eq:norm-constraints} with no poles), and \eqref{eq:Cprice} reduces to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:BS}
C_M(K,T)=S_0\,N(d_1)-Ke^{-rT}\,N(d_2),\qquad
d_{1,2}=\frac{\log(S_0/K)+rT}{\sigma_T}\pm\frac{\sigma_T}{2},
\end{equation}
with $N$ the standard normal CDF.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
With $A_j=0$ the multi-index sum reduces to the single term
$\mathbf n=\mathbf0$, where $S(\mathbf0)=\varnothing$,
$\prod_jB_j^{0}/0!=1$, and $\Psi^{(\delta)}_{\mathbf0}
=R^{(\delta,\mathbf0)}_\delta\mathcal E(\delta)=\mathcal E(\delta)$ (empty
product $=1$). Thus $C_M=K e^{-rT}[\mathcal E(1)-\mathcal E(0)]$. With both
indicators vanishing (any $c>1$ is admissible in the pole-free case),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:BS-pf1}
\mathcal E(\delta)=\tfrac12 e^{g(\delta)}\erfc(z_\delta),\qquad
g(\delta)=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2\delta^2-\delta\xi,\quad
z_\delta=\frac{\xi-\sigma_T^2\delta}{\sigma_T\sqrt2},\quad
\xi=\tilde k+\tfrac12\sigma_T^2.
\end{equation}
At $\delta=0$: $g(0)=0$, $z_0=\xi/(\sigma_T\sqrt2)$, so
$\mathcal E(0)=\tfrac12\erfc\!\bigl(\tfrac{\xi}{\sigma_T\sqrt2}\bigr)
=N\!\bigl(-\tfrac{\xi}{\sigma_T}\bigr)$ via $N(x)=\tfrac12\erfc(-x/\sqrt2)$.
Now $-\xi/\sigma_T=-(\tilde k+\tfrac12\sigma_T^2)/\sigma_T
=\bigl(\log(S_0/K)+rT-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2\bigr)/\sigma_T=d_2$, since
$\tilde k=\log(K/S_0)-rT$. Hence $\mathcal E(0)=N(d_2)$.
At $\delta=1$: $g(1)=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2-\xi=\tfrac12\sigma_T^2-\tilde k
-\tfrac12\sigma_T^2=-\tilde k$, and $z_1=(\xi-\sigma_T^2)/(\sigma_T\sqrt2)$,
so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:BS-pf2}
\mathcal E(1)=\tfrac12 e^{-\tilde k}\erfc\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\xi-\sigma_T^2}
{\sigma_T\sqrt2}\Bigr)=e^{-\tilde k}\,N\!\Bigl(-\tfrac{\xi-\sigma_T^2}
{\sigma_T}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
Here $-(\xi-\sigma_T^2)/\sigma_T=-(\tilde k+\tfrac12\sigma_T^2-\sigma_T^2)/
\sigma_T=\bigl(\log(S_0/K)+rT+\tfrac12\sigma_T^2\bigr)/\sigma_T=d_1$, and
$e^{-\tilde k}=\tfrac{S_0}{K}e^{rT}$ from \eqref{eq:ktilde}, so
\[
K e^{-rT}\mathcal E(1)=K e^{-rT}\cdot\tfrac{S_0}{K}e^{rT}N(d_1)=S_0 N(d_1).
\]
Therefore
\[
C_M=K e^{-rT}\mathcal E(1)-K e^{-rT}\mathcal E(0)=S_0 N(d_1)-K e^{-rT}N(d_2),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:BS}. The exact recovery of Black--Scholes fixes
$\xi=\tilde k+\mu_T$, the image $\hat u_j=iu_j$, $B_j=iA_j$, and the
prefactor $K e^{-rT}$ as in \eqref{eq:Cprice}.
\end{proof}

\section{Absolute convergence for all admissible parameters}\label{sec:absconv}

\begin{theorem}[Unconditional absolute convergence]\label{thm:absconv}
Under \ref{A1}--\ref{A4} the series \eqref{eq:Cprice} converges absolutely,
with the a~priori majorant
\begin{equation}\label{eq:majorant}
\sum_{\mathbf n}\Bigl|\prod_j\frac{B_j^{n_j}}{n_j!}\Bigr|
\bigl(|\Psi^{(1)}_{\mathbf n}|+|\Psi^{(0)}_{\mathbf n}|\bigr)
\;\le\;
\frac{K e^{-rT}}{\sigma_T\sqrt{2\pi}}\cdot
\frac{(2c-1)\,e^{\sigma_T^2c^2/2-c\xi}}{c(c-1)}\cdot
\prod_{j=1}^{2M}e^{|A_j|/\rho}\;<\;\infty .
\end{equation}
Consequently $C_M(K,T)$ and $\Pi_M(K,T)$ are well defined for every
admissible parameter set, and $C_M\to C$ at the Stahl rate of
Theorem~\ref{thm:stahl}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Estimate at the level of the contour integral, before partial fractions, so
that the multi-pole bookkeeping never enters. For $\delta\in\{0,1\}$ and
$w=c+it$, Lemma~\ref{lem:cauchy} bounds the multinomial sum:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:abs-pf1}
\Bigl|\sum_{\mathbf n}\prod_j\frac{B_j^{n_j}}{n_j!(w-\hat u_j)^{n_j}}\Bigr|
\le\sum_{\mathbf n}\prod_j\frac{(|B_j|/\rho)^{n_j}}{n_j!}
=\prod_{j=1}^{2M}e^{|B_j|/\rho},
\end{equation}
while
\begin{equation}\label{eq:abs-pf2}
\Bigl|\frac{e^{g(w)}}{w-\delta}\Bigr|
=\frac{e^{\sigma_T^2(c^2-t^2)/2-c\xi}}{\sqrt{(c-\delta)^2+t^2}} .
\end{equation}
Therefore, integrating along the line and using
$\sqrt{(c-\delta)^2+t^2}\ge|c-\delta|$ with the Gaussian integral
$\int_\R e^{-\sigma_T^2t^2/2}\,dt=\sqrt{2\pi/\sigma_T^2}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:abs-pf3}
\frac{1}{2\pi}\!\int_\R\!\Bigl|\frac{e^{g(c+it)}}{c-\delta+it}\Bigr|\,
\prod_j e^{|B_j|/\rho}\,dt
\le\frac{\prod_j e^{|B_j|/\rho}}{\sigma_T\sqrt{2\pi}}\cdot
\frac{e^{\sigma_T^2c^2/2-c\xi}}{|c-\delta|}.
\end{equation}
By steps (4)--(5) of Theorem~\ref{thm:price} this dominates
$\sum_{\mathbf n}|\prod_jB_j^{n_j}/n_j!|\,|\Psi^{(\delta)}_{\mathbf n}|$
(the term-by-term integral is bounded by the integral of the absolute
majorant). Multiply by $K e^{-rT}$, then add $\delta=1$ ($|c-\delta|=c-1$, since
$c>1$) and $\delta=0$ ($|c-\delta|=c$): $\tfrac{1}{c-1}+\tfrac1c
=\tfrac{2c-1}{c(c-1)}$, and $\prod_je^{|B_j|/\rho}=\prod_je^{|A_j|/\rho}$,
giving \eqref{eq:majorant}. Finiteness uses only $\sigma_T^2>0$ (\ref{A2})
and $\rho>0$ (\ref{A3}); there is no condition on the magnitudes of the
$A_j$, hence the bound holds for every admissible parameter set. The limit
statement is Theorem~\ref{thm:stahl} together with local Lipschitz
continuity of $\exp$ on the contour: $\Phi_M\to\Phi$ uniformly on
$\Real w=c$ implies $\phi_M\to\phi$ uniformly there, so $C_M\to C$ at the
same geometric rate. The put converges by \eqref{eq:Pprice}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Quadrature-free pipeline]\label{cor:pipeline}
$C_M(K,T)$ and $\Pi_M(K,T)$ are computed by:
\textup{(i)} the M\"untz--Tau coefficients $a_k(v)$ and the lattice $d_k(v)$
for $k\le 2M$ (Theorems~\ref{thm:tau},~\ref{thm:cgf});
\textup{(ii)} the Chebyshev recurrence (Theorem~\ref{thm:cheb}), at $O(M^2)$;
\textup{(iii)} root-finding of $Q_M(T^{\mu};\cdot)$ to obtain
$\{u_j,A_j,\sigma_T,\mu_T\}$, at $O(M^2)$ per $T$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:match},
Lemma~\ref{lem:decay});
\textup{(iv)} evaluation of \eqref{eq:Cprice} via the $P_k$ recurrence and
the $\erfc$/Hermite kernel \eqref{eq:dE-explicit}, absolutely convergent with
the $\prod_je^{|A_j|/\rho}$ control of Theorem~\ref{thm:absconv}.
No Fourier inversion, no cosine series, no truncation interval, and no
special function beyond $\erfc$ are used.
\end{corollary}

\section{Summary}\label{sec:summary}

\textbf{T1 (resummation).} The cgf $\Phi(v,T)=T\,\widetilde\Phi(T^{\mu};v)$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:cgf}) is resummed by its diagonal $[M/M]$ Pad\'e
approximant built on the lattice coefficients $\{d_k(v)\}$ through the
Chebyshev recurrence (Theorem~\ref{thm:cheb}) in $O(M^2)$ ring operations,
the denominator being the reversed orthogonal polynomial
(Theorem~\ref{thm:match}); the rate as $M\to\infty$ is Stahl's
(Theorem~\ref{thm:stahl}), with the a-posteriori control of
Theorem~\ref{thm:apost}.

\textbf{T2 (price).} The European call is the series \eqref{eq:Cprice}
(Theorem~\ref{thm:price}) in the partial-fraction data
$(\sigma_T,\mu_T,\{u_j,A_j\})$, with kernel
$\mathcal E=\tfrac12 e^{g}\erfc-\mathbf1\,e^{g}$ and every derivative the
closed Hermite form \eqref{eq:dE-explicit}, derived from the Parseval/Lewis
representation (Theorem~\ref{thm:lewis}; payoff transforms
Lemma~\ref{lem:payoff}), the rotation Lemma~\ref{lem:rotate} ($B_j=iA_j$,
$\hat u_j=iu_j$, $\xi=\tilde k+\mu_T$), the Cauchy product
Lemma~\ref{lem:cauchy}, and the Schwinger identity Lemma~\ref{lem:E}; the
put is \eqref{eq:Pprice} by parity (Remark~\ref{rem:parity}).

\textbf{T3 (convergence and limit).} The series is absolutely convergent for
every admissible parameter set, dominated by the right-hand side of
\eqref{eq:majorant} (Theorem~\ref{thm:absconv}), under the sole structural
requirement $\sigma_T^2>0$; the pole-free specialisation returns
Black--Scholes exactly (Corollary~\ref{cor:BS}), certifying all
normalisations, and $C_M\to C$ at the Stahl rate.
