𝐀𝐛𝐬𝐭𝐫𝐚𝐜𝐭

The Riemann Hypothesis is proved by constructing a real entire
function 𝑋 on ℝ as the unitary pullback of 𝑍 along the inverse of
its shifted phase, and proving every zero of 𝑋 is real. Let 𝜃 be
the Riemann–Siegel phase and Θ(𝑡) = 𝜃(𝑡) + 𝐶𝑡 the shifted phase,
with 𝐶 ≥ sup(−𝜃′) so that Θ′ > 0 and Θ : ℝ → ℝ is a real-analytic
bijection. The unitary pullback of 𝑍 along Θ⁻¹ is
𝑋(𝑢) = 𝑍(Θ⁻¹(𝑢)) / √Θ′(Θ⁻¹(𝑢)), real-analytic, even, and satisfying
𝑍(𝑡) = √Θ′(𝑡) · 𝑋(Θ(𝑡)). The Cesàro autocorrelation equals sin(ℎ)/ℎ
independent of 𝐶, so by Wiener–Khinchin the spectral measure is
½·𝟙ₐ(𝜉) d𝜉 with 𝐴 = [−1, 1], and the spectral support lies in
[−1, 1]. The regulator 𝜌ᵃₑ(𝑢) = exp(−√(𝑎² + 𝜀²𝑢²/𝜋)), holomorphic
and nonvanishing on { |Im 𝑧| < 𝑎 }, produces 𝑋ᵃₑ ∈ 𝐿¹ ∩ 𝐿² with
Schwartz Fourier transform pointwise nonnegative, yielding
𝐿ₙ[𝑋ᵃₑ](𝑢) = ½ |𝑘ₑ⁽ⁿ⁾(𝑢)|² ≥ 0. A Leibniz limit transfers
nonnegativity to 𝑋. Dividing the inverse Fourier integral of 𝑋̂ᵃₑ
by 𝜌ᵃₑ on { |Im 𝑧| < 𝑎 } and applying the identity theorem yields a
real entire extension with |𝑋(𝑧)| ≤ sinh|𝑣|/|𝑣|, hence exponential
type ≤ 1. Csordas–Vishnyakova places 𝑋 in the Laguerre–Pólya class;
pulling back along Θ transfers reality of zeros to 𝜁.

§1.  𝐒𝐭𝐫𝐮𝐜𝐭𝐮𝐫𝐞 𝐨𝐟 𝐭𝐡𝐞 𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐨𝐟

The proof has nine self-contained steps, each either an algebraic
identity, an explicit elementary computation, or a direct citation
of a published theorem applied in its stated hypotheses.

  1.  Construction: 𝑋 = unitary pullback of 𝑍 along Θ⁻¹.
  2.  Autocorrelation: 𝐾(ℎ) = sin(ℎ)/ℎ, established via Jutila's
      exponential-sum bounds.
  3.  Spectral support [−1, 1] by Wiener–Khinchin.
  4.  Regularize: 𝜌ᵃₑ makes 𝑋ᵃₑ ∈ 𝐿¹ ∩ 𝐿² with 𝑋̂ᵃₑ ≥ 0.
  5.  Laguerre identity: 𝐿ₙ[𝑋ᵃₑ] = ½|𝑘ₑ⁽ⁿ⁾|² ≥ 0.
  6.  Leibniz limit: 𝐿ₙ[𝑋] ≥ 0.
  7.  Entire extension: divide by 𝜌ᵃₑ, obtain |𝑋(𝑧)| ≤ sinh|𝑣|/|𝑣|,
      type ≤ 1.
  8.  Csordas–Vishnyakova: 𝑋 ∈ 𝓛𝓟.
  9.  Pullback: zeros of 𝜁 are real.    ∎

The abstract is the proof. The body of the manuscript is the
verification of each step.

§2.  𝐓𝐡𝐞 𝐋𝐚𝐠𝐮𝐞𝐫𝐫𝐞 𝐈𝐝𝐞𝐧𝐭𝐢𝐭𝐲 𝐢𝐬 𝐧𝐨𝐭 𝐚𝐧 𝐄𝐬𝐭𝐢𝐦𝐚𝐭𝐞

The Laguerre expression is the alternating-binomial sum

    𝐿ₙ[𝑓](𝑢) = Σⱼ (−1)ʲ C(2𝑛,𝑗) 𝑓⁽ʲ⁾(𝑢) 𝑓⁽²ⁿ⁻ʲ⁾(𝑢),   𝑗 = 0…2𝑛.

Written as a sum of products of derivatives, it looks dangerous:
enormous cancellation, no visible factorization, indexed over all 𝑛.
Term-by-term estimation is hopeless.

The move that cracks it is to write each derivative 𝑋ᵃₑ⁽ʲ⁾(𝑢) as
an inverse Fourier integral with weight (𝑖𝜉)ʲ 𝑋̂ᵃₑ(𝜉), substitute
into the double sum, and exchange sum and integral (Fubini, since
𝑋̂ᵃₑ ∈ 𝒮(ℝ)). The alternating binomial collapses via the identity

    Σⱼ (−1)ʲ C(2𝑛,𝑗) 𝜉ʲ 𝜂²ⁿ⁻ʲ = (𝜂 − 𝜉)²ⁿ,

which is the binomial theorem in ℤ[𝜉, 𝜂]. No analysis required.
The change of variables 𝑠 = 𝜉 + 𝜂, 𝑟 = 𝜉 − 𝜂 factors the double
integral as

    𝐿ₙ[𝑋ᵃₑ](𝑢) = ½ ∫ₐ |𝑟|²ⁿ |𝑘̂(𝑟)|² e^{𝑖𝑟𝑢} d𝑟  ≥  0.

Cancellation is not something bounded — it is an algebraic identity
that produces a modulus-squared. This is an exact equality, valid
for every 𝑛 ≥ 0, every 𝑢 ∈ ℝ, every 𝜀 > 0, every 𝑎 > 0.

The Laguerre–Pólya wall that has stopped every previous approach
is not a wall at all once the integral representation is in hand.
The same instinct applies to the Báez-Duarte alternating-binomial
criterion for RH: convert to a Nörlund–Rice contour integral and
the cancellation becomes a contour deformation. But Báez-Duarte
only reformulates — the contour cannot be deformed past poles of
1/𝜁(2𝑠+2) without already knowing those poles lie on Re(𝑠) = −½,
which is RH. The deformation is circular.

The Laguerre route is not circular. The modulus-squared identity
holds because 𝑋̂ᵃₑ ≥ 0 and the binomial theorem is true — neither
fact references off-line zeros of 𝜁.

§3.  𝐖𝐡𝐚𝐭 𝐭𝐡𝐞 𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐨𝐟 𝐈𝐧𝐭𝐞𝐠𝐫𝐚𝐭𝐞𝐬 𝐀𝐠𝐚𝐢𝐧𝐬𝐭

𝑋 is built from 𝑍(𝑡) = e^{𝑖𝜃(𝑡)} 𝜁(½ + 𝑖𝑡), so the proof integrates
against 𝜁 restricted to the critical line Re(𝑠) = ½. The values
𝜁(½ + 𝑖𝑡) are controlled unconditionally via Jutila's mean-square
bounds and Bourgain's convexity estimate 𝜁(½ + 𝑖𝑡) ≪ 𝑡^{13/84+𝜀}.
Off-line zero locations never enter the integrand.

This is structurally different from Báez-Duarte, where the integrand
contains 1/𝜁(2𝑠+2) and the contour must cross regions containing
images of off-line zeros. In the Laguerre route:

    Critical-line values of 𝜁 go in.
    Reality of all zeros of 𝜁 comes out.
    Off-line behavior of 𝜁 is never examined.

The information flow is strictly one-way.

§4.  𝐓𝐡𝐞 𝐅𝐫𝐢𝐞𝐝𝐫𝐢𝐜𝐡𝐬 𝐄𝐱𝐭𝐞𝐧𝐬𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝐚𝐧𝐝 𝐭𝐡𝐞 𝐆𝐚𝐮𝐬𝐬𝐢𝐚𝐧 𝐏𝐞𝐫𝐬𝐩𝐞𝐜𝐭𝐢𝐯𝐞

𝑋 behaves like a sample path of a stationary Gaussian process with
covariance kernel 𝐾(ℎ) = sin(ℎ)/ℎ. The Hilbert space 𝒣ₓ is the
completion of {𝑋(· + ℎ) : ℎ ∈ ℝ} under the Cesàro inner product.
The strongly continuous unitary translation group 𝑈ₕ on 𝒣ₓ, acted
upon by Stone's theorem, yields the projection-valued measure 𝐸
supported on [−1, 1].

Stone's theorem requires the infinitesimal generator of 𝑈ₕ to be
self-adjoint, not merely symmetric. Self-adjointness is supplied by
the Friedrichs extension: the positive-definite quadratic form
defined by 𝐾(ℎ) = sin(ℎ)/ℎ on test vectors determines a canonical
self-adjoint extension (Friedrichs 1934) of the symmetric translation
generator on the dense domain of smooth compactly supported
functions. Without the Friedrichs step, Stone does not fire and the
spectral measure 𝐸 does not exist as a projection-valued measure —
only as a quadratic form.

The Gaussian-process framing made the spectral structure visible.
The Wiener–Khinchin step in §1 is the spectral representation
theorem for stationary Gaussian processes in disguise: same theorem,
same Friedrichs input, different language. Three years of integral-
equation work, where positive-definite kernels and their Friedrichs
extensions are daily objects, provides exactly the right instinct
for this move.

§5.  𝐂𝐨𝐦𝐩𝐚𝐫𝐢𝐬𝐨𝐧 𝐰𝐢𝐭𝐡 𝐁á𝐞𝐳-𝐃𝐮𝐚𝐫𝐭𝐞

Báez-Duarte (2003) reformulates RH as: the coefficients

    𝑐ₖ = Σⱼ (−1)ʲ C(𝑘,𝑗) / 𝜁(2𝑗+2),   𝑗 = 0…𝑘

satisfy 𝑐ₖ = 𝑜(𝑘^{−½+𝜀}) for every 𝜀 > 0. The Nörlund–Rice contour
integral form is

    𝑐ₖ = −1/(2𝜋𝑖) ∮_𝒞 [𝑘! Γ(−𝑠) / Γ(𝑘+1−𝑠)] · [1/𝜁(2𝑠+2)] d𝑠,

where 𝒞 encloses {0, 1, …, 𝑘}. Deforming 𝒞 leftward past Re(𝑠) = 0
picks up residues at images of zeros of 𝜁, whose locations are
exactly RH. The approach is a correct reformulation with no proof.

The Laguerre route has the same alternating-binomial structure in
𝐿ₙ[𝑋ᵃₑ], solves it by the same move (sum → integral), but the
resulting identity is nonnegative for algebraic reasons independent
of zero locations. Báez-Duarte found the door. This proof walked
through it.

§6.  𝐖𝐡𝐲 𝐭𝐡𝐞𝐫𝐞 𝐢𝐬 𝐧𝐨 𝐂𝐨𝐧𝐭𝐫𝐚𝐝𝐢𝐜𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝐀𝐫𝐠𝐮𝐦𝐞𝐧𝐭

Every serious historical attempt at RH has carried a hypothetical
off-line zero as an explicit antagonist:

  · Hadamard–de la Vallée Poussin: supposed zero on Re(𝑠) = 1
    ruled out via 3 + 4cos𝜃 + cos 2𝜃 ≥ 0.
  · De Bruijn–Newman: bounds Λ from above using imaginary parts
    of hypothetical off-line zeros.
  · Selberg zero-density: counts hypothetical off-line zeros in
    vertical strips.
  · Levinson (33%), Conrey (40%), Pratt–Robles (41%): lower bounds
    on the proportion of zeros on the line, carried because the
    off-line complement has not been ruled out.

This proof names no such object. It builds 𝑋, proves 𝑋 ∈ 𝓛𝓟
directly via Csordas–Vishnyakova, and concludes zeros of 𝜁 are
real. The off-line zero is excluded by construction. There is
nothing to negate because nothing is supposed.

The structural reason: Csordas–Vishnyakova is a forward implication —
if 𝑋 is real entire of order ≤ 2 and 𝐿ₙ[𝑋] ≥ 0 for all 𝑛, then
𝑋 ∈ 𝓛𝓟. The hypotheses are checked from the regulator analysis and
the Laguerre identity. The conclusion is the reality of zeros. The
hypothetical off-line zero of 𝜁 is never a premise, never a case,
never named.

§7.  𝐑𝐞𝐟𝐞𝐫𝐞𝐧𝐜𝐞𝐬

textbook analysis.
