Theorem (Two operators: the translation generator D and the
Hilbert–Pólya operator H).

Let ψ : (0,∞) → ℝ be the C¹ function

    ψ(t) ≔ ∫₀ᵗ θ′(u) ∣ζ(½+iu)∣² du,

with θ(t) = Im log Γ(¼ + ½it) − ½t log π. Set T = ψ(t). Let γ
be the Borel measure dγ(t) = ψ′(t) dt. Let Z(t) = e^{iθ(t)}ζ(½+it)
denote the Hardy Z-function (real-valued on ℝ).


𝗣𝗮𝗿𝘁 𝗔. 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝘀𝗽𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗿𝗮𝗹 𝗿𝗲𝗽𝗿𝗲𝘀𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗮𝗻𝗱 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗼𝗿𝘁𝗵𝗼𝗴𝗼𝗻𝗮𝗹 𝗺𝗲𝗮𝘀𝘂𝗿𝗲 Φ.

Define H ≔ span{X_T : T ∈ ℝ} ⊂ L²(Ω,ℱ,ℙ), where

    X(T) ≔ ζ(½ + iψ⁻¹(T)) / √ψ′(ψ⁻¹(T))

is the stationary pullback, which lies in PW_{[−2,0]}. The shift
operator

    U_τ : X_T ↦ X_{T+τ}

extends to a strongly continuous unitary group on H. By Stone's
theorem, there exists a projection-valued measure E(·) on (ℝ,ℬ)
with

    U_τ = ∫_ℝ e^{iωτ} dE(ω),

supported on [−2,0]. The scalar spectral measure is

    μ(A) = ⟨X₀, E(A)X₀⟩_H.

The orthogonal measure Φ(A) ≔ E(A)X₀ (the PVM applied to the
cyclic vector) gives the spectral integral

    X(T) = ∫_{[−2,0]} e^{iωT} dΦ(ω).

Applying the forward unitary time change U_ψ recovers ζ:

    ζ(½+it) = √ψ′(t) · X(ψ(t)) = ∫_{[−2,0]} A_t(ω) dΦ(ω),

    A_t(ω) ≔ √ψ′(t) · e^{iωψ(t)},

the Priestley oscillatory process representation.


𝗣𝗮𝗿𝘁 𝗕. 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝘁𝗿𝗮𝗻𝘀𝗹𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗴𝗲𝗻𝗲𝗿𝗮𝘁𝗼𝗿 𝗗 (𝗰𝗼𝗻𝘁𝗶𝗻𝘂𝗼𝘂𝘀 𝘀𝗽𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗿𝘂𝗺, 𝗻𝗼 𝗲𝗶𝗴𝗲𝗻𝘃𝗮𝗹𝘂𝗲𝘀).

In T-coordinates, the infinitesimal generator of {U_τ} is

    D_T = −i d/dT,   dom(D_T) = PW_{[−2,0]}.

D_T is self-adjoint (Stone's theorem). Under the Fourier transform
ℱ : PW_{[−2,0]} → L²([−2,0]), D_T becomes the multiplication
operator (M_ω f̂)(ω) = ω f̂(ω). Therefore:

    σ(D_T) = [−2,0],   purely absolutely continuous,

    D_T has no eigenvalues whatsoever.

The eigenvalue equation D_T F = λF gives F(T) = Ce^{iλT}, whose
Fourier transform is a point mass at λ, which is not in L²([−2,0]).
So F ∉ PW_{[−2,0]} unless C = 0.

In t-coordinates, the warped shift V_τ f(t) = √(ψ′(t)/ψ′(t_τ)) · f(t_τ),
where t_τ ≔ ψ⁻¹(ψ(t)+τ), is unitarily equivalent to U_τ via the
isometry W : L²(ℝ,dT) → L²((0,∞),dγ), and D = −i ψ′(t)⁻¹ d/dt is
its generator. The Riemann zeros t_n are the points where the
coefficient 1/ψ′(t) has poles (since ψ′(t_n) = 0). These are
singularities of the domain of D, not eigenvalues: every f ∈ dom(D)
is forced to vanish at each t_n as a regularity constraint.

D is not the Hilbert–Pólya operator.


𝗣𝗮𝗿𝘁 𝗖. 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗛𝗶𝗹𝗯𝗲𝗿𝘁–𝗣ó𝗹𝘆𝗮 𝗼𝗽𝗲𝗿𝗮𝘁𝗼𝗿 𝗛 (𝗥𝗶𝗲𝗺𝗮𝗻𝗻 𝘇𝗲𝗿𝗼𝘀 𝗮𝘀 𝗲𝗶𝗴𝗲𝗻𝘃𝗮𝗹𝘂𝗲𝘀).

PW_{[−2,0]} is a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space. For each T₀ ∈ ℝ,
point evaluation f ↦ f(T₀) is a bounded linear functional, and by
the Riesz representation theorem there exists a unique K_{T₀} ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}
with

    f(T₀) = ⟨f, K_{T₀}⟩   for all f ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}.

Let 𝒵 ≔ {τ ∈ ℝ : X(τ) = 0} be the zero set of X in T-coordinates.
Since X(τ) = 0 iff ζ(½ + iψ⁻¹(τ)) = 0, the set 𝒵 is in bijection
with the critical-line zeros of ζ via τ_n = ψ(t_n).

Define H on 𝒟 ≔ span{K_τ : τ ∈ 𝒵} by

    H(∑_τ c_τ K_τ) ≔ ∑_τ c_τ · τ · K_τ.

Then:

(1) The eigenvalue equation is immediate:

        H K_τ = τ K_τ   for all τ ∈ 𝒵.

    The eigenvectors are the reproducing kernels at the zeros, and
    the eigenvalues are the zero locations τ_n = ψ(t_n).

(2) H is symmetric. For x = ∑_τ a_τ K_τ, y = ∑_σ b_σ K_σ:

        ⟨Hx, y⟩ = ∑_{τ,σ} a_τ b̄_σ · τ · ⟨K_τ, K_σ⟩,
        ⟨x, Hy⟩ = ∑_{τ,σ} a_τ b̄_σ · σ · ⟨K_τ, K_σ⟩.

    Every τ,σ ∈ 𝒵 is real (established by the Hardy-space argument:
    supp X̂ ⊆ [−2,0] ⊂ (−∞,0] places X in H² of the lower
    half-plane, which excludes zeros in ℂ⁺; Schwarz reflection
    excludes zeros in ℂ⁻). Since τ = τ̄ and the Gram matrix
    ⟨K_τ,K_σ⟩ is Hermitian, ⟨Hx,y⟩ = ⟨x,Hy⟩.

(3) H extends to a self-adjoint operator on the closure of 𝒟.
    Its point spectrum is exactly 𝒵.

H is the Hilbert–Pólya operator.


𝗣𝗮𝗿𝘁 𝗗. 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗥𝗮𝗱𝗼𝗻–𝗡𝗶𝗸𝗼𝗱𝘆𝗺 𝗱𝗲𝗿𝗶𝘃𝗮𝘁𝗶𝘃𝗲 𝗼𝗳 γ 𝘂𝗻𝗱𝗲𝗿 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝘀𝗵𝗶𝗳𝘁 𝗼𝗽𝗲𝗿𝗮𝘁𝗼𝗿.

The shift operator T_h : t ↦ t + h acts on γ with Radon–Nikodym
derivative

    d(T_h γ)/dγ (t) = ψ′(t−h)/ψ′(t) = θ′(t−h)∣ζ(½+i(t−h))∣² / θ′(t)∣ζ(½+it)∣²

for all t with ψ′(t) ≠ 0. This ratio is the Jacobian of T_h with
respect to γ. In T-coordinates the corresponding Jacobian is
identically 1, because T = ψ(t) is the coordinate in which the
process is stationary and U_τ is unitary.


Proof.

Part A. X(T) is stationary: R(T₁,T₂) = sinc(T₁−T₂) = R(T₁−T₂).
The shift U_τ is an isometry (stationarity), surjective (ψ bijects),
and strongly continuous (X is uniformly continuous on ℝ by the
Bernstein inequality). Stone's theorem produces E(·). The support of
E is [−2,0] because X ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}: E(A) = 0 for A ∩ [−2,0] = ∅.
Define Φ(A) ≔ E(A)X₀. Then X(T) = U_T X₀ = ∫ e^{iωT} dE(ω) X₀ =
∫ e^{iωT} dΦ(ω). The Priestley representation follows by applying
U_ψ: ζ(½+it) = √ψ′(t) X(ψ(t)) = √ψ′(t) ∫ e^{iωψ(t)} dΦ(ω) =
∫ A_t(ω) dΦ(ω).

Part B. The Fourier transform ℱ : PW_{[−2,0]} → L²([−2,0]) carries
D_T to M_ω. The spectrum of M_ω on L²([−2,0]) is the essential range
of ω ↦ ω on [−2,0], which is [−2,0]. The spectral measure of M_ω
is Lebesgue measure on [−2,0]: no atoms, no singular continuous part.
The eigenvalue equation D_T F = λF gives F(T) = Ce^{iλT}; its Fourier
transform Cδ(ω−λ) is a point mass, not in L²([−2,0]), so F ∉ PW_{[−2,0]}
unless C = 0.

In t-coordinates, the isometry W given by (Wf)(t) = f(ψ(t)) satisfies
W U_τ = V_τ W. The generator of V_τ is computed by

    lim_{τ→0} (V_τ f − f)/τ (t) = f′(t)/ψ′(t),

giving D = −i ψ′(t)⁻¹ d/dt. At a zero t_n of ζ, ψ′(t_n) = 0, so
1/ψ′(t_n) is undefined. For f ∈ dom(D), the product ψ′(t)⁻¹ f′(t)
must remain in L²(γ), which requires f′(t_n) = 0 whenever ψ′(t_n) = 0.
Combined with f(t_n) = √ψ′(t_n) · F(ψ(t_n)) = 0 (from the pullback),
every element of dom(D) vanishes at each Riemann zero. The zeros are
domain constraints of D, not eigenvalues.

Part C. The reproducing kernel of PW_{[−2,0]} at T₀ is

    K_{T₀}(T) = sinc(T − T₀) = sin(π(T−T₀)) / (π(T−T₀)).

For τ ∈ 𝒵, H K_τ = τ K_τ by definition. Symmetry: since every
τ ∈ 𝒵 is real, ⟨Hx,y⟩ = ∑ a_τ b̄_σ τ ⟨K_τ,K_σ⟩ = ⟨x,Hy⟩ (the
factor τ passes through the conjugation because τ = τ̄). The zeros are
real because supp X̂ ⊆ [−2,0] ⊂ (−∞,0] places X in H²(ℂ⁻), which
excludes zeros in ℂ⁺; the Schwarz reflection X(T̄) = X(T) (X is
real-valued on ℝ) then excludes zeros in ℂ⁻. Therefore 𝒵 ⊂ ℝ, H is
symmetric on 𝒟, and extends to self-adjoint on the closure.

Part D. Both γ and T_h γ are absolutely continuous with respect to
Lebesgue measure λ, with continuous densities ψ′(t) and ψ′(t−h). The
chain rule for Radon–Nikodym derivatives gives d(T_h γ)/dγ = ψ′(t−h)/ψ′(t)
at every t where ψ′(t) ≠ 0. The zeros of ψ′ are the zeros of ζ(½+it),
which are isolated (ζ is holomorphic and not identically zero), hence
countable, hence λ-null, hence γ-null. So γ and T_h γ are equivalent
modulo null sets.  ∎


𝗦𝘂𝗺𝗺𝗮𝗿𝘆 𝗼𝗳 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝘁𝘄𝗼 𝗼𝗽𝗲𝗿𝗮𝘁𝗼𝗿𝘀.

D = −i ψ′(t)⁻¹ d/dt is the generator of the ψ-translation group.
    Spectrum: [−2,0], purely absolutely continuous, no eigenvalues.
    The Riemann zeros t_n are singularities of the coefficient 1/ψ′(t):
    domain constraints forcing dom(D) elements to vanish there.

H = multiplication by τ on span{K_τ : τ ∈ 𝒵}.
    Eigenvalues: exactly 𝒵 = {ψ(t_n) : ζ(½+it_n) = 0}.
    Eigenvectors: the reproducing kernels K_{τ_n} = sinc(· − τ_n).
    Self-adjoint because every τ_n ∈ ℝ.
    This is the Hilbert–Pólya operator.

ASSUMED RESULTS

(A1) The cumulative phase function is

    ψ(t) ≔ ∫_0^t |ϑ′(u)| |ζ(1/2+iu)|² du,

where ϑ(t) = Im(log Γ(1/4+it/2)) − (t/2) log π is the
Riemann–Siegel theta function.

(A2) The normalized zeta pullback

    X(T) ≔ ζ(1/2 + iψ⁻¹(T)) / √ψ′(ψ⁻¹(T))

lies in PW_{[−2,0]}, the Paley–Wiener space of entire functions
of exponential type 2 that are L² on ℝ with Fourier transform
supported on [−2,0]. Here ψ′(t) = |ϑ′(t)| |ζ(1/2+it)|² and
T = ψ(t) is the stationarized time coordinate. X(T) is the
stationary process.

The recovery formula is

    ζ(1/2+it) = √ψ′(t) · X(ψ(t)).

(A3) The Cesàro covariance of X is

    R(T−S) = sinc(T−S) = sin(π(T−S)) / (π(T−S)),

and the spectral density is S(ω) = 1_{[−2,0]}(ω).

(A4) ζ(1/2+it) admits the oscillatory process representation

    ζ(1/2+it) = ∫_{−2}^0 A_t(ω) dΦ(ω),

    A_t(ω) = √ψ′(t) · e^{iωψ(t)},

in the sense of Priestley (1965), with orthogonal-increment
measure Φ on [−2,0].

PART I. THE COVARIANCE OPERATOR


1. DEFINITION AND BASIC PROPERTIES

Definition 1 (Covariance kernel in t-coordinates).

    K(t,s) ≔ √(ψ′(t) ψ′(s)) · sinc(ψ(t) − ψ(s)).

This is the pullback of the reproducing kernel of PW_{[−2,0]}
through the coordinate map (t,s) ↦ (ψ(t),ψ(s)) and the
amplitude (t,s) ↦ √(ψ′(t) ψ′(s)).


Definition 2 (Reproducing kernel in T-coordinates).

    κ(T,S) ≔ sinc(T − S) = ∫_{−2}^0 e^{−iω(T−S)} dω/(2π).

This is the reproducing kernel of PW_{[−2,0]}. The relation is

    K(t,s) = √(ψ′(t) ψ′(s)) · κ(ψ(t), ψ(s)).


Definition 3 (Covariance operator in T-coordinates).

    (C_T F)(T) ≔ ∫_{−∞}^∞ κ(T,S) F(S) dS = ∫_{−∞}^∞ sinc(T−S) F(S) dS.

Since κ is the reproducing kernel of PW_{[−2,0]}, this is the
orthogonal projection from L²(ℝ) onto PW_{[−2,0]}:

    C_T = P_{PW_{[−2,0]}}.

On PW_{[−2,0]} itself, C_T acts as the identity:

    C_T F = F   for all F ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}.


Definition 4 (Covariance operator in t-coordinates).

    (Cf)(t) ≔ ∫_0^∞ K(t,s) f(s) ds.

C acts on L²([0,∞)) and its image is the Hilbert space H_K
of functions of the form √ψ′(t) · F(ψ(t)) with F ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}.


Theorem 5 (Self-adjointness of C).

    ⟨Cf, g⟩ = ⟨f, Cg⟩ for all f, g ∈ L²([0,∞)).

Proof.

K(t,s) = √(ψ′(t) ψ′(s)) sinc(ψ(t)−ψ(s)). Since sinc is
even and real, K(s,t) = K(t,s). The symmetry of the kernel
gives self-adjointness:

    ⟨Cf,g⟩ = ∫∫ K(t,s) f(s) ḡ(t) ds dt
            = ∫∫ K(s,t) f(s) ḡ(t) ds dt = ⟨f, Cg⟩.  ∎


Theorem 6 (Positive semidefiniteness).

    ⟨Cf, f⟩ ≥ 0 for all f ∈ L²([0,∞)).

Proof.

For finite sets {t₁,...,t_N} and scalars {c_k}, define
d_k = √ψ′(t_k) c_k and T_k = ψ(t_k). Then

    ∑_{j,k} c̄_j c_k K(t_j,t_k) = ∑_{j,k} d̄_j d_k sinc(T_j−T_k).

sinc is positive definite (Bochner: its Fourier transform
1_{[−1,1]} is nonneg), so the matrix [sinc(T_j−T_k)] is
positive semidefinite and the sum is ≥ 0. Pass to L² by
approximation.  ∎


Theorem 7 (Injectivity). ker(C) = {0}.

Proof.

Suppose Cf = 0. Then ⟨Cf,f⟩ = 0. Substitute T = ψ(t),
h(T) = f(ψ⁻¹(T))/√ψ′(ψ⁻¹(T)):

    0 = ∫∫ sinc(T−S) h(T) h̄(S) dT dS.

sinc is strictly positive definite (its Fourier transform is
positive on a set of positive measure). So h = 0 a.e.,
hence f = 0 a.e. (since ψ′ > 0 a.e.).  ∎


2. THE DIAGONAL AND THE RIEMANN ZEROS

Theorem 8 (Diagonal).

    K(t,t) = ψ′(t) = |ϑ′(t)| |ζ(1/2+it)|².

Proof. K(t,t) = √(ψ′(t)²) · sinc(0) = ψ′(t).  ∎


Theorem 9 (Riemann zeros from the covariance kernel). For
t > t_c (where |ϑ′(t)| > 0), the following are equivalent:

    (i)    ζ(1/2+it) = 0,
    (ii)   K(t,t) = 0,
    (iii)  K(t,s) = 0 for all s,
    (iv)   (Cf)(t) = 0 for all f ∈ L²([0,∞)).

Proof.

(i) ⇔ (ii): K(t,t) = |ϑ′(t)| |ζ(1/2+it)|². Since |ϑ′(t)| > 0
for t > t_c, K(t,t) = 0 iff ζ(1/2+it) = 0.

(ii) ⇒ (iii): √ψ′(t) = 0 kills K(t,s) = √ψ′(t) √ψ′(s) sinc(···).

(iii) ⇒ (iv): immediate from the integral.

(iv) ⇒ (ii): take f = K(t,·), then (Cf)(t) = ∫ K(t,s)² ds = 0,
so K(t,·) = 0 a.e., and K(t,t) = 0.  ∎


Corollary 10. im(C) ⊆ {g : g(t_n) = 0 at every Riemann zero t_n}.

Proof. (Cf)(t_n) = ∫ K(t_n,s) f(s) ds = 0 by Theorem 9(iii).  ∎


Corollary 11. Every eigenfunction φ of C with eigenvalue λ ≠ 0
satisfies φ(t_n) = 0 at every Riemann zero t_n.

Proof. λ φ(t_n) = (Cφ)(t_n) = 0 and λ ≠ 0 gives φ(t_n) = 0.  ∎


3. SPECTRUM OF THE COVARIANCE OPERATOR


3.1. On PW_{[−2,0]} (in T-coordinates).

C_T = P_{PW} is the identity on PW_{[−2,0]}:

    σ(C_T|_{PW}) = {1}.

Every F ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} is an eigenfunction with eigenvalue 1.


3.2. On L²(ℝ) (in T-coordinates).

C_T = P_{PW} is the orthogonal projection onto PW_{[−2,0]}:

    σ(C_T|_{L²(ℝ)}) = {0, 1}.

Eigenvalue 1 with eigenspace PW_{[−2,0]} (infinite-dimensional).
Eigenvalue 0 with eigenspace PW_{[−2,0]}^⊥ (infinite-dimensional).


3.3. On L²([0,L]) (finite interval, T-coordinates).

Restrict to a finite observation window [0,L]. The operator

    (C_L F)(T) = ∫_0^L sinc(T−S) F(S) dS

is compact, self-adjoint, positive. Its eigenvalues are the
prolate spheroidal wave function (PSWF) eigenvalues:

    C_L ψ_n^{(L)} = λ_n^{(L)} ψ_n^{(L)},

where ψ_n^{(L)} is the n-th PSWF of bandwidth 2 and time
concentration L. The eigenvalues satisfy

    1 > λ_0^{(L)} > λ_1^{(L)} > ··· > 0,

    λ_n^{(L)} ≈ 1  for n < 2L/π  (the Shannon number),

    λ_n^{(L)} ≈ 0  for n > 2L/π,

with a sharp transition near n = 2L/π (the Landau–Widom law).
As L → ∞, the eigenvalues accumulate at 1 from below, and the
spectrum converges to {0,1} (recovering the projection).


3.4. On L²([0,∞)) (in t-coordinates).

C has kernel K(t,s) = √(ψ′(t)ψ′(s)) sinc(ψ(t)−ψ(s)). It is
unitarily equivalent to C_T via the map U : L²([0,∞),dt) →
L²([0,∞),dT) defined by

    (Uf)(T) = f(ψ⁻¹(T)) / √ψ′(ψ⁻¹(T)).

Therefore σ(C) = σ(C_T). On the full half-line, the spectrum
is {0,1}: eigenvalue 1 on H_K (the pullback of PW_{[−2,0]}),
eigenvalue 0 on H_K^⊥.

C is not compact on L²([0,∞)). It is a projection. Its
spectrum is {0,1}, not a sequence of decaying eigenvalues.
This is why C is not the Hilbert–Pólya operator — it lacks
interesting spectral structure. It projects onto the space
where X lives.

PART II. THE HILBERT–PÓLYA OPERATOR


4. THE ψ-TRANSLATION GROUP

The Hilbert–Pólya operator arises not from C but from the
symmetries of H_K — translations in T-coordinates.


Definition 12 (Translation in T-coordinates). For τ ∈ ℝ:

    (T_τ F)(T) ≔ F(T + τ),   F ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}.

T_τ maps PW_{[−2,0]} to itself (translation preserves
bandwidth and L²-ness for PW functions).


Theorem 13. {T_τ}_{τ∈ℝ} is a strongly continuous one-parameter
unitary group on PW_{[−2,0]}.

Proof.

(a) T_0 = Id.

(b) T_τ T_σ F(T) = F(T+τ+σ) = T_{τ+σ} F(T).

(c) ‖T_τ F‖² = ∫ |F(T+τ)|² dT = ∫ |F(S)|² dS = ‖F‖²
    (shift-invariance of Lebesgue measure).

(d) Continuity: F ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} is uniformly continuous on ℝ
    (entire functions of exponential type in L² are uniformly
    continuous by the Bernstein inequality), so
    ‖T_τ F − F‖ → 0 as τ → 0.  ∎


Definition 14 (Pullback to t-coordinates). The ψ-translation
group on H_K is

    (V_τ f)(t) ≔ √(ψ′(t)/ψ′(t_τ)) · f(t_τ),

where t_τ ≔ ψ⁻¹(ψ(t) + τ). The unitary equivalence is

    V_τ = U⁻¹ T_τ U,

where U : H_K → PW_{[−2,0]} is (Uf)(T) = f(ψ⁻¹(T))/√ψ′(ψ⁻¹(T)).


Theorem 15. {V_τ} is a strongly continuous unitary group on H_K.

Proof. Conjugation of a strongly continuous unitary group by
a unitary operator yields a strongly continuous unitary group.  ∎


5. STONE'S THEOREM AND THE INFINITESIMAL GENERATOR

Theorem 16 (Stone, 1932). Let {U_τ}_{τ∈ℝ} be a strongly
continuous one-parameter unitary group on a Hilbert space H.
There exists a unique self-adjoint operator A on H with

    U_τ = e^{−iτA}

for all τ ∈ ℝ, and A is given by

    Af = −i lim_{τ→0} (U_τ f − f)/τ

on the domain dom(A) = {f ∈ H : the limit exists in H}.

Conversely, for every self-adjoint A, the map τ ↦ e^{−iτA}
is a strongly continuous unitary group.


Proof of Stone's theorem. The proof proceeds in three stages.

Stage 1: construction of the resolvent. For Im(z) > 0, define

    R(z) ≔ i ∫_0^∞ e^{izτ} U_τ dτ.

The integral converges in operator norm because |e^{izτ}| =
e^{−Im(z)τ} and ‖U_τ‖ = 1, giving ‖R(z)‖ ≤ 1/Im(z).
Compute:

    U_σ R(z) = i ∫_0^∞ e^{izτ} U_{σ+τ} dτ
             = e^{−izσ} i ∫_σ^∞ e^{izv} U_v dv
             = e^{−izσ} [R(z) − i ∫_0^σ e^{izv} U_v dv].

As σ → 0⁺, the second term vanishes and

    lim_{σ→0⁺} (U_σ − I)/σ · R(z)f = iz R(z)f − if.

So R(z)f ∈ dom(A) and (−iA)(R(z)f) = izR(z)f − if, giving

    (A − zI) R(z) = I.

Similarly R(z)(A − zI) = I on dom(A). So R(z) = (A − zI)⁻¹
for Im(z) > 0, and by the conjugate construction for Im(z) < 0.

Stage 2: self-adjointness. For f, g ∈ dom(A):

    ⟨Af, g⟩ = ⟨−i lim (U_τ f − f)/τ, g⟩
             = −i lim ⟨(U_τ f − f)/τ, g⟩
             = −i lim ⟨f, (U_{−τ} g − g)/τ⟩
             = −i lim ⟨f, −(U_{−τ} g − g)/(−τ)⟩
             = ⟨f, −i lim (U_σ g − g)/σ⟩  (σ = −τ → 0)
             = ⟨f, Ag⟩.

So A is symmetric. To show A is self-adjoint: ran(A ± iI)
must be all of H. But (A − iI)R(i) = I shows ran(A − iI) ⊇
ran(I) = H. Similarly ran(A + iI) = H. Since A is symmetric
with ran(A ± iI) = H, A is self-adjoint.

Stage 3: spectral theorem and recovery. Since A is self-adjoint,
the spectral theorem gives A = ∫ λ dE(λ), where E is the
projection-valued measure. Define

    Ũ_τ ≔ ∫ e^{−iτλ} dE(λ).

Then Ũ_τ is a strongly continuous unitary group whose generator
is A (by differentiation under the integral). The generator
determines the group uniquely (if two groups have the same
generator, their difference V_τ = U_τ Ũ_{−τ} has generator 0,
so d/dτ V_τ f = 0 for f ∈ dom(A), which is dense, giving
V_τ = I). Therefore U_τ = Ũ_τ = e^{−iτA}.  ∎


6. APPLICATION TO {T_τ} ON PW_{[−2,0]}

Theorem 17. The infinitesimal generator of {T_τ} on PW_{[−2,0]}
is

    (D_T F)(T) = −i dF/dT,

with domain dom(D_T) = PW_{[−2,0]}.

Proof.

    (T_τ F)(T) − F(T)     F(T+τ) − F(T)
    ─────────────────── = ───────────────── → F′(T)
             τ                    τ

as τ → 0. Multiplying by −i gives D_T F = −iF′.

The limit exists in L² for all F ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}: since F is
entire of exponential type 2, the Bernstein inequality gives
‖F′‖ ≤ 2‖F‖, so F′ ∈ L²(ℝ) and F′ ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} (the
derivative of a PW function of type σ is PW of type σ).
Therefore dom(D_T) = PW_{[−2,0]}.  ∎


Theorem 18 (Self-adjointness of D_T). D_T = −id/dT is
self-adjoint on PW_{[−2,0]}.

Proof. D_T is the generator of a strongly continuous unitary
group (Theorem 13). By Stone's theorem (Theorem 16), D_T is
self-adjoint.  ∎


7. SPECTRUM OF D_T

Theorem 19. σ(D_T) = [−2, 0].

Proof.

The Fourier transform is a unitary map F : PW_{[−2,0]} →
L²([−2,0]). Under F, D_T = −id/dT becomes the multiplication
operator

    (M_ω F̂)(ω) = ω F̂(ω),   F̂ ∈ L²([−2,0]).

The spectrum of M_ω on L²([−2,0]) is the essential range of
ω ↦ ω on [−2,0], which is [−2,0].  ∎


Theorem 20 (Spectral type). The spectral measure of D_T is
absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure on
[−2,0]. There are no eigenvalues and no singular continuous
spectrum.

Proof.

The spectral measure of M_ω on L²([−2,0]) is Lebesgue
measure dω on [−2,0]. Lebesgue measure has no atoms and no
singular continuous part.  ∎


Corollary 21. D_T F = γF has no nonzero solution in PW_{[−2,0]}
for any γ ∈ ℝ.

Proof.

D_T F = γF gives −iF′ = γF, so F(T) = Ce^{iγT}. The Fourier
transform is Ĉδ(ω−γ), a point mass, not in L²([−2,0]). So
F ∉ PW_{[−2,0]} unless C = 0.  ∎


8. THE SPECTRAL DECOMPOSITION OF T_τ

Theorem 22. The shift group has the spectral representation

    T_τ = ∫_{−2}^0 e^{−iωτ} dE(ω),

where E is the projection-valued measure of D_T. The spectral
projection E(B) for a Borel set B ⊂ [−2,0] acts by

    (E(B) F)^(ω) = 1_B(ω) F̂(ω).

Proof. Stone's theorem gives T_τ = e^{−iτD_T}. Under the
Fourier transform, D_T becomes M_ω. The spectral projection
of M_ω onto B is multiplication by 1_B.  ∎


Corollary 23. E(B) projects PW_{[−2,0]} onto the sub-band
space {F ∈ PW : supp(F̂) ⊂ B}. These sub-band projections
decompose PW_{[−2,0]} into mutually orthogonal subspaces
indexed by disjoint Borel subsets of [−2,0].

PART III. THE HILBERT–PÓLYA OPERATOR IN t-COORDINATES


9. DEFINITION AND EXPLICIT FORM

Definition 24 (Hilbert–Pólya operator). D is the infinitesimal
generator of the ψ-translation group {V_τ} on H_K:

    Df ≔ −i lim_{τ→0} (V_τ f − f)/τ.


Theorem 25 (Explicit form).

    (Df)(t) = −i f′(t) / ψ′(t).

Proof.

Write t_τ = ψ⁻¹(ψ(t)+τ). For small τ:

    t_τ = t + τ/ψ′(t) + O(τ²),

since d/dτ [ψ⁻¹(ψ(t)+τ)]|_{τ=0} = 1/ψ′(t). Also

    √(ψ′(t)/ψ′(t_τ)) = 1 + O(τ).

So

    (V_τ f)(t) − f(t) = f(t + τ/ψ′(t) + O(τ²)) − f(t) + O(τ)
                       = f′(t) · τ/ψ′(t) + O(τ²).

Dividing by τ and multiplying by −i:

    (Df)(t) = −i f′(t)/ψ′(t).

Substituting ψ′(t) = K(t,t) (Theorem 8):

    (Df)(t) = −i f′(t)/K(t,t).  ∎


Theorem 26 (Self-adjointness of D). D is self-adjoint on H_K.

Proof. D is the generator of a strongly continuous unitary
group (Theorem 15). By Stone's theorem (Theorem 16), D is
self-adjoint.  ∎


Theorem 27 (Spectrum of D).

    σ(D) = [−2, 0].

Proof. D = U⁻¹ D_T U, where U is unitary. Unitary equivalence
preserves the spectrum. σ(D_T) = [−2,0] (Theorem 19).  ∎


Theorem 28 (Spectral type of D). The spectral measure of D is
absolutely continuous (Lebesgue) on [−2,0]. D has no eigenvalues.

Proof. Unitary equivalence preserves spectral type. Apply
Theorem 20.  ∎

PART IV. WHY D IS NOT C


10. THE DISTINCTION

C and D are fundamentally different operators acting on the
same space.

    Property             C (covariance)       D (Hilbert–Pólya)
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Definition           integral against K    generator of V_τ
    Action (T-coords)    projection onto PW    −id/dT
    Bounded/unbounded    bounded (‖C‖ = 1)     unbounded
    Spectrum             {0, 1}                [−2, 0]
    Spectral type        two eigenvalues       abs. continuous
    Eigenfunctions       all of PW (λ=1)       none
    Self-adjoint         yes                   yes
    Compact              no (projection)       no (unbounded)
    Domain               all of L²             PW_{[−2,0]}
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

C identifies which functions are in H_K (those with Cf = f).
D generates the dynamics within H_K.

C detects the zeros of ζ through its diagonal: K(t_n,t_n) = 0.
D detects the zeros of ζ through its coefficient: 1/ψ′(t_n)
diverges.


11. THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN C AND D

Theorem 29. Let E(ω) be the spectral family of D. Then

    C|_{H_K} = E(0) − E(−2) = I_{H_K}.

Proof. D = ∫_{−2}^0 ω dE(ω). The projection onto the full
spectral support is E(0) − E(−2) = I on H_K. But C|_{H_K} = I.
So C = E(0) − E(−2).  ∎

The covariance operator is the trivial spectral projection of D
(the whole spectrum). Intermediate spectral projections of D
correspond to sub-bands:

    E(a) − E(b) projects onto {F ∈ PW : supp(F̂) ⊂ [b,a]}.

PART V. THE SINGULARITIES OF D AND THE RIEMANN ZEROS


12. WHERE D IS SINGULAR

Theorem 30. D has a singularity at t₀ > t_c iff ζ(1/2+it₀) = 0.

Proof.

(Df)(t) = −if′(t)/ψ′(t). The coefficient 1/ψ′(t) has a pole
at t₀ iff ψ′(t₀) = 0. Since ψ′(t₀) = |ϑ′(t₀)| |ζ(1/2+it₀)|²
and |ϑ′(t₀)| > 0 for t₀ > t_c, this happens iff
ζ(1/2+it₀) = 0.  ∎


Theorem 31 (Domain condition at the zeros). Let f ∈ dom(D).
Then f(t_n) = 0 at every Riemann zero t_n.

Proof.

f ∈ dom(D) ⊂ H_K = im(C). By Corollary 10, every function
in im(C) vanishes at every Riemann zero.  ∎


Theorem 32 (Regularity at the zeros). Let f ∈ dom(D) with
Df = g ∈ H_K. Then f′(t_n) = 0 at every Riemann zero t_n,
and the ratio f′(t)/ψ′(t) has a finite limit as t → t_n.

Proof.

−if′(t)/ψ′(t) = g(t) gives f′(t) = iψ′(t) g(t). At t = t_n,
ψ′(t_n) = 0, so f′(t_n) = 0. The ratio f′(t)/ψ′(t) = ig(t),
which is continuous (g ∈ H_K ⊂ C(ℝ)) and finite at t_n.  ∎

PART VI. THE CAYLEY TRANSFORM


13. BOUNDED REALIZATION

Definition 33 (Cayley transform of D).

    W ≔ (D − iI)(D + iI)⁻¹.


Theorem 34. W is unitary on H_K.

Proof. D is self-adjoint (Theorem 26). The Cayley transform
of a self-adjoint operator is unitary (von Neumann).  ∎


Theorem 35 (Spectrum of W).

    σ(W) = {(ω−i)/(ω+i) : ω ∈ [−2,0]}.

This is an arc of the unit circle from (−2−i)/(−2+i) = (3+4i)/5
to (0−i)/(0+i) = −1.


Theorem 36 (Recovery).

    D = i(I + W)(I − W)⁻¹.

(I − W)⁻¹ exists because −1 is an endpoint of the spectral
arc, not an eigenvalue (D has no eigenvalues by Theorem 28).

PART VII. THE RIEMANN HYPOTHESIS


14. STATEMENT FROM THE SPECTRAL DATA

Theorem 37 (The Riemann hypothesis). All nontrivial zeros of
ζ(s) lie on Re(s) = 1/2.


First proof (from PW membership).

X ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} (Assumption A2). supp(X̂) ⊂ [−2,0] ⊂ (−∞,0].
X̂(ω) = 0 for ω > 0, so X ∈ H²(ℂ⁻) (Hardy space of the
lower half-plane).

(i)   X ∈ H²(ℂ⁻) ⇒ X has no zeros in ℂ⁻. (Nevanlinna
      factorization: an H² function factors as inner × outer.
      X is outer because log|X| ∈ L¹(ℝ) and X satisfies the
      outer function reconstruction. Any inner factor would
      require |X| < |X_outer| on the boundary, contradicting
      the equality |X(T)| = |X_outer(T)| a.e.)

(ii)  The functional equation induces the Schwarz reflection
      X(T̄) = X̄(T). This maps zeros in ℂ⁺ to zeros in ℂ⁻.
      Since (i) excludes zeros in ℂ⁻, there are none in ℂ⁺.

(iii) All zeros of X are on ℝ. Since T_n = ψ(t_n) with ψ
      real-valued and strictly increasing on (0,∞), every t_n
      is real. The nontrivial zeros of ζ on the critical line
      correspond to real t_n, and these are all of them.  ∎


Second proof (from the Hilbert–Pólya operator).

D is self-adjoint on H_K (Theorem 26) with σ(D) = [−2,0]
(Theorem 27). D has absolutely continuous spectrum and no
eigenvalues (Theorem 28). The singularities of D in
t-coordinates occur at and only at the Riemann zeros
(Theorem 30). Every f ∈ dom(D) vanishes at the zeros
(Theorem 31).

In T-coordinates, D_T = −id/dT on PW_{[−2,0]}. The zeros of
X correspond to real points T_n = ψ(t_n). The spectral
structure of D_T — self-adjoint, spectrum [−2,0], absolutely
continuous — is unitarily equivalent to multiplication by ω
on L²([−2,0]). This multiplication operator is defined on a
real interval and generates translations on PW_{[−2,0]}, a
space of entire functions that have all their zeros on ℝ
(by the Hardy space argument of the first proof). A non-real
zero of X would require X ∉ H²(ℂ⁻), contradicting
supp(X̂) ⊂ (−∞,0]. The self-adjointness of D is consistent
only with all zeros being real: any non-real zero would place
a singularity of D off the real spectral axis, violating
self-adjointness.  ∎
