§11. CONCLUSION

The diagonal Padé approximant of a function analytic at the origin admits
a representation as the solution to a 2×2 matrix Riemann–Hilbert problem.
This formulation replaces the local derivative-matching of Taylor
approximation with the global reconstruction of a spectral measure from
its moments. The convergence domain is determined not by the distance to
the nearest singularity, but by the maximal region on which the associated
Riemann–Hilbert factorization remains solvable, a region governed by the
equilibrium measure of an extremal contour selected by the S-property.

For the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation that governs the
rough Heston characteristic function, this abstract framework acquires
concrete and computable content. The Cole–Hopf linearization reduces the
nonlinear fractional ODE to a linear system solvable by the matrix
Mittag-Leffler function E_{μ,1}(Az). The ratio of two entries of this
entire matrix function yields a globally meromorphic solution g(z) whose
poles are the zeros of E_{11}(z) and whose residues are ratios of the
other Mittag-Leffler entries at those zeros. The discrete spectral measure

    μ_g = Σ_{p ∈ P_g} Res(g,p) · δ_p

concentrated on this pole set generates the moment sequence, the Hankel
matrices, the orthogonal polynomials, the Jacobi operator, and ultimately
the Padé approximant itself.

The fractional Fokas–Its–Kitaev theorem of §9.10 identifies the
Riemann–Hilbert data explicitly. The weight w(s) is not an arbitrary
density but a sum of Dirac masses at the zeros of E_{11}(z), with masses
given by the residues of -E_{21}(z)/(c_2 E_{11}(z)). The contour Σ is not
selected by an abstract variational principle but is the Stahl compact of
a globally meromorphic function, which decomposes into the pole set plus
connecting arcs. The asymptotic matrix carries ordinary polynomial scaling
z^{±n} because the orthogonal polynomials are ordinary polynomials in the
transformed variable z = t^μ; the fractional parameter μ governs the
analytic structure of the Green's function and the equilibrium measure,
not the degree of the approximant.

The closure theorem of §9.10.5 shows that the diagram commutes:

    Fractional Riccati → Mittag-Leffler → meromorphic g
        → spectral measure μ_g → Hankel H_n → Jacobi J_n
        → Padé resolvent → Riemann–Hilbert Y
        → rational function → Stieltjes transform of truncated μ_g

As the truncation order n → ∞, the Padé poles condense on the Stahl
compact, the rational approximant converges uniformly on compact intervals
of the positive real axis by the non-explosion condition, and the
computable a-posteriori error bound of Theorem 9.19 certifies the
precision of the approximation in O(1) time.

The rough Heston model thus furnishes a test case where every object in
the abstract Padé–Riemann–Hilbert theory can in principle be computed from
a special-function exact solution, even though the pole locations are
transcendental numbers. The Müntz spectral method preserves the algebraic
structure of the fractional equation; the Padé resummation converts the
local Puiseux series into a global representation valid on all of
[0,∞); and the Riemann–Hilbert formulation reveals that the approximant
is not merely extrapolating derivatives but reconstructing the spectral
measure that generated the series.

The essential difference remains. Taylor approximation asks: what
polynomial has the same derivatives at this point? It is local, and its
radius is the distance to the nearest singularity. Padé approximation
asks: what spectral measure produces these moments, and what is its
Stieltjes transform? It is global, and its convergence domain is the
maximal region on which the Riemann–Hilbert factorization exists. For
the fractional Riccati equation, that measure is the Mittag-Leffler
spectral measure of Definition 9.32, and its Stieltjes transform is the
meromorphic function -E_{21}(z)/(c_2 E_{11}(z)). The Padé approximant
reconstructs this measure from finitely many moments, and the
Riemann–Hilbert problem encodes the reconstruction as a matrix
factorization. The convergence is global because the measure is global;
the poles are not defects but the discrete spectrum of the truncated Jacobi
operator; and the error is controlled because the logarithmic potential
of the equilibrium measure governs the approximation.

The three paradigms — exact special-function solution, rational Padé
approximation, and matrix Riemann–Hilbert factorization — are
mathematically equivalent for the fractional Riccati equation, with
explicit convergence certificates and computable error bounds. The paper
has presented the general theory in the form of theorems, corollaries,
and limit statements, each conclusion proved under explicitly stated
conditions, and has applied the framework to the rough Heston model in
detail, closing the loop between the abstract Riemann–Hilbert
formulation and the concrete Mittag-Leffler solution.
