The Padé–Müntz Method for Constant-Coefficient Fractional Riccati Equations
Stephen Crowley — April 2026


ABSTRACT

A purely algebraic, discretization-free solution method is developed for the general
constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation Dᵘ y = c₀ + c₁ y + c₂ y² of Caputo
order μ ∈ (0,1) with initial condition I^{1−μ} y(0) = 0. (A reference implementation
is available in arb4j: https://github.com/crowlogic/arb4j/blob/master/src/main/java/
arb/equations/ConstantCoefficientFractionalRiccatiEquation.java.)

Because the Müntz lattice {tᵏμ}_{k≥1} is closed under the Caputo derivative,
fractional integration, and pointwise multiplication, the Müntz spectral Tau
substitution yields an explicit Puiseux expansion y(t) = Σ_{k≥1} aₖ tᵏμ whose
coefficients satisfy a closed Gamma-ratio convolution recurrence.

The induced power series in z = tμ has a strictly positive radius of convergence R,
established by a Banach fixed-point argument on the equivalent Volterra equation, and
is finite for generic parameters because the complexified Riccati flow develops movable
singularities in the complex z-plane; the recurrence by itself therefore produces only
a local representation and is not globally convergent in t.

Global-in-t convergence on the positive real axis is recovered by re-summing the
Puiseux series via the diagonal [M/M] Padé approximant in z, constructed from the
Müntz coefficients through a single Hankel linear system H_M q⃗ = −b⃗. Under the
de Montessus de Ballore and Nuttall–Pommerenke theorems, the diagonal Padé sequence
converges to the analytic continuation of y along z = tμ for all t ≥ 0 whenever the
solution has no real-positive poles and g(z) := y(z^{1/μ}) admits a meromorphic
continuation to ℂ; in that setting a computable a-posteriori error bound

  |y(t) − yₘ(t)| ≤ |Δₘ(tμ)|² / (|Δₘ₋₁(tμ)| − |Δₘ(tμ)|)

in terms of successive Padé differences Δₖ = Rₖ − Rₖ₋₁ is derived from a uniform
geometric tail estimate.

A concluding section identifies the deeper structure: the substitution z = tμ promotes
the solution to a resurgent transseries in z, the Padé step is the computational
instantiation of Borel–Padé resummation, and the global analytic continuation is
governed by a scalar Riemann–Hilbert problem whose jump contours are the Stokes lines
of the transseries. This perspective points to natural generalizations to higher-degree
polynomial nonlinearities, fractional ODE systems via Hermite–Padé simultaneous
approximation, and non-constant coefficients via isomonodromic Riemann–Hilbert
problems of Painlevé type.


§ 1.  FRACTIONAL CALCULUS AND THE MÜNTZ BASIS


§ 1.1.  Riemann–Liouville integral and Caputo derivative.

Definition 1.1 (Riemann–Liouville integral).
Let r > 0 and f ∈ L¹_loc[0,T]. Define

  Iʳ f(t) := 1/Γ(r) · ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{r−1} f(s) ds,   t ∈ [0,T].

Definition 1.2 (Caputo derivative).
Let μ ∈ (0,1) and f satisfy I^{1−μ} f ∈ C¹[0,T]. Define

  Dᵘ f(t) := d/dt (I^{1−μ} f(t)) − f(0) t^{−μ} / Γ(1−μ),   t ∈ (0,T].

The fundamental relations are

  Iᵘ Dᵘ f(t) = f(t) − f(0),     Dᵘ Iᵘ f(t) = f(t).

Remark 1.3 (Vanishing correction term).
For the fractional Riccati equation (2.1) below, the initial condition
I^{1−μ} y(0) = 0 is consistent with the Müntz ansatz y(t) = Σ_{k≥1} aₖ tᵏμ, since
by (1.3) below I^{1−μ}[tᵏμ]|_{t=0} = 0 for all k ≥ 1. The ansatz gives y(0) = 0,
so the correction term −f(0) t^{−μ}/Γ(1−μ) in the Caputo definition vanishes
identically along the Riccati solution, and Dᵘ y(t) = d/dt I^{1−μ} y(t) throughout.

Proposition 1.4 (Action on powers).
Let s > −1 and r > 0. Then

  (1.3)   Iʳ tˢ = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+r+1) · t^{s+r}.

Let μ ∈ (0,1) and s > 0. Then

  (1.4)   Dᵘ tˢ = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+1−μ) · t^{s−μ}.

Proof.
For (1.3), Iʳ tˢ = 1/Γ(r) · ∫₀ᵗ (t−u)^{r−1} uˢ du. The substitution u = tx gives

  Iʳ tˢ = t^{s+r}/Γ(r) · ∫₀¹ (1−x)^{r−1} xˢ dx
         = t^{s+r}/Γ(r) · Γ(s+1) Γ(r)/Γ(s+r+1)
         = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+r+1) · t^{s+r}.

For (1.4): since s > 0, lim_{t→0⁺} tˢ = 0; the Caputo correction term vanishes, and
Dᵘ tˢ = d/dt I^{1−μ} tˢ. By (1.3) with r = 1−μ,

  I^{1−μ} tˢ = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+2−μ) · t^{s+1−μ};

differentiating and using Γ(s+2−μ) = (s+1−μ) Γ(s+1−μ) yields (1.4). ∎

Remark 1.5 (The case s = 0).
The hypothesis s > 0 in (1.4) cannot be relaxed to s ≥ 0. For s = 0, f(t) ≡ 1,
f(0) = 1, and the correction term gives Dᵘ(1) = t^{−μ}/Γ(1−μ) − t^{−μ}/Γ(1−μ) = 0,
whereas (1.4) at s = 0 equals t^{−μ}/Γ(1−μ) ≠ 0. The Caputo derivative of a
constant is zero, consistent with Dᵘ y(0) = 0 for solutions of (2.1) vanishing at
the origin.


§ 1.2.  The Müntz basis.  Fix μ ∈ (0,1) and T > 0.

Definition 1.6 (Müntz system).
For k ∈ ℕ, define eₖ(t) := tᵏμ on [0,T]. The span of {1, eₖ : k ≥ 1} is

  ℳ := span{1, tμ, t²μ, t³μ, …}.

Remark 1.7 (Completeness).
By the Müntz–Szász theorem, {tᵏμ}_{k≥1} is complete in L²[0,1] iff Σ_{k≥1} (kμ)⁻¹ = ∞.
Since Σ_{k≥1} k⁻¹ = ∞, completeness holds for every μ ∈ (0,1). This underwrites the
Müntz–Tau spectral method.

Proposition 1.8 (Closure of ℳ).
For k ≥ 1 and r > 0,

  Dᵘ tᵏμ = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k−1)μ+1) · t^{(k−1)μ},
  Iʳ tᵏμ = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ(kμ+r+1) · t^{kμ+r},
  tʲμ · tᵏμ = t^{(j+k)μ}.

Consequently Dᵘ ℳ ⊂ ℳ, Iʳ ℳ ⊂ ℳ, and ℳ is closed under multiplication.

Proof.
First identity from Prop. 1.4 with s = kμ > 0; second from (1.3) with s = kμ > −1;
third is direct. ∎

These three closure properties — under Dᵘ, under Iʳ, and under multiplication — are
exactly what makes the Müntz lattice the canonical basis for any constant-coefficient
fractional ODE with polynomial nonlinearity. The Müntz–Tau substitution
[Esmaeili–Shamsi 2015] preserves the entire algebraic structure of the equation.


§ 2.  THE CONSTANT-COEFFICIENT FRACTIONAL RICCATI EQUATION


§ 2.1.  Equation and Volterra representation.

Let μ ∈ (0,1) and c₀, c₁, c₂ ∈ ℂ. Consider

  (2.1)   Dᵘ y(t) = c₀ + c₁ y(t) + c₂ y(t)²,   I^{1−μ} y(0) = 0.

Applying Iᵘ and using Iᵘ Dᵘ f = f − f(0) (and y(0) = 0) gives the equivalent
Volterra integral equation

  (2.2)   y(t) = 1/Γ(μ) · ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{μ−1} (c₀ + c₁ y(s) + c₂ y(s)²) ds.

Remark 2.1 (Hölder regularity at the origin).
Evaluating (2.2) at t = 0 gives y(0) = 0. Near the origin,
y(t) = c₀/Γ(μ+1) · tμ + 𝒪(t²μ), so y is Hölder continuous of order μ but not C¹
at 0. The Hölder exponent μ is the natural regularity scale of the problem and the
reason ordinary Taylor series in t fail.


§ 2.2.  The Müntz–Tau Puiseux series solution.

Theorem 2.2 (Series solution in {tᵏμ}).
There exists R > 0 and coefficients (aₖ)_{k≥1} ⊂ ℂ such that

  y(t) = Σ_{k=1}^∞ aₖ tᵏμ,   |t| < R^{1/μ},

with

  a₁ = c₀ / Γ(μ+1),

  aₖ₊₁ = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) · ( c₁ aₖ + c₂ Σ_{j+ℓ=k, 1≤j,ℓ≤k−1} aⱼ aₗ ),  k ≥ 1.

Remark 2.3 (Quadratic convolution at k = 1).
For k = 1 the convolution sum is empty, so a₂ = Γ(μ+1)/Γ(2μ+1) · c₁ a₁. The
quadratic term first contributes at k = 2, giving
a₃ = Γ(2μ+1)/Γ(3μ+1) · (c₁ a₂ + c₂ a₁²).

Proof of Theorem 2.2.
Existence of a positive radius R > 0 and analyticity of y in z = tμ on |z| < R is
established in Theorem 2.4 below by a Banach fixed-point argument on (2.2). Granting
that, write y(t) = Σ_{k≥1} aₖ tᵏμ; by tʲμ tᵏμ = t^{(j+k)μ},

  y(t)² = Σ_{m=2}^∞ ( Σ_{j+ℓ=m, 1≤j,ℓ≤m−1} aⱼ aₗ ) tmμ,

abbreviated bₘ := Σ aⱼ aₗ (with b₁ = 0). Substituting into (2.2) and splitting,

  y(t) = c₀/Γ(μ) · ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{μ−1} ds
       + c₁/Γ(μ) · Σ_{k≥1} aₖ ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{μ−1} sᵏμ ds
       + c₂/Γ(μ) · Σ_{m≥2} bₘ ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{μ−1} smμ ds.

The first integral equals tμ / Γ(μ+1). For the others, (1.3) with s = kμ, r = μ gives

  ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{μ−1} sᵏμ ds = Γ(μ) · Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) · t^{(k+1)μ}.

Reindexing n = k+1 and n = m+1 and matching the coefficient of tnμ for each n ≥ 1
yields a₁ = c₀/Γ(μ+1) at n = 1 and, for n ≥ 2,

  aₙ = c₁ aₙ₋₁ · Γ((n−1)μ+1)/Γ(nμ+1) + c₂ bₙ₋₁ · Γ((n−1)μ+1)/Γ(nμ+1),

which is the recurrence after renaming n ↦ k+1. ∎

Theorem 2.4 (Strictly positive Puiseux radius; coefficient bound; generic finiteness).
Consider (2.2).

  (i)  Existence and R > 0. Set
       T₀μ := min( Γ(μ+1)/(|c₀|+|c₁|+|c₂|+1),  Γ(μ+1)/(2(|c₁|+2|c₂|+1)) ) > 0.
       There exists a unique solution y ∈ C[0,T₀] of (2.2) with ‖y‖∞ ≤ 1; this
       solution is analytic in z = tμ on {|z| < T₀μ}, so the Puiseux series has
       radius of convergence R ≥ T₀μ > 0.

  (ii) Coefficient bound. For every 0 < r < R, with M(r) := sup_{|z|=r} |g(z)|
       where g(z) := y(z^{1/μ}),
       |aₖ| ≤ M(r) / rᵏ,   k ≥ 1.

  (iii) Generic finiteness, R < ∞. For generic parameters with c₂ ≠ 0, R < ∞: the
        complexified Riccati flow develops movable singularities at finite distance
        from the origin in the complex z-plane.

Proof.

(i) Let B₁ := {y ∈ C[0,T₀] : ‖y‖∞ ≤ 1} with the sup norm, and define

  Φ(y)(t) := 1/Γ(μ) · ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{μ−1} (c₀ + c₁ y(s) + c₂ y(s)²) ds
            = Iᵘ(c₀ + c₁ y + c₂ y²)(t).

For y ∈ B₁ and t ∈ [0,T₀], |c₀ + c₁ y(t) + c₂ y(t)²| ≤ |c₀|+|c₁|+|c₂|, so
by (1.3),

  |Φ(y)(t)| ≤ (|c₀|+|c₁|+|c₂|) · tμ/Γ(μ+1) ≤ (|c₀|+|c₁|+|c₂|) · T₀μ/Γ(μ+1).

By the first term in the definition of T₀μ, this is ≤ (|c₀|+|c₁|+|c₂|) /
(|c₀|+|c₁|+|c₂|+1) < 1; hence Φ(B₁) ⊂ B₁.

For y₁, y₂ ∈ B₁,

  |c₁(y₁−y₂) + c₂(y₁²−y₂²)| = |c₁ + c₂(y₁+y₂)| · |y₁−y₂|
                                ≤ (|c₁| + 2|c₂|) · ‖y₁−y₂‖∞,
so
  ‖Φ(y₁)−Φ(y₂)‖∞ ≤ T₀μ/Γ(μ+1) · (|c₁|+2|c₂|) · ‖y₁−y₂‖∞.

By the second term in T₀μ, this is ≤ (|c₁|+2|c₂|) / [2(|c₁|+2|c₂|+1)] < ½; Φ
is a strict contraction on B₁. Banach fixed-point gives a unique y★ ∈ B₁ with
Φ(y★) = y★, solving (2.2). The formal Puiseux series solves the same Volterra
equation order-by-order; by uniqueness, the series equals y★ on [0,T₀], converges
for |tμ| < T₀μ, proving R ≥ T₀μ > 0.

(ii) By (i), g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) = Σ_{k≥1} aₖ zᵏ is analytic on {|z| < R}. For
0 < r < R, Cauchy's integral formula gives

  aₖ = 1/(2πi) · ∮_{|z|=r} g(z)/z^{k+1} dz,
  |aₖ| ≤ sup_{|z|=r} |g(z)| · r^{−k} = M(r) r^{−k}.

(iii) For μ = 1 with c₂ ≠ 0, the linear substitution y = −w′/(c₂ w) reduces
y′ = c₀ + c₁ y + c₂ y² to a second-order linear ODE for w; the zeros of w
generically lie at finite t, giving y a simple pole at each. The same algebraic
structure of the recurrence persists for μ ∈ (0,1), and g(z) generically inherits
movable singularities at finite distance from the origin in the z-plane. Hence R < ∞
for generic parameters with c₂ ≠ 0. ∎

Remark 2.5 (Why this is only a local representation).
Theorem 2.4(iii) is the central obstruction: every aₖ is computed in closed form,
yet the series does not converge for all t ≥ 0. The complex z-plane carries movable
singularities of the Riccati flow, and the Puiseux radius R is the distance to the
nearest such. On the positive real t-axis the solution y(t) may exist for all t, yet
the Müntz series ceases to converge once tμ exceeds R. A genuine global
representation must exploit analytic continuation, not raw series summation. This is
the role played by Padé approximation in § 3.


§ 3.  PADÉ RESUMMATION IN z = tμ


§ 3.1.  Power series in z and the Hankel system.

Let z := tμ and g(z) := y(z^{1/μ}). Then

  g(z) = Σ_{k=1}^∞ aₖ zᵏ,   |z| < R.

Fix M ∈ ℕ and seek polynomials

  Pₘ(z) := Σ_{k=0}^M pₖ zᵏ,    Qₘ(z) := 1 + Σ_{k=1}^M qₖ zᵏ,

satisfying the Padé matching condition

  Qₘ(z) g(z) − Pₘ(z) = 𝒪(z^{2M+1})   (z → 0).

Lemma 3.1 (Hankel form of the denominator system).
Matching coefficients of zⁿ for 0 ≤ n ≤ 2M gives p₀ = 0,

  pₙ = aₙ + Σ_{j=1}^{min(n,M)} qⱼ aₙ₋ⱼ,   1 ≤ n ≤ M,
  0  = aₙ + Σ_{j=1}^M qⱼ aₙ₋ⱼ,           M+1 ≤ n ≤ 2M.

The denominator system is the Hankel linear system

  Hₘ q⃗ = −b⃗,

  where Hₘ is the M×M Hankel matrix with (Hₘ)ᵢⱼ = aₘ₊ᵢ₋ⱼ,
  q⃗ = (q₁, …, qₘ)ᵀ,  b⃗ = (aₘ₊₁, …, a₂ₘ)ᵀ.

Proof. Direct expansion of Qₘ g and identification of coefficients. ∎

Remark 3.2 (Invertibility of Hₘ).
Hₘ is invertible iff g(z) is not a rational function of degree less than M. For μ = 1
the Riccati solution is rational, and Hₘ becomes singular for M beyond the exact pole
order. For μ ∈ (0,1), g(z) admits no finite-order rational representation; det Hₘ ≠ 0
generically for all M ≥ 1. If a particular order M yields det Hₘ = 0, use M ± 1.

Lemma 3.3 (Solution).
If det Hₘ ≠ 0, then Hₘ q⃗ = −b⃗ has the unique solution q⃗ = −Hₘ⁻¹ b⃗, and the
numerator coefficients are given by pₙ = aₙ + Σ_{j=1}^{min(n,M)} qⱼ aₙ₋ⱼ.

The diagonal Padé approximant is then

  Rₘ(z) := Pₘ(z) / Qₘ(z) = ( Σ_{k=0}^M pₖ zᵏ ) / ( 1 + Σ_{k=1}^M qₖ zᵏ ).

Definition 3.4 (Padé domain).
𝒟ₘ := { z ∈ ℂ : det Hₘ ≠ 0,  Qₘ(z) ≠ 0 }.


§ 3.2.  Remainder representation and successive-difference bound.

Define the remainder

  εₘ(z) := g(z) − Rₘ(z).

By construction εₘ(z) = 𝒪(z^{2M+1}), and standard Padé theory [Baker–Graves-Morris
1996] gives the structural representation

  εₘ(z) = Πₘ₊₁(z) / Qₘ(z)² · z^{2M+1}

for some polynomial Πₘ₊₁ of degree at most M+1.

For the a-posteriori bound, define

  Δₖ(z) := Rₖ(z) − Rₖ₋₁(z),   k ≥ 1.

Assumption 3.5 (Uniform geometric tail).
At a fixed z ∈ 𝒟ₘ ∩ 𝒟ₘ₋₁:

  (i)   g has no poles in {|ζ| ≤ |z|};
  (ii)  Qₘ(z) ≠ 0 and Qₘ₋₁(z) ≠ 0;
  (iii) there exists ρ ∈ (0,1) such that |Δₖ₊₁(z)| ≤ ρ |Δₖ(z)| for every k ≥ M−1.

Theorem 3.6 (Computable error bound).
Under Assumption 3.5 with contraction ratio ρ, Rₖ(z) converges and

  |g(z) − Rₘ(z)| ≤ ρ |Δₘ(z)| / (1 − ρ).

In particular, with ρ★ := |Δₘ(z)| / |Δₘ₋₁(z)|,

  |g(z) − Rₘ(z)| ≤ |Δₘ(z)|² / (|Δₘ₋₁(z)| − |Δₘ(z)|).

Proof.
By Assumption 3.5(iii), |Δₘ₊ⱼ(z)| ≤ ρʲ |Δₘ(z)| for every j ≥ 0 (induction on j).
Since Σ_{j=0}^∞ ρʲ |Δₘ(z)| < ∞, the telescoping series

  g(z) − Rₘ(z) = Σ_{k=M+1}^∞ Δₖ(z)

converges absolutely. Estimating term-by-term,

  |g(z) − Rₘ(z)| ≤ Σ_{j=1}^∞ |Δₘ₊ⱼ(z)| ≤ |Δₘ(z)| Σ_{j=1}^∞ ρʲ = ρ |Δₘ(z)| / (1−ρ).

Substituting ρ = ρ★ = |Δₘ|/|Δₘ₋₁| yields ρ★ |Δₘ| / (1−ρ★) = |Δₘ|² / (|Δₘ₋₁|−|Δₘ|). ∎


§ 3.3.  Global convergence on the positive real axis.

Theorem 3.7 (de Montessus de Ballore [Baker–Graves-Morris 1996; Montessus 1902]).
Let g be meromorphic in {|z| < ρ} with exactly n poles (counted with multiplicity) in
that disc and no poles on the boundary. The diagonal Padé approximants Rₘ of order
(M,M) converge to g uniformly on compact subsets of {|z| < ρ} ∖ {poles} as M → ∞.

Theorem 3.8 (Nuttall–Pommerenke [Baker–Graves-Morris 1996; Nuttall 1970]).
Let g be meromorphic in ℂ with at most countably many poles having no finite
accumulation point. The diagonal sequence Rₘ converges to g in capacity on ℂ ∖ E,
where E has logarithmic capacity zero. In particular, convergence is quasi-everywhere,
though not necessarily pointwise at every individual point.

Remark 3.9 (Which theorem applies).
Theorem 3.7 gives pointwise uniform convergence on compacta but requires knowing the
exact pole count. Theorem 3.8 requires no such count but yields only quasi-everywhere
convergence, and presupposes meromorphy on all of ℂ. When g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) is
meromorphic in ℂ with no poles on (0,∞) — the canonical case for globally bounded
Riccati solutions on the real time axis — both theorems apply, and Rₘ(tμ) → y(t)
for every t > 0.

Theorem 3.10 (Global Padé–Müntz representation).
Let y(t) solve (2.1), assume y is bounded on [0,∞) so that g(z) has no poles on the
positive real z-axis, and assume g(z) := y(z^{1/μ}) admits a meromorphic continuation
to ℂ with at most countably many poles having no finite accumulation point. Then

  y(t) = lim_{M→∞} Rₘ(tμ) = lim_{M→∞} Pₘ(tμ) / Qₘ(tμ),   for every t ≥ 0.

Proof.
Under the meromorphy hypothesis, Theorem 3.8 applies and Rₘ → g in capacity on
ℂ ∖ E with cap(E) = 0. Since g is holomorphic on a neighbourhood of every point of
(0,∞), Theorem 3.7 gives uniform convergence on compact subsets of ℂ ∖ {poles};
in particular pointwise convergence at every z = tμ ∈ (0,∞). At t = 0, y(0) = 0
and Rₘ(0) = p₀/Qₘ(0) = 0/1 = 0 for every M, so the limit holds trivially. ∎

Remark 3.11 (Status of the meromorphy hypothesis).
Meromorphy of g on ℂ is established for μ = 1 (where the linearization y = −w′/(c₂ w)
reduces the Riccati equation to a linear ODE with entire solutions) and is conjectured
for μ ∈ (0,1) on the basis of the Painlevé property of the underlying flow and the
empirical pole distribution of the diagonal Padé sequence. A complete proof for
arbitrary μ ∈ (0,1) lies outside the scope of the present paper and is connected to
the Riemann–Hilbert structure discussed in § 4. In practical computation, the
hypothesis is verified a posteriori by the convergence behavior of Rₘ(tμ) at probe
points t of interest, controlled by the bound of Theorem 3.6.


§ 3.4.  Algorithmic summary.

Given (c₀, c₁, c₂, μ) and Padé order M:

  Step 1.  Compute Puiseux coefficients a₁, …, a₂ₘ by the recurrence of Theorem 2.2.
           Cost: 𝒪(M²).

  Step 2.  Form the Hankel matrix Hₘ and right-hand side b⃗.

  Step 3.  Solve Hₘ q⃗ = −b⃗ for the denominator coefficients.
           Cost: 𝒪(M³).

  Step 4.  Recover numerator coefficients via pₙ = aₙ + Σ qⱼ aₙ₋ⱼ.

  Step 5.  Evaluate yₘ(t) = Pₘ(tμ) / Qₘ(tμ) at any t ≥ 0.
           Cost: 𝒪(M) per query.

Steps 1–4 are independent of t; the entire family {yₘ(t) : t ≥ 0} is encoded in a
single rational function in z = tμ. There is no grid, no time stepping, no Newton
iteration, no fixed-point loop.


§ 4.  RESURGENCE, BOREL–PADÉ RESUMMATION, AND RIEMANN–HILBERT

The Padé–Müntz construction solves the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati
equation in closed algebraic form by exploiting two independent structural facts: the
Müntz lattice closes the differential algebra of Dᵘ, Iʳ, · ; and the diagonal Padé in
z = tμ re-sums the resulting Puiseux series past its finite radius of analyticity.
Both are special cases of a deeper principle drawn from the theory of resurgent
functions and the Riemann–Hilbert correspondence.


§ 4.1.  The substitution z = tμ is a power-series algebraization.

A constant-coefficient fractional ODE in t is not algebraic in t — the operator Dᵘ is
non-local, and the solution is generically only Hölder continuous of exponent μ at the
origin (Remark 2.1). After z = tμ, the solution y(t) is represented by the convergent
power series g(z) = Σ_{k≥1} aₖ zᵏ (Theorem 2.2). In this sense, z = tμ is the
canonical analytic variable for the problem: it converts the Hölder-continuous solution
t ↦ y(t) into a power-series-analytic function z ↦ g(z), lifting the problem into the
analytic category and making the full machinery of complex analysis available.

A point of precision: z = tμ does not reduce the Volterra equation (2.2) to a local
ODE in z. A direct change of variables converts (2.2) into the functional equation

  g(z) = z/(μ Γ(μ)) · ∫₀¹ (1−u^{1/μ})^{μ−1} u^{1/μ−1} (c₀ + c₁ g(zu) + c₂ g(zu)²) du,

which involves g(zu) for u ∈ [0,1] and is therefore non-local in z. The
"algebraization" is of the representation of the solution, not of the equation itself.


§ 4.2.  Solutions are conjecturally resurgent transseries.

When g(z) is meromorphic on ℂ and the underlying flow possesses the Painlevé property,
the formal expansion at any singular point can be promoted to a resurgent transseries
in the sense of Écalle [Écalle 1981; Costin 2008]:

  ỹ(z) = Σ_{n≥0} e^{−nA/z} · z^{nβₙ} · Σ_{k≥0} aₙₖ zᵏ,

where the leading sector n = 0 is the Puiseux series of Theorem 2.2 and the higher
sectors n ≥ 1 encode non-perturbative contributions controlled by the action A (the
Riccati pole structure). The alien derivatives ΔₐỹThe Écalle measure the obstruction
to analytic continuation across each singularity, and the Stokes constants Sₐ record
the discontinuities of the lateral Borel sums when crossing Stokes lines. The
resurgent picture is established rigorously for integer-order ODEs with the Painlevé
property and is conjectural in the fractional regime.


§ 4.3.  Padé is computational Borel–Padé resummation.

The diagonal Padé approximant in z is the canonical computational instantiation of
Borel–Padé resummation. The Borel transform sends Σ aₖ zᵏ to ĝ(ζ) = Σ aₖ ζᵏ⁻¹/Γ(k),
then resums by Laplace-transforming ĝ along a ray. The Padé approximant Rₘ computes
essentially the same object: Pₘ/Qₘ is the unique meromorphic continuation that
interpolates the formal series and respects the underlying singularity structure.
Concretely, Theorems 3.7 and 3.8 say that Rₘ recovers exactly the lateral
Borel–Laplace sum on the real z-axis whenever no Stokes ray crosses (0,∞)
[Costin 2008; Pommerenke 1973].


§ 4.4.  The Riemann–Hilbert structure.

The poles of Rₘ for finite M are not arbitrary — as M → ∞, they cluster along Stokes
lines in the z-plane, the branch cuts of the analytic continuation of y.
Nuttall–Pommerenke (Theorem 3.8) is a weak version of this fact: convergence in
capacity means the spurious poles distribute themselves along the natural boundary of
analyticity. The strong version, due to Stahl, Gonchar, and others [Stahl 1997],
identifies the equilibrium measure on those Stokes lines as the limiting pole
distribution of the diagonal Padé sequence. This equilibrium problem is precisely a
scalar Riemann–Hilbert problem for the resolvent of a logarithmic potential, and the
diagonal Padé asymptotics are obtained by the Deift–Zhou steepest descent /
Fokas–Its–Kitaev machinery applied to the associated jump matrix [Deift 1999].

The unifying perspective of this paper:

  ❝ For a constant-coefficient fractional ODE Dᵘ y = F(y) with polynomial F whose
    substituted solution g(z) := y(z^{1/μ}) is meromorphic on ℂ, the substitution
    z = tμ promotes the solution to a resurgent transseries in z. The diagonal Padé
    in z is a Borel–Padé resummation of that transseries, and its global analytic
    continuation along the positive real axis is governed by a Riemann–Hilbert problem
    whose jump contours are the Stokes lines of the transseries. ❞


§ 4.5.  Generalizations.

◆  Higher-degree polynomial nonlinearities (Bernoulli, Abel, and beyond).
For Dᵘ y = F(y) with F any polynomial of degree d, the Müntz lattice remains closed
under the nonlinearity by tʲμ tᵏμ = t^{(j+k)μ}, so the Tau substitution yields a
closed Gamma-ratio recurrence with a d-fold convolution sum. The Padé step and Hankel
system are unchanged. Cubic Riccati (Abel of the second kind), quartic, and arbitrary
polynomial F are all immediate.

◆  Fractional ODE systems via Hermite–Padé.
For a system Dᵘ y⃗ = F⃗(y⃗) with polynomial F⃗, each component admits a Müntz Puiseux
expansion, and the scalar Hankel system is replaced by a matrix Padé (or simultaneous
Hermite–Padé) system. The scalar Riemann–Hilbert problem becomes matrix-valued, with
jump contours determined by the eigenvalue structure of the linearization at the movable
singularities [Deift 1999]. Multi-factor rough Heston, multi-asset rough volatility, and
multi-component fractional reaction–diffusion all fall in this class.

◆  Non-constant coefficients: isomonodromy and Painlevé.
When Dᵘ y = c₀(t) + c₁(t) y + c₂(t) y² with non-constant cᵢ [El Euch–Rosenbaum
2019], the Müntz lattice is generally no longer preserved, the explicit Gamma-ratio
recurrence breaks down, and the Puiseux structure may need to be enlarged to include
logarithmic and Fuchsian terms. The associated Riemann–Hilbert problem is then
isomonodromic rather than equilibrium-type, and the nonlinear special functions that
arise — notably the Painlevé transcendents and their q- and fractional analogs —
replace the elementary rational Padé approximants. This is also where the rough
volatility literature stalls, and where new mathematics is genuinely required.
