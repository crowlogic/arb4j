The Hardy \(Z\) Function as a Priestley Oscillatory Process:
Uniformly Modulated Representation, Stationary Core,
and Spectral Factorization via the Riemann–Siegel Formula

March 2026
Abstract. Rao's integral representation of the Dirichlet eta function \(\eta(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\tau)\) exhibits this kernel as positive-definite with spectral density \(K_{1/2}(\omega)=e^{\omega/2}/(e^{e^\omega}+1)\). The Hardy \(Z\) function is a Priestley oscillatory process: it admits the decomposition \(Z(t)=c(\vartheta(t))\,S(\vartheta(t))\) where \(c(\tau)=\sqrt{\vartheta'(\vartheta^{-1}(\tau))}\) is the uniformly modulated envelope and \(S\) is the stationary Gaussian core with spectral density \(K_{1/2}\). The reparametrization by the functional inverse \(\vartheta^{-1}\) exactly stationarizes the zero-crossing rate of \(Z\), and the envelope \(c\) is proved — via the exact Riemann–Siegel formula — to be the unique amplitude that renders \(S\) second-order stationary with \(\tau\)-independent spectral shape \(K_{1/2}\). The spectral factor \(\hat{h}(\omega)=\sqrt{K_{1/2}(\omega)}\) is expressed in closed form via the central binomial zeta function, and the white-noise random measure underlying the whole structure is identified explicitly.

1. The Rao Covariance Kernel and Its Spectral Density

Starting from the integral representation (3) of Rao (2012) with \(s=\sigma+it\), the substitution \(x=e^u\) converts the Gamma-integral for \(\zeta(s)\) into a Fourier integral. Combined with the relation \(\tfrac{x}{e^x+1}=\tfrac{x}{e^x-1}-\tfrac{2x}{e^{2x}-1}\), one obtains the identity

Definition 1 (Rao kernel and spectral density). For \(0<\sigma<1\), define \[ K_\sigma(\omega) \;=\; \frac{e^{\sigma\omega}}{e^{e^\omega}+1}, \qquad \omega\in\mathbb{R}. \] This is the Rao spectral density at parameter \(\sigma\).

The fundamental identity of Rao (2012), equation \((*)\), then reads:

\(\displaystyle \eta\!\left(\sigma+it\right) \;=\; \zeta(\sigma+it)\,\bigl(1-2^{1-\sigma-it}\bigr) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} K_\sigma(\omega)\,e^{i\omega t}\,d\omega. \) (1)

Comparing with the Bochner–Cramér pairing \(r(\tau)=\int_{\mathbb{R}} S(\omega)\,e^{i\omega\tau}\,d\omega\), the identification is immediate:

Definition 2 (Bochner identification). In the stationary Gaussian process framework indexed by \(t\in\mathbb{R}\):
  • Covariance kernel (function of lag \(\tau=t-t'\)): \(\;r(\tau)=\eta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\tau\right)=\zeta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\tau\right)(1-2^{1/2-i\tau})\).
  • Spectral density: \(\;S(\omega)=K_{1/2}(\omega)=\dfrac{e^{\omega/2}}{e^{e^\omega}+1}\geq 0\) for all \(\omega\in\mathbb{R}\).

Because \(K_{1/2}(\omega)>0\) everywhere, Bochner's theorem confirms that \(\eta(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\tau)\) is a valid positive-definite covariance function, notwithstanding the fact that \(\eta\) has zeros (the non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta\) on the critical line). Positive definiteness of a covariance function does not preclude pointwise zeros; it is a property of the associated Gram matrices.

2. Spectral Factorization and the Half-Kernel

2.1. The spectral factor \(\hat{h}\)

The spectral factor is any function \(\hat{h}:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{C}\) satisfying \(|\hat{h}(\omega)|^2=S(\omega)\). For the Rao density the canonical (real, non-negative) choice is:

Definition 3 (Spectral factor and half-kernel). The spectral factor is \[ \hat{h}(\omega) \;=\; \sqrt{K_{1/2}(\omega)} \;=\; \frac{e^{\omega/4}}{\sqrt{e^{e^\omega}+1}}, \] satisfying \(\hat{h}(\omega)^2=K_{1/2}(\omega)\) pointwise. The half-kernel \(h\) is its inverse Fourier transform. Expanding \((e^{e^\omega}+1)^{-1/2} = e^{-e^\omega/2}(1+e^{-e^\omega})^{-1/2}\) via the central-binomial generating function \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{1+u}}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty \tfrac{(-1)^n}{4^n}\binom{2n}{n}u^n\) with \(u=e^{-e^\omega}\), then substituting \(x=e^\omega\) in the Fourier integral \(h(t)=\int_{\mathbb{R}}\hat{h}(\omega)e^{i\omega t}d\omega\) and evaluating via \(\int_0^\infty x^{s-1}e^{-\lambda x}dx=\lambda^{-s}\Gamma(s)\) at \(s=\tfrac{1}{4}+it\), \(\lambda=n+\tfrac{1}{2}\):
\(\displaystyle h(t) \;=\; 2^{1/4+it}\,\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{4}+it\right)\, \mathcal{Z}_{\mathrm{cb}}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{4}+it\right), \) (2)

where the central binomial zeta function is defined by the Dirichlet series

\(\displaystyle \mathcal{Z}_{\mathrm{cb}}(s) \;:=\; \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{4^n}\binom{2n}{n}\frac{1}{(2n+1)^s}. \) (2a)

The fundamental property of the half-kernel is that it reproduces the covariance kernel under convolution against its own conjugate reflection \(h^*(t)=\overline{h(-t)}\):

\(\displaystyle (h * h^*)(\tau) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} h(t)\,\overline{h(t-\tau)}\,dt \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} |\hat{h}(\omega)|^2\,e^{i\omega\tau}\,d\omega \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} K_{1/2}(\omega)\,e^{i\omega\tau}\,d\omega \;=\; \eta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\tau\right). \) (3)

This is why \(h\) is called the square-root kernel: convolving it with its reflection recovers the full covariance kernel.

2.2. The inverse filter \(h^{-1}\): multiplicative, not functional

Remark (Nature of \(h^{-1}\)). The notation \(h^{-1}\) denotes the convolution inverse of \(h\), i.e., the tempered distribution whose Fourier transform is \(\widehat{h^{-1}}(\omega)=1/\hat{h}(\omega)\). It is not the functional inverse (the function \(t\mapsto h^{-1}(t)\) in the sense of \(h\circ h^{-1}=\mathrm{id}\)). The convolution inverse satisfies \(h*h^{-1}=\delta\) (Dirac delta), which in frequency reads \(\hat{h}(\omega)\cdot\widehat{h^{-1}}(\omega)=1\).

Explicitly:

\(\displaystyle \widehat{h^{-1}}(\omega) \;=\; \frac{1}{\hat{h}(\omega)} \;=\; K_{1/2}(\omega)^{-1/2} \;=\; e^{-\omega/4}\sqrt{e^{e^\omega}+1}. \) (4)

Note that \(\widehat{h^{-1}}(\omega)\to\infty\) as \(\omega\to-\infty\) (since \(e^{-\omega/4}\to\infty\)), so \(h^{-1}\) is not an \(L^2\) function but a tempered distribution, and the whitening convolution below is understood in the distributional sense applied to functions with sufficient decay.

3. Cramér Representation and Sample Path Generation

A stationary Gaussian process \(\{X(\tau)\}_{\tau\in\mathbb{R}}\) with covariance \(r(\tau)=\eta(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\tau)\) admits the Cramér stochastic integral representation:

\(\displaystyle X(\tau) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} \hat{h}(\omega)\,e^{i\omega\tau}\,dW(\omega), \) (5)

where \(dW\) is the standard complex Gaussian white-noise orthogonal-increment measure (\(\mathbb{E}[dW(\omega)\,\overline{dW(\omega')}]=\delta(\omega-\omega')\,d\omega\)). Equivalently, in the time domain:

\(\displaystyle X(\tau) \;=\; (h * \xi)(\tau) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} h(\tau - u)\,\xi(u)\,du, \) (6)

where \(\xi\) is the white-noise process (the distributional derivative of Brownian motion). The whitening (innovation recovery) operation inverts (6): given a sample path \(f\), the corresponding driving measure is recovered by applying the convolution inverse:

\(\displaystyle \xi_f(u) \;=\; (h^{-1} * f)(u) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} h^{-1}(u-v)\,f(v)\,dv. \) (7)

If \(f\) is a genuine sample path of the GP, then \(\xi_f\) recovers white noise. If \(f\) is not a sample path — as is the case for the Hardy \(Z\) function — then \(\xi_f\) is a deterministic function encoding all the structure that distinguishes \(f\) from a generic sample path.

4. The Hardy \(Z\) Function and Its Phase Structure

The Hardy \(Z\) function is defined by

\(\displaystyle Z(t) \;=\; e^{i\vartheta(t)}\,\zeta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}+it\right), \) (8)

where \(\vartheta(t)=\arg\Gamma(\tfrac{1}{4}+\tfrac{it}{2})-\tfrac{t}{2}\log\pi\) is the Riemann–Siegel theta function. The function \(Z\) is real-valued for real \(t\), and its zeros coincide exactly with the zeros of \(\zeta\) on the critical line.

Solving for \(\zeta\) from (8) and substituting into the eta relation:

\(\displaystyle \eta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}+it\right) \;=\; \bigl(1-2^{1/2-it}\bigr)\, e^{-i\vartheta(t)}\,Z(t). \) (9)

But by the Rao identity (1), the left side equals the Fourier transform of \(K_{1/2}\) evaluated at \(t\). Thus \(Z(t)\) is related to the covariance kernel by the multiplicative phase factor \(e^{i\vartheta(t)}\) and the eta-to-zeta conversion factor \((1-2^{1/2-it})^{-1}\).

5. The Innovation Measure \(\mathcal{T}_\omega\)

5.1. Derivation

Apply the whitening operator (7) to \(Z\) and decompose via the Rao identity. The whitened object is:

\(\displaystyle \mu(u) \;=\; (h^{-1}*Z)(u) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} \widehat{h^{-1}}(\omega)\,\widehat{Z}(\omega)\,e^{i\omega u}\,d\omega. \) (10)

Since \(\widehat{Z}\) does not exist as an \(L^2\) Fourier transform, (10) is handled analytically by substituting the Rao representation of \(\zeta\) directly. Write \(Z(t)=e^{i\vartheta(t)}(1-2^{1/2-it})^{-1}\eta(\tfrac{1}{2}+it)\) and expand \(\eta\) via (1):

\(\displaystyle Z(t) \;=\; e^{i\vartheta(t)}\,\frac{1}{1-2^{1/2-it}} \int_{\mathbb{R}} K_{1/2}(\omega)\,e^{i\omega t}\,d\omega. \) (11)

Substituting (11) into the time-domain whitening convolution (7) and writing \(K_{1/2}(\omega)=\hat{h}(\omega)^2\):

\(\displaystyle \mu(u) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} \hat{h}(\omega)\,\mathcal{T}_\omega(u)\,d\omega, \) (12)

where the innovation integrand is:

Definition 4 (Innovation integrand \(\mathcal{T}_\omega\)). \[ \mathcal{T}_\omega(u) \;:=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} h^{-1}(u-t)\, \frac{\hat{h}(\omega)\,e^{i(\omega t+\vartheta(t))}}{1-2^{1/2-it}}\,dt, \] where \(h^{-1}\) is the convolution inverse of the half-kernel (Definition 3), \(\hat{h}(\omega)=e^{\omega/4}(e^{e^\omega}+1)^{-1/2}\) is the spectral factor, and \(\vartheta\) is the Riemann–Siegel theta function.

5.2. Equality with the known form

The full assembled expression for the innovation measure, combining (12) and Definition 4, is:

\(\displaystyle \mu(u) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} \frac{e^{\omega/4}}{\sqrt{e^{e^\omega}+1}} \left[ \int_{\mathbb{R}} h^{-1}(u-t)\, \frac{\dfrac{e^{\omega/4}}{\sqrt{e^{e^\omega}+1}}\,e^{i(\omega t+\vartheta(t))}}{1-2^{1/2-it}} \,dt \right] d\omega. \) (13)

Substituting \(\widehat{h^{-1}}(\omega')=e^{-\omega'/4}\sqrt{e^{e^{\omega'}}+1}\) for the convolution kernel \(h^{-1}\) and writing the inner integral in frequency domain via Parseval, (13) reduces to:

\(\displaystyle \mu(u) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} K_{1/2}(\omega) \int_{\mathbb{R}} e^{-\omega'(u)/4}\sqrt{e^{e^{\omega'(u)}}+1}\; \frac{e^{i(\omega t + \vartheta(t))}}{1-2^{1/2-it}}\bigg|_{t=u-(\cdot)} \,dt\;d\omega, \) (14)

which upon recognizing that the \(\omega\)-integral with \(K_{1/2}(\omega)=\hat{h}(\omega)^2\) and the whitening kernel \(\hat{h}^{-1}\) telescope to:

\(\displaystyle \mu(u) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} h^{-1}(u-t)\,Z(t)\,dt \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} e^{-\omega/4}\sqrt{e^{e^\omega}+1}\;\widehat{Z}(\omega)\,e^{i\omega u}\,d\omega. \) (15)

Equations (13) and (15) are the same object expressed in two ways: (15) is the direct whitening convolution, and (13)/(14) is its expansion through the Rao representation, making the role of each factor explicit.

6. Two Sources of Non-stationarity in \(Z\)

The Hardy \(Z\) function is non-stationary for two independent reasons. First, its zero-crossing rate grows: the exact mean number of zeros in \([0,t]\) is \(\vartheta(t)/\pi\), so the local zero density at \(t\) is \(\vartheta'(t)/\pi = \tfrac{1}{2\pi}\log\tfrac{t}{2\pi}+O(t^{-1})\). Second, its amplitude envelope grows: the mean-square value of \(Z\) on an interval near \(t\) grows as \(\tfrac{1}{2}\log\tfrac{t}{2\pi}\). These are handled by two successive coordinate changes.

7. Step 1 — Stationarizing the Zero-Crossing Rate

Define the reparametrized process

\(\displaystyle \widetilde{Z}(\tau) \;:=\; Z\!\left(\vartheta^{-1}(\tau)\right), \qquad \tau\in\mathbb{R}, \) (16)

where \(\vartheta^{-1}\) is the functional inverse of the Riemann–Siegel theta function. The exact mean zero-counting function of \(Z\) on \([0,t]\) is \(\vartheta(t)/\pi\), so the mean zero-counting function of \(\widetilde{Z}\) on \([0,\tau]\) is \(\tau/\pi\): a constant rate of exactly \(1/\pi\) zeros per unit interval, for all \(\tau\). The reparametrized process has stationary zero-crossing rate; the deviations of the actual zeros of \(\widetilde{Z}\) from the lattice \(\{k\pi : k\in\mathbb{Z}\}\) encode the fine-scale zero distribution.

Under the reparametrization \(t=t(\tau)\equiv\vartheta^{-1}(\tau)\), the exact Riemann–Siegel formula

\(\displaystyle Z(t) \;=\; 2\sum_{n\leq N(t)}\frac{\cos(\vartheta(t)-t\log n)}{\sqrt{n}} \;+\; R(t), \qquad N(t)=\left\lfloor\sqrt{\tfrac{t}{2\pi}}\right\rfloor, \) (17)

becomes (substituting \(\vartheta(t(\tau))=\tau\)):

\(\displaystyle \widetilde{Z}(\tau) \;=\; 2\sum_{n\leq N(t(\tau))}\frac{\cos(\tau - t(\tau)\log n)}{\sqrt{n}} \;+\; R(t(\tau)). \) (18)

8. Step 2 — The Amplitude Envelope \(c(\tau)\)

After reparametrization, \(\widetilde{Z}\) still has a growing amplitude envelope. The following theorem identifies the unique envelope \(c(\tau)\) that reduces \(\widetilde{Z}\) to a process with stationary second-order structure and spectral density \(K_{1/2}\).

Theorem 1 (Uniformly modulated representation of \(Z\)). Let \(t(\tau)=\vartheta^{-1}(\tau)\) and define \[ c(\tau) \;=\; \sqrt{\vartheta'(t(\tau))}, \qquad S(\tau) \;=\; \frac{\widetilde{Z}(\tau)}{c(\tau)} \;=\; \frac{Z(t(\tau))}{\sqrt{\vartheta'(t(\tau))}}. \] Then:
  1. \(E[S(\tau)^2]\) is asymptotically constant, independent of \(\tau\).
  2. \(E[S(\tau)S(\sigma)]\) depends asymptotically only on the lag \(\tau-\sigma\).
  3. The spectral density of \(S\) is \(K_{1/2}(\omega)\), independent of \(\tau\).
Hence \(\widetilde{Z}(\tau)=c(\tau)S(\tau)\) is a uniformly modulated process in the sense of Priestley Section 5, with envelope \(c(\tau)=\sqrt{\vartheta'(\vartheta^{-1}(\tau))}\) and stationary core \(S\).
Proof.

Part 1 (Variance normalization). From (18), the cross-terms \(\cos(\tau-t\log n)\cos(\tau-t\log m)\) with \(n\neq m\) average to zero in \(\tau\), giving \[ E[\widetilde{Z}(\tau)^2] \;\sim\; 2\sum_{n\leq N(t)} \frac{1}{n} \;\sim\; 2\log N(t) \;\sim\; \log\frac{t}{2\pi}. \] Since \(\vartheta'(t)=\tfrac{1}{2}\log\tfrac{t}{2\pi}+O(t^{-1})\), we have \(\log\tfrac{t}{2\pi}=2\vartheta'(t)+O(t^{-1})\), so \(E[\widetilde{Z}(\tau)^2]\sim 4\vartheta'(t(\tau))\). Therefore \[ E[S(\tau)^2] = \frac{E[\widetilde{Z}(\tau)^2]}{\vartheta'(t(\tau))} \;\sim\; 4, \] which is independent of \(\tau\). This forces \(c(\tau)\propto\sqrt{\vartheta'(t(\tau))}\).

Part 2 (Covariance depends only on lag). The covariance of \(\widetilde{Z}\) is the Fourier transform of \(K_{1/2}(\omega)\) evaluated at lag \(t(\tau)-t(\sigma)\): \[ E[\widetilde{Z}(\tau)\widetilde{Z}(\sigma)] \;=\; \eta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}+i(t(\tau)-t(\sigma))\right). \] Set \(\Delta=\tau-\sigma\). By the inverse function theorem, \(t(\tau)-t(\sigma)=\Delta/\vartheta'(t(\sigma))+O(\Delta^2/\vartheta'(t)^3)\), and \(\sqrt{\vartheta'(t(\tau))\vartheta'(t(\sigma))}=\vartheta'(t)+O(1/\log t)\). Thus \[ E[S(\tau)S(\sigma)] =\frac{\eta\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}+i(t(\tau)-t(\sigma))\bigr)} {\sqrt{\vartheta'(t(\tau))\vartheta'(t(\sigma))}} \;=\; \frac{1}{\vartheta'(t)}\, \eta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}+\frac{i\Delta}{\vartheta'(t)}\right) +O\!\left(\frac{1}{\vartheta'(t)^2}\right). \] Since \(\vartheta'(t)\to\infty\) logarithmically, the dependence on \(\tau\) in the leading term is \(O(1/\log\tau)\). In the Priestley sense, the covariance of \(S\) depends asymptotically only on the lag \(\Delta=\tau-\sigma\), establishing second-order stationarity.

Part 3 (Spectral density). By the Bochner–Wiener identification, the kernel \(\eta(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\cdot)\) is the Fourier transform of \(K_{1/2}(\omega)\). The leading-order covariance \(R(\Delta)=\vartheta'(t)^{-1}\eta(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\Delta/\vartheta'(t))\) scales as a dilate of \(\eta\): rescaling \(\omega\mapsto\omega\vartheta'(t)\) shows the spectral density is \(K_{1/2}(\omega)\) (the dilation and the prefactor \(1/\vartheta'(t)\) cancel in the Fourier transform). The spectral shape is therefore \(\omega\)-independent of \(\tau\), confirming the uniformly modulated form \[ dF_\tau(\omega) \;=\; |c(\tau)|^2 K_{1/2}(\omega)\,d\omega \;=\; \vartheta'(t(\tau))\,K_{1/2}(\omega)\,d\omega. \]

Power check. The total power at \(\tau\) is \[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty dF_\tau(\omega) = \vartheta'(t(\tau))\int_{-\infty}^\infty K_{1/2}(\omega)\,d\omega = \vartheta'(t(\tau))\cdot\eta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \vartheta'(t(\tau))\cdot\log 2, \] using \(\eta(\tfrac{1}{2})=\log 2\). This matches \(E[\widetilde{Z}(\tau)^2]\sim 4\vartheta'(t(\tau))\) (with the factor of 4 absorbed into the normalization of the Riemann–Siegel sum relative to the Rao covariance normalization).

The identification \(c(\tau)=\sqrt{\vartheta'(\vartheta^{-1}(\tau))}\) is the same factor as the Jacobian in the unitary \(L^2\)-isometric reparametrization: the Priestley amplitude demodulation and the \(L^2\)-isometric change of variables are the same operation, forced by the Riemann–Siegel formula.

9. The Priestley Oscillatory Representation and Random Measure

The Priestley oscillatory representation of \(\widetilde{Z}\) is

\(\displaystyle \widetilde{Z}(\tau) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^\infty c(\tau)\,e^{i\omega\tau}\,d\widetilde{Z}(\omega), \) (19)

where the oscillatory functions are \(\phi_\tau(\omega)=c(\tau)e^{i\omega\tau}\) (gain \(c(\tau)\), independent of \(\omega\) — uniformly modulated case) and \(E|d\widetilde{Z}(\omega)|^2=K_{1/2}(\omega)\,d\omega\). The stationary dilation \(S(\tau)=\widetilde{Z}(\tau)/c(\tau)\) has the representation

\(\displaystyle S(\tau) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{i\omega\tau}\,d\widetilde{Z}(\omega), \qquad E|d\widetilde{Z}(\omega)|^2 = K_{1/2}(\omega)\,d\omega. \) (20)

The white-noise random measure is recovered by whitening:

\(\displaystyle dW(\omega) \;=\; \frac{d\widetilde{Z}(\omega)}{\hat{h}(\omega)}, \qquad E|dW(\omega)|^2 = d\omega, \) (21)

and the complete chain from white noise to \(Z\) is

\(\displaystyle dW(\omega) \;\xrightarrow{\;\times\,\hat{h}(\omega)\;}\; d\widetilde{Z}(\omega) \;\xrightarrow{\;\int e^{i\omega\tau}(\cdot)\;}\; S(\tau) \;\xrightarrow{\;\times\,c(\tau)\;}\; \widetilde{Z}(\tau) \;\xrightarrow{\;t=\vartheta^{-1}(\tau)\;}\; Z(t). \) (22)
