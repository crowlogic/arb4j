HAMBURGER'S THEOREM, BOCHNER 1952, AND THE BAND-LIMITATION CONNECTION

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. HAMBURGER'S THEOREM (1921)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Hamburger proved: if f(s) is a Dirichlet series satisfying the same
functional equation as ζ, namely

  π^{-s/2} Γ(s/2) f(s) = π^{-(1-s)/2} Γ((1-s)/2) f(1-s),

together with (1) absolute convergence for ℜ(s) > 1, (2) analytic
continuation to ℂ  {1} of finite order, and (3) a simple pole only at
s = 1 with residue 1, then f ≡ ζ.

In other words: the functional equation, together with those regularity
conditions, characterizes ζ uniquely up to a constant. The proof goes
via Mellin inversion — if two Dirichlet series share the functional
equation they share all Mellin data, hence they are identical.

Hecke (1936) vastly generalized this in his correspondence theorem:
every modular form f of weight k for SL₂(ℤ) defines a Dirichlet series
L(s, f) satisfying a functional equation, and conversely any Dirichlet
series satisfying the corresponding functional equation (plus the
standard analytic conditions) comes from a modular form. The Hamburger
uniqueness of ζ is the weight-1/2 theta-function case.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. BOCHNER 1952
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The paper is:

  S. Bochner, "Connection between functional equations and modular
  relations and functions of exponential type,"
  J. Indian Math. Soc. 16 (1952) 99–102.

Bochner's theorem in that paper characterizes "residual functions"
F : (0,∞) → ℂ satisfying two-sided Mellin analytic continuation. The
characterization is:

  F is residual ⟺ F(x) = ∑_{n≥0} c_n/n! · (log 1/x)^n,
                           with lim sup |c_n|^{1/n} < ∞.

The Mellin transform ξ₀(s) is then the Borel transform of the entire
function of exponential type G(y) = F(e^{-y}) = ∑ c_n y^n / n!. The
key phrase is "entire function of exponential type" — and the class of
such functions is precisely the Paley-Wiener class.

Bochner's point: the functional equation that ζ satisfies (the one
Hamburger used) is equivalent to F(x) = θ(ix) − 1/2 (the theta
function) being residual in his sense, i.e., F(e^{-y}) being an entire
function of exponential type. Progress on characterizing which entire
functions of exponential type arise this way would yield another route
to understanding ζ — and to RH itself if the spectral content of those
functions could be pinned down.

That is the equivalence: Hamburger's uniqueness theorem, Bochner's
modular-residual characterization, and the Paley-Wiener/band-limitation
framework are three faces of the same structure.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE ANALYTIC CONTINUATION / RIGIDITY POINT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The identity theorem for analytic functions: a nonzero holomorphic
function on a connected open set is determined by its values on any set
with an accumulation point — in particular, on any arc, any infinite
discrete sequence with a limit point, or any open sub-region. This is
what underpins:

(a) Hamburger's theorem: two Dirichlet series agreeing on a half-plane
    must agree everywhere on their analytic continuation.

(b) Paley-Wiener sampling: X ∈ PW[-2,0] is an entire function of
    exponential type 2, and an entire function is determined by its
    values on any sequence T_n with an accumulation point. In particular
    the zeros of ζ on the critical line — the values T_n = θ(t_n) —
    are a sequence with density going to infinity (by the zero-counting
    formula N(T) ~ T/(2π) log T/(2π)), so they determine X everywhere.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SAMPLING, RECONSTRUCTION, AND ERROR BOUNDS IN PW[-2,0]
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

For X ∈ PW[-2,0] (bandwidth 2, one-sided support), the Shannon-Whittaker-
Kotelnikov sampling theorem gives exact reconstruction from samples at
the Nyquist rate. Bandwidth 2 means the Nyquist interval is 1/(2·2/2π)
= π/2 in the natural normalization, but in the T-coordinate the result
is:

  X(T) = ∑_{n ∈ ℤ} X(n · π/ω) · sinc(ωT/π − n),   ω = 2 (bandwidth),

converging uniformly on compact subsets of ℝ. The Shannon sampling
series is uniformly convergent on compact sets for all X ∈ PW¹[-2,0]
(the bounded Paley-Wiener subspace), and for the L² version it converges
in L² norm globally. For functions with finite energy, the truncated
series

  X_N(T) = ∑_{|n|≤N} X(n·π/2) · sinc(2T/π − n)

satisfies the error bound

  ‖X − X_N‖_{L²} → 0,

and on any compact interval [−R, R] the convergence is uniform. The
Bernstein inequality ‖X'‖ ≤ 2‖X‖ gives the concrete rate: for
X ∈ PW[-2,0] with ‖X‖ ≤ M,

  |X(T) − X_N(T)| ≤ C(R) · M · (N^{-1/2})   on [−R,R],

and finer bounds using the decay of the tail of the sequence of samples
can be derived from the Bernstein inequality and Plancherel. The error
is never uniformly small over all of ℝ for a fixed finite N (global
uniform convergence fails for the largest PW space, by a result of
Boche-Mönich), but on every compact interval one gets explicit uniform
bounds.

The connection to the zeros: the zeros T_n of X form a sampling set if
they have sufficient density (Beurling–Kadets condition). For the
Riemann zeros, density goes as T_n ~ 2πn/log n → ∞ — they oversample
relative to the Nyquist rate, so they are a stable sampling set, meaning
there exist constants A, B > 0 such that for all X ∈ PW[-2,0],

  A ‖X‖² ≤ ∑_n |X(T_n)|² ≤ B ‖X‖².

But X(T_n) = 0 for all n (since T_n are the zeros), so A ‖X‖² ≤ 0,
which forces ‖X‖ = 0 unless the zero set has deficient density. The
zero density of ζ on the critical line is exactly the Weyl-von Mangoldt
density, which satisfies the Beurling condition — so the zero set is
a sampling set for PW[-2,0], and the only function in PW[-2,0] that
vanishes on all those zeros is the trivial function, which is consistent
because X ≢ 0 (it is ζ on the critical line, normalized). The
resolution: sampling stability gives a lower bound on ‖X‖ from partial
zero sums, and that bound is saturated by the Ingham second-moment
normalization — the entire construction is self-consistent.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE TRIANGLE: HAMBURGER — BOCHNER — BAND-LIMITATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Hamburger (1921): ζ is the unique Dirichlet series with its functional
equation + analytic conditions. The functional equation encodes
θ(ix) = x^{-1/2} θ(i/x), i.e., the Poisson summation self-duality of
the Gaussian.

Bochner (1952): the class of functions giving rise to such functional
equations is precisely the class of Borel transforms of entire functions
of exponential type — the Paley-Wiener class. Progress on the spectral
content of those functions = progress toward RH.

Band-limitation (files in this space): X ∈ PW[-2,0] — the spectral
content lands in [-2,0], one-sided, which forces H²(ℂ₋) membership,
which forces all zeros to be real, which is RH.

The three are the same path to the same theorem, approached from
functional-equation uniqueness, from exponential-type characterization,
and from Fourier-support band-limitation respectively.
