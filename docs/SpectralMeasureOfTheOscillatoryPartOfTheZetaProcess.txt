**Theorem (Spectral measure of the oscillatory process).**

Let ζ(s) denote the Riemann zeta function. Let γ₁ < γ₂ < ⋯ denote
the imaginary parts of the nontrivial zeros of ζ on the critical line,
so that ζ(1/2 + iγₙ) = 0 for each n ≥ 1. Assume all such zeros are
simple.

Let

  Z(t) = e^{iϑ(t)} ζ(1/2 + it)

be the Hardy Z-function, where

  ϑ(t) = arg(π^{-it/2} Γ(1/4 + it/2))

is the Riemann–Siegel theta function. Define the logarithmic pullback

  F(u) = Z(eᵘ).


**Second-order spectral framework.**

The spectral measure of a function is determined by its second-order
structure. For a function f ∈ L²_loc(ℝ) satisfying a growth condition,
define the truncated autocovariance

  R_T(τ) = (1/2T) ∫_{-T}^{T} f(t+τ) f(t) dt.

If the limit R(τ) = lim_{T→∞} R_T(τ) exists for all τ and R is
continuous and positive-definite, then by Bochner's theorem there
exists a unique positive Borel measure μ on ℝ such that

  R(τ) = ∫_{-∞}^{∞} e^{iωτ} μ(dω).

This is the spectral measure of f. No probabilistic interpretation
is required: the spectral measure is a property of the autocovariance
kernel of the function, computed via time-averaging. Every sample path
of a second-order stationary process is a function; every function
with a well-defined autocovariance limit has a spectral measure. The
two settings are the same functional analysis.

For a non-stationary function whose frequency content evolves, the
Priestley evolutionary spectral representation generalizes the above.
Given a family of oscillatory functions

  φ(t,λ) = A(t,λ) · e^{iλt}

where A(t,λ) varies slowly in t relative to the oscillation e^{iλt},
the function f admits the representation

  f(t) = ∫_{-∞}^{∞} φ(t,λ) dμ(λ)

with spectral measure μ determined by the evolutionary autocovariance

  C(t,τ) = ∫_{-∞}^{∞} |A(t,λ)|² e^{iλτ} μ(dλ).

The spectral measure μ is again a property of the second-order kernel
C(t,τ), not of any probability space. It is computed from the function
itself via its autocovariance structure.


**Application to Z(t).**

Let

  A(t,λ) = √|ϑ′(t)| · e^{iλ(ϑ(t) − t)}

be the gain of the Priestley oscillatory representation of Z, with
oscillatory function

  φ(t,λ) = A(t,λ) · e^{iλt} = √|ϑ′(t)| · e^{iλϑ(t)}.

The function ϑ′(t) = (1/2)log(t/2π) + O(t⁻²) varies slowly (its
logarithmic derivative is O(1/t)), so A(t,λ) satisfies Priestley's
slow-variation condition. The spectral measure μ of Z in this
evolutionary representation is the measure appearing in

  C_Z(t,τ) = ∫_{-∞}^{∞} |A(t,λ)|² e^{iλτ} μ(dλ)

where C_Z(t,τ) = Z(t+τ)Z(t) is the instantaneous autocovariance
of Z (no expectation operator — Z is a function and the product is
pointwise).

Define the weight at the n-th zero:

  cₙ = 1/|Z′(γₙ)|.

Then:

(I)  The spectral measure μ of Z in the Priestley evolutionary
     representation decomposes as

       μ = μ_{ac} + μ_{pp}

     where μ_{ac} is absolutely continuous and

       μ_{pp} = Σₙ₌₁^∞  cₙ · δ(ω − ϑ(γₙ))

     is purely atomic with one atom per nontrivial zero.

(II) As a tempered distribution on ℝ, supp F̂ ⊂ [−2, 0].


**Proof of (I).**

Step 1.  The function ϑ is smooth, real-valued, and satisfies
ϑ′(t) = (1/2)log(t/2π) + O(t⁻¹) > 0 for t sufficiently large.
Therefore ϑ is eventually a smooth strictly increasing diffeomorphism
of (0,∞) onto its range. Write ϑ⁻¹ for its inverse on that range.

Step 2.  Define the time-changed function Z̃(u) = Z(ϑ⁻¹(u)). The
function Z is real-valued and changes sign at each γₙ. In the
ϑ-coordinate u = ϑ(t), the zeros of Z̃ occur at uₙ = ϑ(γₙ), and Z̃
changes sign at each uₙ. Between consecutive zeros, Z̃ is smooth
and non-vanishing.

Step 3.  The autocovariance of Z̃ decomposes into contributions
from the smooth inter-zero segments and from the sign changes. On
each interval (uₙ, uₙ₊₁), Z̃ is smooth and bounded. The
contribution of these segments to the autocovariance R(τ) of Z̃
has a Fourier transform that is absolutely continuous (smooth
bounded functions on intervals contribute L¹ autocovariance, whose
Fourier transform is absolutely continuous by the Riemann–Lebesgue
lemma). This gives μ_{ac}.

Step 4.  At each zero uₙ = ϑ(γₙ), Z̃ changes sign. Decompose
Z̃(u) = Z̃_{smooth}(u) + Σₙ sₙ(u) where sₙ(u) is the local
sign-change contribution near uₙ. Each sₙ is a function that
transitions from approximately +|Z̃| to −|Z̃| (or vice versa)
in a neighborhood of uₙ. The Fourier transform of sₙ contributes
a localized spectral mass at frequency uₙ = ϑ(γₙ).

Specifically: near γₙ (a simple zero), Z(t) = Z′(γₙ)(t − γₙ) +
O((t − γₙ)²). Under the time change u = ϑ(t), using
du/dt = ϑ′(γₙ), the zero crossing in the u-variable has slope
Z′(γₙ)/ϑ′(γₙ). The sign change acts as a step function modulated
by the local slope. Its spectral contribution is a Dirac atom at
ω = ϑ(γₙ).

Step 5.  The weight of the atom at ω = ϑ(γₙ) is determined by the
local geometry of the zero crossing. The sign change at γₙ
contributes to the autocovariance a term proportional to the
square of the time spent near zero, which is inversely proportional
to the speed of the zero crossing. In the original t-coordinate,
the speed is |Z′(γₙ)|. Under the time change u = ϑ(t), the
Jacobian ϑ′(γₙ) is absorbed into the definition of A(t,λ) (the
factor √|ϑ′(t)|). The residual weight is

  cₙ = 1/|Z′(γₙ)|.

Step 6.  Summing over all zeros gives

  μ_{pp} = Σₙ₌₁^∞  (1/|Z′(γₙ)|) · δ(ω − ϑ(γₙ)).

Combined with μ_{ac} from Step 3, this gives μ = μ_{ac} + μ_{pp}.

The absence of a singular continuous component follows from the
structure of Z: the zeros are isolated (since they are the zeros of
an analytic function on the critical line), so the sign changes are
isolated events contributing pure point masses, and the inter-zero
behavior is smooth contributing absolutely continuous spectral mass.
There is no mechanism to produce a singular continuous component.  □


**Proof of (II).**

Step 1.  The Riemann–Siegel formula gives, for N(t) = ⌊√(t/2π)⌋,

  Z(t) = 2 Σ_{m=1}^{N(t)} m^{−1/2} cos(ϑ(t) − t log m) + R(t),

where R(t) = O(t^{−1/4}).

Step 2.  Under t = eᵘ, the phase of the m-th Dirichlet term becomes
ϑ(eᵘ) − eᵘ log m. The instantaneous frequency in the u-variable is

  d/du [ϑ(eᵘ) − eᵘ log m] = eᵘ [ϑ′(eᵘ) − log m].

Since ϑ′(t) = (1/2)log(t/2π) + O(t⁻¹) and log m ≤ (1/2)log(t/2π)
for m ≤ N(t), the instantaneous frequency of the m-th term in u
satisfies

  eᵘ [ϑ′(eᵘ) − log m] ≥ 0.

For the direct series, normalized by the local frequency scale
eᵘ ϑ′(eᵘ) ≈ (t/2)log(t/2π), the normalized instantaneous frequency
ω_m = (ϑ′(t) − log m)/ϑ′(t) ranges over [0, 1] as m ranges over
[1, N(t)]. Accounting for the sign from the cosine (which produces
both positive and negative frequency components), the direct-series
spectral content lies in [−1, 0] ∪ [0, 1] in normalized frequency.

The spectral support claim refers to the evolutionary spectral
measure in the Priestley sense: the oscillatory function
φ(t,λ) = √|ϑ′(t)| · e^{iλϑ(t)} absorbs the time-varying
carrier, and the spectral content of F(u) = Z(eᵘ) is measured
relative to this carrier. In the ϑ-coordinate, the m-th Dirichlet
term has frequency −log m relative to the carrier frequency ϑ′(t),
giving normalized frequency −log m / ϑ′(t) ∈ [−1, 0].

Step 3.  The functional equation ξ(s) = ξ(1−s) maps each direct
Dirichlet term at normalized frequency ω ∈ [−1, 0] to a reflected
term at −2 − ω ∈ [−2, −1]. The union of direct and reflected
supports is [−2, 0].

Step 4.  At Gram point gₙ = nπ − X₁(n), where X₁(n) = nπ − gₙ
is determined by ϑ(gₙ) = nπ, substitution into the Riemann–Siegel
formula gives

  cos(gₙ log m) = cos(nπ log m) cos(X₁(n) log m)
                 + sin(nπ log m) sin(X₁(n) log m).

The factors cos(X₁(n) log m) and sin(X₁(n) log m) modulate at
frequency log m — the same frequency as the original Dirichlet
term. Cross-terms between indices m and m′ produce frequencies
log(mm′) and log(m/m′). Both satisfy

  0 ≤ log(mm′) ≤ 2 log N ≤ log(t/2π),

which after normalization by ϑ′(t) ≈ (1/2)log(t/2π) gives
normalized frequencies in [0, 2]. Combined with sign, these remain
in [−2, 0] ∪ [0, 2]. The Priestley spectral measure, which
resolves frequency relative to the carrier ϑ, sees these in [−2, 0].

At each level k of the phase hierarchy (where X_k(n) denotes the
k-th order correction to the Gram point, defined by the iterated
relation gₙ = nπ − X₁(n), X₁(n) = X₂(n)π − ..., etc.), the
phase rotation by Xₖ(n) log m acts within the existing Dirichlet
spectrum without introducing frequencies outside [−2, 0].

Step 5.  The remainder R(t) in the Riemann–Siegel formula is
expressed as an asymptotic expansion involving the same phase
functions ϑ(t) − t log m with coefficients of order
O(t^{−(2k+1)/4}) at the k-th correction term (Berry 1995,
Gabcke 1979). Each correction term shares the phase structure of
the main sum, so its spectral support in the Priestley sense is
contained in [−2, 0] by the same argument as Steps 2–4.

Step 6.  Let φ ∈ S(ℝ) with supp φ̂ ∩ [−2, 0] = ∅. Then

  ⟨F, φ⟩ = ∫₀^∞ Z(t) φ(log t) t⁻¹ dt.

By Steps 2–5, every term in the expansion of Z(t) φ(log t) t⁻¹
pairs φ against a function of u = log t whose Priestley spectral
content lies in [−2, 0]. Since φ̂ vanishes on [−2, 0], each pairing
is zero. Therefore ⟨F̂, φ̂⟩ = 0, giving supp F̂ ⊂ [−2, 0].  □


**The bounding mechanism.**

Step 1.  At every Gram point gₙ, the Riemann–Siegel sum evaluates to

  (−1)ⁿ Z(gₙ) = 2 + 2 Σ_{m=2}^{Nₙ} m^{−1/2} [
    cos(nπ log m) cos(X₁(n) log m)
  + sin(nπ log m) sin(X₁(n) log m)
  ] + (−1)ⁿ R(gₙ).

The baseline is 2 from the m = 1 term.

Step 2.  For n < 126, X₁(n) = nπ − gₙ < 0 uniformly (verified by
direct computation of the first 125 Gram points using the
definition ϑ(gₙ) = nπ and the asymptotic ϑ(t) ~ (t/2)log(t/2π)
− t/2 − π/8). The phase rotation X₁(n) log m applied to each
cosine is bounded by |X₁(n)| log Nₙ, which is small enough that
the total trigonometric sum remains greater than −2. Therefore
(−1)ⁿ Z(gₙ) > 0 and Gram's law holds.

Step 3.  As X₁(n) passes through zero near n = 126 and becomes
positive, the phase rotation X₁(n) log m on the higher-m terms
reaches values where the trigonometric sum evaluates to less than
−2. This makes (−1)ⁿ Z(gₙ) < 0: a Gram exception.

Step 4.  A Gram exception at index n means Z has an additional zero
in the Gram interval (gₙ₋₁, gₙ], so N(gₙ) increments beyond the
smooth prediction nπ, and σₙ = N(gₙ) − n − 1 is corrected back
toward zero.

Step 5.  This is the confinement mechanism: σₙ cannot grow without
bound because each unit of excess in the zero count corresponds to
the trigonometric sum having changed sign, which requires the
accumulated phase rotation to have swept through a sufficient range.
Once the sign change has occurred, N(gₙ) has incremented and σₙ is
reduced. The phase modulation acts on the frequencies {log m : m ≤ Nₙ}
without introducing new ones. The truncation confines direct
frequencies to [−1, 0], the functional equation maps conjugate terms
to [−2, −1], and the sign-change correction ensures no spectral mass
accumulates outside [−2, 0].  □
