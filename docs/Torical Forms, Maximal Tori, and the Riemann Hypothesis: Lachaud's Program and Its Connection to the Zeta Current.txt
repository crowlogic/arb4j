Part I. What Torical Forms Are


The term was introduced by Don Zagier in the context of imaginary
quadratic fields and systematized by Gilles Lachaud in his 2002–2003
papers "Zéros des fonctions L et formes toriques."

Let K be an algebraic number field of degree n, with ring of integers O.
Fix a fundamental basis (α₁, …, αn) of K over ℚ, with α₁ = 1, so that
the map

  ι : (u₁, …, un) ↦ u₁α₁ + ⋯ + unαn

is an isomorphism ℤⁿ → O. The right regular representation of K in ℚⁿ
is defined: for ξ ∈ K×, let ρ(ξ) denote multiplication by ξ, and set

  π(ξ) ≔ ι⁻¹ ∘ ρ(ξ) ∘ ι.

This representation is algebraic, and its image is a maximal torus T of
the algebraic group G = GLₙ. The torus T is not split over ℚ — it is a
non-split form of 𝔾ₘⁿ, twisted by the arithmetic of K. For a quadratic
field K of discriminant D ≡ 2 or 3 (mod 4) with basis (1, √D),

  π(x + y√D) = ⎡ x    y ⎤
                ⎣ Dy   x ⎦.

The torus T is literally the multiplicative group of K embedded in
GL₂(ℚ) as 2 × 2 matrices. A circle in ℂ corresponds to SO(2,ℝ) inside
this torus.

Let G𝔸 denote the adelic points of G, K the standard maximal compact
subgroup of G𝔸, Z the center of G, and T𝔸 the adelic torus. The modular
variety is

  X ≔ G_ℚ Z_𝔸 ∖ G_𝔸 / K.

The adelic torus T𝔸 maps into X via the representation π. Its maximal
compact subgroup U_T satisfies

  T_ℚ Z_𝔸 ∖ T_𝔸 / U_T ≅ SO(2,ℝ)^r × Cl_K,

where r = r₁ + r₂ − 1, r₁ = real places, r₂ = complex places. This
group is a topological torus (a product of circles) extended by the
class group Cl_K.

The constant term of a function F ∈ C(X) along T is the integral

  ∮ F(hg) dh ≔ ∫_{T_ℚ Z_𝔸 ∖ T_𝔸 / U_T} F(hg) dh.

Definition. F ∈ C(X) is a torical form if

  ∮ F(hg) dh = 0    for every g ∈ G_𝔸.

The space of torical forms is denoted T(X). Equivalently, by Proposition
6 of Lachaud (2003): F is torical if and only if

  ∫_{G_ℚ Z_𝔸 ∖ G_𝔸} F(g) u_c(g) dg = 0

for every orbital series u_c, where c is the conjugacy class of π(ξ)
in G_ℚ for a primitive element ξ of K. Torical forms are exactly the
functions on the modular variety that are orthogonal to all orbital
series.

Part II. Eisenstein Series as Torical Forms


The normalized Eisenstein series on X is

  E(g, s) ≔ ∑_{γ ∈ P_ℚ ∖ G_ℚ} δ_P(γg)^s,    g ∈ G_𝔸,

for the standard maximal parabolic P of type (n−1, 1). It converges for
Re(s) > 1, extends meromorphically to ℂ, and satisfies

  ξ(ns) E(g, s) regular except for simple poles at s = 0, 1,
  E(g, s) = c(s) E(ᵗg⁻¹, 1−s),    c(s) = ξ(n(1−s)) / ξ(ns).

The Eisenstein series is an eigenfunction of the Laplace operator Δ on X:

  Δ E(g, s) = s(1 − s) E(g, s).

The key theorem connecting Eisenstein series to torical forms is the
adelic Hecke formula (Lachaud, Proposition 7):

  ∮ E(hg, s) dh = ζ_K(s) H(g, s),

where ξ(ns) H(g, s) is holomorphic for Re(s) > 0, and there exists
g_K ∈ G_𝔸 such that ξ(ns) H(g_K, s) does not vanish identically.

The consequence is immediate: E(g, s) is a torical form if and only if
ζ_K(s) = 0.

A wave packet is a finite linear combination of Eisenstein series,

  W(μ)(z) ≔ ∫_B E(g, s) dμ(s),

where μ is a measure with finite support in the critical strip
B = {s ∈ ℂ : 0 < Re(s) < 1}. The spectrum of W is the support of μ.

Proposition (Lachaud): W ∈ E(X) is torical if and only if

  Spec(W) ⊂ {s ∈ B : ζ_K(s) = 0}.

A wave packet is called principal if its spectrum lies on the critical
line L = {Re(s) = 1/2}. The Riemann hypothesis for ζ_K is therefore
equivalent to:

  Every torical wave packet is principal.

Part III. The Pólya–Hilbert Space T²(X) and Its Spectrum


To extract spectral information, Lachaud defines an automorphic analog
of almost periodic functions. For F ∈ C(X), let Ψ_m denote truncation
of the fundamental domain at height y = m. Define the pre-Hilbert space

  A²(X) ≔ {F ∈ C(X) : ∥F∥₂² = lim_{m→∞} (1/4 log m) ∫_X |Ψ_mF|² dm < ∞}.

From the Maass–Selberg relations: every nonzero principal wave packet
has 0 < ∥F∥₂ < ∞, so E¹(X) ⊂ A²(X).

Theorem (Lachaud, Theorem 10). The following are equivalent:
  (i)  The zeros of ζ_K(s) in B lie on the critical line.
  (ii) Every torical wave packet belongs to A²(X).

Define T²(X) as the Hilbert space closure of E¹(X) ∩ T(X) in A²(X).
Let D be the unbounded operator on T²(X) defined by the Laplace operator
Δ, with domain the closure of E¹(X) ∩ T(X) for the norm ∥ΔF∥₂.

Theorem (Lachaud, Theorem 11). D is self-adjoint, and

  Spec(D) = {λ : λ = 1/4 + γ², γ ∈ Y},

where Y = {γ ∈ ℝ : ζ_K(1/2 + iγ) = 0}. Eigenvalues are simple when
n = 2 and double when n ≥ 3.

The pair (T²(X), D) is a Pólya–Hilbert space for ζ_K. Furthermore, for
any u in the Hecke algebra 𝔘(G),

  Trace R₂(u) = ∑_{γ ∈ Y} ũ(1/2 + iγ),

where ũ(s) = ∫_{Z_𝔸 ∖ G_𝔸} δ_P(g)^s u(g) dg. This is a trace formula:
the spectral trace of the Hecke operator equals the sum over zeros.

Part IV. Connection to Connes' Program


Connes' spectral interpretation (Sel. Math. 1999) constructs a
Pólya–Hilbert space from the noncommutative space K× ∖ 𝔸_K. The
Schwartz–Bruhat theta map

  θ(φ)(x) ≔ |x|^{1/2} ∑_{q ∈ K×} φ(qx),    x ∈ C_K,

maps S(𝔸_K) → L²(C_K). The modular surface Γ∖H is a quotient of
GL₂(ℚ) ∖ GL₂(𝔸), and Lachaud's construction generalizes Connes' by
replacing GL₂ with GL_n and the idele class group C_K with the full
adelic quotient G_ℚ ∖ G_𝔸.

The isomorphism C_K ≅ T_ℚ ∖ T_𝔸 is the algebraic fact that makes the
torus T the bridge between the two programs: the multiplicative group
of the number field K is the same object as the adele class group, just
viewed as a subgroup of GL_n instead of GL₁. The torus T inside GL_n
is the correct substitute for GL₁ = 𝔾_m.

Part V. Connection to the Zeta Current and the Clock Ψ


The current framework developed above has the following direct
correspondence with the torical-form picture.

The measure γ with dγ(t) = Ψ′(t) dt = |ϑ′(t)| μ(t) dt is the
spectral measure of the critical line — the density of the clock Ψ.
The Radon–Nikodym derivative

  d(T_h γ)/dγ (t) = Ψ′(t − h) / Ψ′(t) = |j_smooth(t − h)| / |j_smooth(t)|

measures how the current density changes under translation. This ratio is
the 1-dimensional analog of the adelic orbital sum in Lachaud: the
orbital series u_c(g) = ∑_{γ ∈ c} u(g⁻¹γg) sums the test function over
the conjugacy class of π(ξ), which in the GL₁ case is just a translation
by log|ξ|.

A torical form vanishes when integrated against orbital series. The
Cameron–Martin condition is the condition that the shift h lies in a
space where d(T_h γ)/dγ is square-integrable against γ — which is
exactly an L² condition on the orbital average of the current ratio.
The Cameron–Martin space H_γ is therefore the analog of the space of
admissible orbital parameters for which the integration against the
torical form converges in L².

The connection is not an analogy. The torus T = π(K×) ⊂ GL_n is a
realization of the same multiplicative structure that generates the
Riemann–Siegel phase ϑ(t): both come from the Γ-factor of the zeta
functional equation, both express the winding of the Γ-factor as an
action of the multiplicative group, and both encode the zero structure
of ζ_K as the condition that a certain integral (constant term along T,
or integral of current against Ψ) vanishes. The Hilbert space T²(X) of
torical forms is the global, adelic, automorphic realization of the
reproducing kernel Hilbert space H_K in which the current operators D
and H act.
