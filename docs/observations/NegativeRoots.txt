  THE OTHER BRANCH: NEGATIVE ROOTS FROM PARTICLES TO COSMOLOGY
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


ABSTRACT
────────
When a fundamental equation admits two branches of a square root,
physics confronts a choice: discard one branch as unphysical, or
accept that it points to something real. This pattern has appeared
twice at turning points in theoretical physics. In 1928, Dirac's
relativistic electron equation forced the negative-energy branch
into the open, ultimately revealing antimatter. In the 1980s, the
Hartle–Hawking no-boundary wave function encountered an analogous
sign ambiguity in the gravitational path integral, where two
saddle points with opposite-sign actions compete for dominance.
The structural parallel illuminates how the same algebraic fork —
±√ — can generate new physics or signal the breakdown of a
framework, depending on whether a consistent interpretation of
the second branch can be found.

I.  THE SQUARE ROOT AND ITS TWO BRANCHES
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Every quadratic relation of the form 𝑥² = 𝑎 admits two solutions:
𝑥 = +√𝑎 and 𝑥 = −√𝑎. In pure mathematics this is unremarkable.
In physics, where 𝑥 represents a measurable quantity — energy,
amplitude, or the weight of a geometry in a sum over histories —
the second branch demands interpretation. It cannot simply be
erased without justification, because the algebra that produced
it is the same algebra that produced the branch you intend to
keep.

The history of physics contains repeated instances of this
dilemma. The choice made at each juncture — ignore the branch,
reinterpret it, or restrict the formalism to exclude it — has
determined whether new physics emerged or whether the framework
required revision.

II.  DIRAC'S EQUATION AND THE NEGATIVE-ENERGY SOLUTIONS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

In special relativity, the energy-momentum relation for a free
particle is

                  𝐸² = 𝑝²𝑐² + 𝑚²𝑐⁴

which, taken at face value, yields two branches:

                  𝐸 = ±√(𝑝²𝑐² + 𝑚²𝑐⁴)

The positive branch gives the familiar kinetic energy of a
massive particle. The negative branch has no obvious classical
meaning — a particle with negative energy that, under
perturbation, could radiate energy indefinitely by falling to
ever-lower states.

In 1928, Paul Dirac constructed a first-order relativistic wave
equation for the electron that was consistent with both quantum
mechanics and special relativity. The Dirac equation necessarily
contained both branches. The negative-energy solutions could not
be removed without destroying the mathematical consistency of the
theory — they were required for completeness of the solution
space.

Dirac first proposed the "Dirac sea" interpretation: the vacuum
is an infinite sea of filled negative-energy electron states, and
a missing electron in that sea — a "hole" — would behave as a
particle with positive charge. He initially speculated the hole
was the proton, which was wrong. By 1931 he committed to the
interpretation that the negative branch corresponds to a
genuinely new particle, the "anti-electron," with the same mass
as the electron but opposite charge. Carl Anderson found it
experimentally in 1932 in cosmic ray cloud chamber photographs
and named it the positron.

The lesson: the negative branch was not a defect. It was the
first signal of an entire mirror sector of matter — antimatter —
that the formalism had been encoding all along.

III.  THE NO-BOUNDARY PROPOSAL AND THE SIGN PROBLEM
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

In the Hartle–Hawking no-boundary proposal, the wave function of
the universe is written as a gravitational path integral over
compact four-geometries with a single boundary:

                  Ψ ∼ ∫𝒟𝑔 e^(−𝑆[𝑔]/ℏ)

in the Euclideanized description, or its Lorentzian analogue. The
gravitational action 𝑆[𝑔] becomes a multivalued analytic function
on the space of complex metrics. When the integral is evaluated
by saddle-point approximation, every classical solution comes
with two saddles whose actions differ by a sign — again, the two
branches of a square root, now arising from the determinant and
related quantities in the minisuperspace reduction.

Each classical geometry therefore gives a pair of semiclassical
contributions:

                  ∼ exp(+𝑆_cl/ℏ)    and    ∼ exp(−𝑆_cl/ℏ)

Halliwell and Hartle emphasized that because of this double-
valuedness, one must choose an integration contour in the
complexified metric space that suppresses one of the two saddles
— typically the one with Re(√𝑔) < 0 — in order to get a
sensible, normalizable wave function that predicts approximately
classical expanding universes rather than wildly unstable ones.

Feldbrugge, Lehners, and Turok sharpened this by performing the
Lorentzian path integral carefully. They showed that if the
"wrong-sign" saddle is allowed into the contour, fluctuations
blow up: the proposal becomes unstable. The sign problem is
precisely that the naive sum over regular metrics picks up
contributions with the opposite sign in the exponent, converting
suppression into exponential enhancement of inhomogeneous modes.

The current debate centers on whether a consistent treatment of
the sign — by contour choice, Robin boundary conditions, or some
deeper ultraviolet principle — can tame the second branch and
incorporate it into a well-defined quantum state of the universe,
or whether its presence indicates the original proposal cannot be
realized as a convergent path integral at all.

IV.  THE STRUCTURAL PARALLEL
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

In both cases — Dirac's equation and the no-boundary wave
function — the fundamental formalism naturally contains both
branches of a square root, or equivalently both signs in the
exponent of a semiclassical weight. The naive move is to discard
the problematic branch by fiat. In the Dirac case, refusing to do
so and instead reinterpreting the negative-energy branch gave
antimatter and a consistent relativistic quantum theory. The
algebraic structure forced acceptance of an entire mirror sector
of matter from one minus sign under a square root.

In the no-boundary case, the analogous question remains open.
Whether the second saddle is a signal of new physics — some
cosmological analogue of antimatter — or a signal that the
framework itself requires modification, is the content of the
ongoing dispute. The algebra presents the same fork. The
resolution is not yet known.
