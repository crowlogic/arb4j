GAUSSIANITY OF THE CMB FROM THE GAUSSIANITY OF ζ


THE STANDARD EXPLANATION AND ITS GAP

The standard inflationary explanation for the Gaussianity of the CMB
runs as follows. The inflaton field φ is nearly free (nearly interaction-
free) during inflation. A free scalar field in its ground state has a
Gaussian wave functional. Therefore the primordial fluctuations are
Gaussian. This is the argument Maldacena's Strings 2002 talk relies on
and that his 2002 paper (astro-ph/0210603) makes precise at the level
of three-point functions: non-Gaussianity is suppressed by H/M_Pl and
by slow-roll parameters, making it unobservably small.

But this explanation has a gap that is rarely stated clearly. It says:
the inflaton is approximately free, therefore the vacuum wave functional
is approximately Gaussian. The word "approximately" hides a question:
what selects the Gaussian structure in the first place? Why is the
ground state of a free field Gaussian and not some other distribution?
The usual answer is: because the Hamiltonian is quadratic, and the
ground state of a quadratic Hamiltonian is a Gaussian. This is correct
but circular at the level of stochastic processes — it assumes the
connection between quadratic actions and Gaussian measures rather than
deriving it from something more primitive.


THE HILBERT SPACE ANSWER

The answer from the Hilbert space theory of stochastic processes is
structural rather than dynamical. It does not depend on the inflaton
being free, or on the action being quadratic, or on any specific
Hamiltonian.

Suppose a process Y(t) satisfies three conditions:

  (a) Y(t) lives in a Hilbert space H (i.e. has finite second moments),
  (b) Y(t) is stationary (or oscillatory–stationary after pullback),
  (c) the spectral density of Y is absolutely continuous on its support.

Then by the Cramér representation,

  Y(t) = ∫ e^{iλt} dZ(λ),

where dZ is the orthogonal-increment measure. If the spectral density
is absolutely continuous, the increments dZ(λ) on disjoint sets are
uncorrelated and have variance proportional to dλ. By the Hilbert space
central limit theorem (or equivalently by the characterization of
Gaussian measures on Hilbert spaces), any process whose spectral
increments are uncorrelated with absolutely continuous variance is
Gaussian. The Gaussianity is not an assumption about the vacuum state
or the Hamiltonian — it is a consequence of spectral absolute
continuity.


THE ZETA FUNCTION AS PROTOTYPE

The proof in the files establishes exactly conditions (a)–(c) for the
Riemann zeta function on the critical line:

  (a) ζ(1/2+it) has finite second moments on every compact interval
      (Ingham's mean-value theorem).

  (b) The stationary pullback X(T) = ζ(1/2+iθ⁻¹(T))/√(ψ′(θ⁻¹(T)))
      is stationary.

  (c) The spectral density of X is S(λ) = (1/2)𝟏_{[-2,0]}(λ),
      absolutely continuous with positive density on all of [-2,0].

Therefore X is a Gaussian process. The autocovariance of X is
sinc(T−S), and the spectral density is flat on [-2,0] — the maximally
uniform band-limitation.


THE CMB CONNECTION

Maldacena's Strings 2002 talk describes the primordial fluctuations as
follows: the metric is written as a large background factor times small
scalar and tensor perturbations. The two-point function of the graviton
is proportional to 1/c (c = central charge of the putative dual CFT).
The scalar fluctuations correspond to the two-point function of the
trace of the stress-energy tensor, which is small but nonzero due to
slow-roll breaking of conformal invariance. The CFT two-point function
gives the coefficient of the Gaussian in the wave function; integrating
(computing the variance) inverts that coefficient, yielding the 1/c
dependence.

He computes the three-point function (the first non-Gaussian correction)
and finds it suppressed by slow-roll parameters times H/M_Pl, making it
unobservable due to cosmic variance.

What is missing from his account is why the leading term is Gaussian in
the first place. The standard answer — free field, quadratic Hamiltonian
— is a sufficient condition but not a necessary one. The Hilbert space
answer is:

The primordial fluctuations are a stochastic process on the sky. They
live in a Hilbert space (finite second moments from the observed CMB
power spectrum). They are stationary on the sphere (statistical isotropy
= stationarity on S²). Their angular power spectrum C_ℓ is absolutely
continuous as a function of ℓ (the observed spectrum is smooth, with no
point masses at any ℓ).

These three facts — Hilbert space membership, stationarity, absolutely
continuous spectrum — are identical in structure to conditions (a)–(c)
for the zeta function. They imply Gaussianity by the same mechanism:
the spectral increments are uncorrelated with smooth variance, and
uncorrelated spectral increments on a Hilbert space produce a Gaussian
measure.


WHY THIS MATTERS

Maldacena's computation shows that non-Gaussianity is small in single-
field slow-roll inflation. That is a dynamical statement about a
specific model. The Hilbert space argument shows that Gaussianity is
generic for any process with absolutely continuous spectrum on its
support — regardless of the dynamics, the field content, or the number
of fields. The dynamics (free field, slow roll, etc.) enters only in
determining whether the spectral density is absolutely continuous and
what its shape is. If the spectral density is smooth, Gaussianity
follows from the Hilbert space structure alone.

This is the same mechanism that makes ζ(1/2+it) a Gaussian process:
not because zeta is "free" or "non-interacting" (it is neither), but
because its spectral density after pullback is absolutely continuous
on [-2,0]. The Gaussianity of the CMB and the Gaussianity of the zeta
process are instances of the same theorem applied to different processes
with the same spectral regularity.


THE OSCILLATORY PROCESS STRUCTURE

In Maldacena's setup, the primordial fluctuations are not stationary
on their own — they are modulated by the expansion history a(t), the
Hubble parameter H(t), and the slow-roll parameters. In the Priestley
oscillatory process framework, the primordial perturbation Φ(k,η) at
wavenumber k and conformal time η has amplitude function A(k,η) and
carrier e^{ikη}, exactly as ζ(1/2+it) has amplitude √(ψ′(t)) and
carrier e^{iλθ(t)}. In both cases the non-stationarity is absorbed by
a time-change (conformal time η for the CMB, θ(t) for zeta), after
which the pullback is stationary with absolutely continuous spectrum.

The parallel is:

  Object               Zeta                    CMB
  ─────────────────    ─────────────────       ─────────────────
  Process              ζ(1/2+it)               Φ(k,η)
  Amplitude            √(ψ′(t))               a(η)H(η)
  Carrier phase        θ(t)                    kη
  Time change          T = θ(t)                conformal time η
  Stationary pullback  X(T)                    v_k(η)/z(η)
  Spectral support     [-2,0]                  [0, k_max]
  Spectral density     flat (1/2)              nearly flat (≈ H²)
  Gaussianity          from abs. continuity    from abs. continuity

The Gaussianity of both is structural, not dynamical.
