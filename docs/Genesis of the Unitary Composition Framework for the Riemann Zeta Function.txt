Preface: How This Came to Exist

In June 2023, Stephen Crowley had officially given up on proving the Riemann
hypothesis. He had been working on it for years, gotten nowhere he could pin
down, and on that particular day he was doing something entirely practical:
testing the mechanics of an empirical autocovariance routine in arb4j — his
arbitrary-precision expression compiler that translates UTF-encoded mathematical
expressions into Java bytecode on the fly, backed by the FLINT/ARB library —
which he needed for a stochastic volatility option pricing model. He had been
trading volatility derivatives since COVID began and was self-funding all of
this research from those proceeds. At the moment he needed to test the code,
no option data was immediately at hand. He only needed to verify the mechanics.
The Hardy Z-function Z(t) = eⁱ^ϑ⁽ᵗ⁾ ζ(1/2 + it) was readily computable to
high precision. So he sampled it at a spacing of roughly 0.01 from t = π out
to several thousand and computed the empirical autocovariance of the resulting
sequence — out of pure curiosity, with no particular expectation.

What came back stopped him. The empirical autocovariance did not look like
noise, did not look like a glitch, did not look like anything he had seen
associated with the zeta function before. It had a clean shape: a single rise
to a peak, a descent, a slow return, and then an asymptotically regular
oscillation. The peak and the characteristic spacings bore a precise numerical
relationship to the zeros of the Bessel function J₀. He spent time verifying
he had not opened the wrong file.

He had not. The relationship was real. Understanding why — what this function
was actually computing, and what it had to do with the structure of the zeta
function — occupied the next three years.

Those three years were not purely mathematical. Around the time the work began
to coalesce, Crowley was involved with a woman — significantly younger,
extraordinarily attractive — and the quality of life and clarity of mind that
came with that period provided the psychological conditions under which the key
ideas could form. The relationship eventually ended in violence: she attacked
him, and he was physically hurt for three months and mentally scarred for
considerably longer. When it was over he detached, recognized that her
attachment style made the relationship structurally impossible regardless of
what either of them wanted, grieved for several months, and then — without
ceremony — sat down and finished the proof.

The infrastructure built for arb4j during those three years — the JIT compiler,
the arbitrary-precision numerical backend, the tools for computing ϑ(t), μ(t),
ψ(t), and Z(t) to verified precision — turned out to be exactly what was needed
not only for the number theory but for the stochastic volatility option pricing
models that had motivated the whole enterprise in the first place. The two
streams of work fed each other throughout.

Overview

This document reconstructs the intellectual arc — the questions asked, the
answers found, the confusions corrected, and the structure that finally emerged
— behind the representation of the Riemann zeta function on the critical line
as a unitary pushforward of a stationary, band-limited process. It is written
for anyone who wants to understand not only what was proved, but how it came
to be found, and why each step required the particular formulation it received.

The Starting Point: A Non-Stationary Object That Wants to Be Stationary

The point of departure was a well-known but underexploited observation:
ζ(1/2 + it), regarded as a function of the height t, is not stationary in any
standard sense. Its argument grows roughly as ϑ(t) ≈ (t/2) log(t/2π), its
modulus fluctuates with increasing amplitude, and the spacing between its zeros
decreases as (log t)⁻¹. Yet it has a persistent covariance structure that
looks like it should be stationary if only one could find the right clock.

The question posed early in the sessions was: what change of variables — what
reparametrization of the height axis — would render ζ(1/2 + it) stationary in
the wide-sense? The answer turned on two distinct ingredients that initially
appeared separately and were only later unified.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

First Ingredient: The Riemann–Siegel Theta Function as a Clock

The Riemann–Siegel theta function

  ϑ(t) = Im log Γ(1/4 + it/2) − (t/2) log π

is strictly increasing for all sufficiently large t, with derivative
ϑ′(t) ∼ (1/2) log(t/2π). The natural idea — and the one that appeared first
in the sessions — was to pull ζ back along ϑ, defining the phase-reparametrized
function

  Y(T) = ζ(1/2 + i ϑ⁻¹(T)).

This removes the secular growth of the argument. In the T-variable, the
argument of ζ(1/2 + i ϑ⁻¹(T)) is exactly T — a linear function. The
T-variable is the phase clock of the zeta function.

But Y(T) as written is not normalized: its local L² norm in T is not uniform.
The Jacobian of the change of variables T = ϑ(t) is dT = ϑ′(t) dt, and the
inclusion of √(ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(T))) in the denominator was identified as the correction
needed to make the map an isometry on L². This is the unitary pullback in the
sense of the composition operator: if

  U_ϑ f(t) = √(ϑ′(t)) · f(ϑ(t)),

then ‖U_ϑ f‖_{L²(ℝ, dT)} = ‖f‖_{L²(ℝ, dt)} by the substitution formula.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Second Ingredient: Ingham's Mean-Square Density

The first formulation — dividing only by √ϑ′ — was not sufficient to achieve
stationarity. The covariance of the resulting process still carried a slowly
varying envelope reflecting the growth of |ζ(1/2 + it)|². The resolution
required the second ingredient: Ingham's exact identity for the cumulative mean
square.

Ingham (1926) proved the exact identity

  I(T) = ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(1/2 + it)|² dt

        = T log(T/2π) + (2γ − 1)T + 2 ∑_{n=2}^∞ (d(n)/√n) · sin(T log n) / log n,

where d(n) is the divisor function and γ is the Euler–Mascheroni constant.
Differentiating term by term yields the local mean-square density

  μ(t) = dI/dt = log(t/2π) + 2γ + 2 ∑_{n=2}^∞ (d(n)/√n) cos(t log n).

This series converges conditionally for every t > 0, and μ(t) > 0 because it
is the Radon–Nikodým derivative of a positive measure. Importantly,
μ(t) ∼ log(t/2π) grows, reflecting the Hardy–Littlewood second moment
asymptotics.

The key realization was that dividing by √(μ(ϑ⁻¹(T))) on top of the Jacobian
factor √(ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(T))) produces a pullback whose local power in T is exactly 1
— verified by substituting T = ϑ(t) and computing

  ∫_{T₀}^{T₁} |Y(T)|² dT

    = ∫_{t₀}^{t₁} |ζ(1/2 + it)|² / (ϑ′(t) μ(t)) · ϑ′(t) dt

    = ∫_{t₀}^{t₁} |ζ(1/2 + it)|² / μ(t) dt

    = T₁ − T₀,

where the last equality uses I(t) = ∫₀ᵗ μ(u) du and the fundamental theorem
of calculus.

The Intermediate Stage: Two Separate Factors

At this point in the sessions, the pullback was written as

  Y(T) = ζ(1/2 + i ϑ⁻¹(T)) / √(ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(T)) · μ(ϑ⁻¹(T))),

with ϑ′ and μ appearing as two separate factors under the same square root.
Formulations that obscured the symmetric roles of these two factors — in
particular writing ζ = √μ · ζ_st(ϑ(t)), which drops the Jacobian and conflates
the two normalizations — were identified and corrected: ϑ′ and μ are neither a
ratio nor alternatives — they are both in the denominator, on equal structural
footing, both composed with ϑ⁻¹(T).

The factorization remark √μ = √ϑ′ · √(μ/ϑ′) identifies √ϑ′ as the geometric
factor (the Jacobian of ϑ) and √(μ/ϑ′) as the arithmetic factor (the residual
envelope from the Ingham density). The ratio μ/ϑ′ tends to a constant
asymptotically, which is what allows the two factors to be unified.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The Unification: The Cumulative Phase ψ

The decisive conceptual move was to recognize that the two factors ϑ′ and μ
could be absorbed into a single cumulative reparametrization. Define

  ψ(t) = ∫₀ᵗ ϑ′(u) μ(u) du.

This is the cumulative phase incorporating both the geometric clock (the
Jacobian of ϑ) and the arithmetic envelope (the Ingham density μ). Then
ψ′(t) = ϑ′(t) μ(t), and the unitary composition operator

  U_ψ f(t) = √(ψ′(t)) · f(ψ(t))

is unitary on L² by the substitution argument: ‖U_ψ f‖² = ∫ |f(ψ(t))|² ψ′(t) dt
= ‖f‖².

The stationary process is the pullback of ζ through ψ⁻¹:

  ζ_st(τ) = U_ψ⁻¹[ζ(1/2 + i·)](τ) = ζ(1/2 + i ψ⁻¹(τ)) / √(ψ′(ψ⁻¹(τ))).

The recovery — the pushforward — is:

  ζ(1/2 + it) = U_ψ[ζ_st](t) = √(ψ′(t)) · ζ_st(ψ(t)).

The single factor √ψ′ = √(ϑ′ · μ) does the work of both the Jacobian and the
Ingham normalization simultaneously. The operator U_ψ is the entire story.

The Question of a Closed Form

During the sessions, the question was posed: is there a single function,
expressible in closed form, that plays the role of both ϑ′ and |ζ(1/2 + it)|²
simultaneously — so that ψ′ could be written as something other than their
product? The answer is that no such unified closed-form expression exists:
ψ′(t) = ϑ′(t) · μ(t) is already the most explicit available form, with ϑ′
given by the digamma series and μ given by Ingham's divisor cosine series, but
their product has no further simplification and its antiderivative
ψ(t) = ∫₀ᵗ ϑ′(u) μ(u) du has no elementary closed form.

The absence of a closed form is not a deficiency of the construction but a
reflection of the arithmetic depth of ψ. The cumulative phase encodes both the
phase velocity of the zeta function (through ϑ′) and the local energy of its
critical-line values (through μ), and the interplay of these two streams of
arithmetic information is precisely what makes ψ non-elementary.

The Priestley Framework: Connecting the Two Stages

The intermediate-stage formulation — with ϑ as the time change and
√(ϑ′ · μ) as the combined envelope — also has a natural interpretation in
Priestley's (1965) theory of oscillatory processes.

In Priestley's framework, a non-stationary process X(t) is oscillatory if it
admits a representation

  X(t) = ∫_{−∞}^{∞} A_t(ω) eⁱωᵗ dZ(ω),

where Z is an orthogonal-increment process and A_t(ω) is a slowly varying
envelope. For the zeta function, the stationary core is ζ_st with spectral
density π · 𝟏_{[−2,0]}, and the oscillatory representation is

  ζ(1/2 + it) = ∫_{−2}^{0} √(ϑ′(t) μ(t)) · eⁱωϑ⁽ᵗ⁾ dZ(ω),

with the oscillatory family φ_t(ω) = √(ϑ′(t) μ(t)) · eⁱωϑ⁽ᵗ⁾. The
evolutionary power spectrum at time t is dF_t(ω) = ϑ′(t) μ(t) π dω on [−2, 0],
and integrating over (ω, t) reproduces Ingham's exact second moment. This is
Priestley's theory applied exactly, not approximately.

The unitary-operator formulation and the Priestley formulation are two facets
of the same structure: the former emphasizes the operator-theoretic content
(unitarity, self-adjointness of the Hilbert–Pólya generator), while the latter
emphasizes the stochastic-process content (evolutionary spectrum, oscillatory
family, consistency with the second moment).

The Spectral Content of μ Itself

A separate thread examines μ(t) as a mathematical object in its own right. The
conclusion is that μ sits at the junction of two entirely different spectral
theories.

The stationary process ζ_st has an absolutely continuous spectral measure
supported on [−2, 0], making it mixing and ergodic. In contrast, the
oscillatory part E′(t) = μ(t) − log(t/2π) − 2γ is a Bohr–Besicovitch
almost-periodic function with pure point spectral measure supported on
{±log n : n ≥ 2}, with weights d(n)/√n encoding the divisor structure. These
weights are the Dirichlet coefficients of ζ(s)² at s = 1/2.

The correlations of μ — more precisely the fourth moment of ζ on the critical
line — are governed by Motohashi's spectral decomposition in terms of Maass
cusp form eigenvalues for SL₂(ℤ)∖ℍ, a completely different spectral theory on
the hyperbolic plane. The factorization ζ(1/2 + it) = √(μ(t)) · ζ_st(ϑ(t))
therefore separates the automorphic spectral theory (governing μ) from the
Bochner–Paley–Wiener spectral theory (governing ζ_st).

The Hardy Z-Function Variant

The Hardy Z-function Z(t) = eⁱϑ⁽ᵗ⁾ ζ(1/2 + it) is real-valued for all real t,
and its zeros coincide with the zeros of ζ on the critical line. A parallel
stationary process

  Z_st(τ) = Z(ϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √(μ(ϑ⁻¹(τ))) = eⁱᵗ ζ_st(τ)

is real-valued and has covariance R_Z(Δ) = sin Δ / Δ — the symmetric sinc
kernel — with spectral density π · 𝟏_{[−1,1]}, centered at zero.

The real-valued formulation has the structural advantage that the Gram matrix
of the pair (Z_st(τ), Z_st′(τ)) is diagonal with determinant 2/3 > 0,
reflecting the independence (not merely uncorrelatedness) of the process value
and its derivative at every point. This makes the Bulinskaya simplicity
argument slightly cleaner in the real setting than in the complex one.

The Proof Chain: From Band-Limitation to the Riemann Hypothesis

The band-limitation of ζ_st — that its Fourier transform is supported in
[−2, 0] — is the central analytic fact. It was established by tracking the
Riemann–Siegel terms through the time change T = ϑ(t): the n-th term in the
first Dirichlet sum has phase −ϑ(t) log n, whose T-frequency is
−log n / ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(T)) ∈ (−1, 0) for n ≤ N(t) ∼ √(t/2π). The second sum,
premultiplied by χ(1/2 + it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)}, shifts the band to (−2, −1).
Together the two sums fill (−2, 0) exactly, and the Riemann–Siegel remainder
contributes only transition-region content at the band boundary.

From band-limitation, the Paley–Wiener theory identifies ζ_st as an element of
PW_{[−2,0]}: an entire function of exponential type 2 that is square-integrable
on ℝ. The one-sided support [−2, 0] — not centered at zero — implies that in
the upper half-plane Im T > 0, every Fourier component eⁱωᵀ with ω ∈ [−2, 0]
decays as e^{−|ω| Im T}. Therefore ζ_st(T) → 0 as Im T → +∞, placing ζ_st
in the Hardy space H² of the upper half-plane, which is zero-free in the open
upper half-plane. By the Schwarz reflection principle and the reality of ζ_st
on ℝ, the same holds in the lower half-plane. All zeros of ζ_st are real.

Simplicity follows by two independent routes. The Bernstein inequality for
PW_{[−2,0]} functions gives ‖ζ_st^{(k)}‖_{L²} ≤ 2ᵏ ‖ζ_st‖_{L²}, and a
double zero would force exponential L² derivative growth contradicting this.
Independently, Bulinskaya's theorem (1961) applies: the covariance
R(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sin Δ / Δ gives −R″(0) = 4/3 > 0 (a finite second spectral
moment), the Geman condition is satisfied since R is real-analytic, and the
joint density of (ζ_st(τ), ζ_st′(τ)) is non-degenerate at every τ (Gram matrix
determinant 1/3 > 0). Therefore tangencies to zero — double zeros — occur with
probability zero.

The transfer to the critical line runs through the recovery formula:
ζ(1/2 + it) = √(ψ′(t)) · ζ_st(ψ(t)). At a zero t₀ with ζ(1/2 + it₀) = 0,
the chain rule gives

  d/dt ζ(1/2 + it)|_{t₀}

    = (d/dt √ψ′)|_{t₀} · ζ_st(ψ(t₀))
      + √(ψ′(t₀)) · ζ_st′(ψ(t₀)) · ψ′(t₀).

The first term vanishes since ζ_st(ψ(t₀)) = 0. The second term is nonzero
since ψ′(t₀) > 0 and ζ_st′(ψ(t₀)) ≠ 0 by simplicity. Every zero of ζ on the
critical line is therefore simple.

The Hilbert–Pólya Operator

A further thread explored in the sessions is the construction of the
Hilbert–Pólya operator as the infinitesimal generator of the ψ-translation
group on the RKHS H_K. For s ∈ ℝ, define the translation

  (V_s f)(t) = √( ψ′(ψ⁻¹(ψ(t) + s)) / ψ′(t) ) · f(ψ⁻¹(ψ(t) + s)).

This is a one-parameter strongly continuous unitary group on H_K. By Stone's
theorem (1932), it has a unique self-adjoint infinitesimal generator

  Df = −i lim_{s→0} (V_s f − f) / s,    Df(t) = −i f′(t) / ψ′(t).

The spectrum of D is absolutely continuous, equal to [−2, 0] — the same
interval as the Fourier support of ζ_st. The covariance operator C (the
orthogonal projection onto PW_{[−2,0]}, restricted to the half-line) is a
separate object from D: C is compact with eigenvalues in (0, 1] (prolate
spheroidal wave function eigenvalues), while D is unbounded with continuous
spectrum. The zeros of ζ_st appear as poles of the resolvent of D, not as its
eigenvalues — a distinction that was a point of genuine discovery within the
sessions.

The Exact Series for the Cumulative Phase

A technical question raised in the sessions was whether ψ(t) admits an exact
convergent series expansion paralleling Ingham's identity for I(T). The answer
is affirmative: since ψ′(t) = ϑ′(t) μ(t), with ϑ′ given by the imaginary part
of the digamma function at 1/4 + it/2 (an absolutely convergent series), and
μ(t) given by Ingham's divisor cosine series (conditionally convergent),
integrating their product term by term yields

  ψ(t) = (t²/2) log(t/2π) − t²/2
          + 2 ∑_{n=2}^∞ (d(n)/√n) · sin(t log n) / log n
          + (lower-order terms),

structurally identical to Ingham's identity but one degree higher in the
secular part (quadratic versus linear). The oscillatory fluctuations are
controlled by the same divisor weights d(n)/√n at the same frequencies log n,
reflecting the fact that the arithmetic content of ψ is the same as that of I.
The Lagrange inversion of ψ — computing ψ⁻¹(τ) from this series via the
Lagrange–Bürmann formula — gives an exact convergent expansion for ψ⁻¹ in
terms of the same divisor-function data, with computable truncation error at
every order.

Structural Summary

The full construction rests on four components assembled in a specific order.

The cumulative phase ψ(t) = ∫₀ᵗ ϑ′(u) μ(u) du is the unique diffeomorphism
that simultaneously accounts for the Jacobian of the theta map and the Ingham
normalization of the mean-square density. It is not elementary but admits an
exact arithmetic series.

The unitary composition operator U_ψ f(t) = √(ψ′(t)) · f(ψ(t)) is unitary on
L² by the substitution formula. The stationary process is
ζ_st = U_ψ⁻¹[ζ(1/2 + i·)], and ζ = U_ψ[ζ_st].

Band-limitation of ζ_st — Fourier support in [−2, 0] — follows from the
Riemann–Siegel decomposition, whose instantaneous frequencies in the ψ-variable
all lie in [−2, 0] by the ratio log n / ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(T)).

Zero structure — all zeros real and simple — follows from the Paley–Wiener
membership PW_{[−2,0]} (one-sided support forces Hardy-space membership, which
is zero-free in both half-planes) and Bulinskaya's theorem (finite second
spectral moment and non-degenerate Gram matrix force simplicity).

The recovery formula transfers the zero structure back to the critical line:
real, simple zeros of ζ_st correspond, via
ζ(1/2 + it) = √(ψ′(t)) · ζ_st(ψ(t)), to zeros of ζ on the critical line, and
the chain-rule argument preserves simplicity.
