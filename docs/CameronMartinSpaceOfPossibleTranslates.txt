Theorem (Radon–Nikodym derivative of γ under the shift operator).

Let γ be the Borel measure on (0,∞) with density dγ(t) ≔ ψ′(t) dt, where

    ψ′(t) ≔ θ′(t) ∣ζ(½+it)∣²,

and θ(t) = Im log Γ(¼ + ½it) − ½t log π is the Riemann–Siegel phase.
The function ψ is C¹ on (0,∞) and ψ′ is continuous. Let T_h be the shift
operator T_h(t) = t + h. Then the Radon–Nikodym derivative of T_h γ with
respect to γ is

    d(T_h γ) / dγ (t)  =  ψ′(t−h) / ψ′(t)  =  θ′(t−h)∣ζ(½+i(t−h))∣² / θ′(t)∣ζ(½+it)∣²

for all t ∈ (0,∞) with ψ′(t) ≠ 0.

Proof.

Both γ and T_h γ are absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue
measure λ, with continuous densities dγ/dλ = ψ′(t) and d(T_h γ)/dλ =
ψ′(t−h). The chain rule for Radon–Nikodym derivatives gives

    d(T_h γ) / dγ  =  [d(T_h γ) / dλ] / [dγ / dλ]  =  ψ′(t−h) / ψ′(t)

at every t where ψ′(t) ≠ 0. Since θ′(t) > 0 for t > 0, the zeros of ψ′
are exactly the zeros of ζ(½+it). The function ζ is holomorphic and not
identically zero, so its zeros are isolated. A set of isolated points in ℝ
is countable, hence λ-null, hence γ-null. The same holds for the zeros of
ψ′(t−h). Therefore γ and T_h γ are equivalent modulo null sets, and the
Radon–Nikodym derivative is the ratio stated above.  ∎

Remark. The appearance of ∣ζ∣² in both numerator and denominator is the
mechanism: the zeros of ψ′(t−h) and ψ′(t) are both isolated and countable,
so every null set of one measure is a null set of the other.

Theorem (Spectral structure of the ψ-time-changed process and the
Radon–Nikodym derivative as the Jacobian absorbed by the time change).

Let ψ : (0,∞) → ℝ be the C¹ function

    ψ(t) ≔ ∫₀ᵗ θ′(u) ∣ζ(½+iu)∣² du,

with θ(t) = Im log Γ(¼ + ½it) − ½t log π. Set T = ψ(t). Let γ be the
Borel measure on (0,∞) with density dγ(t) = ψ′(t) dt.

(A) Define H ≔ span{X_T : T ∈ ℝ} ⊂ L²(Ω,ℱ,ℙ), where X(T) is the
stationary process obtained from ζ after the time change ψ. The shift
operator

    U_τ : X_T ↦ X_{T+τ}

extends to a strongly continuous unitary group on H. By Stone's theorem,
there exists a projection-valued measure E(·) on (ℝ,ℬ) with

    U_τ = ∫_ℝ e^{iωτ} dE(ω),

supported on [−2,0]. The scalar spectral measure is

    μ(A) = ⟨X₀, E(A)X₀⟩_H,

the covariance is

    R(τ) = ⟨X₀, U_τ X₀⟩_H = ∫_ℝ e^{iωτ} dμ(ω),

and the orthogonal measure Z(A) = E(A)X₀ gives the spectral integral

    X_T = ∫_{[−2,0]} e^{iωT} dZ(ω).

(B) Define the warped shift in t-coordinates:

    V_τ : f(t) ↦ f(ψ⁻¹(ψ(t) + τ)).

The isometry W : L²(ℝ,dT) → L²((0,∞),dγ) given by (Wf)(t) = f(ψ(t))
intertwines U_τ and V_τ, i.e. W U_τ = V_τ W. Therefore V_τ is a strongly
continuous unitary group on L²((0,∞),dγ), and Stone's theorem applied to
V_τ gives the self-adjoint generator

    D = −i ψ′(t)⁻¹ · d/dt,

with spectrum [−2,0].

(C) The naive shift T_h : t ↦ t + h acts on L²(γ) with Radon–Nikodym
derivative

    d(T_h γ)/dγ (t) = ψ′(t−h)/ψ′(t) = θ′(t−h)∣ζ(½+i(t−h))∣² / θ′(t)∣ζ(½+it)∣².

This ratio is exactly the Jacobian that the time change ψ absorbs. In
t-coordinates the naive shift distorts γ by this factor; the construction
of ψ exists to convert that non-stationary shift into the unitary warped
shift V_τ in T-coordinates, where the Jacobian becomes identically 1.
The Radon–Nikodym derivative is not a residual obstruction — it is the
quantity that ψ was built to eliminate.

Proof.

Part (A). The process X(T) is stationary by construction: the time change
ψ absorbs all non-stationarity. Stationarity means R(T₁,T₂) = R(T₁−T₂),
so U_τ is an isometry. It is surjective on H because ψ is a bijection
(0,∞) → (0,∞). Strong continuity follows from the continuity of
T ↦ X_T in L². Stone's theorem produces the PVM E(·). The support of E
is [−2,0] because X(T) ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}: the Fourier transform of every
sample path vanishes outside [−2,0], so E(A) = 0 for A ∩ [−2,0] = ∅. The
orthogonal measure Z(A) = E(A)X₀ and the spectral integral
X_T = U_T X₀ = ∫ e^{iωT} dE(ω) X₀ = ∫ e^{iωT} dZ(ω) follow from the
functional calculus applied to the cyclic vector X₀.

Part (B). The map W is an isometry:

    ⟨Wf, Wg⟩_{L²(γ)} = ∫₀^∞ f(ψ(t)) g(ψ(t)) ψ′(t) dt
                       = ∫_ℝ f(T) g(T) dT = ⟨f,g⟩_{L²(dT)},

by the substitution T = ψ(t). The intertwining holds:

    (V_τ Wf)(t) = (Wf)(ψ⁻¹(ψ(t)+τ)) = f(ψ(t)+τ) = (U_τ f)(ψ(t)) = (W U_τ f)(t).

Since V_τ is unitarily equivalent to U_τ, it is a strongly continuous
unitary group on L²(γ). Its generator:

    lim_{τ→0} (V_τ f − f)/τ (t) = f′(t)/ψ′(t),

so D = −i ψ′(t)⁻¹ d/dt. The spectrum of D equals the spectrum of the
generator of U_τ, which is [−2,0].

Part (C). For the naive shift T_h on L²(γ):

    ∫ ∣f(t+h)∣² ψ′(t) dt = ∫ ∣f(s)∣² ψ′(s−h) ds,

so the density of T_h γ with respect to γ is ψ′(t−h)/ψ′(t). In
T-coordinates, set T = ψ(t), T′ = ψ(t+h). The warped shift V_τ
corresponds to T ↦ T+τ, which has Jacobian 1 with respect to dT. The
naive shift t ↦ t+h corresponds to T ↦ ψ(ψ⁻¹(T)+h), which has Jacobian
ψ′(ψ⁻¹(T)+h)/ψ′(ψ⁻¹(T)) with respect to dT — that is, the same
Radon–Nikodym ratio in T-coordinates. The time change ψ replaces this
non-unit Jacobian with the unit Jacobian of pure shift in T, which is
the content of Part (B): V_τ is unitary on L²(γ).  ∎

Remark. The PVM E(·) and the cyclic vector X₀ together encode both the
covariance structure (via ⟨X₀, E(·)X₀⟩) and the sample paths (via the
H-valued spectral integral). The Radon–Nikodym derivative identifies
what the time change does at each point: it converts the ratio
ψ′(t−h)/ψ′(t) into the constant 1 by passing to T-coordinates. The
band-limitation X(T) ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} then forces the spectrum of D into
[−2,0], completing the spectral characterization.
