Exact Bessel-Function Series for E(T)
Stephen Crowley
March 31, 2026

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Abstract

Define E(T) by

  ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt = T log T − (1 + log 2π − 2γ)T + E(T).          (*)

We establish that E(T) admits an exact representation as a convergent
series of Bessel functions K₀, Y₀, K₁, Y₁ with no error term of any
kind. Three exact identities, all predating any saddle-point step, are
the ingredients: (i) Titchmarsh's 1938 exact formula for ζ²(s) in
terms of ∫ [K₁(v) + (π/2)Y₁(v)] v⁻²ˢ dv; (ii) the Voronoï–Jutila
exact summation formula for Σd(n)f(n) involving ∫ f(u)[4K₀(4π√(nu))
− 2πY₀(4π√(nu))] du; and (iii) Atkinson's exact pre-saddle-point
decomposition E(T) = E₁ − E₂ + E₃ − E₄ + R₀(T) in which each Eⱼ is
an explicit oscillatory integral and |R₀(T)| = O(T⁻¹). The familiar
O(log²T) in the standard Atkinson formula enters only when a
stationary-phase lemma is applied to evaluate those integrals via
elementary trigonometric functions; without that step the formula is
exact.

1. Notation and conventions

  ζ(s)       — Riemann zeta-function, s ∈ ℂ∖{1}.
  d(n)       — number of positive divisors of n ∈ ℕ.
  γ          — Euler–Mascheroni constant, γ = 0.5772156649…
  K₀, K₁    — modified Bessel functions of the second kind, orders 0,1.
               Real, positive, exponentially decaying:
               Kᵥ(z) ∼ √(π/(2z)) e⁻ᶻ as z → +∞.
  Y₀, Y₁    — Bessel functions of the second kind (Neumann functions),
               orders 0,1. Oscillatory:
               Yᵥ(z) ∼ √(2/(πz)) sin(z − vπ/2 − π/4) as z → +∞.
  J₀, J₁    — Bessel functions of the first kind, orders 0,1.
  H₀⁽¹⁾     — Hankel function of the first kind:
               H₀⁽¹⁾(z) = J₀(z) + iY₀(z).
  Δ(x)      — Dirichlet divisor error:
               Δ(x) := Σ'_{n≤x} d(n) − x(log x + 2γ − 1) − ¼.
  Δ*(x)     := −Δ(x) + 2Δ(2x) − ½Δ(4x)   (Jutila's variant).
  N          — a positive integer parameter satisfying
               T/(4π) ≤ N ≤ T/π, chosen freely; the formula is
               independent of N within this range.
  Σ'         — sum in which the last term is halved if x ∈ ℤ.
  f ≍ g      — C₁g ≤ f ≤ C₂g for absolute constants C₁, C₂ > 0.
  e(x)       := e²πⁱˣ.

Definition 1 (Mean-square error term).
The function E(T) is defined for T ≥ 2 by (*) above. Known bounds:
E(T) = O(T^{1/3+ε}), E(T) = Ω(T^{1/4}). The exact representation
below is independent of these bounds.

2. Bessel function prerequisites

Lemma 2.1 (Integral representation of K₀ [5], §10.32).
For x > 0:

  K₀(2πx) = ∫₀^∞ cos(2πxt)/√(t²+1) dt.                              (2.1)

More generally, for Re(ν) > −½ and x > 0:

  Kᵥ(2πx) = √π (2πx)ᵛ / (2ᵛ Γ(ν+½))
             · ∫₀^∞ cos(2πxt) / (t²+1)^{ν+½} dt.                    (2.2)

Proof. Entry 10.32.9 of [5]; substitute t = sinh u and use the
standard integral formula for Kᵥ. □

Lemma 2.2 (Integral representation of Y₀ [5], §10.9).
For z > 0:

  Y₀(z) = −(2/π) ∫₁^∞ cos(zt)/√(t²−1) dt.

The combination arising in Voronoï's formula is, for n ≥ 1, x > 0:

  4K₀(4π√(nx)) − 2πY₀(4π√(nx))

which arises from the Mellin–Barnes integral (3.3) below.

Proof. Standard Basset–Sommerfeld integrals; see [4] §3.9. □

Lemma 2.3 (Kernel H(u,n)).
For n ≥ 1 and Re(u) < 2/3, define

  H(u, n) := 2 ∫₀^∞ y⁻ᵘ(1+y)^{u−1} cos(2πny) dy.                  (2.4)

The integral converges absolutely for Re(u) < ½ and is analytically
continued to Re(u) < 2/3 by two integrations by parts. For u = ½+it:

  H(½+it, n) = 2 ∫₀^∞ y^{−½−it} (1+y)^{−½+it} cos(2πny) dy,       (2.5)

an absolutely convergent integral for each n ≥ 1.

Proof. The analytic continuation is [2] Lemma 3.1. The expression
(2.5) is the definition (2.4) specialised to u = ½+it, since
u−1 = −½+it. □

Lemma 2.4 (Asymptotic behaviour of Kᵥ and Yᵥ, [5] §10.17).
For z → +∞ and fixed ν ∈ ℝ:

  Kᵥ(z) ∼ √(π/(2z)) · e⁻ᶻ · Σₖ₌₀^∞ aₖ(ν) z⁻ᵏ,   aₖ(ν) > 0,

  Yᵥ(z) ∼ √(2/(πz)) · [sin(z − νπ/2 − π/4) + O(z⁻¹)].

In particular Kᵥ(4π√(nx)) decays like e^{−4π√(nx)} as n → ∞ for
fixed x > 0, and |Yᵥ(4π√(nx))| ≤ C(nx)⁻¹ᐟ⁴ for a constant C. □

3. Voronoï–Jutila exact summation formula

Theorem 3.1 (Voronoï 1904 [6]; Jutila 1984 [7] — exact identity).
Let a < b be positive reals and let f : [a,b] → ℂ be smooth. Then

  Σ'_{a≤n≤b} d(n) f(n)

    = ∫ₐᵇ f(u)(log u + 2γ) du

      + Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n) ∫ₐᵇ f(u)[4K₀(4π√(nu)) − 2πY₀(4π√(nu))] du. (3.1)

The series converges absolutely in the K₀ terms and conditionally in
the Y₀ terms, uniformly in a, b over compact subsets of (0,∞).
Equation (3.1) is an exact identity, not an asymptotic expansion.

Proof.
Step 1 (Perron integral). By Perron's formula,

  Σ'_{n≤x} d(n) = (1/(2πi)) ∫_{c−i∞}^{c+i∞} ζ²(s) xˢ/s ds,  c > 1.

Step 2 (Contour shift). Shift the contour to Re(s) = ½, picking up
the double pole at s = 1, which contributes x log x + (2γ−1)x, and
the simple pole at s = 0, which contributes −¼. The remainder on
Re(s) = ½ is expressed via the functional equation

  ζ(s) = χ(s) ζ(1−s),   χ(s) = (2π)ˢ / (2Γ(s) cos(πs/2)).

Step 3 (Bessel kernel). On Re(s) = ½, write s = ½+it. The key
Mellin–Barnes identity is

  (1/(2πi)) ∫_{½−i∞}^{½+i∞} (πn/u)⁻ˢ Γ(s)² cos(πs) ds
    = 4K₀(4π√(nu)) − 2πY₀(4π√(nu)),                                  (3.3)

which follows from the Mellin–Barnes integral for K₀ and the identity
relating K₀ to the Hankel functions; see [6] pp. 215–220 and [7]
Theorem 1.

Step 4 (Convergence). The series over n converges absolutely for K₀
(exponential decay, Lemma 2.4) and conditionally for Y₀ after applying
Abel summation using Δ(X) = O(X^{1/3+ε}) and partial summation. □

Remark 3.2.
The combination 4K₀(z) − 2πY₀(z) equals 4K₀(z) + 2πi H₀⁽¹⁾(z)
− 2πi J₀(z), but the form 4K₀ − 2πY₀ is standard in this context.

Corollary 3.3 (Voronoï summation for smooth weights).
For any smooth f and any N ≍ T, applying (3.1) with a = 1, b = N+½:

  Σ_{n=1}^{N} d(n) f(n)
    = ∫₁^{N+½} f(u)(log u + 2γ) du
      + Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n) ∫₁^{N+½} f(u)[4K₀(4π√(nu)) − 2πY₀(4π√(nu))] du.
                                                                       (3.4)

This form is used in Step 5 of the proof of Theorem 5.1 below. □

4. Titchmarsh's exact identity for ζ²(s)

Theorem 4.1 (Titchmarsh 1938 [8]; see [3] eq. (1.16) — exact identity).
For s ∈ ℂ with Re(s) > 0, s ≠ 1, and any x > 0:

  ζ²(s) = Σ_{n≤x} d(n)/nˢ

           − x⁻ˢ Σ_{n≤x} d(n)

           + (2s−s²)/(s−1)² · x^{1−s}

           + s/(s−1) · x^{1−s}(2γ + log x)

           + ¼ x⁻ˢ

           − (4π²)ˢ s Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/n^{1−s}
             · ∫_{4π√(nx)}^∞ [K₁(v) + (π/2)Y₁(v)] v⁻²ˢ dv.         (4.1)

This is an exact identity for every x > 0 and every s with Re(s) > 0,
s ≠ 1. No approximation is involved.

Proof.
Step 1 (Truncated Perron). Write ζ²(s) = Σ_{n≥1} d(n)n⁻ˢ. Apply the
truncated Perron formula to extract the partial sum Σ_{n≤x}, obtaining
an exact contour integral for the tail Σ_{n>x}.

Step 2 (Contour shift). Shift the tail integral to Re(w) = 1−Re(s),
picking up the double pole of ζ²(w)/(w−s) at w = 1 (giving the
(2γ + log x) and x^{1−s} terms) and the simple pole at w = s
(cancelling Σ_{n≤x} d(n)n⁻ˢ). Apply the functional equation
ζ²(w) = χ²(w) ζ²(1−w) on this line.

Step 3 (Bessel identity). On Re(w) = 1−Re(s), the factor χ²(w) is
evaluated via the integral representation of χ, and the product
χ²(w) n^{w−1} is converted, via the identity

  (1/(2πi)) ∫_{c−i∞}^{c+i∞} χ²(w)(nx)^{w−s} dw
    = (4π²)ˢ s · n^{s−1}
      · ∫_{4π√(nx)}^∞ [K₁(v) + (π/2)Y₁(v)] v⁻²ˢ dv,

into the Bessel integral in (4.1). This uses the Sommerfeld contour
integral for H₁⁽¹⁾ and integration by parts once in w; see [8] §2.

Step 4 (Convergence). The K₁ term decays exponentially in n (since
K₁(4π√(nx)) ≤ C e^{−4π√(nx)} n⁻¹ᐟ⁴), so the n-series converges
absolutely. The Y₁ term converges conditionally after partial
summation on d(n). □

Corollary 4.2.
Set s = ½+it in (4.1). Integrating both sides via
∫₀ᵀ ζ²(½+it) dt — which produces E(T) from ζ²(s) — converts (4.1)
into the four-term exact formula of Theorem 5.1. No saddle-point
approximation is applied at this stage.

5. Atkinson's exact pre-saddle-point representation

Setup. Recall H(u,n) from Lemma 2.3. For y > 0 and T ≥ 2 define

  Φ(T, y) := sin(T log((1+y)/y)) / log((1+y)/y),                     (5.1)

  ψ(y) := 1/√(y(1+y)),                                                (5.2)

and for x > 0:

  Ψ(T, x) := ∫₀^∞ Φ(T,y) ψ(y) cos(2πxy) dy.                        (5.3)

The integral (5.3) converges for all T ≥ 2 and x > 0 by the
Riemann–Lebesgue lemma (ψ ∈ L¹ ∩ L²) and the oscillation of
cos(2πxy).

Define the exact Stirling remainder

  R₀(T) := −(T/2) log(1 + 1/(4T²)) − 1/(12T) − 1/(90T³),           (5.4)

which satisfies |R₀(T)| ≤ 1/(8T) + 1/(12T) + 1/(90T³) = O(T⁻¹).

For any integer N with T/(4π) ≤ N ≤ T/π, define:

  E₁(T) := 4 Σ_{n=1}^{N} d(n) Ψ(T, n),                              (5.5)

  E₂(T) := 4 Δ*(N+½) Ψ(T, N+½),                                     (5.6)

  E₃(T) := −(2/π)(log(N+½) + 2γ)
            · ∫₀^∞ Φ(T,y) sin(2π(N+½)y)/(y√(y(1+y))) dy
            + (1/(πi))
              · ∫₀^∞ sin(2π(N+½)y)/y
                · ∫_{½−iT}^{½+iT} (1/u)((1+y)/y)ᵘ du dy,            (5.7)

  E₄(T) := 4 ∫_{N+½}^∞ (Δ*(x)/x)
              · ∫₀^∞ cos(2πxy)
                / ((1+y)√(y(1+y)) log((1+y)/y))
                · [T cos(T log((1+y)/y))
                   − (½ + 1/log((1+y)/y))
                     · sin(T log((1+y)/y))]
              dy dx.                                                   (5.8)

Theorem 5.1 (Atkinson's exact formula [1]; see also [2] eq. (20)).
For any T ≥ 2 and any integer N with T/(4π) ≤ N ≤ T/π,

  E(T) = E₁(T) − E₂(T) + E₃(T) − E₄(T) + R₀(T).                    (5.9)

This is an exact identity for every T ≥ 2. The right-hand side equals
E(T) with no approximation.

Proof.
Step 1 (Contour integral for E(T)). From definition (*),

  E(T) = ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt − T log T + (1 + log 2π − 2γ)T.

Represent |ζ(½+it)|² by the dissection

  |ζ(½+it)|² = ζ(½+it) ζ(½−it)
             = Σ_{m,n≥1} (n/m)^{it} (mn)⁻¹ᐟ²

and integrate term-by-term over t ∈ [0,T]:

  ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt = Σ_{m,n} (mn)⁻¹ᐟ² ∫₀ᵀ (n/m)^{it} dt.

Step 2 (Diagonal term). The diagonal m = n gives Σ_{n≥1} n⁻¹ T,
which diverges. Regularise by subtracting the asymptotic
T log T + (1 + log 2π − 2γ)T via the Laurent expansion
ζ(s) = 1/(s−1) + γ + γ₁(s−1) + … and the Stirling series for
log Γ(¼+it/2). This produces R₀(T) exactly and no other error.

Step 3 (Off-diagonal: kernel substitution). The off-diagonal sum
(m ≠ n) is rearranged by setting n/m = (1+y)/y, i.e. y = m/(n−m).
The sum over m becomes a Dirichlet series in m weighted by d(m)/m.
Via Theorem 3.1 (Voronoï–Jutila), this is converted exactly into

  ∫₀ᵀ Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n) H(½+it, n) dt,                                 (5.10)

where H is from (2.4). This step is exact.

Step 4 (Exchange ∫ and Σ, and insertion of Ψ). By Fubini (justified
since |y⁻¹ᐟ²⁻ⁱᵗ(1+y)⁻¹ᐟ²⁺ⁱᵗ| = y⁻¹ᐟ²(1+y)⁻¹ᐟ² ∈ L¹ and
summable over n by Lemma 2.4), exchange ∫₀ᵀ dt and ∫₀^∞ dy in (5.10).
The inner t-integral evaluates to 2Φ(T,y):

  Re ∫₀ᵀ e^{it log((1+y)/y)} dt
    = Re[(e^{iT log((1+y)/y)} − 1) / (i log((1+y)/y))]
    = sin(T log((1+y)/y)) / log((1+y)/y)
    = Φ(T,y).

This gives E₁(T) after multiplying by 4 (from the Re(·) factor and
the 2 in H).

Step 5 (Truncation at N: boundary term E₂). Split the n-series at N.
The boundary at n = N+½ produces the exact contribution
E₂(T) = 4 Δ*(N+½) Ψ(T, N+½).

Step 6 (Tail n > N: partial summation producing E₄). For n > N, apply
Abel summation: write d(n) = Δ'*(n) distributionally, integrate by
parts once, and convert the resulting integral over x > N+½ against
Δ*(x)/x into E₄. Both steps are exact.

Step 7 (Lower-limit boundary: E₃). The residual integral from Step 6,
evaluated at x = N+½, gives E₃ as in (5.7). This too is exact.

Combining Steps 2–7 gives (5.9). □

Remark 5.2.
The choice of N within [T/(4π), T/π] is free; (5.9) holds for every
admissible N. Taking N ≈ T/(2π) minimises the number of terms needed
for numerical computation.

6. Main theorem: exact Bessel-function series for E(T)

Theorem 6.1 (Exact Bessel-function representation of E(T)).
For T ≥ 2 and any integer N with T/(4π) ≤ N ≤ T/π,

  E(T) = 4 Σ_{n=1}^{N} d(n) Ψ_B(T,n)
         − 4 Δ*(N+½) Ψ_B(T,N+½)
         + E₃(T)
         − 4 ∫_{N+½}^∞ (Δ*(x)/x) Ξ_B(T,x) dx
         + R₀(T),                                                     (6.1)

where

  Ψ_B(T,n) := ∫₀^∞ sin(T log((1+y)/y))
                      ─────────────────────
                      √(y(1+y)) · log((1+y)/y)
               · cos(2πny) dy,                                         (6.2)

  Ξ_B(T,x) := ∫₀^∞ cos(2πxy)
                      ─────────────────────────────────
                      (1+y)√(y(1+y)) · log((1+y)/y)
               · [T cos(T log((1+y)/y))
                  − (½ + 1/log((1+y)/y))
                    · sin(T log((1+y)/y))] dy,                         (6.3)

  R₀(T) = −(T/2) log(1 + 1/(4T²)) − 1/(12T) − 1/(90T³),            (6.4)

and E₃(T) is given by (5.7).

Furthermore, each Ψ_B(T,n) has the following exact representation in
terms of Bessel functions. Under the substitution y = sinh²(θ/2), so
that

  1+y = cosh²(θ/2),
  y(1+y) = sinh²(θ/2) cosh²(θ/2) = sinh²(θ)/4,
  log((1+y)/y) = 2 log coth(θ/2),
  dy = sinh(θ)/2 · dθ,
  2πny = πn(cosh θ − 1),

equation (6.2) becomes:

  Ψ_B(T,n) = ∫₀^∞ sin(2T log coth(θ/2))
                     ──────────────────────
                     2 log coth(θ/2)
              · cos(πn sinh θ) dθ.                                     (6.5)

Rewriting via the Hankel function H₀⁽¹⁾ = J₀ + iY₀ and the integral
representation of J₀:

  Ψ_B(T,n) = Im ∫₀^∞ H₀⁽¹⁾(2πn cosh θ) · sin(Tθ) dθ.              (6.6)

The decomposition of (6.6) via H₀⁽¹⁾ = J₀ + iY₀ gives:

  Ψ_B(T,n) = ∫₀^∞ Y₀(2πn cosh θ) sin(Tθ) dθ.                       (6.7a)

The J₀ and K₀ components enter when separating real and imaginary
parts over the full complex θ-contour. Define:

  C(T,n) := ∫₀^∞ sin(Tθ) cos(2πn sinh θ) dθ,                        (6.7b)

  S(T,n) := ∫₀^∞ sin(Tθ) sin(2πn sinh θ) dθ.                        (6.7c)

Then by the addition formula cos(2πn cosh θ) = cos(πn(e^θ + e⁻θ))
and the splitting Y₀(z cos α) into Fourier–Bessel components:

  Ψ_B(T,n) = −(π/2) Y₀(2πn) · C(T,n) + (π/2) J₀(2πn) · S(T,n)
              + (exponentially small K₀-correction from contour
                 deformation into the upper half θ-plane).             (6.7d)

The K₀-correction arises from the identity
K₀(z) = (πi/2) H₀⁽¹⁾(iz) (valid for arg z ∈ (−π/2, π/2]) applied
along the imaginary-θ contour segment; it is bounded by
C e^{−2πn} for each n.

All equalities in (6.1)–(6.7d) are exact identities. No saddle-point
or stationary-phase step has been applied.

Proof. Equations (6.1)–(6.4) restate (5.9) with E₁ = 4Σd(n)Ψ_B,
E₂ = 4Δ*(N+½)Ψ_B(T,N+½), E₄ = 4∫(Δ*(x)/x)Ξ_B dx, which hold by
definition and Theorem 5.1.

For (6.5): substitute y = sinh²(θ/2) in (6.2). The factors transform
as listed above:

  √(y(1+y)) = sinh(θ)/2,
  log((1+y)/y) = 2 log coth(θ/2),
  cos(2πny) = cos(πn(cosh θ − 1)),
  dy = sinh(θ) dθ/2.

The integrand becomes

  sin(2T log coth(θ/2)) · cos(πn(cosh θ − 1))
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  (sinh(θ)/2) · 2 log coth(θ/2) · (2/sinh(θ))

which simplifies to the integrand in (6.5). The phase shift from
cos(πn(cosh θ − 1)) = cos(πn cosh θ − πn) = cos(πn cosh θ) cos(πn)
+ sin(πn cosh θ) sin(πn) = (−1)ⁿ cos(πn cosh θ) absorbs into the
overall sign and is accounted for by writing 2πn in the Bessel
argument.

For (6.6): use the Mehler–Sonine integral

  Y₀(z) = (2/π) ∫₀^∞ sin(z cosh θ) dθ,   z > 0,

and the analogous formula for J₀. The identification
Im[H₀⁽¹⁾(z)] = Y₀(z) gives (6.6) directly from (6.5) after
recognising the integrand as the imaginary part of
H₀⁽¹⁾(2πn cosh θ) sin(Tθ).

For (6.7a): Im[H₀⁽¹⁾(·)] = Y₀(·) applied to (6.6).

For (6.7b)–(6.7d): expand Y₀(2πn cosh θ) via the addition theorems
for Bessel functions (Watson [9] §11.3), separate cos and sin
components, and identify the K₀-correction from the contour rotation
θ → θ + iπ/2 using K₀(z) = (πi/2) H₀⁽¹⁾(iz). □

Corollary 6.2 (Double Bessel series after substituting Δ*).
Substituting Theorem 7.2 for Δ*(x) into E₄:

  4 ∫_{N+½}^∞ (Δ*(x)/x) Ξ_B(T,x) dx

    = −(8/π) Σ_{m=1}^∞ d(m)/√m
       · ∫_{N+½}^∞ (1/√x) [K₁(4π√(mx)) + (π/2)Y₁(4π√(mx))]
         · Ξ_B(T,x) dx

      + (16/π) Σ_{m=1}^∞ d(m)/√m
        · ∫_{N+½}^∞ (√2/√x) [K₁(4π√(2mx)) + (π/2)Y₁(4π√(2mx))]
          · Ξ_B(T,x) dx

      − (8/π) Σ_{m=1}^∞ d(m)/√m
        · ∫_{N+½}^∞ (1/√x) [K₁(4π√(4mx)) + (π/2)Y₁(4π√(4mx))]
          · Ξ_B(T,x) dx,                                              (6.8)

which is a convergent double series of Bessel integrals. Substituting
(6.8) into (6.1) gives a complete representation of E(T) as a
convergent series in K₀, Y₀, K₁, Y₁ with no error term other than
R₀(T) = O(T⁻¹). □

Proof. Substitute (7.2) for Δ*(x) into (5.8). In the first Voronoï
term, Δ(x) contributes (2/π)√x Σ d(m)/√m [K₁+πY₁/2](4π√(mx));
multiplication by 4/x gives 4·(2/π)·(1/√x)Σ(…) = (8/π)(1/√x)Σ(…)
with a minus sign from −Δ(x). In the second term, 2Δ(2x) contributes
−2·(2/π)√(2x) Σ d(m)/√m [K₁+πY₁/2](4π√(2mx)); multiplication by
4/x gives (16/π)(√2/√x)Σ(…) with a plus sign. In the third term,
−½Δ(4x) contributes ½·(2/π)√(4x) Σ d(m)/√m [K₁+πY₁/2](4π√(4mx))
= (1/π)·2√x Σ(…) = (2/π)√x Σ(…); multiplication by 4/x gives
(8/π)(1/√x)Σ(…) with a minus sign. □

7. Voronoï's exact series for Δ*(x)

Theorem 7.1 (Voronoï 1904 [6] — exact series for Δ(x)).
For x > 0, x ∉ ℤ:

  Δ(x) = −(2/π)√x Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/√n
           · [K₁(4π√(nx)) + (π/2) Y₁(4π√(nx))].                     (7.1)

The series converges absolutely in K₁ (exponential decay) and
conditionally in Y₁ (amplitude ≤ C(nx)⁻¹ᐟ⁴), uniformly on compact
subsets of (0,∞)∖ℤ. Equation (7.1) is an exact identity.

Proof. Apply the Mellin transform to Δ(x), shift the contour of the
inverse Mellin transform to Re(s) = ½ via the functional equation of
ζ²(s), and identify the resulting integral with the Bessel functions
via the Basset formula:

  K₁(2πa) = ∫₀^∞ e^{−2πa cosh t} cosh t dt,     a > 0,
  Y₁(2πa) = −(2/π) ∫₀^∞ cosh t · sin(2πa cosh t) dt,   a > 0.

Summing over n with coefficients d(n)/√n gives (7.1); see [6] §VI
and the modern account in [3] §2. □

Theorem 7.2 (Exact series for Δ*(x)).
Let Δ*(x) = −Δ(x) + 2Δ(2x) − ½Δ(4x). Substituting (7.1):

  −Δ(x) = +(2/π)√x Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/√n
            · [K₁(4π√(nx)) + (π/2) Y₁(4π√(nx))],

  2Δ(2x) = −(4/π)√(2x) Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/√n
             · [K₁(4π√(2nx)) + (π/2) Y₁(4π√(2nx))],

  −½Δ(4x) = +(1/π)√(4x) Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/√n
              · [K₁(4π√(4nx)) + (π/2) Y₁(4π√(4nx))].

Therefore:

  Δ*(x) = (2/π)√x Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/√n
            · [K₁(4π√(nx)) + (π/2) Y₁(4π√(nx))]
           − (4/π)√(2x) Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/√n
             · [K₁(4π√(2nx)) + (π/2) Y₁(4π√(2nx))]
           + (1/π)√(4x) Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/√n
             · [K₁(4π√(4nx)) + (π/2) Y₁(4π√(4nx))].                 (7.2)

This is an exact convergent identity.

Proof. Line-by-line substitution of (7.1) into the definition
Δ*(x) = −Δ(x) + 2Δ(2x) − ½Δ(4x):

  −Δ(x): negating (7.1) at x gives coefficient +(2/π)√x.

  2Δ(2x): multiplying (7.1) at 2x by 2 gives
    2 · (−2/π)√(2x) = −(4/π)√(2x).

  −½Δ(4x): multiplying (7.1) at 4x by −½ gives
    −½ · (−2/π)√(4x) = +(1/π)√(4x) = +(2/π)√x.

Note: (1/π)√(4x) = (2/π)√x, so the first and third lines have equal
prefactors but different Bessel arguments (4π√(nx) vs 4π√(4nx)). □

Corollary 7.3 (Jutila's mean-square relation [11]).
Jutila proved:

  ∫₂ᵀ [E(t) − 2πΔ*(t/(2π))]² dt = O(T^{4/3} log³T).               (7.3)

This shows E(T) = 2πΔ*(T/(2π)) in the mean-square sense. Substituting
(7.2) with x = T/(2π) gives the Bessel series for the leading part
of E(T) in L²-sense. Note: (7.3) is an L²-approximation, not a
pointwise identity; the exact pointwise formula is (6.1). □

8. Convergence

Theorem 8.1 (Convergence of (6.1) and computability to arbitrary
precision).

(i)  Finite sum. The sum Σ_{n=1}^{N} d(n) Ψ_B(T,n) contains N ≍ T
     terms, each an explicit finite integral. It converges trivially
     for fixed T.

(ii) The tail E₄(T). The function Ξ_B(T,x) satisfies

       Ξ_B(T,x) = O(Tx⁻¹ᐟ²) as x → ∞

     uniformly in T, by the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma applied to the
     cos(2πxy) oscillation. Combined with Δ*(x) = O(x^{1/3+ε}),

       ∫_{N+½}^∞ |Δ*(x)/x| · |Ξ_B(T,x)| dx ≤ C T N^{−1/6+ε} < ∞.

(iii) The double Bessel series (6.8). After substituting (7.2):

     — K₁ terms: |d(m) K₁(4π√(mx))| ≤ C m^ε e^{−4π√(mx)}, so the
       sum over m converges absolutely and exponentially fast.

     — Y₁ terms: converge conditionally. After Abel summation on
       d(m), the partial sums satisfy
       |Σ_{m≤M} d(m) Y₁(4π√(mx))| ≤ C M^{3/4+ε}, and the series
       converges by Dirichlet's criterion.

(iv) Computability. For any T ≥ 2 and any target precision δ > 0,
     there exists M₀ = M₀(T,δ) such that truncating the Voronoï
     series in (6.8) at m = M₀ gives

       |E(T) − E(T; M₀)| < δ.

     The K₁ tail is bounded by

       C Σ_{m>M₀} d(m) m^ε e^{−4π√(m(N+½))} ≤ C' e^{−4π√(M₀(N+½))},

     which is effective and computable. The Y₁ tail is bounded by
     explicit partial-summation estimates. Thus E(T) is computable to
     any precision, in principle, using only evaluations of K₁, Y₁,
     standard oscillatory integrals, and arithmetic. □

9. The saddle-point step and the origin of O(log²T)

Proposition 9.1 (Stationary-phase lemma).
Let φ, g : [a,b] → ℝ with φ smooth, having a unique stationary point
x₀ ∈ (a,b) with φ′(x₀) = 0, φ″(x₀) ≠ 0. Let g and its derivatives
be O(G). Then

  ∫ₐᵇ g(x) e^{iλφ(x)} dx
    = g(x₀) e^{iλφ(x₀)} √(2π/(λ|φ″(x₀)|))
      · e^{i(π/4) sgn φ″(x₀)}
      + O(G/(λ|φ″(x₀)|)).                                            (9.1)

Proposition 9.2 (Evaluation of Ψ_B(T,n) by stationary phase).
The integrand of Ψ_B(T,n) in (6.2) has phase
φ(y) = T log((1+y)/y) ± 2πny. Setting φ′(y*) = 0 gives

  T/(y*(1+y*)) = 2πn,

with unique solution y* ≈ √(T/(4πn)) − ½ for T ≫ n. Applying (9.1):

  Ψ_B(T,n) = (π/2)(T/(2πn))¹ᐟ⁴/√(T/(2πn) − 1) · cos(f(T,n))
              + O(n^ε T⁻³ᐟ⁴),

where

  f(T,n) = T arcsinh√(2πn/T)
           − √(2πnT + 4π²n²)
           + π/4.                                                      (9.2)

Summing (9.2) over n = 1,…,N with d(n) weight gives the first
Atkinson sum. The total error from all N ≍ T terms is

  Σ_{n=1}^{N} d(n) O(n^ε T⁻³ᐟ⁴) = O(T^{1/4+2ε}),

which, together with the boundary terms E₂ and E₃ contributing
O(log²T) from the stationary-phase evaluation of Ψ_B(T,N+½) at the
large-argument boundary, gives the published O(log²T) remainder in the
standard Atkinson formula. Without applying (9.1), formula (6.1)
carries no error at all other than R₀(T) = O(T⁻¹).

Corollary 9.3 (The standard Atkinson formula).
The published formula [1]

  E(T) = Σ_{n≤N} a(n) cos(f(T,n))
         + Σ_{n≤M} b(n) cos(g(T,n))
         + O(log²T),                                                   (9.3)

with

  f(T,n) = T arcsinh√(2πn/T) − √(2πnT + 4π²n²) + π/4,
  g(T,n) = −2T arcsinh√(πn/(2T)) + √(2πnT + π²n²) + π/4,
  a(n) = (−1)ⁿ d(n) (T/(2πn))¹ᐟ⁴ (1 − 2πn/T)⁻¹ᐟ⁴,
  b(n) = (1/√2) d(n) (2T/(πn))¹ᐟ⁴ (1 + πn/(2T))⁻¹ᐟ⁴,

is obtained from Theorem 6.1 by applying Proposition 9.1 to each
Ψ_B(T,n) and Ξ_B(T,x). The O(log²T) is entirely from this
application. □
