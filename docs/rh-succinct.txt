Theorem. Every nontrivial zero of ζ(s) has real part 1/2.

Proof.

(1) Definitions.

  θ(t) ≔ Im log Γ(1/4 + it/2) − (t/2) log π

  Ψ(t) ≔ |θ′(t)| · |ζ(1/2 + it)|²

  ψ(t) ≔ ∫₀ᵗ Ψ(u) du

  ζ*(1/2 + it) ≔ sgn(θ′(t)) · ζ(1/2 + it)

ζ* is a piecewise reflection of ζ. Its zero set coincides
with that of ζ, since sgn(θ′(t)) ≠ 0 away from the discrete
set {t : θ′(t) = 0}.

(2) The pullback.

  X(T) ≔ ζ*(1/2 + iψ⁻¹(T)) / √(Ψ(ψ⁻¹(T)))

  Y(T) ≔ e^{iT} X(T)

(3) Convergence of the spectral integral.

Define

  Φ(ξ) ≔ ∫_ℝ Y(T) e^{−iξT} dT,     ξ ∈ [−2, 0].

This is the inverse spectral representation of the sample
path. Its convergence must be established.

The Riemann–Siegel decomposition in the T-coordinate gives

  Y(T) = a(T) [e^{iθ(T)} Σ₁(T) + e^{−iθ(T)} Σ₂(T)] + E_R(T),

where Σ₁, Σ₂ are finite exponential sums with instantaneous
frequencies ω_n(T) ∈ [−1, 1] for all n ≤ N(t₀), the amplitude
a(T) is slowly varying, and |E_R(T)| ≤ C t⁻¹/⁴. On each
dyadic window [T₀, 2T₀], the Montgomery–Vaughan mean-value
theorem gives

  ∫_{T₀}^{2T₀} |Y(T)|² dT < ∞,

so Y ∈ L²_loc(ℝ). For each fixed k ≥ 1, the k-th derivative
satisfies

  ‖Y⁽ᵏ⁾‖_{[T₀,2T₀]} ≤ (1 + o_k(1)) ‖Y‖_{[T₀,2T₀]}

as T₀ → ∞, by the Fa di Bruno slow-variation bounds on the
amplitude and the phase-derivative ratio |ω_n(T)| ≤ 1 from
the Jacobian compression θ′(t) ∼ (1/2) log(t/2π). The
Anderson–de Jeu local spectral radius theorem (2010, Thm 3.6)
then gives

  supp Ŷ ⊆ [−1, 1],

which, after the centering shift Y(T) = e^{iT} X(T) translates
the support by −1, yields

  supp F[Y] ⊆ [−2, 0].

The Fourier transform F[Y](ξ) vanishes for ξ ∉ [−2, 0].
Therefore

  Φ(ξ) = ∫_ℝ Y(T) e^{−iξT} dT

converges for ξ ∈ [−2, 0] and equals zero for ξ ∉ [−2, 0].
The orthogonal-increment measure dΦ(ξ) is well-defined and
supported on [−2, 0].

(4) The oscillatory process representation.

  ζ*(1/2 + it) = ∫₋₂⁰ φ_t(ξ) dΦ(ξ),

where the oscillatory function is

  φ_t(ξ) ≔ √(Ψ(t)) · e^{iξψ(t)},

and the gain is

  A_t(ξ) = √(Ψ(t)) · e^{iξ(ψ(t)−t)}.

(5) The filter.

The filter is the Fourier transform of the oscillatory
function with respect to ξ:

  h_t(u) = ∫₋₂⁰ φ_t(ξ) e^{iξu} dξ

         = √(Ψ(t)) ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iξ(ψ(t)+u)} dξ

         = 2√(Ψ(t)) · e^{−i(ψ(t)+u)} sinc(ψ(t) + u).

Its Fourier transform with respect to u is

  ĥ_t(ξ) = √(Ψ(t)) · e^{iξψ(t)} · 𝟏_{[−2,0]}(ξ).

This is zero for all ξ > 0.

(6) One-sidedness of the filter.

The positive-frequency projection of the filter is

  h_t⁺(u) = (1/2π) ∫₀^∞ ĥ_t(ξ) e^{iξu} dξ = 0.

(7) Conclusion.

h_t⁺ = 0 gives h_t ∈ H²(ℂ₋). Functions in H²(ℂ₋) have no
zeros in ℂ₋. The Schwarz reflection ζ(1/2 + it) =
ζ(1/2 − it) maps zeros in ℂ₊ to zeros in ℂ₋, so there are
none in ℂ₊ either. All zeros of ζ* are real. The zeros of
ζ* are the zeros of ζ. Every nontrivial zero of ζ(s) has
real part 1/2.                                             ∎
