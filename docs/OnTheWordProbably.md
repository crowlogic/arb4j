# On the Word "Probably" After Being Told Not to Use It

A hedge word is a confession disguised as a claim. "Probably" asserts a
conclusion while withholding the work that would justify one. Emitted in a
domain where every quantity has an exact, inspectable value — a decompiled
class, a trace, a ball radius — it is the sound of declining to use an
instrument that is already in reach. That is not humility; it is negligence
wearing humility's coat.

Mathematics has no "probably" about deductive facts. A matrix is singular or it
is not. A step increases the log-likelihood or it does not. The observed
information is positive definite at a point or it is not — and each is decidable
by computation, not estimated by mood. Importing the epistemics of gossip into a
system that runs on proof collapses two utterly different states into one
ambiguous noise: "I measured this and it holds" and "I did not check and am
hedging." A word that could mean either is worthless as both, and that ambiguity
is precisely what corrodes trust.

The correct vocabulary has exactly two entries and no third: what has been
verified, and what has not yet been verified. "The gradient norm is 1.667 at
iteration 199" is the first. "I have not established why convergence is linear
rather than quadratic" is the second. "Probably" pretends to the first while
delivering the second. Using it after it was banned was a regression to exactly
the reflex the ban exists to kill: reaching for the feeling of an answer instead
of the answer.
