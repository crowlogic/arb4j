Abstract
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Continuous-time Gaussian white noise exists as a generalized
(distribution-valued) stochastic process but not as an ordinary
function-valued one.  The obstruction is that the covariance operator of
white noise is the identity on L²(ℝ), which fails to be trace-class in
infinite dimensions: every eigenvalue equals 1 and the trace diverges.
Three equivalent frameworks resolve this obstruction — cylindrical Wiener
processes, the Bochner–Minlos theorem on nuclear spaces, and Gelfand-triple
variational formulations — each enlarging the state space so that the
identity covariance becomes trace-class or Hilbert–Schmidt in the
appropriate sense.  A distinguished special case arises when the spectral
support is restricted to a compact set [−Ω, Ω]: the covariance operator
becomes a trace-class projection (with finite trace ΩT/π on any interval
[0,T]), the process is ordinary with entire analytic sample paths, and
the limit Ω → ∞ recovers full white noise via an approximate identity
whose convergence is governed by the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma.

1. Introduction
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

A standard Brownian motion B = (Bₜ)_{t∈ℝ} is a Gaussian process with
continuous sample paths, independent increments, and covariance

  E[Bₜ Bₛ] = min(t, s).

One naturally asks whether its derivative ξ(t) = dBₜ/dt exists.  The
difference quotient ΔB/Δt = (B_{t+Δt} − Bₜ)/Δt is Gaussian with mean
zero and variance 1/Δt, which diverges as Δt → 0.  The sample paths of
B have infinite total variation on every compact interval and are almost
surely nowhere differentiable.  No pointwise derivative exists.

The resolution is to abandon pointwise evaluation and instead pair the
noise against test functions φ ∈ L²(ℝ).  Define

  W(φ) = ∫_ℝ φ(t) dBₜ

via the Itô integral.  Each W(φ) is a well-defined Gaussian random
variable with variance ‖φ‖²_{L²}, and the covariance is

  E[W(φ) W(ψ)] = ∫_ℝ φ(t) ψ(t) dt = ⟨φ, ψ⟩_{L²}

The map φ ↦ W(φ) is a linear isometry from L²(ℝ) into L²(Ω).  This
is white noise: not a function of t, but a linear functional on L²(ℝ).

The covariance operator of this functional is the identity I on L²(ℝ).
On any infinite-dimensional Hilbert space, the identity is not trace-class:
Tr(I) = ∑ₖ 1 = ∞.  A centered Gaussian measure on a separable Hilbert
space exists if and only if its covariance operator is trace-class, so no
Gaussian measure with covariance I can live on L²(ℝ) itself.  The measure
must live on a larger space — the space S′(ℝ) of tempered distributions,
or an analogous enlargement.

2. White noise as an isonormal Gaussian process
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Definition.

  Let H be a real separable Hilbert space.  A family

    W = { W(h) : h ∈ H }

  is called an isonormal Gaussian process indexed by H if:

    (i)  For each h ∈ H, W(h) is a centered Gaussian random variable.

    (ii) For all f, g ∈ H,

           E[W(f) W(g)] = ⟨f, g⟩_H.

  The map

    W : H → L²(Ω, ℱ, ℙ),    h ↦ W(h),

  is then a linear isometry:

    E[W(h)²] = ⟨h, h⟩_H,    h ∈ H.

  When H = L²(ℝ), the isonormal Gaussian process W is called
  (real) Gaussian white noise.

Theorem 1 (Gaussian white noise on L²(ℝ)).

  There exists a probability space (Ω, ℱ, ℙ) and a linear map

    W : L²(ℝ) → L²(Ω, ℱ, ℙ)

  such that:

    (a) For each φ ∈ L²(ℝ), W(φ) is a centered Gaussian random variable.

    (b) For all φ, ψ ∈ L²(ℝ),

          E[W(φ) W(ψ)] = ∫_ℝ φ(t) ψ(t) dt.

    (c) In the distributional sense,

          E[W(t) W(s)] = δ(t − s),

        i.e. the covariance kernel is the Dirac delta.

    (d) The covariance operator C : L²(ℝ) → L²(ℝ) is the identity:

          ⟨Cφ, ψ⟩ = E[W(φ) W(ψ)] = ⟨φ, ψ⟩    ⟹    C = I.

    (e) Let A : H → H be a self-adjoint, positive, trace-class operator
        on a separable Hilbert space H.  Then any centered Gaussian
        process X indexed by H with covariance operator A can be
        represented as

          X(φ) = W(A¹ᐟ² φ),    φ ∈ H,

        where W is an isonormal Gaussian process on H.

Proof.

  Step 1.  Construction via Wiener integral.

  Let (Ω, ℱ, ℙ) carry a standard Brownian motion (Bₜ)_{t∈ℝ}.
  For φ ∈ L²(ℝ), define

    W(φ) = ∫_ℝ φ(t) dBₜ,

  the Itô integral of the deterministic integrand φ against B.

  Linearity of W follows from linearity of the Itô integral:

    W(aφ + bψ) = a W(φ) + b W(ψ),

  for all a, b ∈ ℝ and φ, ψ ∈ L²(ℝ).

  Step 2.  Gaussianity and centering.

  Approximate φ by simple functions

    φₙ(t) = ∑ₖ cₖ 1_{(tₖ, tₖ₊₁]}(t).

  For such φₙ,

    W(φₙ) = ∑ₖ cₖ (B_{tₖ₊₁} − B_{tₖ}),

  a finite linear combination of independent Gaussian increments of
  Brownian motion, hence Gaussian.  The L²(Ω)-limit of Gaussian random
  variables is Gaussian, so W(φ) is Gaussian.  Since
  E[B_{tₖ₊₁} − B_{tₖ}] = 0 for all k, each W(φₙ) is centered,
  and the limit W(φ) is centered as well.

  Thus (a) holds.

  Step 3.  Covariance and the Itô isometry.

  For deterministic integrands φ, ψ ∈ L²(ℝ), the Itô isometry gives

    E[W(φ) W(ψ)]
      = E[(∫ φ dBₜ)(∫ ψ dBₜ)]
      = ∫_ℝ φ(t) ψ(t) dt.

  This proves (b):

    E[W(φ) W(ψ)] = ⟨φ, ψ⟩_{L²(ℝ)}.

  Step 4.  Distributional covariance kernel.

  Write the action of the generalized random field W(t) on φ as

    W(φ) = ∫_ℝ W(t) φ(t) dt.

  Then

    E[W(φ) W(ψ)]
      = E[∫_ℝ W(t) φ(t) dt · ∫_ℝ W(s) ψ(s) ds]
      = ∬_{ℝ²} E[W(t) W(s)] φ(t) ψ(s) dt ds.

  On the other hand, by Step 3,

    E[W(φ) W(ψ)] = ∫_ℝ φ(t) ψ(t) dt.

  Therefore, for all test functions φ, ψ,

    ∬_{ℝ²} K(t,s) φ(t) ψ(s) dt ds = ∫_ℝ φ(t) ψ(t) dt,

  where K(t,s) = E[W(t) W(s)] in the sense of distributions.
  The unique distribution K with this property is

    K(t,s) = δ(t − s).

  Thus, in the sense of distributions,

    E[W(t) W(s)] = δ(t − s),

  which is (c).

  Step 5.  Covariance operator.

  Define the covariance operator C : L²(ℝ) → L²(ℝ) by

    ⟨Cφ, ψ⟩ = E[W(φ) W(ψ)]

  From (b),

    ⟨Cφ, ψ⟩ = ⟨φ, ψ⟩    for all φ, ψ ∈ L²(ℝ).

  Hence C = I, proving (d)

  Step 6.  Representation via A¹ᐟ².

  Let H be any real separable Hilbert space and let W be an isonormal
  process on H: for f, g ∈ H,

    E[W(f) W(g)] = ⟨f, g⟩_H

  Let A : H → H be self-adjoint, positive, and trace-class.
  Define X(φ) by

    X(φ) = W(A¹ᐟ² φ),    φ ∈ H

  Then

    E[X(φ) X(ψ)]
      = E[W(A¹ᐟ² φ) W(A¹ᐟ² ψ)]
      = ⟨A¹ᐟ² φ, A¹ᐟ² ψ⟩_H
      = ⟨φ, Aψ⟩_H

  for all φ, ψ ∈ H.  Thus X is a centered Gaussian process with
  covariance operator A, which is (e).  ∎

3. Series construction and the Karhunen–Loève expansion
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Let H be a real separable Hilbert space with orthonormal basis {eᵢ}_{i≥1},
and let {Gᵢ}_{i≥1} be i.i.d. N(0,1) random variables on (Ω, ℱ, ℙ).
Define

  W(h) = ∑_{i=1}^∞ ⟨h, eᵢ⟩_H · Gᵢ,    h ∈ H.

For fixed h, the series converges in L²(Ω) because

  ∑_{i=1}^∞ E[⟨h, eᵢ⟩² Gᵢ²]
    = ∑_{i=1}^∞ ⟨h, eᵢ⟩²
    = ‖h‖²_H < ∞

Thus W : H → L²(Ω) is well-defined, linear, and satisfies

  E[W(f) W(g)]
    = ∑_{i=1}^∞ ⟨f, eᵢ⟩ ⟨g, eᵢ⟩
    = ⟨f, g⟩_H

This is the general isonormal Gaussian process on H.

Now let X be a centered Gaussian process on H with covariance operator
A : H → H, self-adjoint, positive, trace-class.  Let {eₙ} be an
orthonormal basis of eigenvectors of A with eigenvalues {λₙ}
(λₙ ≥ 0, ∑ₙ λₙ < ∞).  For each φ ∈ H,

  X(φ) = W(A¹ᐟ² φ)
       = ∑_{n=1}^∞ ⟨A¹ᐟ² φ, eₙ⟩ Gₙ
       = ∑_{n=1}^∞ √λₙ ⟨φ, eₙ⟩ Gₙ

Equivalently, setting ξₙ = Gₙ and viewing X as a random element of H,

  X = ∑_{n=1}^∞ √λₙ ξₙ eₙ

in H-valued L²(Ω).  This is the Karhunen–Loève expansion.

The key convergence condition is the trace-class requirement:

  ∑_{n=1}^∞ λₙ = Tr(A) < ∞

which guarantees convergence in H.  For white noise, A = I and λₙ = 1
for all n, so

  ∑_{n=1}^∞ λₙ = ∞

and there is no H-valued Gaussian random variable with covariance I on
an infinite-dimensional H.  This is the non-trace-class obstruction.

4. The non-trace-class problem and cylindrical Wiener processes
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Let U be a separable Hilbert space and let Q : U → U be a bounded,
self-adjoint, positive operator.  A U-valued Q-Wiener process is a
Gaussian process W(t) with mean 0 and covariance operator tQ at time t.
Such a process exists as a U-valued random variable if and only if Q is
trace-class.  When Q = I on an infinite-dimensional U, Tr(I) = ∞ and
no U-valued Wiener process with covariance tI exists.  Instead one works
with a cylindrical Wiener process.

Definition.

  Let U be a separable Hilbert space with orthonormal basis {eₖ}.
  Let {βₖ} be independent standard real-valued Brownian motions.
  The formal series

    W(t) = ∑_{k=1}^∞ βₖ(t) eₖ

  does not converge in U, but the family {⟨W(t), u⟩ : u ∈ U} defined by

    ⟨W(t), u⟩ = ∑_{k=1}^∞ βₖ(t) ⟨u, eₖ⟩

  is well-defined in L²(Ω) for each fixed u.  This family is called a
  cylindrical Wiener process on U.

To obtain an actual U₁-valued process, pick a Hilbert space U₁ and a
Hilbert–Schmidt embedding

  J : U → U₁

i.e. ∑_{k=1}^∞ ‖Jeₖ‖²_{U₁} < ∞.  Then

  W(t) = ∑_{k=1}^∞ βₖ(t) Jeₖ

converges in L²(Ω; U₁) and defines a Q₁-Wiener process on U₁ with
covariance operator

  Q₁ = JJ* : U₁ → U₁

which is trace-class on U₁.  The original object W is then viewed as a
cylindrical Wiener process on U with values in U₁.

Stochastic integration.

  Let H be another separable Hilbert space.  For a process Φ(t) taking
  values in the Hilbert–Schmidt operators L₂(U, H), one defines the
  stochastic integral

    ∫₀ᵀ Φ(t) dW(t)

  as an H-valued random variable.  The Itô isometry takes the form

    E[‖∫₀ᵀ Φ(t) dW(t)‖²_H]
      = E ∫₀ᵀ ‖Φ(t)‖²_{L₂(U,H)} dt

  Here the regularity requirement previously encoded in the trace-class
  condition on Q is shifted to the integrand: Φ must be Hilbert–Schmidt
  from U to H.

5. Gelfand triples and SPDEs with non-trace-class noise
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

In stochastic partial differential equations with space-time white noise
or cylindrical Q = I noise, the natural functional-analytic setting is a
Gelfand triple

  V ⊂ H ⊂ V*,

where:

  • H is a separable Hilbert space (e.g. L²(D) for a domain D ⊂ ℝᵈ),
  • V is a reflexive Banach space densely and continuously embedded into H
    (e.g. a Sobolev space H₀¹(D)),
  • V* is the dual of V.

Consider an SPDE of the form

  dX(t) = A(t, X(t)) dt + B(t, X(t)) dW(t)

where:

  • A : [0,T] × V → V* is a (possibly nonlinear) operator,
  • B : [0,T] × V → L₂(U, H) maps into Hilbert–Schmidt operators,
  • W is a cylindrical Wiener process on U (often U = H with Q = I).

Under standard conditions on A (hemicontinuity, monotonicity, coercivity,
growth) and B (Lipschitz-type bounds into L₂(U, H)), there exists a
unique solution X taking values in H, with

  X ∈ Lᵅ(0,T; V) ∩ L²(0,T; H)

for suitable α ≥ 2.  The key structural points are:

  • The drift A maps into V*, not H, which allows unbounded operators
    such as Laplacians.
  • The diffusion coefficient B takes values in L₂(U, H), reflecting that
    the Q = I noise is only cylindrical in U and is realized in H through
    Hilbert–Schmidt operators.

This variational framework is the SPDE analog of enlarging the state
space to handle non-trace-class covariances.

6. Nuclear spaces, Bochner–Minlos, and white noise on S′(ℝ)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The deepest resolution of the non-trace-class problem is measure-theoretic.
The identity covariance C = I is not trace-class on L²(ℝ), so no Gaussian
measure can live on L²(ℝ) itself.  The fix is to enlarge the state space
to the topological dual of a nuclear space, where the Bochner–Minlos
theorem guarantees existence of the measure.

The Schwartz space S(ℝ) of rapidly decreasing smooth functions is a
countably Hilbert nuclear space.  Its topology is generated by a family
of Hilbertian norms ‖·‖ₚ such that, for each p, the embedding

  (S(ℝ), ‖·‖_{p+1}) ↪ (S(ℝ), ‖·‖ₚ)

is Hilbert–Schmidt.  This nuclearity means that the identity operator,
at each scale, factors through a Hilbert–Schmidt map — exactly the
compactness that the identity operator on all of L²(ℝ) lacks.

Theorem (Bochner–Minlos)

  Let N be a nuclear space and C : N → ℂ a continuous, positive-definite,
  normalized functional (C(0) = 1).  Then there exists a unique probability
  measure μ on the dual N′ such that

    C(φ) = ∫_{N′} e^{i⟨ω,φ⟩} dμ(ω)

  for all φ ∈ N

In the white-noise case, take N = S(ℝ).  Define

  C(φ) = exp(−½ ‖φ‖²_{L²(ℝ)})

This functional is continuous on S(ℝ) (the nuclear topology is finer than
the L² norm) and positive-definite.  By Bochner–Minlos, there exists a
unique probability measure μ on S′(ℝ), the space of tempered distributions,
such that

  C(φ) = ∫_{S′(ℝ)} e^{iω(φ)} dμ(ω)

for all φ ∈ S(ℝ).

Under μ, the random variable ω(φ) is centered Gaussian with variance
‖φ‖²_{L²}.  Thus the process

  W(φ) = ω(φ),    φ ∈ L²(ℝ)

is the isonormal Gaussian process on L²(ℝ), with sample points ω lying
in S′(ℝ).  White noise is a random tempered distribution, not an
L²-function.  The non-trace-class obstruction is resolved by enlarging
the state space from L²(ℝ) to S′(ℝ):

  • On L²(ℝ): the covariance operator I is not trace-class, so no Gaussian
    measure with covariance I can live on L²(ℝ).
  • On S′(ℝ): the same covariance structure is realized via μ, thanks to
    nuclearity of S(ℝ) and the Bochner–Minlos theorem.

7. The Hida triple
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Hida's 1975 programme takes the white noise measure μ on S′(ℝ) as the
primary object rather than deriving it from Brownian motion.  Each sample
ω ∈ S′(ℝ) is a tempered distribution, and nonlinear functionals of white
noise are studied within a further Gelfand triple:

  (S) ⊂ L²(S′(ℝ), μ) ⊂ (S)*

where:

  • (S) consists of Hida test functionals: elements of L²(μ) with
    rapidly decaying Wiener chaos coefficients, stable under Wick
    product and the Hida derivative ∂ₜ.
  • (S)* is the space of Hida distributions, including Wick powers of
    white noise and other renormalized functionals that cannot be
    represented as ordinary L² random variables.

This triple stands to L²(S′, μ) as the Schwartz triple S ⊂ L² ⊂ S′
stands to L²(ℝ).  The isonormal process W(φ) occupies the first Wiener
chaos; higher Wiener–Itô chaoses and Wick products generate all of
L²(S′, μ), and elements of (S)* extend the calculus beyond
square-integrable functionals.

The L²(S′, μ) space admits the Wiener–Itô chaos decomposition

  L²(Ω, ℱ, ℙ) = ⊕_{n=0}^∞ ℋₙ

where ℋₙ is the n-th Wiener chaos, ℋₙ ≅ L²_sym(ℝⁿ) via multiple
Wiener–Itô integrals Iₙ, and

  E[F²] = ∑_{n=0}^∞ n! ‖fₙ‖² < ∞

for F = ∑_{n=0}^∞ Iₙ(fₙ).  The Hida derivative ∂ₜ is the white noise
analogue of the Malliavin derivative, defined by

  ∂ₜF(ω) = lim_{ε→0} [F(ω + εδₜ) − F(ω)] / ε

differentiating in the direction of a point mass at t.

8. Three equivalent resolutions of non-trace-class covariance
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The same obstruction — the failure of the identity operator to be
trace-class on an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space — admits three
equivalent resolutions:

  (A) Cylindrical Wiener process
      Original space : U
      Enlargement    : U₁ ⊃ U via Hilbert–Schmidt embedding J : U → U₁
      Covariance on U₁: Q₁ = JJ*, trace-class on U₁

  (B) Gelfand triple / variational SPDE
      Original "energy" space : H
      Enlargement : V* ⊃ H, with V ⊂ H ⊂ V*
      Drift A : V → V*; diffusion B : V → L₂(U, H); noise is cylindrical
      in U but realized via Hilbert–Schmidt maps into H

  (C) Nuclear space / Bochner–Minlos
      Original Hilbert space : L²(ℝ)
      Enlargement : S′(ℝ) ⊃ L²(ℝ), where S(ℝ) is nuclear
      Covariance implemented via characteristic functional on S(ℝ),
      giving a Gaussian measure on S′(ℝ)

In all three cases the Gaussian structure with "covariance = identity" is
preserved, but the space on which the measure or process lives is enlarged
so that the corresponding operator becomes trace-class or Hilbert–Schmidt
in the appropriate sense.

9. Band-limited white noise: the trace-class special case
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Restricting the spectral support to a compact set [−Ω, Ω] while holding
the PSD at 1 converts the entire analysis from generalized to ordinary.
The resulting process exists as a genuine function-valued Gaussian process
with smooth — in fact entire — sample paths.

Power spectral density and autocorrelation.

  S(ω) = 𝟏_{[−Ω,Ω]}(ω)

  By Wiener–Khinchin inversion,

    R(τ) = (1/2π) ∫_{−Ω}^{Ω} e^{iωτ} dω = sin(Ωτ) / (πτ)

  Pointwise variance:

    R(0) = Ω/π < ∞

  This finite variance is the decisive difference from full white noise,
  where R(0) = δ(0) = ∞.

Covariance operator.

  The covariance operator acts on L²(ℝ) as convolution with the sinc
  kernel:

    (Cφ)(t) = ∫_ℝ [sin(Ω(t−s)) / (π(t−s))] φ(s) ds

  which in the Fourier domain is the bounded multiplication operator

    (Cφ̂)(ω) = 𝟏_{[−Ω,Ω]}(ω) · φ̂(ω)

  C = P_Ω is the L² orthogonal projection onto the Paley–Wiener space
  PW_Ω.  Restricted to any compact time interval [0,T], the operator has
  trace

    Tr(C|_{[0,T]}) = ∫₀ᵀ R(0) dt = ΩT/π < ∞

  This equals the number of effective degrees of freedom on [0,T],
  consistent with the Nyquist theorem which gives ΩT/π independent
  samples at spacing π/Ω.

Karhunen–Loève expansion.

  Since the sinc kernel is continuous and positive-definite on [0,T],
  Mercer's theorem yields the absolutely and uniformly convergent
  expansion

    sin(Ω(t−s)) / (π(t−s)) = ∑_{k=1}^∞ λₖ ψₖ(t) ψₖ(s)

  with ∑ₖ λₖ = ΩT/π.  The eigenfunctions ψₖ are the prolate
  spheroidal wave functions, the unique basis simultaneously orthogonal
  on [0,T] and on [−Ω, Ω].  The Karhunen–Loève expansion of the
  band-limited noise on [0,T] is

    ξ(t) = ∑_{k=1}^∞ √λₖ Zₖ ψₖ(t),    t ∈ [0,T]

  with {Zₖ} i.i.d. N(0,1), converging in L²(Ω×[0,T]) and uniformly in
  t almost surely.

Sample-path regularity: Paley–Wiener

  Since the spectral measure is supported on [−Ω, Ω], the Paley–Wiener
  theorem identifies the sample paths as elements of

    PW_Ω = { f ∈ L²(ℝ) : f̂(ω) = 0 for |ω| > Ω }

  Every f ∈ PW_Ω extends to an entire function of exponential type at
  most Ω, satisfying |f(z)| ≤ Ce^{Ω|Im z|} for z ∈ ℂ.  The sample
  paths of band-limited white noise are almost surely analytic on ℝ and
  extend holomorphically to ℂ.

  All mean-square derivatives exist:

    E[ξ⁽ᵏ⁾(t)²] = (1/2π) ∫_{−Ω}^{Ω} ω²ᵏ dω
                  = Ω²ᵏ⁺¹ / ((2k+1)π) < ∞,    ∀k ≥ 0

Shannon–Nyquist reconstruction.

  With tₙ = nπ/Ω

    ξ(t) = ∑_{n=−∞}^∞ ξ(tₙ) · sin(Ωt − nπ) / (Ωt − nπ)

  converging in L²(ℝ) and uniformly on ℝ almost surely.  The Nyquist
  sample covariance is

    E[ξ(tₙ) ξ(tₘ)] = (Ω/π) δₙₘ

  so the samples are i.i.d. N(0, Ω/π), and ξ is recovered from its
  discrete i.i.d. skeleton via sinc interpolation.

10. The Ω → ∞ limit: approximate identities and the
    Riemann–Lebesgue lemma
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

As Ω → ∞:

  sin(Ωτ)/(πτ) → δ(τ)  distributionally
  R(0) = Ω/π → ∞
  Nyquist spacing π/Ω → 0,
  P_Ω → I  strongly in L²(ℝ)

The band-limited noise converges in law, as a generalized process, to the
cylindrical white noise.  The trace-class framework collapses: K–L
eigenvalues all approach 1 and their sum diverges, the Gaussian measure
migrates from L² to S′, and sample-path analyticity is lost.

Approximate identities.

  The family {φ_Ω}_{Ω>0} defined by φ_Ω(τ) = sin(Ωτ)/(πτ) is an
  approximate identity (approximation to the identity) for convolution
  on Lᵖ(ℝ).  The Dirac delta δ is the identity element for convolution:
  f ∗ δ = f for any distribution f.  No element of L¹(ℝ) achieves this —
  the convolution algebra L¹ has no identity element.  The family {φ_Ω}
  approximates δ in the sense that f ∗ φ_Ω → f as Ω → ∞.  In the
  Fourier domain: φ̂_Ω(ω) = 𝟏_{[−Ω,Ω]}(ω) → 1 for every fixed ω, and
  Plancherel gives L² convergence of f ∗ φ_Ω → f directly.

Good kernels (Stein–Shakarchi).

  The standard term good kernel for an approximate identity originates in
  Stein and Shakarchi's Fourier Analysis: An Introduction (Princeton
  Lectures in Analysis, Vol. I).  A family {K_λ} is a good kernel if:

    (1) ∫_ℝ K_λ(t) dt = 1  for all λ
    (2) sup_λ ‖K_λ‖_{L¹} < ∞
    (3) For every δ > 0, ∫_{|t|>δ} |K_λ(t)| dt → 0  as λ → ∞

  The main theorem: if {K_λ} is a family of good kernels and f ∈ L¹,
  then f ∗ K_λ → f pointwise at every point of continuity of f, and
  uniformly whenever f is continuous.  The Fejér kernel satisfies all
  three conditions and is the canonical example.

  The sinc family φ_Ω fails condition (2):

    ‖φ_Ω‖_{L¹} ∼ (2/π) log Ω → ∞

  a logarithmic divergence identical to the blowup of the L¹ norm of the
  Dirichlet kernel on the circle.  Consequently {φ_Ω} is not a good kernel
  in Stein's sense.  The L² convergence f ∗ φ_Ω → f still holds for all
  f ∈ L² by Plancherel, and pointwise convergence holds at every Lebesgue
  point.  It is the uniform and L¹ convergences that the logarithmic growth
  obstructs — the same pathology responsible for the existence of continuous
  functions whose Fourier partial sums diverge pointwise.

Riemann–Lebesgue lemma.

  Condition (3) for the sinc family is verified by the Riemann–Lebesgue
  lemma.  The lemma states: for any f ∈ L¹(ℝⁿ),

    f̂(ξ) = ∫_{ℝⁿ} f(x) e^{−iξ·x} dx  →  0    as |ξ| → ∞

  The Fourier transform f̂ is continuous and belongs to C₀(ℝⁿ), and the
  map f ↦ f̂ is a bounded linear operator L¹ → C₀ with operator norm 1.

  For the tail decay of φ_Ω: fixing δ > 0, the function

    g_δ(t) = (πt)⁻¹ 𝟏_{|t|>δ}

  is in L¹(ℝ), and

    ∫_{|t|>δ} sin(Ωt)/(πt) dt = ĝ_δ(−Ω) → 0    as Ω → ∞

  by the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma applied to g_δ.  This is the analytic
  content of condition (3) for the sinc approximate identity.

11. The Cameron–Martin space and regularity
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Every centered Gaussian measure on a Banach or Hilbert space has an
associated reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), also called the
Cameron–Martin space.  This space encodes the directions along which
translations have finite energy.

For the Gaussian measure μ = N(0, Q) on a Hilbert space H, the
Cameron–Martin space is

  H_Q = Q¹ᐟ²(H),    ⟨u, v⟩_{H_Q} = ⟨Q⁻¹ᐟ² u, Q⁻¹ᐟ² v⟩_H

Since Q is trace-class, Q¹ᐟ² is Hilbert–Schmidt, and the embedding
H_Q ↪ H is compact.  The Cameron–Martin theorem asserts that translation
by h ∈ H is absolutely continuous with respect to μ if and only if
h ∈ H_Q, with Radon–Nikodym derivative

  d(μ ∘ T_h⁻¹)/dμ (x)
    = exp(⟨Q⁻¹ᐟ² h, Q⁻¹ᐟ² x⟩_H − ½ ‖Q⁻¹ᐟ² h‖²_H)

Draws from μ = N(0, Q) almost surely lie outside H_Q, since H_Q has
μ-measure zero.

For Brownian motion on [0,∞), the Cameron–Martin space is

  H₀¹(0,∞) = { f : f(0) = 0, f absolutely continuous, f′ ∈ L²(0,∞) }

and the inclusion H₀¹ ↪ L² is Hilbert–Schmidt on bounded intervals.
This matches the general principle: the RKHS always exists, but whether
the Gaussian measure lives on the ambient Hilbert space depends on whether
the embedding of the RKHS is sufficiently compact (trace-class /
Hilbert–Schmidt) relative to that space.

In the white-noise setting on S′(ℝ), the RKHS is realized via the
L²-structure on the index space of the isonormal process.  The roughness
of sample paths is quantified by how many derivatives or smoothing
operations are needed to map the distribution-valued process into a
function-valued space — the analytic content behind the need to enlarge
spaces and work with generalized random fields.
