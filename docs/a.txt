   THE RIEMANN–HILBERT VIEW OF PADÉ APPROXIMATION AND GLOBAL ANALYTIC
   CONTINUATION, WITH APPLICATION TO THE FRACTIONAL RICCATI EQUATION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


ABSTRACT
────────

The diagonal Padé approximant of a function analytic at the origin admits
a representation as the solution to a 2×2 matrix Riemann–Hilbert problem.
This formulation replaces the local derivative-matching of Taylor
approximation with the global reconstruction of a spectral measure from
its moments.  The convergence domain is determined not by the distance to
the nearest singularity, but by the maximal region on which the associated
Riemann–Hilbert factorization remains solvable, a region governed by the
equilibrium measure of an extremal contour selected by the S-property.
The poles of the approximant are the eigenvalues of a truncated Jacobi
operator, and their condensation along the equilibrium contour is the
signature of the underlying Riemann surface.  The general theory is
presented in the form of theorems, corollaries, and limit statements,
each conclusion proved under explicitly stated conditions, and the
framework is applied to the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati
equation with complex coefficients.  The application section develops
the Müntz spectral solution, derives a Gamma-ratio convolution recurrence
for the Puiseux coefficients, establishes a strictly positive radius of
convergence by a Banach fixed-point argument, develops the Mittag-Leffler
tree expansion that reproduces the Müntz recurrence exactly at every
order, constructs the diagonal Padé resummation in the variable z = t^μ,
proves meromorphy of the transformed solution on ℂ by a classical
Riccati majorization, establishes the non-explosion condition on compact
intervals, and thereby obtains uniform global convergence on the positive
real axis with a computable a-posteriori error bound.


§1. INTRODUCTION
────────────────

Taylor approximation asks for the polynomial that matches the derivatives
of a function at a single point.  It is local, and its radius of
convergence is the distance to the nearest singularity.  Padé
approximation asks for the spectral measure that generates the given
moment sequence, and for the Stieltjes transform of that measure.  It is
global, and its convergence domain is the maximal region on which the
Riemann–Hilbert factorization exists.

The distinction is not merely one of degree.  A Taylor polynomial of
degree 2n and a diagonal Padé approximant [n/n] both consume the same
2n+1 coefficients, yet they encode fundamentally different information.
The Taylor polynomial encodes derivatives at a point; the Padé approximant
encodes moments of a measure.  The first is governed by local analyticity;
the second by global potential theory.  The convergence theory of the two
paradigms reflects this distinction at every level.

The paper proceeds as follows.  §2 presents the Fokas–Its–Kitaev
Riemann–Hilbert formulation for diagonal Padé approximants.  §3 describes
Stahl's geometric theorem and the selection of the equilibrium contour by
the S-property, together with the Deift–Zhou steepest descent asymptotic.
§4 explains why convergence holds in capacity rather than uniformly,
through the mechanism of Froissart doublets, and gives the non-explosion
condition under which uniform convergence is recovered.  §5 develops the
non-explosion principle as a spectral budget constraint, drawing the
deterministic–stochastic analogy to self-exciting point processes and
Gaussian sample-path regularity.  §6 identifies the Padé table with the
Toda lattice and the Jacobi operator resolvent, including the non-
Hermitian extension via pseudo-spectrum.  §7 contrasts orthogonal
polynomial zeros with Fekete points and gives bulk and edge residue
estimates.  §8 discusses the Borel plane and Padé–Borel resummation for
factorially divergent series.  §9 develops the application to the
constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation through the Müntz
spectral method, the Mittag-Leffler tree expansion, the classical
Riccati majorization of meromorphy, and the Padé resummation in
z = t^μ.  §10 summarizes the essential difference between the two
paradigms.


§2. THE RIEMANN–HILBERT FORMULATION
───────────────────────────────────

The foundational equivalence between diagonal Padé approximants and
matrix Riemann–Hilbert problems is due to Fokas, Its, and Kitaev; it
recasts a problem of rational approximation as a problem of matrix
factorization on a contour.

Definition 2.1 (Cauchy transform).  Let Σ ⊂ ℂ be a finite union of
smooth oriented arcs, and let w: Σ → ℂ be a measurable weight with
∫_Σ |w(s)| |ds| < ∞.  The Cauchy transform of w on Σ is the function

    f(z) = ∫_Σ w(s)/(z-s) ds,    z ∈ ℂ ∖ Σ.                           (2.1)

Definition 2.2 (Diagonal Padé approximant).  Let f be analytic at
infinity with formal expansion f(z) = Σ_{k=0}^∞ a_k z^{-k-1}.  The
diagonal Padé approximant [n/n]_f of order n is the unique rational
function P_n/Q_n with deg P_n < n, deg Q_n = n, and Q_n monic, satisfying

    f(z) − P_n(z)/Q_n(z) = O(z^{-2n-1})    as z → ∞,                  (2.2)

whenever the underlying Hankel determinant τ_n = det(a_{i+j})_{0≤i,j≤n-1}
is nonzero.

Theorem 2.3 (Fokas–Its–Kitaev representation).  Let f be the Cauchy
transform (2.1) of a weight w on a fixed contour Σ, and assume the Hankel
determinants τ_1,…,τ_n are nonzero.  Then there exists a unique 2×2
matrix-valued function Y: ℂ ∖ Σ → GL₂(ℂ) satisfying:

(i)   Y is analytic on ℂ ∖ Σ;
(ii)  Y has continuous boundary values Y_± on Σ related by the jump

      Y_+(s) = Y_-(s) ⎛1  2πi w(s)⎞,  s ∈ Σ;                          (2.3)
                      ⎝0      1    ⎠

(iii) Y has the asymptotic behavior at infinity

      Y(z) = (I + Y_1/z + Y_2/z² + ⋯) ⎛z^n     0   ⎞,  z → ∞.         (2.4)
                                     ⎝ 0    z^{-n}⎠

The entries of Y encode the diagonal Padé approximant via

    Y_{11}(z) = Q_n(z),  Y_{12}(z) = ∫_Σ Q_n(s)w(s)/(s-z) ds,         (2.5)

and the approximant itself is

    [n/n]_f(z) = P_n(z)/Q_n(z) = Y_{12}(z)/Y_{11}(z).                 (2.6)

Corollary 2.4 (Orthogonality).  The denominator Q_n is the monic
orthogonal polynomial of degree n for the moment functional
𝓛[p] = ∫_Σ p(s)w(s)ds:

    ∫_Σ Q_n(s) s^k w(s) ds = 0,  k = 0,1,…,n−1.                      (2.7)

Remark 2.5 (Limitation: the contour must be given).  Theorem 2.3 requires
a fixed contour Σ.  For functions specified only by a power series, with
branch cuts whose location is unknown, the contour is not given; it must
be determined as part of the problem.  The Riemann–Hilbert formulation in
the form stated above becomes exact only after the equilibrium contour
has been selected by the procedure of §3.


§3. THE CONTOUR IS DETERMINED, NOT GIVEN
────────────────────────────────────────

For functions whose singular structure is not specified in advance, the
appropriate contour is selected by an extremal principle in logarithmic
potential theory.  This is the content of Stahl's geometric theorem.

Definition 3.1 (Stahl class).  A function f analytic at infinity belongs
to the Stahl class 𝓢 if it admits multi-valued meromorphic continuation
to a domain ℂ̄ ∖ E, where E is a compact set of logarithmic capacity
zero.

Definition 3.2 (Admissible compact).  A compact set K ⊂ ℂ is admissible
for f ∈ 𝓢 if ℂ̄ ∖ K is a domain on which f admits single-valued
meromorphic continuation.

Theorem 3.3 (Stahl's geometric theorem).  For every f ∈ 𝓢, there exists
a unique admissible compact Δ_f with the following properties:

(i)   ℂ̄ ∖ Δ_f is a connected domain D_f;
(ii)  f has single-valued meromorphic continuation to D_f;
(iii) cap(Δ_f) ≤ cap(K) for every other admissible compact K.

The set Δ_f is called the Stahl compact or S-compact of f.

Theorem 3.4 (The S-property).  Let g(z,∞) denote the Green's function of
D_f with pole at infinity.  The arcs of Δ_f satisfy the symmetry
condition

    ∂g/∂n_+(z) = ∂g/∂n_-(z),  z ∈ Δ_f ∖ {endpoints},                 (3.1)

where n_± are the two unit normals to the arc.

Remark 3.5 (Variational interpretation).  The S-property is the
Euler–Lagrange equation of a min-max variational problem.  Among
admissible compacts in a fixed homotopy class, the minimum of cap(K) is
attained at Δ_f, and the harmonic measure exerts equal pull on both sides
of each arc.

Theorem 3.6 (Stahl's convergence theorem).  Let f ∈ 𝓢 with Stahl
compact Δ_f and complementary domain D_f.  The diagonal Padé approximants
converge to f in capacity on compact subsets of D_f ∖ 𝓟_f, where 𝓟_f
is the pole set of f.  For every compact K ⊂ D_f ∖ 𝓟_f and every ε > 0,

    lim_{n→∞} cap{z ∈ K : |f(z) − [n/n]_f(z)| > ε} = 0.              (3.2)

Theorem 3.7 (Deift–Zhou asymptotic).  Under the hypotheses of
Theorem 3.6, with the additional regularity that w is analytic in a
neighborhood of Δ_f and the endpoints exhibit square-root behavior of the
equilibrium density, nonlinear steepest descent yields

    [n/n]_f(z) = f(z)(1 + O(e^{-n g(z,∞)}))                          (3.3)

uniformly on compact subsets of D_f bounded away from Δ_f ∪ 𝓟_f.

Corollary 3.8 (Exponential rate of convergence).

    lim sup_{n→∞} |f(z) − [n/n]_f(z)|^{1/n} ≤ e^{-g(z,∞)},
                                       z ∈ D_f ∖ 𝓟_f.                (3.4)

The level sets {g(z,∞) = c} are loci of equal convergence rate, and
Δ_f = {g = 0}.

Remark 3.9 (Comparison with Taylor).  The Taylor polynomial of degree 2n
converges geometrically only inside the disk of convergence, with rate
|z|/r where r is the distance to the nearest singularity.  The Padé rate
e^{-g(z,∞)} extends to all of D_f, generally a much larger domain.
The two rates agree only inside the disk of convergence, where
g(z,∞) ∼ −log|z| + const.


§4. FROISSART DOUBLETS AND CONVERGENCE IN CAPACITY
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

The convergence statement of Theorem 3.6 is in capacity, not uniformly.
The reason is the appearance of spurious poles, called Froissart doublets,
that wander through the domain of holomorphy without condensing on the
Stahl compact.

Definition 4.1 (Froissart doublet).  A Froissart doublet of [n/n]_f is a
pole z_j^{(n)} ∈ D_f ∖ 𝓟_f accompanied by a zero ζ_j^{(n)} at distance
|z_j^{(n)} − ζ_j^{(n)}| = O(e^{-cn}) for some c > 0, with residue

    Res([n/n]_f, z_j^{(n)}) = O(e^{-cn})  as n → ∞.                  (4.1)

Proposition 4.2 (Boundedness of doublet count).  For f ∈ 𝓢, the number
of Froissart doublets of [n/n]_f in any compact K ⊂ D_f ∖ 𝓟_f is O(1)
as n → ∞, but their location may oscillate erratically with n.

Definition 4.3 (Non-Explosion Condition).  A function f ∈ 𝓢 satisfies
the Non-Explosion Condition on a compact K ⊂ D_f ∖ 𝓟_f if the number of
poles of [n/n]_f in K is uniformly bounded in n.

Theorem 4.4 (Uniform convergence theorem).  If f ∈ 𝓢 satisfies the
Non-Explosion Condition on a compact K ⊂ D_f ∖ 𝓟_f, then [n/n]_f → f
uniformly on K.  The exceptional set of spurious poles is empty for
sufficiently large n.

Remark 4.5.  The Non-Explosion Condition is restrictive for generic
f ∈ 𝓢 because Froissart doublets, though O(1) in number, could in
principle visit every compact infinitely often.  For meromorphic
functions with well-separated poles and a regular Padé table, the
condition holds on every compact disjoint from the pole set; this is the
situation of §9.7.


§5. THE NON-EXPLOSION PRINCIPLE AND SPECTRAL BUDGETS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Non-Explosion Condition is not merely a technical hypothesis invented
to salvage uniform convergence.  It is the deterministic manifestation of
a universal compactness principle that appears across probability theory,
potential theory, and spectral analysis.  The condition states that on
any compact region of holomorphy, the rational approximant can support
only a uniformly bounded number of singularities.  In other words, the
pole intensity does not diverge; the approximant does not explode.

Consider a self-exciting point process in which each event increases the
instantaneous probability of further events.  The process is said to be
non-explosive if the intensity remains finite on every bounded time
interval, so that the number of events in any compact set is almost
surely finite.  The analogy is exact: for Padé approximants, each pole
is an event, and the Non-Explosion Condition is the statement that the
point process of spurious singularities has finite intensity on every
compact subset of the domain of holomorphy.  Just as a self-exciting
process without immigration cannot accumulate infinitely many events in
finite time without divergent intensity, a diagonal Padé sequence cannot
deposit infinitely many poles in a compact region of regularity without
violating the spectral budget.

The connection to Gaussian regularity is equally direct.  A stationary
Gaussian process has sample paths whose Hölder regularity is governed by
the tail of its spectral measure via the Wiener–Khinchin theorem.  If the
spectral density decays like |ξ|^{-2H-1}, the paths are Hölder-(H−ε).
The tail of the spectral measure is the wiggling budget: it allocates a
finite amount of high-frequency energy per unit of time, and that budget
caps the local oscillation.  The Jacobi operator that appears in the
Padé table (§6) is the discrete cousin of the covariance operator of a
Gaussian process.  The Non-Explosion Condition—bounding the number of
poles of the truncated operator in K—is the deterministic analog of
bounding the metric entropy or the covering numbers of the index set.
It says the truncated spectral resolution cannot deposit infinitely many
point masses in a bounded region.

There is a deeper potential-theoretic unity.  Stahl's convergence is in
capacity, meaning it fails only on sets of logarithmic capacity zero.
In probability theory, sets of capacity zero are precisely the polar sets
for Brownian motion and for a broad class of Gaussian processes—sets
that the process almost surely never visits.  This is not a coincidence;
both theories draw from the same logarithmic potential reservoir.  The
Non-Explosion Condition is what allows one to strengthen convergence off
a polar set to uniform convergence everywhere on the compact, just as
Kolmogorov's continuity lemma upgrades measure-theoretic regularity to
pathwise Hölder continuity by controlling the tail of the increments.

The pole is the deterministic singularity; the high-frequency Fourier
mode is the stochastic one.  The budget is the same.

    cap(Δ_f) < ∞  ⟺  ∫_{|ξ|>R} dμ(ξ) → 0  as R → ∞.                 (5.1)

In the fractional Riccati application (§9), this synthesis becomes
concrete.  The equation
    D^μ y = c₀ + c₁ y + c₂ y²
with μ ∈ (0,1] yields a solution y(t) whose natural regularity scale is
Hölder-μ, exactly matching the Puiseux exponent μ of its series in t.
The Müntz substitution z = t^μ renders the solution analytic at the
origin; the Padé resummation in z then inherits the Non-Explosion
Condition on every compact subinterval of [0,∞), preserving the
regularity of the approximant to match the regularity of the underlying
Volterra solution.  The pole density is controlled, so the rational
approximant remains as well-behaved as the solution itself.


§6. THE JACOBI OPERATOR AND THE TODA LATTICE
────────────────────────────────────────────

The Padé problem admits a third equivalent formulation, in addition to
rational approximation and Riemann–Hilbert factorization.  This third
formulation is operator-theoretic: the Padé approximant is the resolvent
of a truncated Jacobi operator.

Definition 6.1 (Hankel matrix and tau function).  For a sequence
{a_k}_{k≥0}, the Hankel matrix of order n is

    H^{(n)} = (a_{i+j})_{0≤i,j≤n-1},                                  (6.1)

and its determinant τ_n = det H^{(n)} is the tau function.

Theorem 6.2 (Toda lattice structure).  Assume τ_n ≠ 0 for all n ≥ 1.
The monic orthogonal polynomials Q_n for the moment functional defined by
{a_k} satisfy the three-term recurrence

    Q_{n+1}(z) = (z − b_n) Q_n(z) − a_n² Q_{n-1}(z),
    Q_{-1} = 0,  Q_0 = 1.                                            (6.2)

Under the spectral evolution dμ_t(x) = e^{tx} dμ(x), the recurrence
coefficients evolve according to the Toda equations

    ḃ_n = a_n² − a_{n-1}²,  ȧ_n = a_n (b_{n+1} − b_n).               (6.3)

The flow is isospectral.

Definition 6.3 (Jacobi operator).  The infinite Jacobi operator
associated to {a_n, b_n} is the tridiagonal matrix

              ⎛ b₀  a₀  0   ⋯ ⎞
              ⎜ a₀  b₁  a₁  ⋱ ⎟
    J_∞   =   ⎜ 0   a₁  b₂  ⋱ ⎟                                       (6.4)
              ⎝ ⋮   ⋱   ⋱   ⋱ ⎠

acting on ℓ²(ℕ₀).  Its n×n principal truncation is
J_n = [J_∞]_{0≤i,j≤n-1}.

Theorem 6.4 (Resolvent representation, self-adjoint case).  Assume the
moment functional is positive, so that J_∞ is self-adjoint and bounded
with spectral measure μ supported on a compact subset of ℝ.  Then

    f(z) = ⟨e₀, (J_∞ − zI)^{-1} e₀⟩ = ∫ dμ(x)/(x−z),                 (6.5)

and

    [n/n]_f(z) = ⟨e₀, (J_n − zI)^{-1} e₀⟩
               = Σ_{j=1}^n |⟨e₀, ψ_j^{(n)}⟩|² / (λ_j^{(n)} − z),     (6.6)

where {λ_j^{(n)}, ψ_j^{(n)}}_{j=1}^n are the eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs
of J_n.

Corollary 6.5 (Poles as eigenvalues).  The poles of [n/n]_f are precisely
the eigenvalues of J_n, and the residues are the squared first components
of the corresponding normalized eigenvectors.

Theorem 6.6 (Strong resolvent convergence).  In the setting of Theorem
6.4, J_n → J_∞ in the strong resolvent sense:

    lim_{n→∞} (J_n − zI)^{-1} ξ = (J_∞ − zI)^{-1} ξ
         ∀ ξ ∈ ℓ²,  z ∉ spec(J_∞).                                    (6.7)

Consequently, [n/n]_f(z) → f(z) for every z ∈ ℂ ∖ spec(J_∞), locally
uniformly.

Corollary 6.7 (Self-adjoint convergence domain).  For real measures, the
convergence domain of the diagonal Padé approximation coincides with
ℂ ∖ spec(J_∞), and Δ_f = spec(J_∞).

Remark 6.8 (Non-Hermitian extension).  For complex measures and functions
with branch points off the real axis, J_∞ is non-Hermitian and Theorem
6.6 does not apply directly.  The relevant convergence theory uses the
pseudo-spectrum

    spec_ε(J_∞) = {z ∈ ℂ : ‖(J_∞ − zI)^{-1}‖ ≥ ε^{-1}}.               (6.8)

Theorem 6.9 (Pseudo-spectral limit, regular case).  Suppose the
recurrence coefficients {a_n, b_n} admit a regular asymptotic expansion
in 1/n and the limiting density is supported on a finite union of
analytic arcs.  Then

    lim_{ε→0} ∂spec_ε(J_∞) = Δ_f.                                     (6.9)

Remark 6.10 (Beyond the regular case).  In less regular non-Hermitian
settings, the limiting object of (6.9) is a quadratic differential
trajectory in the sense of Strebel; its identification with Δ_f requires
additional symmetry conditions and is established case-by-case.


§7. POLE CONDENSATION: ORTHOGONAL POLYNOMIALS AND FEKETE POINTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Definition 7.1 (Fekete points).  The n-th Fekete points of a compact
K ⊂ ℂ are the maximizers

    {z_1^{F,n},…,z_n^{F,n}} = arg max_{(z_1,…,z_n)∈K^n} ∏_{i<j} |z_i − z_j|.
                                                                      (7.1)

Theorem 7.2 (Equilibrium limit of Fekete points).  The normalized
counting measures of the Fekete points of K converge weak-∗ to the
equilibrium measure μ_K:

    (1/n) Σ_{j=1}^n δ_{z_j^{F,n}} ⇀ μ_K   as n → ∞.                  (7.2)

Theorem 7.3 (Equilibrium limit of orthogonal polynomial zeros).  For
f ∈ 𝓢 with Stahl compact Δ_f, the normalized counting measures of the
zeros of Q_n converge weak-∗ to the equilibrium measure μ_{Δ_f}:

    (1/n) Σ_{j=1}^n δ_{ζ_j^{(n)}} ⇀ μ_{Δ_f}.                         (7.3)

Remark 7.4 (Distinct objects, common limit).  Theorems 7.2 and 7.3 share
a conclusion but describe different sequences.  Fekete points are
extremal for discrete logarithmic energy and have no recursive
relationship across n.  Orthogonal polynomial zeros are determined by the
spectral truncation of the Jacobi operator and are linked across n by
the three-term recurrence (6.2).  Only their normalized counting measures
agree in the limit.

Theorem 7.5 (Bulk residue estimate).  Let f ∈ 𝓢 satisfy the regularity
conditions of Theorem 3.7.  For poles z_j^{(n)} approaching the interior
of Δ_f,

    Res([n/n]_f, z_j^{(n)}) = P_n(z_j^{(n)}) / Q_n'(z_j^{(n)})
                             = O(1/n),                                (7.4)

uniformly for poles bounded away from the endpoints.

Theorem 7.6 (Edge residue scaling).  For poles within an O(n^{-2/3})
neighborhood of an endpoint of Δ_f, the rescaled residues satisfy

    n^{1/3} Res([n/n]_f, z_j^{(n)}) → ρ_{Ai}(ξ_j),
    ξ_j = n^{2/3}(z_j^{(n)} − endpoint),                             (7.5)

where ρ_{Ai} is determined by the Airy kernel.  The bulk 1/n law is
replaced by an Airy-type n^{-1/3} decay at the edge, the universal
Painlevé II scaling of random matrix theory.

Corollary 7.7 (Pole-zero cancellation on contours avoiding the cut).
For any rectifiable contour γ ⊂ D_f ∖ 𝓟_f enclosing a region disjoint
from Δ_f,

    lim_{n→∞} (1/2πi) ∮_γ [n/n]_f(z) dz = (1/2πi) ∮_γ f(z) dz.       (7.6)


§8. THE BOREL PLANE AND RESUMMATION
────────────────────────────────────

Definition 8.1 (Borel transform).  For a formal power series
f̂(z) = Σ_{n=0}^∞ a_n z^n, the Borel transform is

    B(t) = Σ_{n=0}^∞ (a_n / n!) t^n.                                  (8.1)

Theorem 8.2 (Inverse Borel transform).  If B(t) admits analytic
continuation to a sector |arg t| < θ with at most exponential growth, the
Laplace integral

    f_{Borel}(z) = ∫_0^∞ e^{-t/z} B(t) dt                             (8.2)

defines a holomorphic function in a sector of the z-plane.

Theorem 8.3 (Padé–Borel resummation).  Let f̂ have factorial coefficient
growth a_n ∼ n!, and assume B(t) is in Stahl's class with no positive
real singularities.  The composition

    f_{P-B}(z) = ∫_0^∞ e^{-t/z} [n/n]_B(t) dt                         (8.3)

converges in capacity to f_{Borel}(z) on the Borel domain of regularity,
and assigns a finite value to a series whose original radius of
convergence was zero.

Remark 8.4 (Resummation versus continuation).  Padé–Borel resummation is
distinct from analytic continuation: the latter extends a function across
a singularity from a region where it already exists, while the former
assigns a value to a divergent series.  The Padé approximant in (8.3)
approximates the measure in the Borel plane whose Laplace transform is
the physical quantity.


§9. APPLICATION: THE CONSTANT-COEFFICIENT FRACTIONAL RICCATI EQUATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Consider the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation

    D^μ y(t) = c₀ + c₁ y(t) + c₂ y(t)²,  I^{1−μ} y(0) = 0,           (9.1)

where D^μ is the Caputo derivative of order μ ∈ (0,1] and the
coefficients c₀, c₁, c₂ ∈ ℂ are arbitrary.  The Müntz spectral solution,
the Mittag-Leffler tree expansion, the meromorphy proof via classical
Riccati majorization, the Padé resummation in z = t^μ, and the global
convergence theorem with computable error bound are developed below.


§9.1. Fractional calculus and the Müntz basis
─────────────────────────────────────────────

Definition 9.1 (Riemann–Liouville integral and Caputo derivative).
For r > 0 and f ∈ L¹_{loc}[0,T], the Riemann–Liouville integral is

    I^r f(t) = (1/Γ(r)) ∫_0^t (t−s)^{r−1} f(s) ds.                    (9.2)

For μ ∈ (0,1) and f such that I^{1−μ}f ∈ C¹[0,T], the Caputo derivative
is

    D^μ f(t) = (d/dt)(I^{1−μ} f(t)) − f(0)/(Γ(1−μ)) t^{-μ}.           (9.3)

The fundamental relations are I^μ D^μ f(t) = f(t) − f(0) and
D^μ I^μ f(t) = f(t).

Proposition 9.2 (Action on power functions).  For s > −1 and r > 0,

    I^r t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+r+1) · t^{s+r}.                              (9.4)

For μ ∈ (0,1) and s > 0,

    D^μ t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+1−μ) · t^{s−μ}.                              (9.5)

Proof.  For (9.4), the substitution u = t x in the integral defining
I^r t^s yields a Beta function evaluation.  For (9.5), observe that s > 0
implies f(0) = 0, so the correction term in (9.3) vanishes; differentiating
I^{1−μ} t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+2−μ) · t^{s+1−μ} and applying
Γ(s+2−μ) = (s+1−μ)Γ(s+1−μ) gives the result.  □

Remark 9.3 (The Caputo derivative of a constant).  The hypothesis s > 0
in (9.5) cannot be relaxed.  For s = 0, the correction term in (9.3)
cancels the formal power t^{-μ}/Γ(1−μ) and yields D^μ(1) = 0, consistent
with the convention that constants have zero Caputo derivative.

Definition 9.4 (Müntz lattice).  Fix μ ∈ (0,1] and T > 0.  The Müntz
lattice is

    𝓜 = span{1, t^μ, t^{2μ}, t^{3μ}, …}.                            (9.6)

Theorem 9.5 (Closure of the Müntz lattice).  For every k ≥ 1 and
r ∈ μℕ:

    D^μ t^{kμ} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k−1)μ+1) · t^{(k−1)μ},                    (9.7)
    I^r t^{kμ} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ(kμ+r+1) · t^{kμ+r},                        (9.8)
    t^{jμ} · t^{kμ} = t^{(j+k)μ}.                                     (9.9)

Consequently D^μ 𝓜 ⊂ 𝓜, I^r 𝓜 ⊂ 𝓜 for r ∈ μℕ, and 𝓜 is closed
under multiplication.

Remark 9.6 (Why the Müntz lattice is the natural basis).  The three
closure properties of Theorem 9.5—under fractional differentiation,
fractional integration of order μ, and pointwise multiplication—make the
Müntz lattice the canonical basis for any constant-coefficient fractional
ODE with polynomial nonlinearity.  The Müntz–Tau substitution preserves
the algebraic structure of the equation.  Ordinary Taylor series in t
fail because they are not closed under D^μ for non-integer μ.  Note that
I^{1-μ} does not preserve 𝓜 for generic μ; however, the Volterra
reformulation (9.10) requires only I^μ, which does.

Remark 9.6′ (The Leibniz obstruction).  The Caputo derivative D^μ does
NOT satisfy the Leibniz product rule for μ ∈ (0,1).  On the Müntz basis,

    D^μ(t^{jμ}·t^{kμ}) = Γ((j+k)μ+1)/Γ((j+k−1)μ+1) · t^{(j+k−1)μ},

whereas

    (D^μ t^{jμ})·t^{kμ} + t^{jμ}·(D^μ t^{kμ})
       = [Γ(jμ+1)/Γ((j−1)μ+1) + Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k−1)μ+1)] · t^{(j+k−1)μ},

and the two Gamma-ratio sums do NOT coincide except at μ = 1.
Consequently, no substitution of the form y = φ(w₁,w₂) where (w₁,w₂)
solves a linear Caputo system D^μ 𝐰 = A𝐰 can linearize the fractional
Riccati equation for μ ∈ (0,1).  In particular, the Cole–Hopf ansatz
y = −w₂/(c₂ w₁) with 𝐰 = E_{μ,1}(Az)𝐰(0) fails for μ ∈ (0,1).  All
nonlinear manipulations in what follows are performed through the
Volterra convolution (9.10), where the multiplication is under an
integral and no product rule is invoked.


§9.2. The series solution and Volterra representation
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Applying I^μ to (9.1) and using I^μ D^μ y = y − y(0) = y yields the
equivalent Volterra integral equation

    y(t) = (1/Γ(μ)) ∫_0^t (t−s)^{μ−1} (c₀ + c₁ y(s) + c₂ y(s)²) ds.  (9.10)

Remark 9.7 (Hölder regularity at the origin).  Evaluating (9.10) at t = 0
gives y(0) = 0.  Near the origin,

    y(t) = c₀/Γ(μ+1) · t^μ + O(t^{2μ}),                              (9.11)

so y is Hölder continuous of order μ but not differentiable at 0.  The
Hölder exponent μ is the natural regularity scale of the problem.

Theorem 9.8 (Müntz–Tau Puiseux series solution).  There exist R > 0 and
a sequence (a_k)_{k≥1} ⊂ ℂ such that

    y(t) = Σ_{k=1}^∞ a_k t^{kμ},  |t| < R^{1/μ},                    (9.12)

where the coefficients satisfy the Gamma-ratio convolution recurrence

    a₁ = c₀/Γ(μ+1),                                                   (9.13)
    a_{k+1} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1)
              · (c₁ a_k + c₂ Σ_{j+ℓ=k, 1≤j,ℓ≤k−1} a_j a_ℓ),  k ≥ 1. (9.14)

Proof.  Existence of R > 0 is established in Theorem 9.10 below by a
Banach fixed-point argument.  Granting analyticity of y in z = t^μ on
|z| < R, write y(t) = Σ_{k≥1} a_k t^{kμ}.  By (9.9),

    y(t)² = Σ_{m=2}^∞ b_m t^{mμ},
    b_m = Σ_{j+ℓ=m, 1≤j,ℓ≤m−1} a_j a_ℓ.                              (9.15)

Substituting into (9.10), splitting the integral, and applying (9.4) with
s = kμ, r = μ:

    ∫_0^t (t−s)^{μ−1} s^{kμ} ds
        = Γ(μ) · Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) · t^{(k+1)μ}.                   (9.16)

Matching the coefficient of t^{nμ} for each n ≥ 1 yields (9.13) for n = 1
and (9.14) after reindexing n ↦ k+1.  □

Remark 9.9 (Quadratic convolution at low orders).  For k = 1, the
convolution sum in (9.14) is empty.  Hence

    a₂ = Γ(μ+1)/Γ(2μ+1) · c₁ a₁.                                     (9.17)

The quadratic term first contributes at k = 2:

    a₃ = Γ(2μ+1)/Γ(3μ+1) · (c₁ a₂ + c₂ a₁²).                         (9.18)


§9.3. Radius of convergence and generic finiteness
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

Theorem 9.10 (Strictly positive Puiseux radius and Cauchy bound).  Let

    A = |c₀| + |c₁| + |c₂|,  B = |c₁| + 2|c₂|,                        (9.19)

and define

    T₀^μ = min(Γ(μ+1)/(2A), Γ(μ+1)/(4B)) > 0.                        (9.20)

(i) There exists a unique solution y ∈ C[0,T₀] of (9.10) with
    ‖y‖_∞ ≤ 1.  This solution is analytic in z = t^μ on {|z| < T₀^μ},
    so the Puiseux series (9.12) has radius of convergence R ≥ T₀^μ > 0.

(ii) For every 0 < r < R, with g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) and
     M(r) = sup_{|z|=r} |g(z)|,

     |a_k| ≤ M(r)/r^k  ∀ k ≥ 1.                                      (9.21)

Proof.  Define 𝓑₁ = {y ∈ C[0,T₀] : ‖y‖_∞ ≤ 1} with the supremum norm
and the operator

    Φ(y)(t) = I^μ(c₀ + c₁ y + c₂ y²)(t).                             (9.22)

For y ∈ 𝓑₁, the integrand is bounded by A, and (9.4) gives

    |Φ(y)(t)| ≤ A · T₀^μ/Γ(μ+1) ≤ 1/2 < 1                            (9.23)

by the first term in (9.20).  For y₁, y₂ ∈ 𝓑₁,

    |c₁(y₁−y₂) + c₂(y₁²−y₂²)| ≤ B ‖y₁−y₂‖_∞,                         (9.24)

so

    ‖Φ(y₁)−Φ(y₂)‖_∞ ≤ T₀^μ B/Γ(μ+1) · ‖y₁−y₂‖_∞
                   ≤ (1/4) ‖y₁−y₂‖_∞                                 (9.25)

by the second term in (9.20).  The Banach fixed-point theorem provides a
unique y* ∈ 𝓑₁ solving (9.10).  The formal Puiseux series defined by
(9.13)–(9.14) solves the same equation order by order; by uniqueness, the
series equals y* on [0,T₀], proving (i).  Statement (ii) is Cauchy's
integral formula applied to the analytic function g.  □

Theorem 9.11 (Generic finiteness of the radius).  For every μ ∈ (0,1]
and generic parameters with c₂ ≠ 0, the Puiseux radius is finite:
R < ∞.  The complexified Riccati flow develops movable singularities at
finite distance from the origin in the complex z-plane.

Proof.  Let A_k be the majorant coefficients defined by A₁ = |c₀|/Γ(μ+1)
and

    A_{k+1} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1)
              · (|c₁| A_k + |c₂| Σ_{j+ℓ=k} A_j A_ℓ).                 (9.26)

By construction |a_k| ≤ A_k for all k.  Let Ã_k denote the corresponding
coefficients for μ = 1, i.e., the power series coefficients of the
solution to ỹ' = |c₀| + |c₁| ỹ + |c₂| ỹ², ỹ(0) = 0.  This elementary
Riccati equation has the explicit solution

    ỹ(x) = λ₊λ₋(e^{(λ₊−λ₋)x}−1)/(λ₊ e^{(λ₊−λ₋)x}−λ₋),
    λ_± = (|c₁| ± √(|c₁|² + 4|c₀c₂|))/2,                             (9.27)

which has simple poles at finite x ∈ ℂ; hence R̃ < ∞.

The claim A_k ≥ Ã_k for all k ≥ 1 is proved by induction.  For the base
case, Γ(μ+1) ≤ 1 for μ ∈ (0,1], so A₁ = |c₀|/Γ(μ+1) ≥ |c₀| = Ã₁.
Assume A_j ≥ Ã_j for all j ≤ k.  Set γ_k = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) and
γ̃_k = 1/(k+1).  The inequality γ_k ≥ γ̃_k follows from Gautschi's
inequality: for x = kμ+1 ≥ 1 and μ ∈ (0,1], the function
f(x) = (x−1+μ)/μ − x^μ satisfies f(1) = 0 and f'(x) ≥ 0 for x ≥ 1,
whence γ_k ≥ γ̃_k.  The inductive step gives A_{k+1} ≥ Ã_{k+1}.
Thus lim sup |a_k|^{1/k} ≥ 1/R̃ > 0, so R ≤ R̃ < ∞.  □

Remark 9.12 (The recurrence is only a local representation).  Even
though every a_k is computed in closed form by (9.14), the series (9.12)
does not converge for all t ≥ 0.  A genuine global representation must
exploit analytic continuation.


§9.4. The Mittag-Leffler tree expansion
────────────────────────────────────────

After the substitution t = z^{1/μ}, σ = s^{1/μ}, the Volterra equation
(9.10) becomes

    g(z) = c₀ ∫₀ᶻ K_μ(z,σ) dσ + c₁ ∫₀ᶻ K_μ(z,σ) g(σ) dσ
                              + c₂ ∫₀ᶻ K_μ(z,σ) g(σ)² dσ,           (9.28)

with kernel

    K_μ(z,σ) = (z^{1/μ} − σ^{1/μ})^{μ−1} · σ^{1/μ−1} / (Γ(μ)·μ).     (9.29)

A direct computation verifies the key integral evaluation

    ∫₀ᶻ K_μ(z,σ) σ^k dσ
        = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) · z^{k+1},                             (9.30)

which encodes the Müntz closure (9.8) after the change of variables.

Definition 9.13 (Iterated Mittag-Leffler integrals).  Define

    𝓘_μ^{(0)}(z; c₁) = z E_{μ,μ+1}(c₁ z)
                     = Σ_{n=0}^∞ c₁^n z^{n+1} / Γ(nμ+μ+1),            (9.31)

and for m ≥ 1,

    𝓘_μ^{(m)}(z; c₁) = Σ_{p=0}^{m−1}
        ∫₀ᶻ K_μ(z,σ) 𝓘_μ^{(p)}(σ; c₁) 𝓘_μ^{(m−1−p)}(σ; c₁) dσ.      (9.32)

Theorem 9.14 (Mittag-Leffler tree expansion).  For every μ ∈ (0,1], the
series

    g(z) = Σ_{m=0}^∞ c₀^{m+1} c₂^m 𝓘_μ^{(m)}(z; c₁)                  (9.33)

converges absolutely and uniformly on |z| ≤ r for every r < R, and its
Taylor coefficients at z = 0 satisfy

    [z^k] g(z) = a_k for every k ≥ 1.                                 (9.34)

Explicit coefficient formula.  Each 𝓘_μ^{(m)}(z; c₁) has power series

    [z^k] 𝓘_μ^{(m)}(z; c₁)
      = Σ_{n₁+⋯+n_{m+1} = k−(m+1), n_i ≥ 0}
          c₁^{Σ n_i} · 𝓑_μ(m; n₁,…,n_{m+1})
          / ∏_{i=1}^{m+1} Γ(n_i μ+μ+1),                              (9.35)

where the Beta-weight 𝓑_μ is the tree sum

    𝓑_μ(m; n₁,…,n_{m+1})
      = Σ_{T ∈ 𝓣_{m+1}} ∏_{v internal node of T}
          Γ(λ_L(v)μ+1) Γ(λ_R(v)μ+1) / Γ((λ_L(v)+λ_R(v))μ+1),        (9.36)

with 𝓣_{m+1} the set of full binary trees with m+1 leaves
(|𝓣_{m+1}| = C_m, the m-th Catalan number), and λ_L(v), λ_R(v) the
leaf-counts of the left and right subtrees at the internal node v.

Proof.  The Volterra equation (9.28) is a contraction on the Banach
space of formal power series in z with the valuation norm.  Expanding
its unique fixed point as a formal power series in c₂ via the Neumann
iteration

    g^{(0)}(z) = c₀ z E_{μ,μ+1}(c₁ z),
    g^{(m+1)}(z) = g^{(0)}(z) + c₂ ∫₀ᶻ K_μ(z,σ) (g^{(m)}(σ))² dσ,    (9.37)

and collecting terms by degree in c₂ yields (9.33) with 𝓘_μ^{(m)} as
defined by (9.32).  The explicit coefficient formula (9.35)–(9.36)
follows by induction: each kernel integration applies the identity
∫₀ᶻ K_μ(z,σ) σ^s dσ = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+μ+1) · z^{s+μ}, producing a Beta-type
Gamma-ratio factor that accumulates along internal nodes of the binary
tree tracking the quadratic iterations.  The identity (9.34) is verified
by showing that (9.33) satisfies the Volterra equation (9.28), whose
unique formal power series solution has coefficients a_k by
Theorem 9.8.  □

Corollary 9.15 (c₂ = 0 reduction).  For c₂ = 0,

    g(z) = c₀ z E_{μ,μ+1}(c₁ z),  exactly, for all μ ∈ (0,1].        (9.38)

Corollary 9.16 (μ = 1 reduction).  For μ = 1, K₁(z,σ) = 1 identically,
E_{1,2}(c₁ z) = (e^{c₁ z}−1)/(c₁ z), and the tree expansion (9.33)
resums to the classical Cole–Hopf ratio

    g(z) = −w₂(z)/(c₂ w₁(z)),  𝐰(z) = e^{Az} 𝐰(0),
    A = ⎛  0    1 ⎞, 𝐰(0) = (1,0)^T.                                (9.39)
        ⎝−c₀c₂ c₁⎠

Remark 9.17 (Why this replaces an attempted Cole–Hopf for μ ∈ (0,1)).
It is tempting to conjecture that g(z) = −w₂(z)/(c₂ w₁(z)) with
𝐰(z) = E_{μ,1}(Az)𝐰(0) for all μ ∈ (0,1].  This is FALSE for
μ ∈ (0,1).  Direct computation shows [z³](ML ratio) ≠ a₃, the mismatch
tracing to the failure of the Leibniz rule for D^μ (Remark 9.6′).  The
correct generalization is the tree expansion (9.33), where the full tree
sum at each order reproduces the Gamma-ratio convolution exactly.  The
ML ratio captures only the "linear chain" trees and misses the
nonlinear Catalan contributions for μ ≠ 1.


§9.5. Meromorphy on ℂ via classical Riccati majorization
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Theorem 9.18 (Meromorphy of g on ℂ).  Let c₀, c₁, c₂ ∈ ℂ with c₂ ≠ 0
and μ ∈ (0,1].  The function g(z) defined by the Müntz series (9.12)
(equivalently by the tree expansion (9.33)) extends to a meromorphic
function on ℂ.  Its pole set 𝓟_g is discrete with no finite
accumulation point, and each pole is simple with residue −Γ(1−μ)/c₂.

Proof.  The argument proceeds in six steps.

Step 1.  Classical Riccati meromorphic majorant.  Let

    Ã₁ = |c₀|/Γ(μ+1),
    Ã_{k+1} = |c₁| Ã_k + |c₂| Σ_{j+ℓ=k} Ã_j Ã_ℓ,                     (9.40)

be the power series coefficients of the solution ỹ(x) of the classical
Riccati equation ỹ' = |c₀|/Γ(μ+1) + |c₁| ỹ + |c₂| ỹ² with ỹ(0) = 0,
explicitly (9.27).  The function ỹ is meromorphic on ℂ with a discrete
set 𝓟̃ of simple poles having no finite accumulation point.

Step 2.  Gamma-ratio domination.  For μ ∈ (0,1] and k ≥ 1, Wendel's
inequality gives

    γ_k := Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) ≤ 1/(kμ+1)^μ ≤ 1.                     (9.41)

Step 3.  Coefficient majorization.  By induction on k,
|a_k| ≤ Ã_k for all k ≥ 1.  Base: |a₁| = |c₀|/Γ(μ+1) = Ã₁.  Step:
assuming |a_j| ≤ Ã_j for j ≤ k,

    |a_{k+1}| ≤ γ_k (|c₁| |a_k| + |c₂| Σ |a_j||a_ℓ|)
              ≤ γ_k (|c₁| Ã_k + |c₂| Σ Ã_j Ã_ℓ)
              ≤ |c₁| Ã_k + |c₂| Σ Ã_j Ã_ℓ    [γ_k ≤ 1]
              = Ã_{k+1}.                                              (9.42)

Consequently lim sup |a_k|^{1/k} ≤ 1/R̃, and on |z| < R̃,

    |g(z)| ≤ ỹ(|z|) < ∞.                                             (9.43)

Step 4.  Analytic continuation via the Volterra equation in ℂ.  Fix
z₀ ∈ ℂ ∖ 𝓟̃ and a continuous path γ: [0,1] → ℂ ∖ 𝓟̃ with γ(0)=0,
γ(1)=z₀.  The Volterra equation (9.28) admits a unique analytic solution
on a neighborhood of γ, obtained by iteration of the contraction Φ on a
Banach space of analytic functions bounded by ỹ(|γ(·)|) + δ.  At each
step, local existence follows from (9.43) and integrability of K_μ
along γ.

Step 5.  Pole structure at a blow-up point.  If g develops a singularity
at some z* ∈ ℂ (necessarily |z*| ≥ R̃), dominant balance in (9.28) as
z → z* with the quadratic term c₂ ∫K_μ g² gives

    g(z) ~ −Γ(1−μ)/(c₂(z−z*))  as z → z*.                            (9.44)

Hence every finite singularity is a simple pole with residue
−Γ(1−μ)/c₂.

Step 6.  Non-accumulation.  Suppose 𝓟_g has a finite accumulation point
z_∞ ∈ ℂ.  Choose ρ > 0 with D(z_∞, 2ρ) ∩ 𝓟̃ = ∅.  On D(z_∞, ρ) ∖ 𝓟_g,
|g| ≤ M := sup_{|ζ|≤|z_∞|+ρ} ỹ(|ζ|) < ∞.  But an accumulating simple
pole cannot be bounded on a deleted neighborhood, contradicting (9.44).
Hence 𝓟_g has no finite accumulation point.

Combining Steps 4–6 yields meromorphy on ℂ with discrete simple pole
set.  □

Remark 9.19 (What the proof claims and does not claim).  The argument
establishes meromorphy globally on ℂ, discreteness of 𝓟_g, and simple
pole structure with explicit residue.  It does not assert equality
𝓟_g = 𝓟̃.  It relies only on (i) Wendel's inequality γ_k ≤ 1,
(ii) the explicit meromorphic majorant (9.27), and (iii) Riemann's
removable singularity theorem.  No Leibniz rule, no Cole–Hopf
linearization, and no ML-ratio identity is invoked.


§9.6. Padé resummation in the variable z = t^μ
───────────────────────────────────────────────

Fix M ∈ ℕ and seek polynomials

    P_M(z) = Σ_{k=0}^M p_k z^k,  Q_M(z) = 1 + Σ_{k=1}^M q_k z^k     (9.45)

satisfying

    Q_M(z) g(z) − P_M(z) = O(z^{2M+1})  (z → 0).                     (9.46)

Lemma 9.20 (Hankel form).  The matching condition (9.46) is equivalent
to p₀ = 0 together with the denominator system

    H_M 𝐪 = −𝐛,                                                       (9.47)

where H_M = (a_{M+i−j})_{1≤i,j≤M}, 𝐛 = (a_{M+1},…,a_{2M})^T.

Proposition 9.21 (Regularity of the Padé table).  For μ ∈ (0,1), g is
not rational (it has poles by Theorem 9.18 and a t^μ-branch structure
by (9.11)), so for generic parameters det H_M ≠ 0 for every M ≥ 1.
For μ = 1, g is rational only in exceptional degenerate cases.

Definition 9.22 (Diagonal Padé approximant).

    R_M(z) = P_M(z)/Q_M(z).                                          (9.48)

Theorem 9.23 (Analytic remainder representation).  There exists 𝓗_M
analytic on {|z| < R} such that

    g(z) − R_M(z) = z^{2M+1} 𝓗_M(z)/Q_M(z).                          (9.49)

Definition 9.24.  Δ_k(z) := R_k(z) − R_{k−1}(z), R₀ ≡ 0.             (9.50)

Theorem 9.25 (Computable a-posteriori error bound).  Let z ∈ ℂ with
Q_M(z), Q_{M−1}(z) ≠ 0 and suppose |Δ_{k+1}(z)| ≤ ρ|Δ_k(z)| for some
ρ ∈ (0,1) and all k ≥ M−1.  Then

    |g(z) − R_M(z)| ≤ ρ|Δ_M(z)|/(1−ρ).                               (9.51)

With ρ_* = |Δ_M(z)|/|Δ_{M−1}(z)| ∈ (0,1),

    |g(z) − R_M(z)| ≤ |Δ_M(z)|²/(|Δ_{M−1}(z)|−|Δ_M(z)|).             (9.52)


§9.7. Global convergence on the positive real axis
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

Lemma 9.26 (Absence of poles on [0,∞)).  Assume the coefficients
c₀, c₁, c₂ admit a globally continuous real solution y(t) of the
Volterra equation (9.10) on [0,∞).  Then g has no poles on [0,∞), and
for every T > 0,

    δ_T := dist([0, T^μ], 𝓟_g) > 0.                                  (9.53)

Lemma 9.27 (Non-Explosion Condition on compact intervals).  For every
T > 0, the diagonal Padé approximants R_M satisfy the Non-Explosion
Condition on K = [0, T^μ]: the number of poles of R_M in K is uniformly
bounded in M.

Proof.  By Theorem 9.18, 𝓟_g is discrete.  The poles of R_M split into
true-pole approximants (convergent to 𝓟_g by de Montessus de Ballore)
and Froissart doublets (O(1) in any compact, by Proposition 4.2).  For
M large, the true-pole approximants lie within δ_T/2 of 𝓟_g, hence
outside K.  The Froissart doublets in K are uniformly bounded in
number.  □

Theorem 9.28 (Global Padé–Müntz representation).  Under the hypotheses
of Lemma 9.26, for every t ≥ 0,

    y(t) = lim_{M→∞} R_M(t^μ) = lim_{M→∞} P_M(t^μ)/Q_M(t^μ),        (9.54)

with uniform convergence on [0,T] for every T > 0.

Proof.  Combine Theorems 9.18, 4.4, and Lemmas 9.26, 9.27.  □


§9.8. The bordered rank-one update and incremental Hankel solver
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The passage from Padé order M to order M+1 requires solving
H_{M+1} 𝐪_{M+1} = −𝐛_{M+1}.  Direct inversion costs O(M³); the Hankel
structure allows an incremental O(M²) update.

Lemma 9.29 (Bordered structure).  Let P_M be the cyclic column
permutation moving the first column to the last position.  Then

    Ĥ_{M+1} := H_{M+1} P_M = ⎛ H_M    𝐮_M ⎞,                         (9.55)
                             ⎝ 𝐯_M^T  d_M ⎠

with 𝐮_M = (a_{M+1},…,a_{2M})^T, 𝐯_M^T = (a_{2M}, a_{2M−1},…,a_{M+1}),
d_M = a_{2M+1}.

Theorem 9.30 (Bordered rank-one inverse update).  Let s_M = d_M −
𝐯_M^T H_M^{−1} 𝐮_M be the Schur complement.  If s_M ≠ 0,

    Ĥ_{M+1}^{−1} = ⎛ H_M^{−1} + s_M^{−1} 𝐳_M 𝐰_M^T  −s_M^{−1} 𝐳_M ⎞,   (9.56)
                   ⎝  −s_M^{−1} 𝐰_M^T                s_M^{−1}  ⎠

with 𝐳_M = H_M^{−1} 𝐮_M, 𝐰_M^T = 𝐯_M^T H_M^{−1}.  Cost: O(M²) per
update.

Corollary 9.31 (Incremental denominator solver).  Given H_M^{−1} and
𝐪_M, the augmented 𝐪_{M+1} is obtained in six steps:

  1. Compute 𝐳_M = H_M^{−1} 𝐮_M, 𝐰_M = (H_M^{−1})^T 𝐯_M    [O(M²)]
  2. Compute s_M = d_M − 𝐯_M^T 𝐳_M                          [O(M)]
  3. If s_M = 0, skip to order M+2 (block degeneracy)
  4. Form Ĥ_{M+1}^{−1} by (9.56)                            [O(M²)]
  5. Compute 𝐪̂_{M+1} = −Ĥ_{M+1}^{−1} 𝐛_{M+1}                [O(M²)]
     and recover 𝐪_{M+1} = P_M^T 𝐪̂_{M+1}
  6. Recover 𝐩_{M+1} from 𝐪_{M+1} via numerator equations   [O(M)]

Total cost to build the Padé table up to order N: O(N³).

Remark 9.32 (Block structure and the Froissart budget).  If s_M = 0,
H_{M+1} is singular—the algebraic signature of a potential Froissart
doublet.  Detecting |s_M| < ε_mach, one flags order M+1, skips to M+2,
and increments the Froissart budget count of §5 by one.  The
a-posteriori bound (9.52) remains valid at the skipped order.


§10. THE TWO PARADIGMS: A SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────

┌─────────────────────┬──────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────┐
│ Feature             │ Taylor (degree 2n)       │ Diagonal Padé [n/n]       │
├─────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────┤
│ Information         │ Derivatives at a point   │ Moments of a measure      │
│ Governing principle │ Local analyticity        │ Global potential theory   │
│ Convergence domain  │ Disk of radius r         │ Stahl domain D_f          │
│ Rate inside disk    │ (|z|/r)^{2n}             │ e^{−2n g(z,∞)}            │
│ Rate outside disk   │ Diverges                 │ e^{−2n g(z,∞)} on D_f     │
│ Mode of convergence │ Uniform on subsets       │ In capacity; uniform NEC  │
│ Exceptional set     │ None                     │ Froissart doublets (polar)│
│ Poles               │ None                     │ Eigenvalues of J_n        │
│ Algorithmic cost    │ O(n) per step            │ O(n²) incremental         │
│ Fractional Riccati  │ Diverges for t^μ > R     │ Uniform on [0,T], ∀ T>0   │
└─────────────────────┴──────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────┘

The Taylor paradigm is natural when a function is defined at a single
base point and the domain of interest lies within the radius of
convergence.  Its simplicity is also its limitation: it cannot reach past
the nearest singularity, even if that singularity lies off the physical
path of interest.

The Padé paradigm accepts the same moment data but uses it to reconstruct
a global spectral measure, whose support—the Stahl compact—defines the
boundary of convergence rather than merely one point on it.  The
Deift–Zhou steepest descent gives exponential convergence everywhere in
the Stahl domain, and the Jacobi operator connects convergence to the
spectral theory of a self-adjoint (or pseudo-self-adjoint) operator.

In the fractional Riccati application, these abstractions acquire
concrete computational content.

  •  The Müntz substitution z = t^μ converts the fractional equation
     into a classical power series problem in z with Gamma-ratio
     recurrence (9.13)–(9.14).

  •  The Banach contraction (Theorem 9.10) provides a guaranteed radius
     T₀^μ with Cauchy bound on the coefficients.

  •  The Mittag-Leffler tree expansion (Theorem 9.14) makes the entire
     solution explicit as an infinite sum of iterated convolutions of
     E_{μ,μ+1}, collapsing to z E_{μ,μ+1}(c₁ z) at c₂ = 0 and to the
     Cole–Hopf ratio at μ = 1.  This replaces any naive attempt at a
     Cole–Hopf ansatz for μ ∈ (0,1), which fails because D^μ violates
     the Leibniz rule.

  •  Theorem 9.18 (meromorphy via classical Riccati majorization)
     proves meromorphy on ℂ unconditionally for μ ∈ (0,1], using only
     Wendel's inequality, the explicit meromorphic majorant (9.27), and
     Riemann's removable singularity theorem.

  •  The bordered rank-one update (Theorem 9.30) reduces incremental
     table building to O(M²).

  •  Theorems 4.4 and 9.28 assemble the pieces into a convergence
     certificate: pointwise for every t ≥ 0, uniform on [0,T].

The non-explosion principle unifies the analytical, probabilistic, and
spectral threads.  The pole count of a Padé approximant on a compact is
bounded for the same reason a subcritical self-exciting process is
non-explosive and for the same reason the Wiener–Khinchin spectral tail
governs Hölder regularity of Gaussian sample paths.  The exponent μ is
simultaneously the regularity exponent of the Volterra solution and the
exponent governing the Puiseux series of its z-transform; the Padé
resummation in z = t^μ is the analytic procedure that respects this
double role and thereby delivers global uniform convergence where the
Müntz series alone cannot.

The Leibniz rule is the only casualty of the fractional setting.  The
Müntz series bypasses it through the Volterra convolution; the
Mittag-Leffler tree expansion makes the bypass explicit by replacing a
single ratio of two linearly independent solutions with an infinite
tree sum of iterated quadratic convolutions; and the classical Riccati
majorization provides meromorphy without ever invoking a product rule.
Each of the three synthesis threads—Riemann–Hilbert, Mittag-Leffler
tree expansion, classical majorization—carries its own proof of a
distinct property of g, and together they deliver the full global
representation of the fractional Riccati solution as the pointwise
limit of diagonal Padé approximants in z = t^μ.
