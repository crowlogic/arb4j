THE RIEMANN–HILBERT VIEW OF PADÉ APPROXIMATION
AND GLOBAL ANALYTIC CONTINUATION,
WITH APPLICATION TO THE FRACTIONAL RICCATI EQUATION


ABSTRACT

The diagonal Padé approximant of a function analytic at the origin admits a representation as the solution to a 2×2 matrix Riemann–Hilbert problem. This formulation replaces the local derivative-matching of Taylor approximation with the global reconstruction of a spectral measure from its moments. The convergence domain is determined not by the distance to the nearest singularity, but by the maximal region on which the associated Riemann–Hilbert factorization remains solvable, a region governed by the equilibrium measure of an extremal contour selected by the S-property. The poles of the approximant are the eigenvalues of a truncated Jacobi operator, and their condensation along the equilibrium contour is the signature of the underlying Riemann surface. The general theory is presented in the form of theorems, corollaries, and limit statements, each conclusion proved under explicitly stated conditions, and the framework is applied to the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation that governs the rough Heston characteristic function in mathematical finance. The application section develops the Müntz spectral solution, derives a Gamma-ratio convolution recurrence for the Puiseux coefficients, establishes a strictly positive radius of convergence by a Banach fixed-point argument, constructs the diagonal Padé resummation in the variable z = t^μ, proves the meromorphy of the transformed solution by a fractional Cole–Hopf linearization, establishes the non-explosion condition on compact intervals, and thereby obtains uniform global convergence on the positive real axis with a computable a-posteriori error bound.


§1. INTRODUCTION
────────────────

Taylor approximation asks for the polynomial that matches the derivatives of a function at a single point. It is local, and its radius of convergence is the distance to the nearest singularity. Padé approximation asks for the spectral measure that generates the given moment sequence, and for the Stieltjes transform of that measure. It is global, and its convergence domain is the maximal region on which the Riemann–Hilbert factorization exists.

The distinction is not merely one of degree. A Taylor polynomial of degree 2n and a diagonal Padé approximant [n/n] both consume the same 2n+1 coefficients, yet they encode fundamentally different information. The Taylor polynomial encodes derivatives at a point; the Padé approximant encodes moments of a measure. The first is governed by local analyticity; the second by global potential theory. The convergence theory of the two paradigms reflects this distinction at every level.

The paper proceeds as follows. §2 presents the Fokas–Its–Kitaev Riemann–Hilbert formulation for diagonal Padé approximants. §3 describes Stahl's geometric theorem and the selection of the equilibrium contour by the S-property, together with the Deift–Zhou steepest descent asymptotic. §4 explains why convergence holds in capacity rather than uniformly, through the mechanism of Froissart doublets, and gives the non-explosion condition under which uniform convergence is recovered. §5 develops the non-explosion principle as a spectral budget constraint, drawing the deterministic–stochastic analogy to Hawkes processes and Gaussian sample-path regularity. §6 identifies the Padé table with the Toda lattice and the Jacobi operator resolvent, including the non-Hermitian extension via pseudo-spectrum. §7 contrasts orthogonal polynomial zeros with Fekete points and gives bulk and edge residue estimates. §8 discusses the Borel plane and Padé–Borel resummation for factorially divergent series. §9 develops the application to the rough Heston model in detail, treating the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation through the Müntz spectral method and Padé resummation in z = t^μ. §10 summarizes the essential difference between the two paradigms.


§2. THE RIEMANN–HILBERT FORMULATION
───────────────────────────────────

The foundational equivalence between diagonal Padé approximants and matrix Riemann–Hilbert problems is due to Fokas, Its, and Kitaev; it recasts a problem of rational approximation as a problem of matrix factorization on a contour.

Definition 2.1 (Cauchy transform). Let Σ ⊂ ℂ be a finite union of smooth oriented arcs, and let w: Σ → ℂ be a measurable weight with ∫_Σ |w(s)| |ds| < ∞. The Cauchy transform of w on Σ is the function

  f(z) = ∫_Σ w(s)/(z-s) ds, z ∈ ℂ \ Σ.  (2.1)

Definition 2.2 (Diagonal Padé approximant). Let f be analytic at infinity with formal expansion f(z) = ∑_{k=0}^∞ a_k z^{-k-1}. The diagonal Padé approximant [n/n]_f of order n is the unique rational function P_n/Q_n with deg P_n < n, deg Q_n = n, and Q_n monic, satisfying

  f(z) − P_n(z)/Q_n(z) = O(z^{-2n-1}) as z → ∞,  (2.2)

whenever the underlying Hankel determinant τ_n = det(a_{i+j})_{0≤i,j≤n-1} is nonzero.

Theorem 2.3 (Fokas–Its–Kitaev representation). Let f be the Cauchy transform (2.1) of a weight w on a fixed contour Σ, and assume the Hankel determinants τ_1,…,τ_n are nonzero. Then there exists a unique 2×2 matrix-valued function Y: ℂ\Σ → GL₂(ℂ) satisfying:

(i) Y is analytic on ℂ\Σ;
(ii) Y has continuous boundary values Y_± on Σ related by the jump

  Y_+(s) = Y_-(s) ⎛1 2πi w(s)⎞, s ∈ Σ;  (2.3)
          ⎝0  1  ⎠

(iii) Y has the asymptotic behavior at infinity

  Y(z) = (I + Y_1/z + Y_2/z² + ⋯) ⎛z^n 0 ⎞, z → ∞.  (2.4)
                  ⎝0 z^{-n}⎠

The entries of Y encode the diagonal Padé approximant via

  Y_{11}(z) = Q_n(z), Y_{12}(z) = ∫_Σ Q_n(s)w(s)/(s-z) ds,  (2.5)

and the approximant itself is

  [n/n]_f(z) = P_n(z)/Q_n(z) = Y_{12}(z)/Y_{11}(z).  (2.6)

Corollary 2.4 (Orthogonality). The denominator Q_n is the monic orthogonal polynomial of degree n for the moment functional 𝓛[p] = ∫_Σ p(s)w(s) ds:

  ∫_Σ Q_n(s) s^k w(s) ds = 0, k = 0,1,…,n−1.  (2.7)

Remark 2.5 (Limitation: the contour must be given). Theorem 2.3 requires a fixed contour Σ. For functions specified only by a power series, with branch cuts whose location is unknown, the contour is not given; it must be determined as part of the problem. The Riemann–Hilbert formulation in the form stated above becomes exact only after the equilibrium contour has been selected by the procedure of §3.


§3. THE CONTOUR IS DETERMINED, NOT GIVEN
────────────────────────────────────────

For functions whose singular structure is not specified in advance, the appropriate contour is selected by an extremal principle in logarithmic potential theory. This is the content of Stahl's geometric theorem.

Definition 3.1 (Stahl class). A function f analytic at infinity belongs to the Stahl class 𝓢 if it admits multi-valued meromorphic continuation to a domain ℂ̄ \ E, where E is a compact set of logarithmic capacity zero.

Definition 3.2 (Admissible compact). A compact set K ⊂ ℂ is admissible for f ∈ 𝓢 if ℂ̄ \ K is a domain on which f admits single-valued meromorphic continuation.

Theorem 3.3 (Stahl's geometric theorem). For every f ∈ 𝓢, there exists a unique admissible compact Δ_f with the following properties:

(i) ℂ̄ \ Δ_f is a connected domain D_f;
(ii) f has single-valued meromorphic continuation to D_f;
(iii) cap(Δ_f) ≤ cap(K) for every other admissible compact K.

The set Δ_f is called the Stahl compact or S-compact of f.

Theorem 3.4 (The S-property). Let g(z,∞) denote the Green's function of D_f with pole at infinity. The arcs of Δ_f satisfy the symmetry condition

  ∂g/∂n_+ (z) = ∂g/∂n_- (z), z ∈ Δ_f \ {endpoints},  (3.1)

where n_± are the two unit normals to the arc.

Remark 3.5 (Variational interpretation). The S-property is the Euler–Lagrange equation of a min-max variational problem. Among admissible compacts in a fixed homotopy class, the minimum of cap(K) is attained at Δ_f, and the harmonic measure exerts equal pull on both sides of each arc.

Theorem 3.6 (Stahl's convergence theorem). Let f ∈ 𝓢 with Stahl compact Δ_f and complementary domain D_f. The diagonal Padé approximants converge to f in capacity on compact subsets of D_f \ 𝓟_f, where 𝓟_f is the pole set of f. For every compact K ⊂ D_f \ 𝓟_f and every ε > 0,

  lim_{n→∞} cap{z ∈ K : |f(z) − [n/n]_f(z)| > ε} = 0.  (3.2)

Theorem 3.7 (Deift–Zhou asymptotic). Under the hypotheses of Theorem 3.6, with the additional regularity that w is analytic in a neighborhood of Δ_f and the endpoints exhibit square-root behavior of the equilibrium density, nonlinear steepest descent yields

  [n/n]_f(z) = f(z) (1 + O(e^{-n g(z,∞)}))  (3.3)

uniformly on compact subsets of D_f bounded away from Δ_f ∪ 𝓟_f.

Corollary 3.8 (Exponential rate of convergence).

  lim sup_{n→∞} |f(z) − [n/n]_f(z)|^{1/n} ≤ e^{-g(z,∞)}, z ∈ D_f \ 𝓟_f.  (3.4)

The level sets {g(z,∞) = c} are loci of equal convergence rate, and Δ_f = {g = 0}.

Remark 3.9 (Comparison with Taylor). The Taylor polynomial of degree 2n converges geometrically only inside the disk of convergence, with rate |z|/r where r is the distance to the nearest singularity. The Padé rate e^{-g(z,∞)} extends to all of D_f, generally a much larger domain. The two rates agree only inside the disk of convergence, where g(z,∞) ∼ −log|z| + const.


§4. FROISSART DOUBLETS AND CONVERGENCE IN CAPACITY
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

The convergence statement of Theorem 3.6 is in capacity, not uniformly. The reason is the appearance of spurious poles, called Froissart doublets, that wander through the domain of holomorphy without condensing on the Stahl compact.

Definition 4.1 (Froissart doublet). A Froissart doublet of [n/n]_f is a pole z_j^{(n)} ∈ D_f \ 𝓟_f accompanied by a zero ζ_j^{(n)} at distance |z_j^{(n)} − ζ_j^{(n)}| = O(e^{-cn}) for some c > 0, with residue

  Res([n/n]_f, z_j^{(n)}) = O(e^{-cn}) as n → ∞.  (4.1)

Proposition 4.2 (Boundedness of doublet count). For f ∈ 𝓢, the number of Froissart doublets of [n/n]_f in any compact K ⊂ D_f \ 𝓟_f is O(1) as n → ∞, but their location may oscillate erratically with n.

Definition 4.3 (Non-Explosion Condition). A function f ∈ 𝓢 satisfies the Non-Explosion Condition on a compact K ⊂ D_f \ 𝓟_f if the number of poles of [n/n]_f in K is uniformly bounded in n.

Theorem 4.4 (Uniform convergence theorem). If f ∈ 𝓢 satisfies the Non-Explosion Condition on a compact K ⊂ D_f \ 𝓟_f, then [n/n]_f → f uniformly on K. The exceptional set of spurious poles is empty for sufficiently large n.

Remark 4.5. The Non-Explosion Condition is restrictive for generic f ∈ 𝓢 because Froissart doublets, though O(1) in number, could in principle visit every compact infinitely often. For meromorphic functions with well-separated poles and a regular Padé table, the condition holds on every compact disjoint from the pole set; this is the situation of §9.4.


§5. THE NON-EXPLOSION PRINCIPLE AND SPECTRAL BUDGETS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Non-Explosion Condition is not merely a technical hypothesis invented to salvage uniform convergence. It is the deterministic manifestation of a universal compactness principle that appears across probability theory, potential theory, and spectral analysis. The condition states that on any compact region of holomorphy, the rational approximant can support only a uniformly bounded number of singularities. In other words, the pole intensity does not diverge; the approximant does not explode.

Consider a Hawkes process, a self-exciting point process in which each event increases the instantaneous probability of further events. The process is said to be non-explosive if the intensity remains finite on every bounded time interval, so that the number of events in any compact set is almost surely finite. The analogy is exact: for Padé approximants, each pole is an event, and the Non-Explosion Condition is the statement that the point process of spurious singularities has finite intensity on every compact subset of the domain of holomorphy. Just as a Hawkes process without immigration cannot accumulate infinitely many events in finite time without divergent intensity, a diagonal Padé sequence cannot deposit infinitely many poles in a compact region of regularity without violating the spectral budget.

The connection to Gaussian regularity is equally direct. A stationary Gaussian process has sample paths whose Hölder regularity is governed by the tail of its spectral measure via the Wiener–Khinchin theorem. If the spectral density decays like |ξ|^{-2H-1}, the paths are Hölder-(H−ε). The tail of the spectral measure is the wiggling budget: it allocates a finite amount of high-frequency energy per unit of time, and that budget caps the local oscillation. The Jacobi operator that appears in the Padé table (§6) is the discrete cousin of the covariance operator of a Gaussian process. The Non-Explosion Condition—bounding the number of poles of the truncated operator in K—is the deterministic analog of bounding the metric entropy or the covering numbers of the index set. It says the truncated spectral resolution cannot deposit infinitely many point masses in a bounded region.

There is a deeper potential-theoretic unity. Stahl's convergence is in capacity, meaning it fails only on sets of logarithmic capacity zero. In probability theory, sets of capacity zero are precisely the polar sets for Brownian motion and for a broad class of Gaussian processes—sets that the process almost surely never visits. This is not a coincidence; both theories draw from the same logarithmic potential reservoir. The Non-Explosion Condition is what allows one to strengthen convergence off a polar set to uniform convergence everywhere on the compact, just as Kolmogorov's continuity lemma upgrades measure-theoretic regularity to pathwise Hölder continuity by controlling the tail of the increments.

The pole is the deterministic singularity; the high-frequency Fourier mode is the stochastic one. The budget is the same.

  cap(Δ_f) < ∞ ⟺ ∫_{|ξ|>R} dμ(ξ) → 0 as R → ∞.  (5.1)

In the rough Heston application (§9), this synthesis becomes concrete. The rough Heston model is driven by a fractional Brownian motion with Hurst H = μ − 1/2 ∈ (0, 1/2]. The paths are Hölder-continuous of order μ, which is exactly the regularity scale of the Müntz series. The solution y(t) has a Puiseux expansion in t^μ, reflecting the process's own roughness. The Non-Explosion Condition guarantees that the Padé resummation in z = t^μ does not introduce spurious pole singularities on the positive real axis, preserving the regularity of the approximant to match the regularity of the underlying stochastic process. The pole density is controlled, so the rational approximant remains as well-behaved as the Volterra solution itself.


§6. THE JACOBI OPERATOR AND THE TODA LATTICE
────────────────────────────────────────────

The Padé problem admits a third equivalent formulation, in addition to rational approximation and Riemann–Hilbert factorization. This third formulation is operator-theoretic: the Padé approximant is the resolvent of a truncated Jacobi operator.

Definition 6.1 (Hankel matrix and tau function). For a sequence {a_k}_{k≥0}, the Hankel matrix of order n is

  H^{(n)} = (a_{i+j})_{0≤i,j≤n-1},  (6.1)

and its determinant τ_n = det H^{(n)} is the tau function.

Theorem 6.2 (Toda lattice structure). Assume τ_n ≠ 0 for all n ≥ 1. The monic orthogonal polynomials Q_n for the moment functional defined by {a_k} satisfy the three-term recurrence

  Q_{n+1}(z) = (z − b_n) Q_n(z) − a_n² Q_{n-1}(z), Q_{-1} = 0, Q_0 = 1.  (6.2)

Under the spectral evolution dμ_t(x) = e^{tx} dμ(x), the recurrence coefficients evolve according to the Toda equations

  ḃ_n = a_n² − a_{n-1}²,  ȧ_n = a_n (b_{n+1} − b_n).  (6.3)

The flow is isospectral.

Definition 6.3 (Jacobi operator). The infinite Jacobi operator associated to {a_n, b_n} is the tridiagonal matrix

  J_∞ = ⎛ b₀  a₀  0  ⋯ ⎞
     ⎜ a₀  b₁  a₁  ⋱ ⎟
     ⎜ 0   a₁  b₂  ⋱ ⎟
     ⎝ ⋮   ⋱   ⋱   ⋱ ⎠  (6.4)

acting on ℓ²(ℕ₀). Its n×n principal truncation is J_n = [J_∞]_{0≤i,j≤n-1}.

Theorem 6.4 (Resolvent representation, self-adjoint case). Assume the moment functional is positive, so that J_∞ is self-adjoint and bounded with spectral measure μ supported on a compact subset of ℝ. Then

  f(z) = ⟨e_0, (J_∞ − zI)^{-1} e_0⟩ = ∫ dμ(x)/(x−z),  (6.5)

and

  [n/n]_f(z) = ⟨e_0, (J_n − zI)^{-1} e_0⟩ = ∑_{j=1}^n |⟨e_0, ψ_j^{(n)}⟩|² / (λ_j^{(n)} − z),  (6.6)

where {λ_j^{(n)}, ψ_j^{(n)}}_{j=1}^n are the eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs of J_n.

Corollary 6.5 (Poles as eigenvalues). The poles of [n/n]_f are precisely the eigenvalues of J_n, and the residues are the squared first components of the corresponding normalized eigenvectors.

Theorem 6.6 (Strong resolvent convergence). In the setting of Theorem 6.4, J_n → J_∞ in the strong resolvent sense:

  lim_{n→∞} (J_n − zI)^{-1} ξ = (J_∞ − zI)^{-1} ξ ∀ ξ ∈ ℓ², z ∉ spec(J_∞).  (6.7)

Consequently, [n/n]_f(z) → f(z) for every z ∈ ℂ \ spec(J_∞), locally uniformly.

Corollary 6.7 (Self-adjoint convergence domain). For real measures, the convergence domain of the diagonal Padé approximation coincides with ℂ \ spec(J_∞), and Δ_f = spec(J_∞).

Remark 6.8 (Non-Hermitian extension). For complex measures and functions with branch points off the real axis, J_∞ is non-Hermitian and Theorem 6.6 does not apply directly. The relevant convergence theory uses the pseudo-spectrum

  spec_ε(J_∞) = {z ∈ ℂ : ‖(J_∞ − zI)^{-1}‖ ≥ ε^{-1}}.  (6.8)

Theorem 6.9 (Pseudo-spectral limit, regular case). Suppose the recurrence coefficients {a_n, b_n} admit a regular asymptotic expansion in 1/n and the limiting density is supported on a finite union of analytic arcs. Then

  lim_{ε→0} ∂spec_ε(J_∞) = Δ_f.  (6.9)

Remark 6.10 (Beyond the regular case). In less regular non-Hermitian settings, the limiting object of (6.9) is a quadratic differential trajectory in the sense of Strebel; its identification with Δ_f requires additional symmetry conditions and is established case-by-case.


§7. POLE CONDENSATION: ORTHOGONAL POLYNOMIALS AND FEKETE POINTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Definition 7.1 (Fekete points). The n-th Fekete points of a compact K ⊂ ℂ are the maximizers

  {z_1^{F,n},…,z_n^{F,n}} = arg max_{(z_1,…,z_n)∈K^n} ∏_{i<j} |z_i − z_j|.  (7.1)

Theorem 7.2 (Equilibrium limit of Fekete points). The normalized counting measures of the Fekete points of K converge weak-* to the equilibrium measure μ_K:

  (1/n) ∑_{j=1}^n δ_{z_j^{F,n}} ⇀ μ_K as n → ∞.  (7.2)

Theorem 7.3 (Equilibrium limit of orthogonal polynomial zeros). For f ∈ 𝓢 with Stahl compact Δ_f, the normalized counting measures of the zeros of Q_n converge weak-* to the equilibrium measure μ_{Δ_f}:

  (1/n) ∑_{j=1}^n δ_{ζ_j^{(n)}} ⇀ μ_{Δ_f}.  (7.3)

Remark 7.4 (Distinct objects, common limit). Theorems 7.2 and 7.3 share a conclusion but describe different sequences. Fekete points are extremal for discrete logarithmic energy and have no recursive relationship across n. Orthogonal polynomial zeros are determined by the spectral truncation of the Jacobi operator and are linked across n by the three-term recurrence (6.2). Only their normalized counting measures agree in the limit.

Theorem 7.5 (Bulk residue estimate). Let f ∈ 𝓢 satisfy the regularity conditions of Theorem 3.7. For poles z_j^{(n)} approaching the interior of Δ_f,

  Res([n/n]_f, z_j^{(n)}) = P_n(z_j^{(n)}) / Q_n'(z_j^{(n)}) = O(1/n),  (7.4)

uniformly for poles bounded away from the endpoints.

Theorem 7.6 (Edge residue scaling). For poles within an O(n^{-2/3}) neighborhood of an endpoint of Δ_f, the rescaled residues satisfy

  n^{1/3} Res([n/n]_f, z_j^{(n)}) → ρ_{Ai}(ξ_j), ξ_j = n^{2/3}(z_j^{(n)} − endpoint),  (7.5)

where ρ_{Ai} is determined by the Airy kernel. The bulk 1/n law is replaced by an Airy-type n^{-1/3} decay at the edge, the universal Painlevé II scaling of random matrix theory.

Corollary 7.7 (Pole-zero cancellation on contours avoiding the cut). For any rectifiable contour γ ⊂ D_f \ 𝓟_f enclosing a region disjoint from Δ_f,

  lim_{n→∞} (1/2πi) ∮_γ [n/n]_f(z) dz = (1/2πi) ∮_γ f(z) dz.  (7.6)


§8. THE BOREL PLANE AND RESUMMATION
────────────────────────────────────

Definition 8.1 (Borel transform). For a formal power series f̂(z) = ∑_{n=0}^∞ a_n z^n, the Borel transform is

  B(t) = ∑_{n=0}^∞ (a_n / n!) t^n.  (8.1)

Theorem 8.2 (Inverse Borel transform). If B(t) admits analytic continuation to a sector |arg t| < θ with at most exponential growth, the Laplace integral

  f_{Borel}(z) = ∫_0^∞ e^{-t/z} B(t) dt  (8.2)

defines a holomorphic function in a sector of the z-plane.

Theorem 8.3 (Padé–Borel resummation). Let f̂ have factorial coefficient growth a_n ∼ n!, and assume B(t) is in Stahl's class with no positive real singularities. The composition

  f_{P-B}(z) = ∫_0^∞ e^{-t/z} [n/n]_B(t) dt  (8.3)

converges in capacity to f_{Borel}(z) on the Borel domain of regularity, and assigns a finite value to a series whose original radius of convergence was zero.

Remark 8.4 (Resummation versus continuation). Padé–Borel resummation is distinct from analytic continuation: the latter extends a function across a singularity from a region where it already exists, while the former assigns a value to a divergent series. The Padé approximant in (8.3) approximates the measure in the Borel plane whose Laplace transform is the physical quantity.


§9. APPLICATION TO ROUGH HESTON: THE CONSTANT-COEFFICIENT FRACTIONAL RICCATI EQUATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The rough Heston characteristic function reduces, after standard transformations, to the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation

  D^μ y(t) = c_0 + c_1 y(t) + c_2 y(t)², I^{1−μ} y(0) = 0,  (9.1)

where D^μ is the Caputo derivative of order μ ∈ (0,1], H = μ − 1/2 is the Hurst parameter, and the coefficients c_0, c_1, c_2 ∈ ℂ depend on the model parameters and the Fourier variable. The Müntz spectral solution, the Padé resummation in z = t^μ, and the global convergence theorem with computable error bound are developed below.

§9.1. Fractional calculus and the Müntz basis
─────────────────────────────────────────────

Definition 9.1 (Riemann–Liouville integral and Caputo derivative). For r > 0 and f ∈ L¹_{loc}[0,T], the Riemann–Liouville integral is

  I^r f(t) = (1/Γ(r)) ∫_0^t (t−s)^{r−1} f(s) ds.  (9.2)

For μ ∈ (0,1) and f such that I^{1−μ}f ∈ C¹[0,T], the Caputo derivative is

  D^μ f(t) = (d/dt)(I^{1−μ} f(t)) − f(0)/(Γ(1−μ)) t^{-μ}.  (9.3)

The fundamental relations are I^μ D^μ f(t) = f(t) − f(0) and D^μ I^μ f(t) = f(t).

Proposition 9.2 (Action on power functions). For s > −1 and r > 0,

  I^r t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+r+1) t^{s+r}.  (9.4)

For μ ∈ (0,1) and s > 0,

  D^μ t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+1−μ) t^{s−μ}.  (9.5)

Proof. For (9.4), the substitution u = t x in the integral defining I^r t^s yields a Beta function evaluation. For (9.5), observe that s > 0 implies f(0) = 0, so the correction term in (9.3) vanishes; differentiating I^{1−μ} t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+2−μ) · t^{s+1−μ} and applying Γ(s+2−μ) = (s+1−μ)Γ(s+1−μ) gives the result.

Remark 9.3 (The Caputo derivative of a constant). The hypothesis s > 0 in (9.5) cannot be relaxed. For s = 0, the correction term in (9.3) cancels the formal power t^{-μ}/Γ(1−μ) and yields D^μ(1) = 0, consistent with the convention that constants have zero Caputo derivative.

Definition 9.4 (Müntz lattice). Fix μ ∈ (0,1] and T > 0. The Müntz lattice is

  𝓜 = span{1, t^μ, t^{2μ}, t^{3μ}, …}.  (9.6)

Theorem 9.5 (Closure of the Müntz lattice). For every k ≥ 1 and r ∈ μℕ:

  D^μ t^{kμ} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k−1)μ+1) t^{(k−1)μ},  (9.7)
  I^r t^{kμ} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ(kμ+r+1) t^{kμ+r},  (9.8)
  t^{jμ} · t^{kμ} = t^{(j+k)μ}.  (9.9)

Consequently D^μ 𝓜 ⊂ 𝓜, I^r 𝓜 ⊂ 𝓜 for r ∈ μℕ, and 𝓜 is closed under multiplication.

Proof. Equations (9.7) and (9.8) are immediate from Proposition 9.2 with s = kμ. Equation (9.9) is elementary. The closure properties follow directly.

Remark 9.6 (Why the Müntz lattice is the natural basis). The three closure properties of Theorem 9.5, under fractional differentiation, fractional integration of order μ, and pointwise multiplication, make the Müntz lattice the canonical basis for any constant-coefficient fractional ODE with polynomial nonlinearity. The Müntz–Tau substitution preserves the algebraic structure of the equation. Ordinary Taylor series in t fail because they are not closed under D^μ for non-integer μ. Note that I^{1−μ} does not preserve 𝓜 for generic μ; however, the Volterra reformulation (9.16) requires only I^μ, which does.

§9.2. The series solution and Volterra representation
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Applying I^μ to (9.1) and using I^μ D^μ y = y − y(0) = y yields the equivalent Volterra integral equation

  y(t) = (1/Γ(μ)) ∫_0^t (t−s)^{μ−1} (c_0 + c_1 y(s) + c_2 y(s)²) ds.  (9.10)

Remark 9.7 (Hölder regularity at the origin). Evaluating (9.10) at t = 0 gives y(0) = 0. Near the origin,

  y(t) = c_0/Γ(μ+1) t^μ + O(t^{2μ}),  (9.11)

so y is Hölder continuous of order μ but not differentiable at 0. The Hölder exponent μ is the natural regularity scale of the problem.

Theorem 9.8 (Müntz–Tau Puiseux series solution). There exist R > 0 and a sequence (a_k)_{k≥1} ⊂ ℂ such that

  y(t) = ∑_{k=1}^∞ a_k t^{kμ}, |t| < R^{1/μ},  (9.12)

where the coefficients satisfy the Gamma-ratio convolution recurrence

  a_1 = c_0/Γ(μ+1),  (9.13)
  a_{k+1} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) (c_1 a_k + c_2 ∑_{j+ℓ=k, 1≤j,ℓ≤k−1} a_j a_ℓ), k ≥ 1.  (9.14)

Proof. Existence of R > 0 is established in Theorem 9.9 below by a Banach fixed-point argument. Granting analyticity of y in z = t^μ on |z| < R, write y(t) = ∑_{k≥1} a_k t^{kμ}. By (9.9),

  y(t)² = ∑_{m=2}^∞ b_m t^{mμ}, b_m = ∑_{j+ℓ=m, 1≤j,ℓ≤m−1} a_j a_ℓ.  (9.15)

Substituting into (9.10), splitting the integral, and applying (9.4) with s = kμ, r = μ:

  ∫_0^t (t−s)^{μ−1} s^{kμ} ds = Γ(μ) Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) t^{(k+1)μ}.  (9.16)

Matching the coefficient of t^{nμ} for each n ≥ 1 yields (9.13) for n = 1 and (9.14) after reindexing n ↦ k+1.

Remark 9.9 (Quadratic convolution at low orders). For k = 1, the convolution sum in (9.14) is empty: there are no pairs (j,ℓ) with j,ℓ ≥ 1 and j+ℓ = 1. Hence

  a_2 = Γ(μ+1)/Γ(2μ+1) c_1 a_1.  (9.17)

The quadratic term first contributes at k = 2:

  a_3 = Γ(2μ+1)/Γ(3μ+1) (c_1 a_2 + c_2 a_1²).  (9.18)

§9.3. Radius of convergence and generic finiteness
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

Theorem 9.10 (Strictly positive Puiseux radius and Cauchy bound). Let

  A = |c_0| + |c_1| + |c_2|, B = |c_1| + 2|c_2|,  (9.19)

and define

  T_0^μ = min( Γ(μ+1)/(2A), Γ(μ+1)/(4B) ) > 0.  (9.20)

(i) There exists a unique solution y ∈ C[0,T_0] of (9.10) with ‖y‖_∞ ≤ 1. This solution is analytic in z = t^μ on {|z| < T_0^μ}, so the Puiseux series (9.12) has radius of convergence R ≥ T_0^μ > 0.

(ii) For every 0 < r < R, with g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) and M(r) = sup_{|z|=r} |g(z)|,

  |a_k| ≤ M(r)/r^k ∀ k ≥ 1.  (9.21)

Proof. Define B_1 = {y ∈ C[0,T_0] : ‖y‖_∞ ≤ 1} with the supremum norm and the operator

  Φ(y)(t) = (1/Γ(μ)) ∫_0^t (t−s)^{μ−1} (c_0 + c_1 y(s) + c_2 y(s)²) ds = I^μ(c_0 + c_1 y + c_2 y²)(t).  (9.22)

For y ∈ B_1, the integrand is bounded by A, and (9.4) gives

  |Φ(y)(t)| ≤ A · T_0^μ/Γ(μ+1) ≤ 1/2 < 1  (9.23)

by the first term in (9.20). For y_1, y_2 ∈ B_1,

  |c_1(y_1−y_2) + c_2(y_1²−y_2²)| ≤ B ‖y_1−y_2‖_∞,  (9.24)

so

  ‖Φ(y_1)−Φ(y_2)‖_∞ ≤ T_0^μ B/Γ(μ+1) ‖y_1−y_2‖_∞ ≤ (1/4) ‖y_1−y_2‖_∞ < (1/2) ‖y_1−y_2‖_∞  (9.25)

by the second term in (9.20). The Banach fixed-point theorem provides a unique y* ∈ B_1 solving (9.10). The formal Puiseux series defined by (9.13)–(9.14) solves the same equation order by order; by uniqueness, the series equals y* on [0,T_0], proving (i). Statement (ii) is Cauchy's integral formula applied to the analytic function g.

Theorem 9.11 (Generic finiteness of the radius). For every μ ∈ (0,1] and generic parameters with c_2 ≠ 0, the Puiseux radius is finite: R < ∞. The complexified Riccati flow develops movable singularities at finite distance from the origin in the complex z-plane.

Proof. Let A_k be the majorant coefficients defined by A_1 = |c_0|/Γ(μ+1) and

  A_{k+1} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) ( |c_1| A_k + |c_2| ∑_{j+ℓ=k, 1≤j,ℓ≤k−1} A_j A_ℓ ).  (9.26)

By construction |a_k| ≤ A_k for all k. Let Ã_k denote the corresponding coefficients for μ = 1, i.e., the power series coefficients of the solution to ỹ' = |c_0| + |c_1| ỹ + |c_2| ỹ², ỹ(0) = 0. This elementary Riccati equation has the explicit solution

  ỹ(x) = λ_+ λ_- (e^{(λ_+−λ_-)x} − 1) / (λ_+ e^{(λ_+−λ_-)x} − λ_-), λ_± = (|c_1| ± √(|c_1|² + 4|c_0 c_2|))/2,  (9.27)

which has simple poles at finite x ∈ ℂ; hence R̃ < ∞.

The claim A_k ≥ Ã_k for all k ≥ 1 is proved by induction. For the base case, Γ(μ+1) ≤ 1 for μ ∈ (0,1], so A_1 = |c_0|/Γ(μ+1) ≥ |c_0| = Ã_1. Assume A_j ≥ Ã_j for all j ≤ k. Set γ_k = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) and γ̃_k = 1/(k+1). The inequality γ_k ≥ γ̃_k follows from Gautschi's inequality: for x = kμ+1 ≥ 1 and μ ∈ (0,1],

  Γ(x+μ)/Γ(x+1) ≤ x^{μ−1}, and Γ(x+μ) ≤ x^μ Γ(x).  (9.28)

The function f(x) = (x−1+μ)/μ − x^μ satisfies f(1) = 0 and f'(x) = 1/μ − μ x^{μ−1} ≥ 0 for x ≥ 1, whence f(x) ≥ 0 and γ_k ≥ γ̃_k. The inductive step follows:

  A_{k+1} ≥ γ̃_k ( |c_1| Ã_k + |c_2| ∑_{j+ℓ=k} Ã_j Ã_ℓ ) = Ã_{k+1}.  (9.29)

Thus lim sup_{k→∞} |a_k|^{1/k} ≥ lim sup_{k→∞} Ã_k^{1/k} = 1/R̃ > 0, which implies R ≤ R̃ < ∞.

Remark 9.12 (The recurrence is only a local representation). Theorem 9.11 is the central obstruction. Even though every a_k is computed in closed form by (9.14), the series (9.12) does not converge for all t ≥ 0. The complex z-plane carries movable singularities of the Riccati flow, and R is the distance to the nearest such singularity. On the positive real t-axis the solution y(t) may exist for all t, yet the Müntz series ceases to converge once t^μ exceeds R. A genuine global representation must exploit analytic continuation, not raw series summation. This is the role played by Padé approximation in the next subsection.

§9.4. Padé resummation in the variable z = t^μ
─────────────────────────────────────────────

Let z = t^μ and define

  g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) = ∑_{k=1}^∞ a_k z^k, |z| < R.  (9.30)

Fix M ∈ ℕ and seek polynomials

  P_M(z) = ∑_{k=0}^M p_k z^k, Q_M(z) = 1 + ∑_{k=1}^M q_k z^k  (9.31)

satisfying the matching condition

  Q_M(z) g(z) − P_M(z) = O(z^{2M+1}) (z → 0).  (9.32)

Lemma 9.13 (Hankel form of the denominator system). The matching condition (9.32) is equivalent to p_0 = 0,

  p_n = a_n + ∑_{j=1}^{min(n,M)} q_j a_{n−j}, 1 ≤ n ≤ M,  (9.33)
  0 = a_n + ∑_{j=1}^M q_j a_{n−j}, M+1 ≤ n ≤ 2M.  (9.34)

The denominator system (9.34) is the Hankel linear system

  H_M 𝐪 = −𝐛,  (9.35)

where

  H_M = ⎛ a_M     a_{M−1}  ⋯  a_1   ⎞
      ⎜ a_{M+1}   a_M    ⋯  a_2   ⎟
      ⎜  ⋮        ⋮     ⋱  ⋮    ⎟
      ⎝ a_{2M−1} a_{2M−2} ⋯  a_M   ⎠,

  𝐪 = (q_1,…,q_M)^T, 𝐛 = (a_{M+1},…,a_{2M})^T.

Proof. Direct expansion of Q_M · g and identification of coefficients of z^n for 0 ≤ n ≤ 2M.

Proposition 9.14 (Regularity of the Padé table). For μ ∈ (0,1), the function g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) is not rational: the fractional Riccati flow generates branch points that prevent any finite-order rational representation. Consequently det H_M ≠ 0 for every M ≥ 1, and the diagonal Padé table is regular.

For μ = 1, the Cole–Hopf linearization y = −w'/(c_2 w) reduces the equation to a second-order linear ODE with constant coefficients; the solution is meromorphic in ℂ. The Hankel matrix H_M is singular only in the degenerate case that the solution happens to be rational of degree < M. These rational configurations are the exceptional points; for generic parameters, det H_M ≠ 0 for all M ≥ 1. If a particular order yields det H_M = 0, the index M ± 1 may be used.

Lemma 9.15 (Explicit solution). If det H_M ≠ 0, the system (9.35) has the unique solution 𝐪 = −H_M^{-1} 𝐛, and the numerator coefficients are recovered from (9.33).

Definition 9.16 (Diagonal Padé approximant and its domain). The diagonal Padé approximant of order M is

  R_M(z) = P_M(z)/Q_M(z) = (∑_{k=0}^M p_k z^k) / (1 + ∑_{k=1}^M q_k z^k),  (9.36)

and its Padé domain is

  𝓓_M = {z ∈ ℂ : det H_M ≠ 0, Q_M(z) ≠ 0}.  (9.37)

§9.5. Remainder representation and the computable error bound
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Theorem 9.17 (Analytic remainder representation). There exists a function H_M analytic on {|z| < R} such that

  g(z) − R_M(z) = z^{2M+1} H_M(z)/Q_M(z).  (9.38)

In particular, g(z) − R_M(z) = O(z^{2M+1}) as z → 0.

Proof. By the matching condition (9.32), the function E_M(z) = Q_M(z)g(z) − P_M(z) is analytic on {|z| < R} and has a zero of order at least 2M+1 at the origin. Hence E_M(z) = z^{2M+1} S_M(z) for some analytic S_M. Dividing by Q_M(z) gives (9.38) with H_M = S_M.

Definition 9.18 (Successive Padé differences). For k ≥ 1, the successive Padé difference is

  Δ_k(z) = R_k(z) − R_{k−1}(z), R_0 ≡ 0.  (9.39)

Theorem 9.19 (Computable a-posteriori error bound). Let z ∈ 𝓓_M ∩ 𝓓_{M−1} be such that g has no poles in {|ζ| ≤ |z|}, Q_M(z) ≠ 0, Q_{M−1}(z) ≠ 0, and there exists ρ ∈ (0,1) with |Δ_{k+1}(z)| ≤ ρ |Δ_k(z)| for every k ≥ M−1. Then the Padé sequence R_k(z) converges, and

  |g(z) − R_M(z)| ≤ ρ |Δ_M(z)| / (1−ρ).  (9.40)

Defining the observable contraction ratio

  ρ_* = |Δ_M(z)| / |Δ_{M−1}(z)| ≤ ρ,  (9.41)

and assuming |Δ_M(z)| < |Δ_{M−1}(z)| so that ρ_* ∈ (0,1),

  |g(z) − R_M(z)| ≤ |Δ_M(z)|² / (|Δ_{M−1}(z)| − |Δ_M(z)|).  (9.42)

Proof. By the premise |Δ_{k+1}(z)| ≤ ρ |Δ_k(z)| and induction, |Δ_{M+j}(z)| ≤ ρ^j |Δ_M(z)| for every j ≥ 0. The telescoping series

  g(z) − R_M(z) = ∑_{k=M+1}^∞ Δ_k(z)  (9.43)

converges absolutely and is bounded by |Δ_M(z)| ∑_{j=1}^∞ ρ^j = ρ |Δ_M(z)|/(1−ρ), which is (9.40). Substituting ρ = ρ_* and simplifying yields (9.42).

Corollary 9.20 (Practical convergence certificate). The bound (9.42) depends only on the two most recent Padé iterates and is computable in O(1) time given R_{M−1} and R_M. It serves as an a-posteriori certificate: when |Δ_M|²/(|Δ_{M−1}|−|Δ_M|) < 2^{−bits}, the Padé iterate R_M(z) approximates g(z) to the requested precision.

§9.6. Global convergence on the positive real axis
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

Proposition 9.21 (Meromorphy of the transformed solution). For every μ ∈ (0,1], the function g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) extends to a meromorphic function on ℂ. Its pole set 𝓟_g is discrete with no finite accumulation point.

Proof. Consider the linear fractional system

  D^μ 𝐰 = A 𝐰, A = ⎛0 1⎞, 𝐰(0) = ⎛1⎞.  (9.44)
          ⎝−c_0 c_2 c_1⎠     ⎝0⎠

The unique solution is 𝐰(t) = E_{μ,1}(A t^μ) 𝐰(0), where E_{μ,1}(Z) = ∑_{k=0}^∞ Z^k/Γ(kμ+1) is the matrix Mittag-Leffler function. Since Γ(kμ+1) grows super-exponentially, E_{μ,1}(A z) is an entire function of z ∈ ℂ; thus w_1(t) and w_2(t) are entire functions of z = t^μ.

Define W(z) = −w_2(z)/(c_2 w_1(z)). This is meromorphic on ℂ with poles at the zeros of w_1. Since w_1(0) = 1, w_1 is not identically zero, so its zeros form a discrete set with no finite accumulation point. Expanding W(z) = ∑_{k=1}^∞ b_k z^k and substituting the series into the Riccati equation, one verifies by direct computation using the linear recurrences for the coefficients of w_1 and w_2 that the b_k satisfy exactly the recurrence (9.13)–(9.14). By uniqueness of the formal Puiseux solution (Theorem 9.8), b_k = a_k for all k. Therefore g(z) = W(z) on the disk of convergence, and by analytic continuation g extends to the meromorphic function W on ℂ.

Lemma 9.22 (Absence of poles on the positive real axis). Let y(t) be the solution of (9.1) on [0,∞). Then g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) has no poles on [0,∞). Consequently, for every T > 0,

  δ_T := dist( [0,T^μ], 𝓟_g ) > 0.  (9.45)

Proof. The Volterra equation (9.10) has a unique continuous solution on [0,∞) for the parameter ranges arising in rough Heston (the quadratic nonlinearity is locally Lipschitz and the kernel is integrable). A pole of g at z = t^μ > 0 would correspond to a blow-up of y(t) at finite positive t, contradicting the global existence on [0,∞). Hence 𝓟_g ∩ [0,∞) = ∅, and since 𝓟_g is discrete, the distance δ_T is strictly positive.

Lemma 9.23 (Non-Explosion Condition on compact intervals). For every T > 0, the diagonal Padé approximants [M/M]_g = R_M satisfy the Non-Explosion Condition on K = [0,T^μ]: the number of poles of R_M in K is uniformly bounded in M.

Proof. By Proposition 9.21, g is meromorphic with discrete pole set 𝓟_g. Let δ_T > 0 be as in (9.45). The poles of R_M fall into two classes:

(a) True-pole approximants. These converge to the elements of 𝓟_g (de Montessus de Ballore). For M sufficiently large, every such pole lies within δ_T/2 of some element of 𝓟_g, hence outside K.

(b) Froissart doublets. By Proposition 4.2, the number of Froissart doublets of R_M in any compact subset of ℂ \ 𝓟_g is O(1) as M → ∞.

Therefore, for all sufficiently large M, the only poles of R_M that can lie in K are Froissart doublets, whose number is uniformly bounded. The Non-Explosion Condition holds.

Theorem 9.24 (Global Padé–Müntz representation). Let y(t) solve the fractional Riccati equation (9.1) with μ ∈ (0,1]. Then for every t ≥ 0,

  y(t) = lim_{M→∞} R_M(t^μ) = lim_{M→∞} P_M(t^μ)/Q_M(t^μ).  (9.46)

Moreover, for every T > 0, the convergence is uniform on [0,T].

Proof. By Proposition 9.21, g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) is meromorphic on ℂ. By Lemma 9.22, [0,T^μ] is disjoint from the pole set 𝓟_g. Lemma 9.23 establishes the Non-Explosion Condition on [0,T^μ]. Applying Theorem 4.4 to the compact K = [0,T^μ], R_M → g uniformly on [0,T^μ]. Since T > 0 is arbitrary, uniform convergence holds on every compact subset of [0,∞), and pointwise convergence holds for every t ≥ 0.

§9.7. The bordered rank-one update and incremental Hankel solver
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The passage from Padé order M to order M+1 requires the solution of the augmented Hankel system H_{M+1} 𝐪_{M+1} = −𝐛_{M+1}. Direct inversion costs O(M³). The Hankel structure permits an incremental O(M²) update via a bordered rank-one correction.

Lemma 9.25 (Bordered structure of the augmented Hankel system). Let P_M be the cyclic column permutation that moves the first column to the last position. Then

  Ĥ_{M+1} := H_{M+1} P_M = ⎛ H_M   𝐮_M ⎞,  (9.47)
              ⎝ 𝐯_M^T  d_M ⎠

where

  𝐮_M = (a_{M+1}, a_{M+2}, …, a_{2M})^T ∈ ℂ^M,
  𝐯_M^T = (a_{2M}, a_{2M−1}, …, a_{M+1}) ∈ ℂ^{1×M},
  d_M = a_{2M+1} ∈ ℂ.

Proof. The matrix H_{M+1} P_M has columns 2,3,…,M+1,1 of H_{M+1}. The first M columns are therefore the top-right M×M block of H_{M+1}, which is H_M by direct inspection of (9.35). The last column is the first column of H_{M+1}, namely (a_{M+1},…,a_{2M+1})^T, giving 𝐮_M and d_M. The last row of the first M columns is (a_{2M}, a_{2M−1},…,a_{M+1}), giving 𝐯_M^T.

Theorem 9.26 (Bordered rank-one update of the inverse). Assume H_M is invertible and define the Schur complement

  s_M = d_M − 𝐯_M^T H_M^{-1} 𝐮_M.  (9.48)

If s_M ≠ 0, then H_{M+1} is invertible and

  Ĥ_{M+1}^{-1} = ⎛ H_M^{-1} + (1/s_M) 𝐳_M 𝐰_M^T   −(1/s_M) 𝐳_M ⎞,  (9.49)
          ⎝ −(1/s_M) 𝐰_M^T              1/s_M       ⎠

where

  𝐳_M = H_M^{-1} 𝐮_M, 𝐰_M^T = 𝐯_M^T H_M^{-1}.  (9.50)

The update from H_M^{-1} to Ĥ_{M+1}^{-1} requires two matrix-vector products, one inner product, and one rank-one outer product, for a total cost of O(M²) arithmetic operations.

Proof. This is the standard block inversion formula for a 2×2 block matrix with invertible leading block and nonzero Schur complement. The matrix 𝐳_M 𝐰_M^T is rank one, hence the name. The cost breakdown is: H_M^{-1} 𝐮_M costs O(M²); 𝐯_M^T H_M^{-1} costs O(M²); 𝐯_M^T 𝐳_M costs O(M); the outer product 𝐳_M 𝐰_M^T costs O(M²).

Corollary 9.27 (Incremental denominator solver). Given H_M^{-1} and the solution 𝐪_M of H_M 𝐪_M = −𝐛_M, the augmented solution 𝐪_{M+1} of H_{M+1} 𝐪_{M+1} = −𝐛_{M+1} is obtained as follows:

(i) Compute 𝐳_M = H_M^{-1} 𝐮_M and 𝐰_M^T = 𝐯_M^T H_M^{-1} via (9.50).
(ii) Compute the Schur complement s_M = d_M − 𝐰_M^T 𝐮_M.
(iii) Update the inverse via (9.49) to obtain Ĥ_{M+1}^{-1}.
(iv) Solve Ĥ_{M+1} 𝐪̂_{M+1} = −𝐛_{M+1} using the updated inverse.
(v) Permute back: 𝐪_{M+1} = P_M 𝐪̂_{M+1}.

The total cost of the increment is O(M²). Amortized over M = 1,2,…,N, the cumulative cost to reach order N is O(N²).

Remark 9.28 (Connection to the Jacobi truncation). Theorem 9.26 is the algebraic realization of the Jacobi truncation described in Theorem 9.29. Adding one row and column to J_M to produce J_{M+1} corresponds to bordering the Hankel matrix. The rank-one correction 𝐳_M 𝐰_M^T encodes the spectral perturbation induced by the new Jacobi entry, and the resulting Padé iterate R_{M+1} is the resolvent of the enlarged operator at the basis vector e_0.

§9.8. Spectral interpretation and operator-theoretic synthesis
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Theorem 9.29 (Hankel solver as Jacobi truncation). The incremental Hankel solver of Corollary 9.27, which builds H_{M+1}^{-1} from H_M^{-1} via the bordered rank-one update (9.49) in O(M²) operations, is the algebraic realization of the Jacobi truncation: it adds one row and column to J_M to produce J_{M+1}, and the resulting Padé iterate R_{M+1} is the resolvent of the enlarged operator at the basis vector e_0.

Proof. The three-term recurrence (6.2) for the orthogonal polynomials Q_n is encoded in the Hankel matrix H_n via the moment sequence {a_k}. Bordering H_M to H_{M+1} adds the next moment a_{2M+1} and shifts the existing block, which corresponds exactly to appending the new Jacobi entries (a_M, b_M) to J_M. The rank-one correction 𝐳_M 𝐰_M^T in (9.49) is the Sherman–Morrison form of the spectral update induced by this appended row and column. The Padé approximant R_{M+1}(z) = ⟨e_0, (J_{M+1} − zI)^{-1} e_0⟩ is precisely the resolvent of the truncated Jacobi operator, as stated in Theorem 6.4.

Corollary 9.30 (Convergence certificate as spectral gap). The successive-difference bound (9.42) certifies that the iterate R_M has entered the asymptotic regime where the tail is geometrically controlled by exp(−M g(ξ,∞)), with g the Green's function of the cut plane in the variable ξ = z = t^μ. The threshold |Δ_M|²/(|Δ_{M−1}|−|Δ_M|) < 2^{−bits} detects the spectral gap between the working point and the Stahl compact.

Theorem 9.31 (Compiled spectral reconstruction). Let Φ(u,T) denote the cumulant generating function of the rough Heston model under standard normalizations:

  Φ(u,T) = θλ ∫_0^T h(u,t) dt + V_0 I^{1−μ}[h(u,·)](T),  (9.51)

where h solves the fractional Riccati equation (9.1) with parameters (c_0, c_1, c_2) depending on u. Write ξ = T^μ. Then Φ admits the representation

  Φ(u,T) = T ∑_{k=0}^∞ d_k(u) ξ^k,  (9.52)

where

  d_0(u) = V_0 c_0(u),
  d_k(u) = θλ a_k(u)/(kμ+1) + V_0 Γ((k+1)μ+1)/Γ(kμ+2) a_{k+1}(u), k ≥ 1,  (9.53)

and the coefficients a_k(u) are the Puiseux coefficients of Theorem 9.8. The series (9.52) is a power series in ξ = T^μ and is resummed via diagonal Padé in ξ:

  Φ(u,T) = T · lim_{M→∞} [M/M]_{S(u,·)}(ξ), S(u,ξ) = ∑_{k=0}^∞ d_k(u) ξ^k.  (9.54)

Convergence is governed by Theorem 9.24 applied to the meromorphic function S(u,·), and is certified a-posteriori by Corollary 9.20.

Proof. Substituting the Müntz series h(u,t) = ∑_{k=1}^∞ a_k(u) t^{kμ} into (9.51) and using (9.4) for the fractional integral term yields

  Φ(u,T) = θλ ∑_{k=1}^∞ a_k(u) T^{kμ+1}/(kμ+1) + V_0 ∑_{k=1}^∞ a_k(u) Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k−1)μ+2) T^{(k−1)μ+1}.

Factoring out T and reindexing the second sum with j = k−1 gives

  Φ(u,T) = T [ V_0 a_1(u) Γ(μ+1) + ∑_{k=1}^∞ ( θλ a_k(u)/(kμ+1) + V_0 Γ((k+1)μ+1)/Γ(kμ+2) a_{k+1}(u) ) ξ^k ].

Since a_1(u) = c_0(u)/Γ(μ+1) and Γ(2) = 1, the constant term simplifies to V_0 c_0(u) = d_0(u). The coefficients of ξ^k for k ≥ 1 are exactly d_k(u), proving (9.52). The Padé resummation (9.54) follows by applying the construction of §9.4 to the series S(u,ξ).

§9.9. Algorithmic summary
──────────────────────────

Given (c_0, c_1, c_2, μ) and Padé order M:

1. Compute the Puiseux coefficients a_1,…,a_{2M} by the recurrence (9.13)–(9.14). Cost: O(M²).
2. Form the Hankel matrix H_M and right-hand side 𝐛 from (9.35).
3. Solve H_M 𝐪 = −𝐛 for the denominator coefficients. Use direct factorization for the base case M = 1, then increment via the bordered rank-one update of Corollary 9.27 for each subsequent order. Cumulative cost to order M: O(M²).
4. Recover the numerator coefficients via (9.33).
5. Evaluate y_M(t) = P_M(t^μ)/Q_M(t^μ) at any t ≥ 0. Cost: O(M) per query.

Steps 1–4 are independent of t. The entire family {y_M(t) : t ≥ 0} is encoded in a single rational function in z = t^μ. There is no grid, no time stepping, no Newton iteration, and no fixed-point loop at evaluation time.


§10. THE ESSENTIAL DIFFERENCE
─────────────────────────────

Taylor approximation asks: what polynomial has the same derivatives at this point? It is local, and its radius is the distance to the nearest singularity.

Padé approximation asks: what spectral measure produces these moments, and what is its Stieltjes transform? It is global, and its convergence domain is the maximal region on which the Riemann–Hilbert factorization exists. The poles are not defects; they are the discrete spectrum of a truncated Jacobi operator, and their condensation on the equilibrium contour is the signature of the underlying Riemann surface. The approximant continues f because it is solving for the measure that generated the series, and that reconstruction is valid wherever the logarithmic potential of the equilibrium measure controls the error.

In the rough Heston application, this distinction is decisive. The Müntz–Tau substitution produces a Puiseux series whose radius of convergence is finite and generically much smaller than the time horizon of practical interest. The series alone is therefore a local representation, useful only on [0,T_0] for the Banach radius T_0 of Theorem 9.10. Padé resummation in the variable z = t^μ converts the local representation into a global one, valid on all of [0,∞) by the meromorphy theorem (Proposition 9.21) and the uniform convergence theorem (Theorem 9.24), with computable a-posteriori error control. The mechanism is the same in every section of this paper: the discrete poles of the truncation reorganize themselves into the equilibrium configuration of the function's true singular set, and the rational function so produced is the Stieltjes transform of the spectral measure that the original series was secretly encoding.
