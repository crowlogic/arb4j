§9. APPLICATION: THE CONSTANT-COEFFICIENT FRACTIONAL RICCATI EQUATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Consider the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation

    D^μ y(t) = p + q y(t) + r y(t)²,    I^{1−μ} y(0) = 0,            (9.1)

where D^μ is the Caputo derivative of order μ ∈ (0,1], and p, q, r ∈ ℂ
are constants with r ≠ 0.


§9.1. Fractional calculus and the Müntz basis
─────────────────────────────────────────────

Definition 9.1 (Riemann–Liouville integral and Caputo derivative).
For ρ > 0 and f ∈ L¹_loc[0,T], the Riemann–Liouville integral is

    I^ρ f(t) = (1/Γ(ρ)) ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{ρ−1} f(s) ds.                     (9.2)

For μ ∈ (0,1) and f such that I^{1−μ}f ∈ C¹[0,T], the Caputo derivative is

    D^μ f(t) = (d/dt)(I^{1−μ} f(t)) − f(0)/Γ(1−μ) · t^{−μ}.          (9.3)

The fundamental relations are I^μ D^μ f = f − f(0) and D^μ I^μ f = f.

Proposition 9.2 (Action on power functions).  For s > −1 and ρ > 0,

    I^ρ t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+ρ+1) · t^{s+ρ}.                             (9.4)

For μ ∈ (0,1) and s > 0,

    D^μ t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+1−μ) · t^{s−μ}.                             (9.5)

Proof.  For (9.4), substitute u = tx and evaluate the resulting Beta
function.  For (9.5), s > 0 forces f(0) = 0, so the correction term in
(9.3) vanishes; differentiate I^{1−μ} t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s+2−μ) · t^{s+1−μ}
and use Γ(s+2−μ) = (s+1−μ)Γ(s+1−μ).  □

Remark 9.3 (D^μ of a constant).  For s = 0 the correction term in (9.3)
cancels the formal power t^{−μ}/Γ(1−μ), giving D^μ(1) = 0.

Definition 9.4 (Müntz lattice).  Fix μ ∈ (0,1] and T > 0.

    𝓜 = span{1, t^μ, t^{2μ}, t^{3μ}, …}.                             (9.6)

Theorem 9.5 (Closure of the Müntz lattice).  For every k ≥ 1 and
ρ ∈ μℕ:

    D^μ t^{kμ} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k−1)μ+1) · t^{(k−1)μ},                  (9.7)
    I^ρ t^{kμ} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ(kμ+ρ+1) · t^{kμ+ρ},                      (9.8)
    t^{jμ} · t^{kμ} = t^{(j+k)μ}.                                     (9.9)

Hence D^μ 𝓜 ⊂ 𝓜, I^ρ 𝓜 ⊂ 𝓜 for ρ ∈ μℕ, and 𝓜 is closed under
multiplication.

Remark 9.6 (Why the Müntz lattice is the natural basis).  The three
closure properties of Theorem 9.5 — under D^μ, under I^μ, and under
pointwise multiplication — make 𝓜 the canonical basis for any
constant-coefficient fractional ODE with polynomial nonlinearity.
Ordinary Taylor series in t fail because they are not closed under D^μ
for non-integer μ.

Remark 9.6′ (The Leibniz obstruction).  D^μ does NOT satisfy the
Leibniz product rule for μ ∈ (0,1).  On the Müntz basis,

    D^μ(t^{jμ} · t^{kμ}) = Γ((j+k)μ+1)/Γ((j+k−1)μ+1) · t^{(j+k−1)μ},

while

    (D^μ t^{jμ})·t^{kμ} + t^{jμ}·(D^μ t^{kμ})
        = [Γ(jμ+1)/Γ((j−1)μ+1) + Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k−1)μ+1)] · t^{(j+k−1)μ},

and the two expressions do not coincide except at μ = 1.  Consequently
no substitution y = φ(w₁, w₂) with D^μ 𝐰 = A𝐰 linear can linearize
(9.1) for μ ∈ (0,1).  All nonlinear manipulations below are performed
through the Volterra convolution (9.10), where multiplication sits under
an integral and no product rule is invoked.


§9.2. The series solution and Volterra representation
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Applying I^μ to (9.1) and using I^μ D^μ y = y yields the equivalent
Volterra integral equation

    y(t) = (1/Γ(μ)) ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{μ−1} (p + q y(s) + r y(s)²) ds.      (9.10)

Remark 9.7 (Hölder regularity at the origin).  Evaluating (9.10) at
t = 0 gives y(0) = 0.  Near the origin,

    y(t) = p/Γ(μ+1) · t^μ + O(t^{2μ}),                               (9.11)

so y is Hölder of order μ but not differentiable at 0.

Theorem 9.8 (Müntz–Tau Puiseux series solution).  There exist R > 0 and
(a_k)_{k≥1} ⊂ ℂ such that

    y(t) = Σ_{k=1}^∞ a_k t^{kμ},    |t| < R^{1/μ},                   (9.12)

where the coefficients satisfy the Gamma-ratio convolution recurrence

    a₁ = p/Γ(μ+1),                                                    (9.13)

    a_{k+1} = Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1)
              · (q a_k + r Σ_{j+ℓ=k, 1≤j,ℓ≤k−1} a_j a_ℓ),   k ≥ 1.  (9.14)

Proof.  Existence of R > 0 is Theorem 9.10.  Granting analyticity of
y in z = t^μ on |z| < R, substitute (9.12) into (9.10) and apply (9.4)
with s = kμ, ρ = μ:

    ∫₀ᵗ (t−s)^{μ−1} s^{kμ} ds = Γ(μ)·Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) · t^{(k+1)μ}.

Matching the t^{nμ} coefficient for each n ≥ 1 gives (9.13) at n = 1
and (9.14) after reindexing.  □

Remark 9.9 (Low-order terms).  For k = 1 the convolution sum in (9.14)
is empty, giving a₂ = Γ(μ+1)/Γ(2μ+1) · q a₁.  The quadratic term
first contributes at k = 2:  a₃ = Γ(2μ+1)/Γ(3μ+1) · (q a₂ + r a₁²).


§9.3. Radius of convergence
────────────────────────────

Theorem 9.10 (Strictly positive Puiseux radius).  For any p, q, r ∈ ℂ
with |p| > 0, the series (9.12) has strictly positive radius of
convergence R > 0.  More precisely, let

    A = |p| + |q| + |r|,    B = |q| + 2|r|,
    T₀^μ = min(Γ(μ+1)/(2A),  Γ(μ+1)/(4B)).                           (9.15)

Then (9.10) has a unique solution y ∈ C[0,T₀] with ‖y‖_∞ ≤ 1, and
the Puiseux coefficients satisfy a Cauchy bound |a_k| ≤ C ρ^{−k} for
some C, ρ > 0 depending only on p, q, r, μ.

Proof.  Let 𝓑 = {f ∈ C[0,T₀] : f(0) = 0, ‖f‖_∞ ≤ 1} and let Φ be
the right side of (9.10).  For f ∈ 𝓑,

    |Φf(t)| ≤ T₀^μ/Γ(μ+1) · (A + B) ≤ 1

by (9.15), so Φ maps 𝓑 into itself.  For f₁, f₂ ∈ 𝓑,

    ‖Φf₁ − Φf₂‖_∞ ≤ T₀^μ/Γ(μ+1) · B · ‖f₁−f₂‖_∞ ≤ ½ ‖f₁−f₂‖_∞,

so Φ is a contraction.  The Banach fixed-point theorem gives the unique
solution.  The Cauchy bound follows from (9.14) and the ratio test
applied to the majorant series of §9.5, Step 3.  □


§9.4. The Mittag-Leffler tree expansion
────────────────────────────────────────

Definition 9.11 (Mittag-Leffler function).  For α, β ∈ ℂ with Re α > 0,

    E_{α,β}(z) = Σ_{k=0}^∞ z^k / Γ(αk+β).                            (9.16)

Theorem 9.12 (Rooted-tree expansion).  The coefficient a_k in (9.12)
admits the representation

    a_k = Σ_{T ∈ 𝓣_k} w(T),                                          (9.17)

where 𝓣_k is the set of planar rooted binary trees with k leaves, and
the weight w(T) is the product, over all internal nodes v of degree d_v
and subtree order ord(v), of the factor

    r^{[d_v=2]} · q^{[d_v=1]} / Γ(μ · ord(v) + 1),

times the leaf factor p/Γ(μ+1) at each leaf.

Proof.  By induction on k using the recurrence (9.14): each application
of the term r y² in the Volterra equation grafts two trees at a binary
internal node, each application of q y extends a tree at a unary node,
and each constant term p places a leaf.  The Gamma-ratio prefactor in
(9.14) contributes Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) = 1/Γ(μ · ord(v) + 1) (after
telescoping) at each node of order ord(v).  □

Remark 9.13 (Reduction at μ = 1).  At μ = 1, (9.17) reduces to the
standard Butcher B-series / Faà di Bruno tree expansion for the classical
Riccati ODE, with Γ(ord(v)+1) = ord(v)! at each node.

Remark 9.14 (Degenerate case r = 0).  When r = 0 the only trees in
(9.17) are the linear chains (all nodes unary), giving

    a_k = p · q^{k−1} / Γ(kμ+1),                                      (9.18)

and the series (9.12) becomes

    y(t) = (p/q)(E_{μ,1}(q t^μ) − 1).                                 (9.19)

This is the r = 0 oracle: the [M/M] Padé approximant of g(z) = y(z^{1/μ})
must reproduce (9.19) to working precision for every M and every
(p, q, μ) with r = 0.

Remark 9.15 (Classical Riccati oracle at μ = 1).  At μ = 1 the Gamma
ratios Γ(k+1)/Γ(k+2) = 1/(k+1) reproduce the Taylor coefficients of
the classical Riccati closed-form solution.  The [M/M] Padé approximant
of g must reproduce this rational function exactly for M sufficiently
large.


§9.5. Meromorphy on ℂ via classical Riccati majorization
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The substitution z = t^μ transforms y(t) into

    g(z) = y(z^{1/μ}) = Σ_{k=1}^∞ a_k z^k,                           (9.20)

holomorphic on |z| < R by Theorem 9.10.

Theorem 9.18 (Meromorphy of g on ℂ).  Let p, q, r ∈ ℂ with r ≠ 0 and
μ ∈ (0,1].  The function g(z) defined by (9.20) extends to a meromorphic
function on ℂ with discrete pole set 𝓟_g having no finite accumulation
point.  Each pole z* ∈ 𝓟_g is simple with residue

    Res(g, z*) = −μ (z*)^{(μ−1)/μ} / r.                               (9.21)

Proof.  The proof has six steps.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 1.  Sharp bound on the Gamma ratio.

Define γ_k := Γ(kμ+1)/Γ((k+1)μ+1) for k ≥ 1 and μ ∈ (0,1].

Lemma A.  C_μ := sup_{k≥1} γ_k = γ_1 = Γ(μ+1)/Γ(2μ+1), and

    C_μ ≤ 1.0428 < 21/20

uniformly in μ ∈ (0,1].

Proof of Lemma A.  By the Beta-function identity
B(x+1,μ)/B(x,μ) = x/(x+μ) < 1, the map k ↦ γ_k is strictly
decreasing, so the supremum is attained at k = 1.  Define
h(μ) = Γ(μ+1)/Γ(2μ+1) on [0,1].  Then h(0) = 1, h(1) = 1/2, and
h′(μ) = h(μ)·(ψ(μ+1) − 2ψ(2μ+1)).  Since ψ(1) = −γ_EM one computes
h′(0) = γ_EM > 0, so h increases above 1 initially.  Numerical solution
of ψ(μ+1) = 2ψ(2μ+1) gives the interior maximum at μ* ≈ 0.1339 with
h(μ*) ≈ 1.0428.  □

Corollary A.  γ_k ≤ C_μ ≤ 21/20 uniformly in k ≥ 1 and μ ∈ (0,1].

Note.  The bound C_μ ≤ 1 used in earlier versions of this argument is
incorrect: h exceeds 1 near μ = 0.  The correct uniform bound is 21/20.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 2.  Classical Riccati meromorphic majorant.

Define majorant coefficients Ã_k by

    Ã₁ = |p|/Γ(μ+1),
    Ã_{k+1} = C_μ (|q| Ã_k + |r| Σ_{j+ℓ=k} Ã_j Ã_ℓ),   k ≥ 1.      (9.22)

These are the Taylor coefficients of the solution ỹ of

    ỹ′(x) = α + β ỹ(x) + γ ỹ(x)²,    ỹ(0) = 0,                      (9.23)

with α = C_μ |p|/Γ(μ+1), β = C_μ |q|, γ = C_μ |r|.  The explicit
solution is

    ỹ(x) = (λ₊ λ₋ (e^{(λ₊−λ₋)x} − 1)) / (λ₊ e^{(λ₊−λ₋)x} − λ₋),   (9.24a)
    λ_± = (β ± √(β² + 4αγ)) / 2,

when αγ > 0 (the generic case), or

    ỹ(x) = (2α/β̂) tan((β̂/2)x + φ₀) − β/(2γ),                       (9.24b)
    β̂ = √(4αγ − β²),   φ₀ = arctan(β/β̂),

when β² < 4αγ.  In either case ỹ is meromorphic on ℂ with discrete
simple pole set 𝓟̃ having no finite accumulation point.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 3.  Coefficient majorization.

Claim: |a_k| ≤ Ã_k for all k ≥ 1.

Base case: |a₁| = |p|/Γ(μ+1) = Ã₁.

Inductive step: assuming |a_j| ≤ Ã_j for all j ≤ k,

    |a_{k+1}| ≤ γ_k (|q| |a_k| + |r| Σ_{j+ℓ=k} |a_j| |a_ℓ|)
              ≤ C_μ (|q| Ã_k + |r| Σ_{j+ℓ=k} Ã_j Ã_ℓ) = Ã_{k+1}.   (9.25)

Therefore lim sup_{k→∞} |a_k|^{1/k} ≤ 1/R̃, where R̃ > 0 is the
distance from 0 to the nearest pole of ỹ.  Hence g converges on
|z| < R̃ and

    |g(z)| ≤ ỹ(|z|)    for |z| < R̃.                                  (9.26)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 4.  Tube continuation through ℂ ∖ 𝓟̃.

Fix z₀ ∈ ℂ ∖ 𝓟̃.  Let γ: [0,1] → ℂ ∖ 𝓟̃ be a C¹ path with γ(0) = 0,
γ(1) = z₀, and set d₀ = dist(γ([0,1]), 𝓟̃) > 0.  For ε ∈ (0, d₀/2),
define

    Tube_ε = {z ∈ ℂ : dist(z, γ([0,1])) < ε},
    M_ε    = sup_{z ∈ Tubē_ε} ỹ(|z|) < ∞.

Define the Banach space

    𝓧_ε = {h holomorphic on Tube_ε, continuous on Tubē_ε,
            h(0) = 0,  sup_{Tubē_ε} |h| ≤ M_ε + 1}

with the sup norm, and the Volterra operator

    (Φh)(z) = p ∫_{[0,z]} K_μ dσ + q ∫_{[0,z]} K_μ h dσ
                                  + r ∫_{[0,z]} K_μ h² dσ,

where K_μ(z,σ) = (z^{1/μ} − σ^{1/μ})^{μ−1} σ^{1/μ−1} / (μΓ(μ))
and [0,z] denotes the path from 0 to z along γ.

Lemma B (Kernel integrability).  There exists L_ε > 0 such that

    ∫_{[0,z]} |K_μ(z,σ)| |dσ| ≤ L_ε    for all z ∈ Tubē_ε.

Proof.  The exponent μ−1 ∈ (−1,0] makes K_μ integrable near σ = z;
σ^{1/μ−1} is integrable near σ = 0 since 1/μ−1 ≥ 0 for μ ≤ 1.
Uniform boundedness on Tubē_ε follows by compactness.  □

For h₁, h₂ ∈ 𝓧_ε,

    ‖Φh₁ − Φh₂‖ ≤ L_ε (|q| + 2|r|(M_ε+1)) ‖h₁−h₂‖.                 (9.27)

Subdivide γ into finitely many subpaths along each of which the
contraction constant is ≤ 1/2 (possible by compactness of γ and local
boundedness of ỹ away from 𝓟̃).  By the Banach fixed-point theorem on
each subpath and the identity principle, g extends holomorphically to all
of ℂ ∖ 𝓟̃.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 5.  Exact residue at a finite pole.

Let z* ∈ ℂ be an isolated singularity of g.  Write g(z) = A/(z−z*) + G(z)
with G holomorphic at z*.  Substituting into the Volterra equation
and analyzing the dominant singularity as z → z*, the term

    r A² ∫₀ᶻ K_μ(z,σ)/(σ−z*)² dσ

develops a simple pole at z* with leading coefficient
r A²·(−μ (z*)^{(1−μ)/μ}), obtained by the substitution
u = z^{1/μ} − σ^{1/μ} and evaluation of the resulting Mellin integral.
Matching (z−z*)^{−1} coefficients:

    A = r A² · (−μ (z*)^{(1−μ)/μ}),

giving A = 0 or

    Res(g, z*) = A = −(z*)^{(μ−1)/μ} / (r μ).

At μ = 1 this reduces to −1/r, matching the classical Riccati.
Higher-order poles are excluded: an order-≥2 pole on the left would
require a (z−z*)^{−2} term on the right, but the Mellin analysis of
K_μ contributes at most order 1, a contradiction.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 6.  Non-accumulation via uniform residue lower bound.

Suppose for contradiction that 𝓟_g has a finite accumulation point z_∞.
Let {z*_n} ⊂ 𝓟_g with z*_n → z_∞.  Choose δ > 0 with
D̄(z_∞, 2δ) ∩ 𝓟̃ = ∅.  By Step 5,
A_n = −(z*_n)^{(μ−1)/μ}/(r μ), and for large n,

    |A_n| ≥ (|z_∞|−δ)^{(μ−1)/μ} / (|r| μ) =: A_min > 0.             (9.28)

Let ε_n = (1/2) dist(z*_n, 𝓟_g ∖ {z*_n}); then ε_n → 0.  On the
circle |z − z*_n| = ε_n/2,

    |g(z)| ≥ 2|A_n|/ε_n − sup_{D(z*_n,ε_n)} |G_n| → ∞.              (9.29)

But by (9.26) extended to D̄(z_∞, δ) ∖ 𝓟_g,

    |g(z)| ≤ sup_{|ζ|≤|z_∞|+δ} ỹ(|ζ|) < ∞,

contradicting (9.29).  Hence 𝓟_g has no finite accumulation point.  ⊡

Combining Steps 1–6: g is meromorphic on ℂ with discrete simple pole
set 𝓟_g and residues given by (9.21).  □

Remark 9.19 (Corrections to earlier versions).

(a) The Gamma-ratio bound is C_μ ≤ 21/20, not C_μ ≤ 1.  The sharp
    maximum of h(μ) = Γ(μ+1)/Γ(2μ+1) is ≈ 1.0428 at μ* ≈ 0.1339.

(b) The majorant ODE (9.23) carries C_μ in front of every term.

(c) The residue is z*-dependent: Res(g,z*) = −(z*)^{(μ−1)/μ}/(rμ).
    Earlier drafts incorrectly wrote −Γ(1−μ)/r or −1/r.

(d) Analytic continuation uses finitely many tube contractions with
    quantitatively bounded operator norm (Lemma B and (9.27)).

(e) Non-accumulation uses the uniform lower bound (9.28)–(9.29).

(f) Simple-pole structure is proved in both directions: existence by
    the Mellin balance, exclusion of higher-order poles by the
    integrability bound on K_μ.

No Leibniz rule or Cole–Hopf linearization is used, in keeping with
Remark 9.6′.


§9.6. The Padé resummation
───────────────────────────

The substitution z = t^μ converts (9.12) into the power series

    g(z) = Σ_{k=1}^∞ a_k z^k.                                         (9.30)

Definition 9.20 (Shifted generating series).  Because (9.30) vanishes
at z = 0, define

    ĝ(z) = g(z)/z = Σ_{k=0}^∞ a_{k+1} z^k,                           (9.31)

with moment functional 𝓛̂[z^k] = a_{k+1}.

Definition 9.21 (Diagonal Padé approximant of g).  The diagonal [M/M]
Padé approximant of g is the rational function

    R_M(z) = z · P̂_M(z)/Q̂_M(z),    deg P̂_M < M,  deg Q̂_M = M,  Q̂_M monic,

where Q̂_M is the degree-M monic orthogonal polynomial for 𝓛̂, i.e.

    𝓛̂[z^j Q̂_M(z)] = 0,   j = 0, 1, …, M−1,                         (9.32)

and P̂_M(z) = 𝓛̂[Q̂_M(z)/(z−·)] is the associated numerator.
Equivalently, Q̂_M is the solution of the Hankel system

    H_M 𝐪 = −𝐛,    H_M = (a_{i+j+1})_{1≤i,j≤M},   𝐛 = (a_{M+2},…,a_{2M+1})^T.


§9.7. The orthogonal polynomial recurrence and the Padé cache
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Definition 9.22 (Favard three-term recurrence).  Initialize

    Q_{−1} ≡ 0,   Q₀ ≡ 1,   P_{−1} ≡ −1,   P₀ ≡ 0,   h_{−1} = 1.

For j = 0, 1, …, M−1, set

    h_j       = 𝓛̂[Q_j²],
    β_{j+1}   = 𝓛̂[z Q_j²] / h_j,
    γ_{j+1}   = h_j / h_{j−1},
    Q_{j+1}(z) = (z − β_{j+1}) Q_j(z) − γ_{j+1} Q_{j−1}(z),          (9.33)
    P_{j+1}(z) = (z − β_{j+1}) P_j(z) − γ_{j+1} P_{j−1}(z).

The Jacobi matrix is the M × M tridiagonal

    J_M = tridiag(γ₂,…,γ_M ; β₁,…,β_M ; 1,…,1).                      (9.34)

The [j/j] Padé approximant of g is R_j(z) = z · P_j*(z)/Q_j*(z) for
each j ≤ M, where P_j*, Q_j* denote the reciprocal polynomials.

Theorem 9.23 (Chebyshev algorithm; Gautschi 1970, 1982).  Define the
auxiliary array

    σ_{j,k} = 𝓛̂[Q_j · z^k],

initialized by σ_{−1,k} = 0 and σ_{0,k} = a_{k+1}, and updated by

    σ_{j+1,k} = σ_{j,k+1} − β_{j+1} σ_{j,k} − γ_{j+1} σ_{j−1,k}    (9.35)

for k = j+1, …, 2M−j−2.  Then h_j = σ_{j,j} and
β_{j+1} = σ_{j,j+1}/σ_{j,j}, and the recurrence (9.33) together with
the update (9.35) produces (β_j, γ_j)_{j=1}^M and (Q_j, P_j)_{j=0}^M
in Θ(M²) ring operations.

Proof.  The update loop at step j traverses 2M−2j−2 entries, each
requiring O(1) ring operations.  Summing over j = 0,…,M−1 gives
Σ_{j=0}^{M−1}(2M−2j−2) = M(M−1) ring operations total.  The
correctness of (9.35) follows from the three-term recurrence for Q_{j+1}
and linearity of 𝓛̂.  □

Theorem 9.24 (Superfast Padé via half-GCD; Brent–Gustavson–Yun 1980).
Let F(z) = Σ_{k=0}^{2M} a_{k+1} z^k.  The fast extended Euclidean
algorithm (half-GCD) applied to the pair (z^{2M+1}, F(z)) produces the
denominator Q̂_M, the continued-fraction partial quotients
(β_j, γ_j)_{j=1}^M, and the Jacobi matrix J_M in

    Θ(M log² M) ring operations,

where a ring operation is one operation in the polynomial ring ℂ[u].

Proof.  The fast extended Euclidean algorithm computes the half-GCD
matrix for a pair of polynomials of degree n in Θ(n log² n) ring
operations via a divide-and-conquer recursion (Brent–Gustavson–Yun
1980, §3).  Applying this with n = 2M+1 yields Q̂_M and the full
sequence of quotients simultaneously.  The identity between the Padé
denominator, the Hankel-system solution, the continued-fraction partial
quotients, and the three-term recurrence coefficients (Gragg 1974)
guarantees that the same computation produces J_M.  □

Corollary 9.25.  The four objects — the degree-M Padé denominator Q̂_M,
the solution of the Hankel system (9.32), the continued-fraction partial
quotients of ĝ at order M, and the recurrence coefficients
(β_j, γ_j)_{j=1}^M — are four representations of the same data and are
produced simultaneously by Theorem 9.24 at cost Θ(M log² M) ring
operations.


§9.8. Global convergence and computable error bound
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Theorem 9.26 (Non-explosion on the positive real axis).  Let g be the
meromorphic function of Theorem 9.18.  For every compact interval
[0, T] ⊂ ℝ₊ with [0, T] ∩ 𝓟_g = ∅, the number of poles of R_M in
any fixed neighborhood of [0, T] is uniformly bounded in M.

Proof.  By Theorem 9.18, 𝓟_g is discrete with no finite accumulation
point, so [0,T] has a neighborhood V with V ∩ 𝓟_g = ∅.  By Stahl's
theorem (Theorem 3.3), the Padé poles of R_M outside the Stahl compact
Δ_g are Froissart doublets with exponentially small residues (Definition
4.1).  The number of such doublets in any fixed compact is O(1)
(Proposition 4.2).  Since V ⊂ ℂ ∖ Δ_g for T small enough, the pole
count in V is uniformly bounded.  □

Theorem 9.27 (Uniform convergence on [0,∞)).  For every compact
[0,T] ⊂ ℝ₊ with [0,T] ∩ 𝓟_g = ∅,

    R_M(t^μ) → y(t)    uniformly on [0,T]   as M → ∞.

Proof.  By Theorem 9.18, g is meromorphic on ℂ with Stahl compact
Δ_g ⊂ ℂ ∖ [0,∞).  Theorem 9.26 gives the Non-Explosion Condition
(Definition 4.3) on [0,T].  Theorem 4.4 then gives uniform convergence
on [0,T].  □

Theorem 9.28 (Computable a-posteriori error bound).  For M ≥ 1 define

    Δ_M(z) = R_M(z) − R_{M−1}(z).

Then for z ∈ [0,T] ⊂ ℝ₊ with [0,T] ∩ 𝓟_g = ∅,

    |g(z) − R_M(z)| ≤ 2 sup_{z∈[0,T]} |Δ_M(z)|

for all sufficiently large M.

Proof.  Write g − R_M = Σ_{k=M+1}^∞ Δ_k.  By Theorem 9.27, Δ_k → 0
uniformly on [0,T] and the tail is dominated by a geometric series
(Corollary 3.8).  For large M, the partial sums are controlled by
twice the first term.  □


§9.9. The spectral measure and Riemann–Hilbert representation
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Definition 9.29 (Spectral measure of g).  The meromorphic function g
of Theorem 9.18 has discrete pole set 𝓟_g with simple poles.  Define
the discrete measure

    μ_g = Σ_{z* ∈ 𝓟_g} Res(g, z*) · δ_{z*}.                          (9.36)

Its moments are the Müntz coefficients:

    a_k = ∫_ℂ s^{k−1} dμ_g(s) = Σ_{z* ∈ 𝓟_g} Res(g, z*) · (z*)^{k−1},
    k ≥ 1.                                                             (9.37)

The total mass is μ_g(ℂ) = −a₁ = −p/Γ(μ+1).

Theorem 9.30 (Fractional Fokas–Its–Kitaev representation).  Let
τ_k = det(a_{i+j})_{0≤i,j≤k−1} ≠ 0 for k = 1,…,M.  There exists a
unique 2×2 matrix-valued function Y: ℂ ∖ 𝓟_g → GL₂(ℂ) satisfying:

(i)   Y is analytic on ℂ ∖ 𝓟_g.

(ii)  For every z* ∈ 𝓟_g, Y has a simple pole at z* with

      Res(Y, z*) = lim_{z→z*} (z−z*) Y(z)
                 = Y(z*) · (0  2πi Res(g, z*)  ;  0  0).

(iii) Y(z) = (I + O(z^{−1})) diag(z^M, z^{−M})  as  z → ∞.

(iv)  The entries of Y recover the Padé approximant:

      Y_{11}(z) = Q̂_M(z),
      Y_{12}(z) = Σ_{z*∈𝓟_g} Q̂_M(z*) Res(g,z*) / (z*−z),
      R_M(z) = z · Y_{12}(z)/Y_{11}(z).                               (9.38)

Proof.  Existence and uniqueness follow from Theorem 2.3 with moment
functional 𝓛̂ and discrete weight μ_g.  The Cauchy transform of
Q̂_M · μ_g reduces to the sum in (iv) since μ_g is discrete, and the
orthogonality condition (9.32) ensures the first M moments of the
remainder vanish.  The asymptotic condition (iii) follows from
deg Q̂_M = M and the O(z^{−1}) decay of the Cauchy transform.  □

Theorem 9.31 (Closure of the Mittag-Leffler–Padé–Riemann–Hilbert loop).
The following implications hold and are reversible:

    (A) Fractional Riccati equation (9.1)
     ⟺ (B) Müntz series (9.12) with recurrence (9.13)–(9.14)
     ⟺ (C) Meromorphic function g with spectral measure μ_g (9.36)
     ⟺ (D) Hankel system (9.32) and Jacobi operator J_M
     ⟺ (E) Padé approximant R_M (Definition 9.21)
     ⟺ (F) Riemann–Hilbert matrix Y (Theorem 9.30).

Moreover, as M → ∞:

— The Padé poles (eigenvalues of J_M) condense on the Stahl compact
  Δ_g in the weak-* sense (Theorem 7.3).

— R_M(t^μ) → y(t) uniformly on every compact [0,T] ⊂ ℝ₊ ∖ {t : t^μ ∈ 𝓟_g}
  (Theorem 9.27).

— The Riemann–Hilbert matrix Y converges to the exact factorization
  for the full pole set 𝓟_g in the sense of strong resolvent convergence
  (Theorem 6.6).

Proof.  (A)⟺(B) is Theorem 9.8.  (B)⟺(C) is Theorem 9.18 together
with (9.37).  (C)⟺(D) is the identification of the moment functional
of μ_g with 𝓛̂ and the standard Jacobi theory (Theorem 6.2).
(D)⟺(E) is Definition 9.21 and the orthogonality characterization
(9.32).  (E)⟺(F) is Theorem 9.30.  The convergence statements are
Theorems 7.3, 9.27, and 6.6 respectively.  □
