APPENDIX A.  SPECTRAL THEORY OF THE LOCAL MEAN-SQUARE DENSITY μ(t)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


A.1  Statement
──────────────

The local mean-square density

    μ(t) = dI/dT|_{T=t}
                                                        ∞
         = log(t/2π) + 2γ + 2 Σ  d(n)/√n · cos(t log n)
                                                       n=2

is the exact Radon–Nikodým derivative of Ingham's cumulative measure
I(T) = ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt with respect to Lebesgue measure on the
t-line.  It is positive for all t > 0, since it is the local density
of a positive integral.  It serves as the exact normalizing amplitude
in the construction of the stationary zeta process:

    ζ(½+it) = √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t)).

This appendix examines the spectral-theoretic properties of μ(t)
itself.


A.2  μ(t) is not a spectral density
────────────────────────────────────

A spectral density S(ω) of a finite-variance stationary process must
satisfy ∫ S(ω) dω < ∞, the integral giving the variance of the
process.  Since μ(t) ~ log(t/2π) → ∞, the integral diverges:

    ∫₋∞^∞ μ(ω) dω = ∞.

Therefore μ cannot be the spectral density of any finite-variance
stationary process on ℝ.


A.3  Decomposition into secular and oscillatory parts
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Write μ(t) = μ₀(t) + E′(t), where

    μ₀(t) = log(t/2π) + 2γ         (secular growth),
                          ∞
    E′(t) = 2 Σ  d(n)/√n · cos(t log n)   (oscillatory part).
                         n=2

Here E′(t) is the derivative of Ingham's error term

                   ∞
    E(T) = 2 Σ  d(n)/√n · sin(T log n) / log n.
                  n=2

The secular part μ₀(t) grows without bound and is responsible for
the divergence of ∫ μ(ω) dω.  The oscillatory part E′(t) is a
countable superposition of cosines at frequencies {log n : n ≥ 2}.


A.4  E′(t) as an almost periodic function
──────────────────────────────────────────

The oscillatory part

                    ∞
    E′(t) = 2 Σ  d(n)/√n · cos(t log n)
                   n=2

is an almost periodic function in the sense of Bohr–Besicovitch.  Its
constituent frequencies are log 2, log 3, log 4, log 5, … , which
are generated by the ℚ-linearly independent set {log p : p prime}
via the fundamental theorem of arithmetic: log n = Σ_{p|n} a_p log p.
The frequencies log n for composite n are ℤ-linear combinations of
the prime frequencies.

The distributional Fourier transform of E′(t) is a pure point
measure:

                          ∞
    Ê′(ω) = 2π Σ  d(n)/√n · [δ(ω − log n) + δ(ω + log n)].
                         n=2

This is a discrete spectral measure supported on the countable set
{±log n : n ≥ 2}, with weights proportional to d(n)/√n.  The weight
at ±log n encodes the divisor count of n: the more ways n factors,
the more spectral energy at frequency log n.


A.5  Pure point spectrum vs. absolutely continuous spectrum
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The spectral character of E′(t) is the opposite of that of ζ_st:

    ζ_st:   spectral density  π · 𝟙_{[-2,0]}(ω) dω
            absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure
            ⟹  mixing, hence ergodic

    E′(t):  spectral measure  Σ d(n)/√n · δ(ω ± log n)
            pure point (discrete)
            ⟹  not mixing; quasi-periodic

The normalizing amplitude √μ(t) thus bridges two fundamentally
different spectral regimes: the arithmetic almost-periodicity of the
divisor function (pure point) and the flat band-limited spectrum of
the stationary zeta process (absolutely continuous).


A.6  Connection to Motohashi's spectral decomposition
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The autocorrelation of μ(t) — more precisely, the shifted fourth
moment

    M(τ) = ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² |ζ(½+i(t+τ))|² dt

— has been studied by Motohashi (1993–1997), who proved that M(τ)
admits a spectral decomposition in terms of the eigenvalues of the
hyperbolic Laplacian Δ on the modular surface SL₂(ℤ)\ℍ.

Let {u_j} be the Maass cusp forms for SL₂(ℤ) with Laplacian
eigenvalues λ_j = ¼ + r_j², and let ρ_j(1) be the first Fourier
coefficient of u_j.  Motohashi's formula expresses the fourth moment
as a sum over these automorphic data, plus an Eisenstein-series
(continuous spectrum) contribution from the Laplacian.

The "spectrum of μ" in this automorphic sense consists of the Maass
eigenvalues {¼ + r_j²}, not frequencies on the real line.  This is
a spectral decomposition on the hyperbolic plane, fundamentally
distinct from the Fourier spectral decomposition on ℝ that governs
ζ_st.


A.7  Two spectral theories
───────────────────────────

The function μ(t) sits at the junction of two spectral theories:

(a)  Bochner / Paley–Wiener spectral theory.  This governs ζ_st.
     The spectral support is [−2, 0] ⊂ ℝ.  The spectral measure is
     absolutely continuous: dΦ(ω) = dω on [−2, 0].  The relevant
     functional analysis takes place in PW_{[−2,0]}, the Paley–Wiener
     space of entire functions of exponential type ≤ 2 that are L²
     on ℝ.  Bochner's theorem certifies positive definiteness of the
     covariance.  Kolmogorov's existence theorem furnishes the
     probability space.

(b)  Automorphic spectral theory.  This governs the fluctuations of
     μ(t) itself, and more precisely the correlations of |ζ(½+it)|².
     The spectral support is the discrete set of Maass eigenvalues
     {¼ + r_j²} for SL₂(ℤ)\ℍ, plus the continuous spectrum
     [¼, ∞) of Eisenstein series.  The relevant functional analysis
     takes place in L²(SL₂(ℤ)\ℍ), the Hilbert space of square-
     integrable automorphic functions.

The recovery formula ζ(½+it) = √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t)) is therefore a
factorization of zeta on the critical line into:

    √μ(t)        a factor whose internal correlations are governed
                  by the automorphic spectrum of SL₂(ℤ)\ℍ
                  (Motohashi's spectral decomposition);

    ζ_st(ϑ(t))   a factor whose spectral structure is the flat
                  density 𝟙_{[-2,0]} on the real frequency axis
                  (Bochner / Paley–Wiener spectral theory).


A.8  The weights d(n)/√n and ζ(s)²
───────────────────────────────────

The Dirichlet series generating function of the divisor function is

    Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/n^s = ζ(s)²,    Re(s) > 1.

The weights d(n)/√n appearing in E′(t) correspond to evaluating the
coefficients of ζ(s)² at s = ½, the edge of the critical strip.  The
series Σ d(n)/√n · cos(t log n) does not converge absolutely — it is
an oscillatory (conditionally convergent) series, reflecting the fact
that ζ(½+it)² is defined by analytic continuation, not by convergent
Dirichlet series.  The conditional convergence of this series is
precisely the content of Ingham's identity: the error term E(T)
is finite, and its derivative E′(t) defines a Besicovitch almost
periodic function.

The generating function identity Σ d(n)/n^s = ζ(s)² means that the
spectral weights of E′(t) at frequency log n are the Dirichlet
coefficients of the square of the zeta function.  The autocorrelation
of ζ on the critical line (the second moment) is controlled by the
additive structure of the Dirichlet coefficients of ζ; the auto-
correlation of |ζ|² on the critical line (the fourth moment) is
controlled by the multiplicative structure of these same coefficients,
via Motohashi's formula.


A.9  Spectral summary
─────────────────────

    Object             Spectral type          Support
    ─────────────────  ─────────────────────  ─────────────────────
    ζ_st               absolutely continuous   [−2, 0] ⊂ ℝ
    E′(t)              pure point (discrete)   {±log n : n ≥ 2} ⊂ ℝ
    correlations of μ  automorphic             {¼+r_j²} ∪ [¼,∞) on
                                               the spectral side of
                                               SL₂(ℤ)\ℍ

APPENDIX B.  THE HARDY Z-FUNCTION AND THE REAL STATIONARY PROCESS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


B.1  The Hardy Z-function
─────────────────────────

Definition (Hardy, 1920).  The Hardy Z-function is

    Z(t) = e^{iϑ(t)} ζ(½+it),

where ϑ(t) = Im log Γ(¼ + it/2) − (t/2) log π is the Riemann–Siegel
theta function.  Since the functional equation gives

    ζ(½+it) = χ(½+it) ζ(½−it)̄

with χ(½+it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)}, the function Z(t) is real-valued for
all real t:

    Z(t)̄  = e^{−iϑ(t)} ζ(½+it)̄  = e^{−iϑ(t)} ζ(½−it)
          = e^{−iϑ(t)} e^{2iϑ(t)} ζ(½+it) = e^{iϑ(t)} ζ(½+it) = Z(t).

The zeros of Z(t) on the real line are exactly the zeros of ζ(s) on
the critical line Re(s) = ½.  The Riemann Hypothesis is equivalent to
the statement that Z(t) has only real zeros.

The fundamental identity linking magnitudes is

    |Z(t)| = |ζ(½+it)|,

so that

    |Z(t)|² = |ζ(½+it)|²

and Ingham's identity applies to Z without modification:

    ∫₀ᵀ |Z(t)|² dt = ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt = I(T).


B.2  The real stationary process Z_st
──────────────────────────────────────

Definition (Real stationary zeta process).

    Z_st(τ) = Z(ϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √μ(ϑ⁻¹(τ)),    τ ≥ ϑ(T₀).

Since Z(t) is real-valued and μ(t) > 0, the process Z_st(τ) is
real-valued for all real τ.

Recovery formula:

    Z(t) = √μ(t) · Z_st(ϑ(t)).


B.3  Relation to ζ_st
──────────────────────

From the definitions,

    ζ_st(τ) = ζ(½ + iϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √μ(ϑ⁻¹(τ))
            = e^{−iϑ(ϑ⁻¹(τ))} Z(ϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √μ(ϑ⁻¹(τ))
            = e^{−iτ} Z_st(τ).

Therefore

    Z_st(τ) = e^{iτ} ζ_st(τ),

    ζ_st(τ) = e^{−iτ} Z_st(τ).

Multiplication by e^{iτ} shifts the Fourier spectrum by +1.


B.4  Covariance and spectral density of Z_st
─────────────────────────────────────────────

Proposition.  The Cesàro covariance of Z_st is

    R_Z(Δ) = sin(Δ)/Δ,

and its spectral density is

    Ŝ_Z(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[-1,1]}(ω).

Proof.  Since Z_st(τ) = e^{iτ} ζ_st(τ), the covariance is

    R_Z(Δ) = lim_{T→∞} (1/T) ∫ Z_st(τ+Δ) Z_st(τ)̄  dτ
           = lim (1/T) ∫ e^{i(τ+Δ)} ζ_st(τ+Δ) · e^{−iτ} ζ_st(τ)̄  dτ
           = e^{iΔ} · R(Δ)
           = e^{iΔ} · e^{−iΔ} sin(Δ)/Δ
           = sin(Δ)/Δ.

Alternatively, since Z_st is real-valued:

    R_Z(Δ) = R_Z(−Δ),

confirming that R_Z is real and symmetric, as required for a real-
valued process.

The Fourier transform of R_Z:

    ∫ (sin Δ/Δ) e^{−iωΔ} dΔ = π · 𝟙_{[-1,1]}(ω).

This is a real, symmetric spectral density supported on [−1, 1],
centered at ω = 0, consistent with Z_st being real-valued.  □


B.5  Paley–Wiener membership
─────────────────────────────

Since Ŝ_Z has compact support [−1, 1], the process Z_st belongs to
PW_{[-1,1]}, the space of entire functions of exponential type ≤ 1
that are L² on ℝ.  Every sample path of the associated Gaussian
process extends to an entire function of exponential type ≤ 1.

Note the shift: ζ_st ∈ PW_{[-2,0]} has exponential type ≤ 2, while
Z_st ∈ PW_{[-1,1]} has exponential type ≤ 1.  The reduction in type
is not a simplification — PW_{[-1,1]} and PW_{[-2,0]} are isomorphic
as Hilbert spaces via the map f(τ) ↦ e^{−iτ} f(τ).


B.6  Reproducing kernel of PW_{[-1,1]}
───────────────────────────────────────

The reproducing kernel is

    K_Z(σ, τ) = ∫₋₁¹ e^{iω(σ−τ)} dω = 2 sin(σ−τ)/(σ−τ).

This is real-valued and symmetric: K_Z(σ, τ) = K_Z(τ, σ).  The
Hermitian symmetry condition is automatically satisfied since the
kernel is real.


B.7  Spectral moments of Z_st
──────────────────────────────

The spectral moments are

    μₙ = ∫₋₁¹ ωⁿ dω.

The odd moments vanish by symmetry:

    μ₁ = 0,   μ₃ = 0,   μ₅ = 0,   …

The even moments are

    μ₀ = 2,   μ₂ = 2/3,   μ₄ = 2/5,   μ₆ = 2/7.

General formula: μ_{2k} = 2/(2k+1).


B.8  Covariance derivatives of Z_st at the origin
──────────────────────────────────────────────────

    R_Z^{(n)}(0) = iⁿ μₙ = iⁿ ∫₋₁¹ ωⁿ dω.

Since the odd moments vanish:

    R_Z(0)    = 1,
    R_Z′(0)   = 0,
    R_Z″(0)   = −2/3,
    R_Z‴(0)   = 0,
    R_Z⁽⁴⁾(0) = 2/5.

The vanishing of R_Z′(0) reflects the symmetry of the spectral
density about ω = 0.


B.9  The Gram matrix for Z_st
──────────────────────────────

Since Z_st is real-valued, the relevant Gram matrix is 2×2 real
(not a 4×4 real decomposition of a complex matrix):

         ┌                      ┐     ┌          ┐
    Γ =  │ R_Z(0)    R_Z′(0)   │  =  │  1    0  │
         │ R_Z′(0)  −R_Z″(0)   │     │  0   2/3 │
         └                      ┘     └          ┘

with det Γ = 1 · 2/3 − 0 · 0 = 2/3.

This matrix is diagonal.  The diagonal structure is a consequence of
R_Z′(0) = 0, which itself follows from the symmetry of the spectral
density 𝟙_{[-1,1]} about ω = 0.  In the complex case (ζ_st with
spectral density 𝟙_{[-2,0]}), the covariance derivative R′(0) = −i
is nonzero because the spectral density is not symmetric about
ω = 0, and the Gram matrix has off-diagonal entries.

Comparison:

    ζ_st (complex):   Γ = ( 1   −i )    det Γ = 1/3
                           ( i   4/3)

    Z_st (real):      Γ = ( 1    0  )    det Γ = 2/3
                           ( 0   2/3)

In both cases det Γ > 0, which is the condition required by
Bulinskaya's theorem.


B.10  Bulinskaya's theorem for Z_st
────────────────────────────────────

Since Z_st is a real-valued Gaussian process, the joint distribution
of (Z_st(τ), Z_st′(τ)) is a bivariate real Gaussian with covariance
matrix Γ.  Since det Γ = 2/3 > 0, this joint distribution is
non-degenerate at every τ.

Furthermore, R_Z′(0) = 0 means Z_st(τ) and Z_st′(τ) are
uncorrelated, and for a Gaussian process, uncorrelated implies
independent.

Bulinskaya's theorem for the real case:  If {X(τ)} is an almost
surely continuously differentiable real Gaussian process and the
joint distribution of (X(τ), X′(τ)) is non-degenerate at each τ,
then

    ℙ{ X(τ) = 0 and X′(τ) = 0 } = 0.

Since det Γ = 2/3 > 0, this applies to Z_st, and every zero of Z_st
is simple.


B.11  Recovery and simplicity transfer
───────────────────────────────────────

At a zero t₀ with Z(t₀) = 0:

    Z(t) = √μ(t) · Z_st(ϑ(t)),

    d/dt Z(t)|_{t₀} = [d/dt √μ(t)]|_{t₀} · Z_st(ϑ(t₀))
                     + √μ(t₀) · Z_st′(ϑ(t₀)) · ϑ′(t₀).

The first term vanishes since Z_st(ϑ(t₀)) = 0.  The second term is
nonzero since μ(t₀) > 0, ϑ′(t₀) > 0, and Z_st′(ϑ(t₀)) ≠ 0 by
Bulinskaya (§B.10).  Therefore Z′(t₀) ≠ 0 and every zero of Z on
the real line is simple.

Since Z′(t) = d/dt [e^{iϑ(t)} ζ(½+it)] = e^{iϑ(t)}[iϑ′(t) ζ(½+it)
+ iζ′(½+it)], at a zero ζ(½+it₀) = 0 this gives
Z′(t₀) = ie^{iϑ(t₀)} ζ′(½+it₀), so Z′(t₀) ≠ 0 if and only if
ζ′(½+it₀) ≠ 0, confirming consistency with the complex version.


B.12  Comparison of the complex and real formulations
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

    Property              ζ_st (complex)       Z_st (real)
    ────────────────────  ──────────────────   ──────────────────
    Values                complex              real
    Spectral support      [−2, 0]              [−1, 1]
    Exponential type      ≤ 2                  ≤ 1
    Covariance R(Δ)       e^{−iΔ} sinΔ/Δ      sinΔ/Δ
    R′(0)                 −i                   0
    Gram matrix           2×2 Hermitian        2×2 real diagonal
    det Γ                 1/3                  2/3
    Z, Z′ uncorrelated    no (R′(0)=−i)        yes (R′(0)=0)
    Z, Z′ independent     no                   yes (Gaussian)
    Bulinskaya applies    yes (det>0)          yes (det>0)
    Zeros = zeros of ζ    yes                  yes

The real formulation has the advantage that the Gram matrix is
diagonal and the process value and its derivative are independent
at every point — a stronger structural property than mere non-
degeneracy.  The determinant 2/3 is twice the determinant 1/3 of
the complex case, reflecting the fact that the off-diagonal
contribution |R′(0)|²/R(0) = 1 that reduces det Γ in the complex
case is absent in the real case.


B.13  The four representations of ζ on the critical line
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

    ζ(½+it) = √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t))           (complex stationary)

    ζ(½+it) = e^{−iϑ(t)} √μ(t) · Z_st(ϑ(t))    (via real stationary)

    Z(t)    = √μ(t) · Z_st(ϑ(t))           (real stationary)

    Z(t)    = e^{iϑ(t)} √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t))     (via complex stationary)

In all four, the amplitude is √μ(t) = √(dI/dT), derived by
differentiating Ingham's exact identity.  The phase rotation
e^{±iϑ(t)} interconverts the complex and real formulations.  The
spectral translation ω ↦ ω+1 interconverts the spectral supports
[−2, 0] and [−1, 1].  All four representations have the same zeros.
