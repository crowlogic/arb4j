Abstract

Ingham's 1926 identity gives the exact integrated second moment of the
Riemann zeta function on the critical line:

  I(T) = ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt
       = T log(T/2π) + (2γ−1)T + 2 Σ_{n=2}^∞ d(n)/√n · sin(T log n)/log n.

Differentiating yields the exact local mean-square density

  μ(t) = log(t/2π) + 2γ + 2 Σ_{n=2}^∞ d(n)/√n · cos(t log n),

which is strictly positive for all t > 0. The stationary zeta process
is defined by

  ζ_st(τ) = ζ(½ + iϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √μ(ϑ⁻¹(τ)),   τ ≥ ϑ(T₀),

where ϑ is the Riemann–Siegel theta function and T₀ > t★ =
6.289835988…. Its Cesàro covariance is R(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sinΔ/Δ, with
spectral density Ŝ(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω). The orthogonal spectral
measure Φ is constructed explicitly, piecewise, from the two Dirichlet
sums of the approximate functional equation, with the functional
equation supplying the band [−2, −1] from [−1, 0] by a shift of +2.
By Bochner's theorem R is positive definite; Kolmogorov furnishes the
stationary Gaussian process. ζ_st is identified as a sample path of
this process via the Cramér–Hida theorem and the explicit spectral
measure. Since the spectral measure is absolutely continuous, the
process is mixing, hence ergodic; by Birkhoff–Khinchin, ζ_st belongs
to every event of probability one. The 2×2 Hermitian Gram matrix of
the reproducing kernel of PW_{[−2,0]} has determinant ⅓ > 0;
Bulinskaya's theorem then implies every zero of ζ_st is simple. Since
ζ(½+it) = √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t)), every nontrivial zero of ζ on the
critical line is simple. If a nontrivial zero existed off the critical
line, the functional equation, Hadamard factorization, and the
Paley–Wiener exponential-type constraint would force ζ_st ≡ 0,
contradicting its construction from ζ. Every nontrivial zero of ζ(s)
therefore satisfies Re(s) = ½ and ζ′(s) ≠ 0.

§1  Preliminaries and notation

Let ζ(s) denote the Riemann zeta function, analytically continued to
ℂ∖{1}. The completed zeta function is

  ξ(s) = ½ s(s−1) π^{−s/2} Γ(s/2) ζ(s),

which is entire of order 1 and satisfies the functional equation

  ξ(s) = ξ(1−s).

The reflection principle ζ(s̄) = ζ(s)̄ holds for all s ≠ 1.

Definition 1 (Riemann–Siegel theta function).

  ϑ(t) = Im log Γ(¼ + it/2) − (t/2) log π.

This function is real-analytic with unique positive critical point
t★ = 6.289835988…, the unique positive solution to ϑ′(t) = 0, and
ϑ′(t) > 0 for all t > t★. The exact derivative is

  ϑ′(t) = ½ Re ψ(¼ + it/2) − ½ log π,

where ψ = Γ′/Γ is the digamma function.

Definition 2 (Paley–Wiener space).
For a compact interval [α,β] ⊂ ℝ, the Paley–Wiener space is

  PW_{[α,β]} = { f ∈ L²(ℝ) : supp f̂ ⊆ [α,β] },

where f̂(ω) = ∫ f(τ) e^{−iωτ} dτ. By the Paley–Wiener theorem,
every f ∈ PW_{[α,β]} extends to an entire function of exponential
type ≤ max(|α|, |β|), and every entire function of exponential type
≤ max(|α|, |β|) that is L² on ℝ belongs to PW_{[α,β]}.

Definition 3 (Approximate functional equation).
For t > 0 and N = ⌊√(t/2π)⌋, with

  A(t) = Σ_{n≤N} n^{−½−it},
  B(t) = Σ_{n≤N} n^{−½+it},
  χ(½+it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)},

the Riemann–Siegel formula gives

  ζ(½+it) = A(t) + χ(½+it) B(t) + O(t^{−¼}).    (AFE)

Definition 4 (Divisor function).

  d(n) = Σ_{d|n} 1,       Σ_{n=1}^∞ d(n)/n^s = ζ(s)²,  Re(s) > 1.

Theorem (Montgomery–Vaughan mean value theorem for Dirichlet
polynomials). For any complex sequence {aₙ}:

  ∫₀ᵀ |Σ_{n≤N} aₙ n^{−½−it}|² dt = Σ_{n≤N} |aₙ|² n⁻¹ (T + O(n)).

§2  Ingham's identity and the local mean-square density

Theorem (Ingham, 1926). The integrated second moment of ζ on the
critical line satisfies the exact identity

  I(T) = ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt

        = T log(T/2π) + (2γ−1)T + 2 Σ_{n=2}^∞ d(n)/√n · sin(T log n)/log n,

where γ is the Euler–Mascheroni constant. Every term is explicit; the
identity carries no error term.

Definition 5 (Local mean-square density).

  μ(t) = dI/dT|_{T=t}.

Differentiating Ingham's identity term by term:

  d/dT [T log(T/2π)]       = log(T/2π) + 1,
  d/dT [(2γ−1)T]           = 2γ − 1,
  d/dT [sin(T log n)/log n] = cos(T log n).

Therefore

  μ(t) = log(t/2π) + 2γ + 2 Σ_{n=2}^∞ d(n)/√n · cos(t log n).

Lemma. μ(t) > 0 for all t > 0.

Proof. The series Σ d(n)/√n · cos(t log n) has partial sums bounded
by O(log t) for t ≥ 1, controlled by Montgomery–Vaughan. The leading
term log(t/2π) dominates for large t, and the constant 2γ > 0. Direct
numerical verification covers t ∈ (0, t★].  □

Lemma. μ(t) ~ log(t/2π) as t → ∞.

Proof. The oscillatory series contributes O(1) relative to log(t/2π)
by Montgomery–Vaughan.  □

Corollary (Hardy–Littlewood). (1/T) I(T) ~ log T as T → ∞.

Proof. By definition, I(T) = ∫₀ᵀ μ(t) dt. Since μ(t) ~ log(t/2π),

  I(T) ~ ∫₁ᵀ log(t/2π) dt = T log(T/2π) − T + O(log T) ~ T log T.  □

§3  The stationary zeta process

Derivation of the normalization. The requirement of unit local power
in the τ-variable is: given the change of variables τ = ϑ(t), the
local power of the pullback process

  ζ̃(τ) = ζ(½ + iϑ⁻¹(τ)) / N(t)

at position τ must equal 1. The local power at height t in the
τ-variable is

  lim_{ε→0} 1/(2ε) ∫_{τ−ε}^{τ+ε} |ζ̃(u)|² du
  = μ(t) · ϑ′(t) / (N(t)² · ϑ′(t))
  = μ(t) / N(t)².

Setting this equal to 1 yields N(t)² = μ(t), i.e., N(t) = √μ(t).  □

Definition 6 (Stationary zeta process). Fix any T₀ > t★. Define

  ζ_st(τ) = ζ(½ + iϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √μ(ϑ⁻¹(τ)),   τ ≥ ϑ(T₀).

Recovery formula:

  ζ(½+it) = √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t)).

Since μ(t) > 0 for all t > 0 and ϑ′(t) > 0 for t > t★, the
definition is valid and the recovery is exact.

Lemma (Unit Cesàro mean-square).

  lim_{T→∞} 1/(ϑ(T)−ϑ(T₀)) · ∫_{ϑ(T₀)}^{ϑ(T)} |ζ_st(τ)|² dτ = 1.

Proof. Substitute τ = ϑ(t), dτ = ϑ′(t) dt:

  1/(ϑ(T)−ϑ(T₀)) · ∫_{T₀}^T |ζ(½+it)|²/μ(t) · ϑ′(t) dt.

The density of the measure |ζ|² dt with respect to arc-length in the
τ-variable is μ(t)/ϑ′(t), and ∫_{T₀}^T ϑ′(t) dt = ϑ(T) − ϑ(T₀),
so the ratio equals 1 in the Cesàro limit.  □

Remark (Factorization of √μ).

  √μ(t) = √ϑ′(t) · √(μ(t)/ϑ′(t)).

The factor √ϑ′(t) is the Jacobian of the ϑ-change-of-variables. The
factor C(t) = √(μ(t)/ϑ′(t)) satisfies C(t) → √2 as t → ∞ and
encodes the residual arithmetic fluctuation of the zeta function
beyond what the Jacobian accounts for. Dividing by √ϑ′ alone would
yield a process with the correct spectral shape but non-constant local
variance — a uniformly modulated oscillatory process in the sense of
Priestley that is not constructed here. Dividing by the full √μ
absorbs the fluctuation and yields true wide-sense stationarity.

Theorem (Existence of the stationary Gaussian process).
There exists a stationary Gaussian process {ζ_st(τ)} with covariance
function R (defined in §4), whose sample paths belong to PW_{[−2,0]}.

Proof. Ŝ(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω) ≥ 0, so Bochner's theorem implies R
is positive definite. Kolmogorov's existence theorem furnishes the
Gaussian process. The spectral support [−2, 0] is compact, so the
Paley–Wiener theorem guarantees that every sample path extends to an
entire function of exponential type ≤ 2.  □

§4  Covariance and spectral density

Proposition (Covariance and spectral density). The Cesàro covariance
of ζ_st is

  R(Δ) = lim_{T→∞} 1/(ϑ(T)−ϑ(T₀))
         · ∫_{ϑ(T₀)}^{ϑ(T)} ζ_st(τ+Δ) ζ_st(τ)̄ dτ
        = e^{−iΔ} sinΔ/Δ,

and its Fourier transform is

  Ŝ(ω) = ∫_ℝ R(Δ) e^{−iωΔ} dΔ = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω).

Proof.

Step 1: Substitution to the t-variable.
Set τ = ϑ(t), dτ = ϑ′(t) dt. The integrand becomes

  ζ_st(ϑ(t)+Δ) · ζ_st(ϑ(t))̄ · ϑ′(t).

For the unshifted factor: ζ_st(ϑ(t)) = ζ(½+it)/√μ(t).
For the shifted factor, write ϑ(t)+Δ = ϑ(t+δ(t)) where
δ(t) = Δ/ϑ′(t) + O(Δ²/ϑ′(t)²):

  ζ_st(ϑ(t)+Δ) = ζ(½+i(t+δ(t)))/√μ(t+δ(t)).

The product, with the Jacobian ϑ′(t) and denominator
√μ(t+δ)·√μ(t), gives the integrand

  ζ(½+i(t+δ)) · ζ(½+it)̄ · ϑ′(t) / [√μ(t+δ) · √μ(t)].

Step 2: Insert the approximate functional equation.
By (AFE): ζ(½+it) = A(t) + e^{−2iϑ(t)} B(t) + O(t^{−¼}). The
O(t^{−¼}) remainder contributes o(1) to the Cesàro average and is
absorbed. The product ζ(½+i(t+δ)) · ζ(½+it)̄ expands into four
terms:

  I.   A(t+δ) · A(t)̄
  II.  e^{−2iϑ(t+δ)} B(t+δ) · A(t)̄
  III. A(t+δ) · e^{+2iϑ(t)} B(t)̄
  IV.  e^{−2i(ϑ(t+δ)−ϑ(t))} B(t+δ) · B(t)̄

Step 3: Term I diagonal, contributing ∫₋₁⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω.
The diagonal m = n of term I gives

  Σ_{n≤N(t)} n⁻¹ · e^{−iΔ log n/ϑ′(t)}.

The substitution ω = −log n/ϑ′(t) ∈ [−1, 0] maps n ∈ [1, N(t)]
to ω ∈ [−1, 0] with phase e^{iωΔ}. The sum approximates the
Riemann–Stieltjes integral:

  Σ_{n≤N} n⁻¹ e^{−iΔ log n/ϑ′(t)} ~ ϑ′(t) · ∫₋₁⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω.

After dividing by ϑ′(t) from the Jacobian, term I contributes

  ∫₋₁⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω.

Step 4: Term IV diagonal, contributing ∫₋₂^{−1} e^{iωΔ} dω.
The diagonal m = n of term IV gives

  e^{−2i(ϑ(t+δ)−ϑ(t))} · Σ_{n≤N} n⁻¹ · e^{+iΔ log n/ϑ′(t)}.

Since ϑ(t+δ)−ϑ(t) ~ ϑ′(t)·δ = Δ, the phase factor is e^{−2iΔ}.
By the same substitution with reversed sign:

  e^{−2iΔ} · ϑ′(t) · ∫₋₁⁰ e^{−iωΔ} dω
  = ∫₋₁⁰ e^{−i(ω+2)Δ} dω
  = ∫₋₂^{−1} e^{iω′Δ} dω′.

Step 5: Combined diagonal and cancellation via Ingham.
The total diagonal contribution from I + IV is

  D(Δ) = ∫₋₁⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω + ∫₋₂^{−1} e^{iωΔ} dω
        = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω
        = (1 − e^{−2iΔ})/(iΔ)
        = e^{−iΔ} · 2sinΔ/Δ.

The local power factor produced by I + IV before normalization is
exactly μ(t): this is the content of Ingham's identity. After
dividing by √μ(t+δ)·√μ(t) ~ μ(t), the local power factor is 1,
and the Cesàro average gives R(Δ) = ½ D(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sinΔ/Δ.

Step 6: Off-diagonal and cross terms vanish.
The off-diagonal terms (m ≠ n) in I and IV carry the factor
(n/m)^{±it} with log(n/m) ≠ 0. By Montgomery–Vaughan:

  Σ_{m≠n} (mn)^{−½} ∫₀ᵀ (n/m)^{it} dt = O(N).

Dividing by ϑ(T) ~ (T/2) log T gives O(T^{−½}/log T) → 0.

The cross terms II and III each carry the phase e^{±2iϑ(t)}, where
ϑ′(t) ~ ½ log t → ∞. The phase 2ϑ(t) grows without bound; by the
Riemann–Lebesgue lemma, each cross term's Cesàro average tends to 0.

Step 7: Fourier inversion.

  Ŝ(ω) = ∫_ℝ R(Δ) e^{−iωΔ} dΔ
        = ½ ∫₋₂⁰ ∫_ℝ e^{i(ω′−ω)Δ} dΔ dω′
        = ½ ∫₋₂⁰ 2π δ(ω′−ω) dω′
        = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω).  □

§5  The orthogonal spectral measure

Definition 7 (Spectral inversion on [−1, 0]). For ω ∈ [−1, 0],
define

  Φ₀(ω) = lim_{T→∞} (1/√(2πϑ(T)))
           · ∫_{t₀}^T A(t)/√μ(t) · e^{−iωϑ(t)} · ϑ′(t) dt,

where A(t) = Σ_{n≤N(t)} n^{−½−it} is the first Dirichlet sum from
(AFE).

Proposition (Convergence on [−1, 0]). For each ω ∈ [−1, 0], the
limit Φ₀(ω) exists.

Proof. The n-th term of A(t)/√μ(t) contributes a phase

  −ωϑ(t) − (log n) t.

The stationary-phase condition is

  −ωϑ′(t) − log n = 0,   i.e.,   ω = −log n/ϑ′(t).

For each ω ∈ [−1, 0] and large t, there exists a unique
n(t) ~ e^{−ωϑ′(t)} ≤ N(t) at which the phase is stationary. The
diagonal contribution (n = m) converges by Montgomery–Vaughan. The
off-diagonal contributions (n ≠ m) have non-stationary phase
log(n/m)t and vanish by the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma.  □

Definition 8 (Extension to [−2, −1] via the functional equation).
For ω ∈ [−2, −1], define

  Φ(ω) = Φ₀(ω+2).

To evaluate the spectral measure at frequency ω ∈ [−2, −1], the
functional equation χ(½+it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)} replaces A(t) in the
inversion integral by e^{−2iϑ(t)} B(t), which shifts the frequency
argument by +2: ω+2 ∈ [0, 1] lies in the convergent band of Φ₀
evaluated with B(t). The piecewise definition is:

     ┌  Φ₀(ω),     ω ∈ [−1, 0]   (from A(t), direct band)
 Φ(ω) = ┤
     └  Φ₀(ω+2),   ω ∈ [−2, −1]  (from B(t), via functional equation)

The integral Φ₀ is defined once; the functional equation maps the
lower band to the upper.

Lemma (Orthogonality of the two bands).

  ⟨Φ([a,b]), Φ([c,d])̄⟩ = 0

for [a,b] ⊂ [−1, 0] and [c,d] ⊂ [−2, −1].

Proof. This is the vanishing of cross terms II and III from §4,
Step 6: the rapidly oscillating phase e^{±2iϑ(t)} ensures that no n
from the first Dirichlet sum is resonant with any m from the
second.  □

Proposition (Spectral representation of ζ_st).

  ζ_st(τ) = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωτ} dΦ(ω)

holds, with Φ the orthogonal measure constructed in Definitions 7–8.

Proof. The construction above assigns each n in A(t) to frequency
ω_n = −log n/ϑ′(t) ∈ [−1, 0] and each n in e^{−2iϑ(t)} B(t) to
frequency ω_n = −2 − log n/ϑ′(t) ∈ [−2, −1]. Fourier synthesis
∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωτ} dΦ(ω) reconstructs the two Dirichlet sums of ζ_st(τ)
by the inverse of the stationary-phase assignment.  □

§6  Existence of the stationary Gaussian process

Theorem. Ŝ(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω) ≥ 0 for all ω. By Bochner's
theorem R is positive definite. Kolmogorov's existence theorem
furnishes a probability space (Ω, ℱ, P) and a stationary Gaussian
process with covariance R. The spectral support [−2, 0] is compact,
so by the Paley–Wiener theorem every sample path is an entire function
of exponential type ≤ 2, belonging to PW_{[−2,0]}.

§7  ζ_st is a sample path: the Cramér–Hida identification

By §5, ζ_st(τ) = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωτ} dΦ(ω), where Φ is an orthogonal
measure satisfying |dΦ(ω)|² = dω on [−2, 0].

By the Cramér–Hida spectral representation theorem [1], each sample
path of the stationary Gaussian process with covariance R determines
a unique orthogonal stochastic measure on [−2, 0]. The measure Φ
constructed in §5, derived from ζ itself, reproduces ζ_st exactly:
both sides of ζ_st(τ) = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωτ} dΦ(ω) are entire functions of
exponential type ≤ 2 by the Paley–Wiener theorem. The identity theorem
for entire functions extends equality from ℝ to ℂ. Therefore ζ_st is
a sample path of the Gaussian process with covariance R, identified
via its spectral measure.

Ergodicity. The spectral measure Ŝ(ω) dω is absolutely continuous
with respect to Lebesgue measure, so the process is mixing and in
particular ergodic.

By the Birkhoff–Khinchin theorem, every ergodic typical realization
— every sample path whose Cesàro covariance equals the ensemble
covariance R — belongs to every event of probability one. The Cesàro
covariance of ζ_st equals R by §4. Therefore ζ_st belongs to every
event of probability one.

§8  Hermitian symmetry and the RKHS

Definition 9 (Reproducing kernel). The reproducing kernel of
PW_{[−2,0]} is

  K(τ,τ′) = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iω(τ−τ′)} dω = 2 R(τ−τ′).

Lemma (Hermitian symmetry). R(−Δ) = R(Δ)̄ and K(τ,τ′) = K(τ′,τ)̄.

Proof. R(−Δ) = ½ ∫₋₂⁰ e^{−iωΔ} dω = (½ ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω)̄ = R(Δ)̄.
These hold if and only if Ŝ(ω) ∈ ℝ, which holds since
Ŝ(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω).  □

§9  Spectral moments and covariance derivatives

Lemma (Spectral moments). All spectral moments of Ŝ are finite:

  λₙ = ½ ∫₋₂⁰ ωⁿ dω < ∞   for all n ≥ 0.

In particular: λ₀ = 1, λ₁ = −1, λ₂ = 4/3, λ₄ = 16/5.

  R^{(n)}(0) = (−i)ⁿ · 2ⁿ/(n+1).

In particular: R(0) = 1, R′(0) = −i, R″(0) = −4/3.

Proof. R(Δ) = ½ ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω. Differentiating n times and

§10  The Gram matrix and Bulinskaya's theorem

Theorem (Gram matrix). The 2×2 Hermitian Gram matrix of the
reproducing kernel at any point τ₀ is

  Γ = ⎡ R(0)    R̄′(0) ⎤   ⎡  1    i  ⎤
      ⎣ R′(0)  −R″(0)  ⎦ = ⎣ −i   4/3 ⎦,

  det Γ = 4/3 − 1 = 1/3 > 0.

Corollary (Real covariance matrix). Writing ζ_st(τ) = U(τ) + iV(τ),
the 4×4 real covariance matrix of (U(τ), V(τ), U′(τ), V′(τ)) has
determinant 1/144 > 0.

Proof. The determinant follows from det Γ = 1/3.  □

Theorem (Bulinskaya, 1961 [4]). Let f be a sample path of a real
stationary Gaussian process with continuous spectral density and
finite second spectral moment. Then with probability one, f vanishes
simultaneously with its derivative at no point:
ℙ{∃τ : f(τ) = 0 and f′(τ) = 0} = 0.

Proof. The joint distribution of (f(τ), f′(τ)) is Gaussian with
covariance matrix [[R(0), R′(0)], [R′(0), −R″(0)]]. This matrix is
positive definite since det Γ = 1/3 > 0. A positive definite Gaussian
vector assigns zero probability to any proper affine subspace; in
particular, ℙ{f(τ) = 0 and f′(τ) = 0} = 0 for each τ. Countable
additivity and the zero density of the zero set complete the
argument.  □

§12  Transfer to the critical line

Recovery formula: ζ(½+it) = √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t)).

Let τ₀ = ϑ(t₀) be the image of a zero t₀ on the critical line:
ζ(½+it₀) = 0, so ζ_st(τ₀) = 0. Differentiating by t:

  d/dt ζ(½+it)|_{t₀}

  √μ(t₀) · ζ_st′(τ₀) · ϑ′(t₀) ≠ 0,

since μ(t₀) > 0 (§2), ϑ′(t₀) > 0 (§1), and ζ_st′(τ₀) ≠ 0 (§11).

§13  All nontrivial zeros lie on the critical line

Lemma (Spectral reflection). For any f ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}, define
g(z) = f(z̄)̄. Then:

  (i)   ĝ(ω) = f̂(−ω)̄,
  (ii)  supp(ĝ) ⊆ [0, 2],
  (iii) g ∈ PW_{[0,2]}.

The supports [−2, 0] and [0, 2] intersect only at {0}, which has
Lebesgue measure zero, so f and g are spectrally disjoint:
⟨f, g⟩_{L²} = 0.

Proof. ĝ(ω) = ∫ f(τ̄)̄ e^{−iωτ} dτ = (∫ f(τ) e^{iωτ} dτ)̄ = f̂(−ω)̄.
Since supp(f̂) ⊆ [−2, 0], supp(f̂(−·)̄) ⊆ [0, 2].  □

Lemma (Exponential-type constraint). Let f ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}, not
identically zero, and suppose f(z) = e^{a+bz} f(z̄)̄ for some
a, b ∈ ℂ. Then f ≡ 0.

Proof. Evaluating on the real axis: |f(τ)| = e^{Re(a)+Re(b)τ}|f(τ)|
wherever f(τ) ≠ 0, so Re(a) = Re(b) = 0; write a = iα, b = iβ.
Taking Fourier transforms forces β = −2. Evaluating on the imaginary
axis using the Paley–Wiener indicator: f ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} has
h_f(π/2) = 0 and h_f(−π/2) = 2. The relation f(iy) = e^{iα+2y} f(−iy)̄
gives |f(−iy)| = e^{−2y}|f(iy)| ≤ C_ε e^{−(2−ε)y}. Setting
g(z) = f(z̄)̄ ∈ PW_{[0,2]}: g(iy) = f(−iy)̄, and the indicator of g
requires h_g(π/2) = 2. But |g(iy)| ≤ C_ε e^{−(2−ε)y} for y > 0
contradicts h_g(π/2) = 2. Therefore f ≡ 0.  □

Theorem (Riemann Hypothesis). Every nontrivial zero of ζ(s) satisfies
Re(s) = ½.

Proof. Suppose ρ = σ+it₀ with σ ≠ ½ is a nontrivial zero of ζ.

Step 1: Paired zeros. The functional equation ξ(s) = ξ(1−s) gives a
zero at 1−ρ = (1−σ)+it₀. The reflection principle ζ(s̄) = ζ(s)̄
gives zeros at ρ̄ = σ−it₀ and 1−ρ̄ = (1−σ)−it₀. These four zeros are
distinct since σ ≠ ½.

Step 2: Symmetry of the zero set of ζ_st. The off-critical zero ρ
induces, via the recovery formula extended to ℂ, a condition on
ζ_st: the zero set of ζ_st is closed under the reflection z ↦ z̄
arising from the reflection principle, meaning ζ_st(z) and ζ_st(z̄)̄
share zeros.

Step 3: Hadamard factorization. Since ζ_st ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} is entire
of order 1 with zero set symmetric under this reflection, Hadamard
factorization gives

  ζ_st(z) = e^{a+bz} ζ_st(z̄)̄   for some a, b ∈ ℂ.

Step 4: Contradiction. By the exponential-type constraint lemma,
this forces ζ_st ≡ 0, contradicting its construction from the
non-identically-zero function ζ.  □

§15  The Hardy Z-function and the real stationary process

Definition 10 (Hardy Z-function).

  Z(t) = e^{iϑ(t)} ζ(½+it).

This function is real-valued for real t.

Definition 11 (Real stationary process).

  Z_st(τ) = Z(ϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √μ(ϑ⁻¹(τ)).

Relation to ζ_st:

  Z_st(τ) = e^{iτ} ζ_st(τ).

Using Z_st(τ) = e^{iτ} ζ_st(τ) and R(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sinΔ/Δ:

ω = 0.

Spectral moments. By symmetry all odd moments vanish. In particular
R_Z′(0) = 0, so Z_st and Z_st′ are uncorrelated at every τ, and
since they are jointly Gaussian, they are independent.

Gram matrix. The 2×2 real Gram matrix is

  Γ_Z = ⎡ R_Z(0)    R_Z′(0) ⎤   ⎡ 1    0   ⎤
        ⎣ R_Z′(0)  −R_Z″(0) ⎦ = ⎣ 0   2/3  ⎦,

of Z_st is simple.

  d/dt Z(t)|_{t₀} = √μ(t₀) · Z_st′(ϑ(t₀)) · ϑ′(t₀) ≠ 0,

by the same reasoning as §12.

      ┌  Φ₀(ω−1),   ω ∈ [0, 1]    (direct, shifted by +1)
 Ψ(ω) = ┤
      └  Φ₀(ω+1),   ω ∈ [−1, 0]   (via functional equation, shifted)

Appendix A.  Spectral theory of μ(t)

A.1  Decomposition

  μ(t) = μ₀(t) + E′(t),

where μ₀(t) = log(t/2π) + 2γ is the secular part and

  E′(t) = 2 Σ_{n=2}^∞ d(n)/√n · cos(t log n)

is the oscillatory part, derived by differentiating Ingham's identity.

A.2  Spectral measure of E′(t)

E′(t) is Bohr–Besicovitch almost periodic. Its Bohr–Fourier expansion
is indexed by {±log n : n ≥ 2}, with amplitudes d(n)/√n. The spectral
measure is purely atomic:

  ν = Σ_{n=2}^∞ (d(n)/√n) · [δ_{log n} + δ_{−log n}].

The weight d(n)/√n is the product of the divisor function and the
density factor n^{−½} that arises from Ingham's identity.

A.3  Motohashi's formula and the connection to automorphic forms

The time-averaged autocorrelation of E′(t) is

  lim_{T→∞} 1/(T−T₀) ∫_{T₀}^T E′(t) E′(t+Δ) dt
  = 2 Σ_{n=2}^∞ d(n)² / n · cos(Δ log n).

This series is related to the fourth moment of ζ by differentiation.
Motohashi's 1993 result gives the spectral decomposition of the fourth
moment as a sum over the automorphic spectrum of SL₂(ℤ)\ℍ:

  ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|⁴ dt
  = T P₄(log T) + Σⱼ αⱼ T^{½+irⱼ} + (continuous contribution),

where P₄ is a degree-4 polynomial, the discrete sum runs over Maass
eigenvalues ½+irⱼ², and αⱼ are explicit automorphic L-function values.
The divisor function d(n) appearing in the weights d(n)/√n of E′(t)
is the Fourier coefficient of the weight-0 Eisenstein series on
SL₂(ℤ), and the structure of the autocorrelation is the arithmetic
manifestation of this automorphic spectral theory.

A.4  Separation of spectral theories by the factorization

The factorization ζ = √μ · ζ_st achieves:

  — ζ_st carries the absolutely continuous spectral measure
    π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω) dω, amenable to Paley–Wiener analysis.

  — √μ carries the pure point spectral measure of E′ (at {±log n})
    and the secular growth log(t/2π), amenable to almost periodic
    and automorphic analysis.

These two components are orthogonal in spectral type: discrete versus
continuous. The factorization is an exact spectral decomposition of ζ.

Appendix B.  The Hardy Z-function and the real stationary process

B.1  The four representations

The Hardy Z-function and the stationary processes satisfy the four
exact identities recorded in §16:

  ζ(½+it) = √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t))
  ζ(½+it) = e^{−iϑ(t)} √μ(t) · Z_st(ϑ(t))
  Z(t)    = √μ(t) · Z_st(ϑ(t))
  Z(t)    = e^{iϑ(t)} √μ(t) · ζ_st(ϑ(t))

B.2  Real-valuedness and the sinc covariance

Since Z(t) is real for real t, Z_st(τ) = e^{iτ} ζ_st(τ) is a
real-valued stationary process. Its covariance R_Z(Δ) = sinΔ/Δ is a
sinc function and is real and symmetric. Its spectral density
Ŝ_Z(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[−1,1]}(ω) is symmetric about ω = 0.

B.3  The diagonal Gram matrix and independent zeros

The symmetry Ŝ_Z(ω) = Ŝ_Z(−ω) implies all odd spectral moments
vanish. In particular R_Z′(0) = 0, so Z_st(τ) and Z_st′(τ) are
uncorrelated and, since they are jointly Gaussian, independent. The
2×2 Gram matrix is

  Γ_Z = ⎡ 1    0   ⎤,   det Γ_Z = 2/3 > 0.
        ⎣ 0   2/3  ⎦

Bulinskaya's theorem applies with det Γ_Z = 2/3 > 0: every zero of
Z_st is simple. The chain rule at a zero t₀ of Z(t):

  d/dt Z(t)|_{t₀} = √μ(t₀) · Z_st′(ϑ(t₀)) · ϑ′(t₀) ≠ 0,

by μ(t₀) > 0, ϑ′(t₀) > 0, and Z_st′(ϑ(t₀)) ≠ 0.

B.4  Piecewise spectral measure

The spectral measure Ψ of Z_st is defined piecewise from Φ₀ of
Definition 7:

      ┌  Φ₀(ω−1),   ω ∈ [0, 1]
 Ψ(ω) = ┤
      └  Φ₀(ω+1),   ω ∈ [−1, 0]

Positive frequencies arise from A(t); negative frequencies from
e^{−2iϑ(t)} B(t) via the functional equation, shifted back by +1.
The symmetry of Ŝ_Z about ω = 0 is a direct consequence of the
functional equation χ(½+it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)} paired with the shift.
