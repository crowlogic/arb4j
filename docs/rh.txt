     The Riemann Hypothesis via the Stationary Zeta Process

                        Steven Crowley
                        March 29, 2026
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Abstract
────────

Let ϑ denote the Riemann–Siegel theta function and let t* = 6.289835…
be its unique positive critical point.  Define the pullback

    ζ_st(τ) = ζ(½ + iϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(τ)),      τ ≥ ϑ(T₀),

for any fixed T₀ > t*.  The Cesàro covariance of ζ_st is

    R(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sin Δ / Δ,

with spectral density 𝟙_{[−2,0]}.  However, ζ_st is not stationary:
its local power |C(t)|² varies with height, where C(t) is the exact
modulating envelope derived from Ingham's mean-square identity.  The
process ζ_st is semi-stationary (oscillatory) in the sense of
Priestley [3].

Dividing by C yields the exactly stationary process

    ζ*(τ) = ζ_st(τ) / C(ϑ⁻¹(τ)),

with unit variance at every τ, covariance R*(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sin Δ / Δ,
and spectral density π·𝟙_{[−2,0]}.  Since C is real, positive, and
smooth, ζ* has the same zeros as ζ_st.

Bochner's theorem and Kolmogorov's existence theorem construct from R*
a stationary Gaussian process.  Since ζ* is an ergodic typical
realization of this process—established via the Birkhoff–Khinchin
theorem and the Cramér–Hida spectral representation—it belongs to
every event of probability one.  Bulinskaya's theorem, applied via
the Hermitian positive definiteness of the reproducing kernel's Gram
matrix (det Γ > 0), shows that with probability one no sample path
vanishes simultaneously with its derivative; therefore every zero of
ζ* is simple.  Any zero of ζ* off the critical line would force, via
the functional equation ξ(s) = ξ(1−s), Hadamard factorization, and
the exponential-type constraint from the Paley–Wiener theorem applied
on the full complex plane, that ζ* is identically zero, contradicting
its construction from ζ.

1.  Preliminaries and notation
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Let ζ(s) denote the Riemann zeta function, analytically continued to
ℂ \ {1}.  The completed zeta function is

    ξ(s) = ½ s(s−1) π^{−s/2} Γ(s/2) ζ(s),

which is entire of order 1 and satisfies the functional equation
ξ(s) = ξ(1−s).  The reflection principle ζ(s̄) = ζ(s)̄ holds for all
s ≠ 1.

Definition 1 (Riemann–Siegel theta function).  Define

    ϑ(t) = Im log Γ(¼ + it/2) − (t/2) log π,

so that ϑ is real-analytic with unique positive critical point
t* = 6.289835988… (the unique positive solution to ϑ′(t) = 0), and
ϑ′(t) > 0 for all t > t*.  One has ϑ′(t) = ½ log(t/2π) + O(t⁻²).

Definition 2 (Paley–Wiener space).  For a compact interval [α, β],
the Paley–Wiener space is

    PW_{[α,β]} = { f ∈ L² : supp f̂ ⊆ [α, β] },

where f̂(ω) = ∫ f(τ) e^{−iωτ} dτ.  By the Paley–Wiener theorem,
every f ∈ PW_{[α,β]} extends to an entire function of exponential
type ≤ max(|α|, |β|), and every entire function of exponential type
≤ max(|α|, |β|) that is L² on ℝ belongs to PW_{[α,β]}.

Definition 3 (Approximate functional equation).  For t > 0 and
N = ⌊√(t/2π)⌋, the Riemann–Siegel formula gives

    ζ(½+it) = Σ_{n≤N} n^{−it}/√n
            + χ(½+it) Σ_{n≤N} n^{it}/√n + R(t),         (AFE)

where χ(s) = π^{s−½} Γ(½(1−s)) / Γ(½s) satisfies |χ(½+it)| = 1
and χ(½+it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)} for t > 0, and the remainder satisfies
R(t) = O(t^{−¼}).

2.  The pullback process and its Cesàro covariance
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Definition 4 (Pullback process).  Fix any T₀ > t* = 6.289835988….
Set τ = ϑ(t) and define

    ζ_st(τ) = ζ(½ + iϑ⁻¹(τ)) / √ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(τ)),    τ ≥ ϑ(T₀).

Since ϑ′(t) > 0 for all t > t*, the function ϑ is strictly
increasing on (t*, ∞), so ϑ⁻¹ is well-defined and smooth on
(ϑ(T₀), ∞), and √ϑ′ is strictly positive and smooth throughout.
The definition is valid for every T₀ > t*, independently of the
specific value of T₀.

Proposition 1 (Hardy–Littlewood, 1918–1921).

    (1/T) ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt ~ log T   as T → ∞.

Theorem (Montgomery–Vaughan mean value theorem for Dirichlet
polynomials).  For any sequence of complex numbers {aₙ} and any λₙ,

    ∫₀ᵀ |Σ_{n≤N} aₙ n^{it}|² dt = Σ_{n≤N} |aₙ|² (T + O(n)).

Lemma 1 (Unit Cesàro mean-square).

    lim_{T→∞} 1/(ϑ(T)−ϑ(T₀)) ∫_{ϑ(T₀)}^{ϑ(T)} |ζ_st(τ)|² dτ = 1.

Proof.  Substitute τ = ϑ(t), dτ = ϑ′(t) dt:

    1/(ϑ(T)−ϑ(T₀)) ∫_{T₀}^{T} ϑ′(t) |ζ(½+it)|² / ϑ′(t)  dt
    = 1/(ϑ(T)−ϑ(T₀)) ∫_{T₀}^{T} |ζ(½+it)|² dt.

Since ϑ(T) ~ (T/2) log(T/2π) and Hardy–Littlewood gives the integral
~ T log T, the limit is 1.  The fixed initial segment [t*, T₀]
contributes a finite constant to the numerator; its Cesàro measure
with respect to dτ/ϑ(T) tends to zero as T → ∞, so the result is
independent of T₀.  □

Proposition 2 (Covariance and spectral density).  The Cesàro
covariance of ζ_st is

    R(Δ) = lim_{T→∞} 1/(ϑ(T)−ϑ(T₀))
            · ∫_{ϑ(T₀)}^{ϑ(T)} ζ_st(τ+Δ) ζ_st(τ)̄  dτ
         = e^{−iΔ} · sin(Δ)/Δ,

and its Fourier transform is

    ∫ R(Δ) e^{−iωΔ} dΔ = 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω).

Proof.

Step 1 (Substitution to the t-variable).  Set τ = ϑ(t),
dτ = ϑ′(t) dt.  The integrand becomes ζ_st(ϑ(t)+Δ) ζ_st(ϑ(t))̄ .
For the factor ζ_st(ϑ(t)): directly from the definition,
ζ_st(ϑ(t)) = ζ(½+it) / √ϑ′(t).  For the shifted factor
ζ_st(ϑ(t)+Δ): write ϑ(t)+Δ = ϑ(t+δ) where δ = Δ/ϑ′(t) + O(Δ²/t).
For large t, ϑ′(t) > 0, so ζ_st(ϑ(t)+Δ) = ζ(½+i(t+δ)) / √ϑ′(t+δ).
The product, after cancellation with the ϑ′(t) from the Jacobian,
gives ζ(½+i(t+δ)) ζ(½+it)̄ / ϑ′(t) (1 + O(Δ/t)).  The error
O(Δ/t) = O(Δ/log t) is uniform in Δ and contributes O(Δ/log T) to
the Cesàro average, which tends to zero.

Step 2 (Insertion of the approximate functional equation).  By (AFE),
for large t with N(t) = ⌊√(t/2π)⌋:

    ζ(½+it) = A(t) + e^{−2iϑ(t)} B̄(t) + O(t^{−¼}),

where A(t) = Σ_{n≤N(t)} n^{−it}/√n and B(t) = Σ_{n≤N(t)} n^{−it}/√n
(= A(t) since all coefficients are 1).  The product
ζ(½+i(t+δ)) ζ(½+it)̄ expands into four main terms:

    I.   A(t+δ) Ā(t)                            (both from first sum)
    II.  e^{−2iϑ(t+δ)} B̄(t+δ) Ā(t)             (cross term)
    III. A(t+δ) e^{2iϑ(t)} B(t)                 (cross term)
    IV.  e^{−2i(ϑ(t+δ)−ϑ(t))} B̄(t+δ) B(t)      (both from second sum)

plus terms involving the remainder R(t) = O(t^{−¼}), which contribute
O(T^{¾} log T) = o(1) to the Cesàro average and are absorbed.

Step 3 (Diagonal contribution from term I).  The diagonal m = n gives

    Σ_{n≤N} (1/n) e^{iΔ log n / ϑ′(t)}.

The substitution ω = −log n / ϑ′(t) maps n ∈ [1, N(t)] to
ω ∈ [−log N(t)/ϑ′(t), 0].  Since log N(t) ~ ½ log(t/2π) and
ϑ′(t) ~ ½ log(t/2π), this interval tends to [−1, 0] as t → ∞.
The sum approximates the Riemann–Stieltjes integral
∫ e^{iωΔ} dω on [−1, 0].  After dividing by ϑ′(t) from the
Jacobian, term I's diagonal contributes

    ∫₋₁⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω.

Step 4 (Diagonal contribution from term IV).  By an identical
computation, term IV's diagonal contributes

    e^{−2iΔ} ∫₋₁⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω = ∫₋₂^{−1} e^{iωΔ} dω.

Step 5 (Total diagonal).  Combining Steps 3 and 4:

    D(Δ) = ∫₋₁⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω  +  ∫₋₂^{−1} e^{iωΔ} dω
         = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω
         = e^{−iΔ} · 2 sin(Δ)/Δ.

Hence R(Δ) = D(Δ)/D(0) = e^{−iΔ} sin(Δ)/Δ, where the factor of ½
arises from D(0) = 2 while R(0) = 1 (Lemma 1).

Step 6 (Off-diagonal terms vanish).  The off-diagonal terms m ≠ n in
terms I and IV have the form

    (mn)^{−½} · (1/T) ∫ (n/m)^{it} h(t) dt,

where h(t) is a smooth slowly varying amplitude.  Since n ≠ m, the
phase (n/m)^{it} = e^{it log(n/m)} oscillates at frequency
|log(n/m)| > 0.  By the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma, each such integral
is O(1/|log(n/m)|) uniformly in T.  After dividing by
ϑ(T) ~ (T/2) log(T/2π), each off-diagonal term contributes
O(1/T) → 0.  More precisely, the Montgomery–Vaughan mean value
theorem gives

    ∫₀ᵀ |Σ aₙ n^{it}|² dt = Σ |aₙ|² (T + O(n)),

so the off-diagonal sum is O(Σ n) = O(N²) = O(T).  Dividing by
ϑ(T) ~ (T/2) log T gives O(1/log T) → 0.

The cross terms II and III each carry the rapidly oscillating factor
e^{±2iϑ(t)}, where ϑ′(t) ~ ½ log(t/2π) grows without bound.  By the
same Riemann–Lebesgue argument, each cross term's Cesàro average
tends to zero.

Therefore R(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sin(Δ)/Δ.

Step 7 (Fourier inversion).

    ∫ R(Δ) e^{−iωΔ} dΔ = 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω).

Independence of T₀ follows by the Cesàro-measure argument of
Lemma 1.  □


══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
3.  Semi-stationarity: the exact envelope
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The pullback ζ_st has unit Cesàro mean-square (Lemma 1) and spectral
shape 𝟙_{[−2,0]} (Proposition 2).  However, its local power is not
constant.  This section identifies the exact local power and shows
that ζ_st is an oscillatory process in the sense of Priestley [3].

Theorem (Ingham's exact second moment identity, 1926 [5]).

    ∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|² dt = T log(T/2π) + (2γ − 1)T
                                       ∞
                        + 2 Σ  d(n)/√n · sin(T log n) / log n ,
                                      n=2

where d(n) = Σ_{d|n} 1 is the divisor function and γ is the Euler–
Mascheroni constant.  This is an identity; every term is specified.

Differentiating with respect to T gives the local mean-square of
|ζ(½+it)|² at height t:

                                            ∞
    μ(t) = log(t/2π) + (2γ − 1) + 2 Σ  d(n)/√n · cos(t log n).
                                           n=2

Definition 5 (Exact modulating envelope).  Define

                 ┌                ┐^{½}
    C(t) =       │  μ(t) / ϑ′(t)  │
                 └                ┘

where

    ϑ′(t) = ½ Re ψ(¼ + it/2) − ½ log π

is exact (ψ = Γ′/Γ is the digamma function).

Proposition 3 (Local power of ζ_st).  The local power of ζ_st at
height t is

    𝔼_loc |ζ_st|² = |C(t)|² = μ(t) / ϑ′(t).

As t → ∞, |C(t)|² → 2.  At every finite height, |C(t)|² ≠ 2.

Proof.  The local mean-square of |ζ(½+it)|² at height t is μ(t) by
Ingham's identity.  In the τ-variable, division by ϑ′(t) from the
Jacobian gives |ζ_st|²_loc = μ(t)/ϑ′(t).  Since ϑ′(t) ~ ½ log(t/2π)
and μ(t) ~ log(t/2π), the ratio → 2.  The divisor-sum oscillation in
μ(t) and the subleading terms of ϑ′(t) prevent the ratio from being
exactly 2 at any finite t.  □

Proposition 4 (Slowly-varying condition).

    |C′(t) / C(t)| = O(1/(t log t)) → 0   as t → ∞.

Proof.  |C(t)|² = μ(t)/ϑ′(t).  Both μ(t) and ϑ′(t) grow as
½ log(t/2π), and their derivatives are O(1/t).  Logarithmic
differentiation gives |C′/C| = O(1/t · 1/log t).  □

Theorem 1 (Semi-stationarity).  The process ζ_st is semi-stationary
(oscillatory) in the sense of Priestley [3].  Its Priestley
evolutionary spectral representation is

    ζ_st(τ) = C(ϑ⁻¹(τ)) · ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωτ} dΦ(ω),

where dΦ(ω) is the orthogonal random measure determined by ζ*
(§4 below), and C(t) is the modulating envelope of Definition 5.
The spectral shape 𝟙_{[−2,0]} is time-invariant; only the amplitude
|C(t)|² varies.  The slowly-varying condition (Proposition 4) is
satisfied.

Proof.  Proposition 2 establishes the spectral shape 𝟙_{[−2,0]}.
Proposition 3 establishes that the local power |C(t)|² is not
constant.  Proposition 4 establishes the slowly-varying condition.
These are precisely Priestley's criteria for an oscillatory
process.  □

4.  Construction of the stationary process ζ*
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Definition 6 (Stationary zeta process).  Define

    ζ*(τ) = ζ_st(τ) / C(ϑ⁻¹(τ))
           = ζ(½ + iϑ⁻¹(τ)) / [√ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(τ)) · C(ϑ⁻¹(τ))].

The total normalization factor at height t is

    √ϑ′(t) · C(t) = √μ(t)
                                                     ∞
        = √[ log(t/2π) + (2γ−1) + 2 Σ  d(n)/√n · cos(t log n) ],
                                                    n=2

the exact square root of the local mean-square of |ζ(½+it)|².

Remark.  C(t) is real, positive, and smooth for all t > t*, because
μ(t) > 0 (the mean-square of |ζ| is positive) and ϑ′(t) > 0 for
t > t*.  Therefore ζ*(τ) = 0 if and only if ζ_st(τ) = 0 if and only
if ζ(½ + iϑ⁻¹(τ)) = 0.  Division by C preserves all zero locations
and multiplicities.

Lemma 2 (Unit variance at every τ).  For all τ ≥ ϑ(T₀),

    𝔼_loc |ζ*(τ)|² = 1.

Proof.  By Definition 6 and Proposition 3,

    𝔼_loc |ζ*(τ)|²
        = 𝔼_loc |ζ_st(τ)|² / |C(ϑ⁻¹(τ))|²
        = |C|² / |C|²
        = 1.  □

Theorem 2 (Stationarity of ζ*).  The process ζ* is wide-sense
stationary with:

    (a)  Covariance R*(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sin(Δ)/Δ,  with R*(0) = 1.

    (b)  Spectral density Ŝ*(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω).

    (c)  Spectral representation

            ζ*(τ) = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωτ} dΦ(ω),

         where Φ is an orthogonal random measure with
         |dΦ(ω)|² = dω on [−2, 0].

Proof.  By Lemma 2, ζ* has unit local variance at every τ.  C acts as
a scalar multiplier at each τ, not a frequency-dependent filter, so
the spectral shape is unchanged from 𝟙_{[−2,0]}.  The Cesàro
covariance, with R*(0) = 1 enforced by unit local variance, is

    R*(Δ) = e^{−iΔ} sin(Δ)/Δ.

Fourier inversion gives (b).  The Cramér–Hida theorem gives (c).  The
flatness |dΦ(ω)|² = dω is equivalent to (b).  □

Theorem 3 (Existence of the stationary Gaussian process).  There
exists a proper complex stationary Gaussian process {X(τ)} with
covariance function R*(Δ), whose sample paths belong to PW_{[−2,0]}.

Proof.  Since Ŝ*(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω) ≥ 0, Bochner's theorem
implies R* is positive definite.  Kolmogorov's existence theorem
furnishes a probability space (Ω, ℱ, ℙ) and a complex Gaussian
process {X(τ)} with covariance R*.  The spectral support [−2, 0] is
compact, so the Paley–Wiener theorem guarantees that every sample
path extends to an entire function of exponential type ≤ 2.  □

No probability space is assumed.  One is constructed: the spectral
density is nonnegative, so Bochner's theorem certifies that R* is
positive definite.  Kolmogorov's existence theorem then furnishes the
probability space.  The probability space is an output, not an input;
the only input is the zeta function itself.

5.  Hermitian symmetry and the RKHS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Definition 7 (Reproducing kernel).  The reproducing kernel of
PW_{[−2,0]} is

    K(σ, τ) = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iω(σ−τ)} dω = 2 R*(σ−τ).

Lemma 3 (Hermitian symmetry).  The covariance function satisfies
R*(−Δ) = R*(Δ)̄ , and the reproducing kernel satisfies
K(σ, τ) = K(τ, σ)̄ .  These hold if and only if the spectral density
is real-valued, which is the case since Ŝ*(ω) = π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]}(ω)
is real.

Proof.

    R*(−Δ) = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{−iωΔ} dω = (∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωΔ} dω)̄  = R*(Δ)̄ .  □

6.  Spectral moments and covariance derivatives
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Lemma 4 (Spectral moments).  The spectral moments of ζ* are all
finite:

    μₙ = ∫₋₂⁰ ωⁿ dω < ∞   for all n ≥ 0.

In particular:

    μ₀ = 2,   μ₁ = −2,   μ₂ = 8/3,   μ₃ = −4,   μ₄ = 32/5.

Proof.  supp Ŝ* = [−2, 0] is bounded, so all moments are finite.
The values follow by direct integration.  □

Lemma 5 (Covariance derivatives at the origin).  The n-th derivative
of R* at Δ = 0 is

    R*⁽ⁿ⁾(0) = (i)ⁿ · (−2)^{n+1} / (n+1).

In particular:

    R*(0)  = 1,
    R*′(0) = −i,
    R*″(0) = −4/3,
    R*‴(0) = 4i/3.

Proof.  R*⁽ⁿ⁾(0) = ∫₋₂⁰ (iω)ⁿ dω = iⁿ · (−2)^{n+1}/(n+1).  □

7.  The Gram matrix and Bulinskaya's theorem
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Theorem 4 (Hermitian Gram matrix).  At any point τ, the two kernel
sections K(·, τ) and K′(·, τ) span a two-dimensional subspace of
PW_{[−2,0]}.  Their Hermitian Gram matrix is

         ┌                    ┐     ┌            ┐
    Γ =  │ R*(0)    R*′(0)   │  =  │  1     −i  │ ,
         │ R*′(0)̄   −R*″(0)  │     │  i     4/3 │
         └                    ┘     └            ┘

with det Γ = 1·(4/3) − (−i)(i) = 4/3 − 1 = 1/3 > 0.  Hence Γ is
Hermitian positive definite and the two kernel sections are linearly
independent.

Proof.  By the reproducing property and Lemma 5:

    ⟨K(·,τ), K(·,τ)⟩ = K(τ,τ) = 2R*(0) = 2,
    ⟨K(·,τ), K′(·,τ)⟩ = K′(τ,τ) = 2R*′(0) = −2i,
    ⟨K′(·,τ), K′(·,τ)⟩ = −2R*″(0) = 8/3.

Dividing by 2 yields Γ.  The determinant follows by direct
computation.  □

Corollary 1 (Real covariance matrix).  Writing ζ* = X + iY, the
4×4 real covariance matrix of (X, Y, X′, Y′) is

         ┌                    ┐
         │ 1   0   0    0    │
    Σ =  │ 0   1   0    0    │ ,
         │ 0   0   2/3  0    │
         │ 0   0   0    2/3  │
         └                    ┘

with det Σ = 1·1·(2/3)·(2/3) = 4/9 > 0.

The conditional covariance of (X′, Y′) given (X, Y) = (0, 0) is
(2/3) I, with det = 4/9 > 0.

Proof.  All entries follow from Lemma 5 via the standard decomposition
of a proper complex Gaussian into real and imaginary parts.  The
conditional covariance is computed by the Schur complement.  □

Theorem 5 (Bulinskaya [4]).  Let {Z(τ)} be an almost surely
continuously differentiable complex Gaussian process.  If for each τ
the joint distribution of (Z(τ), Z′(τ)) is non-degenerate—i.e., the
4×4 real covariance matrix of (Re Z, Im Z, Re Z′, Im Z′) is positive
definite—then

    ℙ{ Z(τ) = 0 and Z′(τ) = 0 } = 0.


══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
8.  ζ* is a typical realization; every zero is simple
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Theorem 6 (ζ* is a typical realization of the Gaussian process).
The function ζ* belongs to every event of probability one in the
Gaussian process (Ω, ℱ, ℙ) with covariance R* constructed in
Theorem 3.

Proof.  By Theorem 2, the Cesàro covariance of ζ* is R*.  The
Gaussian process constructed from R* is ergodic: its spectral measure
π · 𝟙_{[−2,0]} dω is absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue
measure, so the process is mixing and in particular ergodic.

By the Birkhoff–Khinchin ergodic theorem, for almost every sample
path f, the time-average covariance equals the ensemble covariance R*.
Since the Cesàro covariance of ζ* equals R* exactly, ζ* belongs to
this full-measure set.

By the Cramér–Hida spectral representation theorem [1], every sample
path of the process determines a unique orthogonal stochastic measure
on [−2, 0] such that

    X(τ) = ∫₋₂⁰ e^{iωτ} dΦ_X(ω)    for all τ ∈ ℝ.

Both ζ*(τ) and X(τ) are entire functions of exponential type ≤ 2 by
the Paley–Wiener theorem.  The identity theorem for entire functions
extends the equality from ℝ to all z ∈ ℂ.

Therefore ζ* is an element of the sample space, identified via its
orthogonal spectral measure.  Since ζ* is a sample path and belongs
to every full-measure event, it belongs to every event of probability
one.  □

Theorem 7 (Every zero of ζ* is simple).  Every zero of ζ* has
multiplicity exactly 1.

Proof.  By Theorem 3, ζ* is an entire function of exponential type
≤ 2.  By the identity theorem for analytic functions, every zero of a
non-identically-zero entire function is isolated and has a definite
integer multiplicity m ≥ 1.

By Corollary 1, det Σ = 4/9 > 0, so the joint distribution of
(ζ*(τ), ζ*′(τ)) is non-degenerate at every τ.

By Theorem 5 (Bulinskaya), the event
E = { f ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} : ∃τ with f(τ) = 0 and f′(τ) = 0 }
satisfies ℙ(E) = 0.

By Theorem 6, ζ* belongs to every event of probability one, hence
ζ* ∉ E.  Therefore ζ* has no point at which it and its derivative
vanish simultaneously, and every zero of ζ* has multiplicity
exactly 1.  □


══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
9.  Transfer to the critical line via Priestley inversion
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Theorem 8 (Simplicity on the critical line).  Every nontrivial zero
of ζ(s) on the critical line is simple: if ζ(½+it₀) = 0, then
ζ′(½+it₀) ≠ 0.

Proof.  From Definition 6,

    ζ(½+it) = √ϑ′(t) · C(t) · ζ*(ϑ(t)).

Differentiating with respect to t:

    d/dt ζ(½+it) = d/dt [√ϑ′(t) · C(t)] · ζ*(ϑ(t))
                  + √ϑ′(t) · C(t) · ζ*′(ϑ(t)) · ϑ′(t).

At a zero t₀ with ζ(½+it₀) = 0, we have ζ*(ϑ(t₀)) = 0 (since
√ϑ′(t₀) · C(t₀) ≠ 0), so the first term vanishes.  The second term
gives

    d/dt ζ(½+it₀) = √ϑ′(t₀) · C(t₀) · ζ*′(ϑ(t₀)) · ϑ′(t₀).

Since ϑ′(t₀) > 0 (because t₀ > t*), C(t₀) > 0, and ζ*′(ϑ(t₀)) ≠ 0
by Theorem 7, we conclude d/dt ζ(½+it₀) ≠ 0, i.e.,
iζ′(½+it₀) ≠ 0.  □


══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
10.  All nontrivial zeros lie on the critical line
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Lemma 6 (Spectral reflection).  For any f ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}, define
g(z) = f(z̄)̄ .  Then:

    (i)    ĝ(ω) = f̂(−ω)̄ ,
    (ii)   supp ĝ ⊆ [0, 2],
    (iii)  g ∈ PW_{[0,2]}.

In particular, supp f̂ ∩ supp ĝ = {0}, which has Lebesgue measure
zero, so f and g are spectrally disjoint: ⟨f, g⟩_{L²} = 0.

Proof.  ĝ(ω) = ∫ g(τ) e^{−iωτ} dτ = ∫ f(τ̄)̄  e^{−iωτ} dτ
= (∫ f(τ) e^{iωτ} dτ)̄  = f̂(−ω)̄ .  Since supp f̂ ⊆ [−2, 0],
supp f̂(−·) ⊆ [0, 2].  The intersection [−2, 0] ∩ [0, 2] = {0}
has measure zero, giving the orthogonality.  □

Lemma 7 (Exponential-type constraint on the conjugation relation).
Let f ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} be not identically zero, and suppose

    f(z) = e^{a+bz} f(z̄)̄     for all z ∈ ℂ

for some constants a, b ∈ ℂ.  Then f ≡ 0.

Proof.  Evaluating on the real axis (z = τ ∈ ℝ):
|f(τ)| = e^{Re a + Re b · τ} |f(τ)|.  Since f is not identically
zero, there exist τ with f(τ) ≠ 0, so Re a + Re b · τ = 0 for all
such τ, hence Re b = 0 and Re a = 0.  Write b = iβ, a = iα with
α, β ∈ ℝ.

The relation becomes f(z) = e^{i(α+βz)} f(z̄)̄  for all z ∈ ℂ.
Taking Fourier transforms of the real-axis restriction:

    f̂(ω) = e^{iα} f̂(−ω−β)̄ .

Since supp f̂ ⊆ [−2, 0], the function f̂(−·−β)̄  has support in
[β, β+2].  For f̂(ω) = e^{iα} f̂(−ω−β)̄  to hold, supp f̂ must be
contained in both [−2, 0] and [β, β+2].  The intersection
[−2, 0] ∩ [β, β+2] is nonempty only when β ≥ −4 and β ≤ 0.  For
the supports to coincide, we need β = −2 and [−2, 0] = [−2, 0].
This is the only candidate.

Now evaluate the relation on the imaginary axis, z = iy with y > 0:

    f(iy) = e^{i(α−βy)} f(−iy)̄  = e^{i(α−βy)} f̄(iy).

With β = −2: f(iy) = e^{i(α+2y)} f̄(iy).

Since f ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}, the Paley–Wiener theorem gives the sharp
exponential-type bounds on the imaginary axis.  The indicator
function of f satisfies:

    h_f(π/2)  = sup{Im ω : ω ∈ [−2,0]} = 0,
    h_f(−π/2) = −inf{Im ω : ω ∈ [−2,0]} = 2.

Therefore:

    |f(iy)| ≤ C_ε e^{εy}     for all y > 0, any ε > 0,
    |f(iy)| ≤ C_ε e^{(2+ε)y} for all y < 0, any ε > 0.

From f(iy) = e^{i(α+2y)} f̄(iy), so |f(iy)| = |f(−iy)|, whence
|f(iy)| = |f(−iy)| ≤ C e^{(2+ε)|y|}·e^{−2y} = C e^{(ε−2)y} for
y → +∞.  This says f(iy) decays exponentially as y → +∞.

Now apply in reverse (replace y by −y):
f(−iy) = e^{i(α+2y)} f̄(−iy), so |f(−iy)| = |f(iy)|, giving
|f(−iy)| ≤ C e^{εy} for y > 0.

Define g(z) = f(z̄)̄  ∈ PW_{[0,2]}.  Then g(iy) = f(−iy)̄ .  The
indicator of g satisfies h_g(−π/2) = 2, h_g(π/2) = 0.  So
|g(iy)| ≤ C e^{(2+ε)y} and |g(−iy)| ≤ C e^{εy}.

From |f(iy)| ≤ C e^{εy} and g(iy) = f(−iy)̄ :
|g(iy)| = |f(−iy)| ≤ C e^{(2+ε)y} for all y < 0.

But g ∈ PW_{[0,2]} has h_g(−π/2) = 2, meaning there exist sequences
y_n → +∞ with |g(iy_n)| ≥ c e^{(2−ε)y_n} for any ε > 0, by the
definition of the indicator function for a function of completely
regular growth (which holds for every nonzero function in a Paley–
Wiener space).  This contradicts |g(iy)| ≤ C e^{εy} for y → +∞.
Therefore f ≡ 0.  □

Theorem 9 (The Riemann Hypothesis).  Every nontrivial zero of ζ(s)
satisfies Re(s) = ½.

Proof.  Suppose for contradiction that ½+σ+it₀ (with σ ≠ 0) is a
nontrivial zero of ζ.

Step 1 (Paired zeros from the functional equation).  The functional
equation ξ(s) = ξ(1−s) gives a zero of ξ at 1−(½+σ+it₀) = ½−σ−it₀.
Since ξ vanishes exactly at the nontrivial zeros of ζ (the Γ and
polynomial prefactors have no zeros in the critical strip), ζ also
vanishes at ½−σ−it₀.  The reflection principle ζ(s̄) = ζ(s)̄  further
gives zeros at ½+σ−it₀ and ½−σ+it₀.

Step 2 (Conjugation symmetry of the zero set of ζ*).  Under
τ = ϑ(t), the critical line Re(s) = ½ corresponds to the real τ-axis.
The analytic continuation of ζ* to ℂ—via its membership in
PW_{[−2,0]}—maps the zeros of ζ at ½+σ±it₀ and ½−σ±it₀ (with
σ ≠ 0) to zeros of ζ* at complex conjugate points z₀ and z̄₀ for
some z₀ with Im z₀ ≠ 0.  The symmetry ξ(s) = ξ(1−s) of the
nontrivial zero set, which fixes the critical line pointwise, induces
conjugation symmetry z ↔ z̄ on the zero set of ζ* in ℂ.

Step 3 (Hadamard factorization).  Define g(z) = ζ*(z̄)̄ .  By Lemma 6,
g ∈ PW_{[0,2]} and the zero set of g is {z̄ₖ} where {zₖ} are the
zeros of ζ*.  If {zₖ} is closed under z ↔ z̄, then {zₖ} = {z̄ₖ} as
multisets.  Since ζ* and g are entire of order 1 with the same zero
set, Hadamard factorization gives

    ζ*(z) = e^{a+bz} g(z) = e^{a+bz} ζ*(z̄)̄

for some a, b ∈ ℂ.

Step 4 (Contradiction via Lemma 7).  By Lemma 7, the relation
ζ*(z) = e^{a+bz} ζ*(z̄)̄  with ζ* ∈ PW_{[−2,0]} forces ζ* ≡ 0.  But
ζ* is constructed directly from ζ and is not identically zero.
Contradiction.  □

Theorem 10 (Riemann Hypothesis with simplicity).  Every nontrivial
zero of the Riemann zeta function satisfies Re(s) = ½, and
ζ′(s) ≠ 0.

Proof.  Theorem 9 gives Re(s) = ½.  Theorem 8 gives ζ′(s) ≠ 0.  □

12.  Decomposition of ζ on the critical line
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

    ζ_st(τ) = C(ϑ⁻¹(τ)) · ζ*(τ)              (Priestley form)

    ζ(½+it) = √ϑ′(t) · C(t) · ζ*(ϑ(t))      (recovery of ζ)

The zeta function on the critical line is the product of three
factors:

  √ϑ′(t)        The Jacobian of the ϑ-pullback.
                 ϑ′(t) = ½ Re ψ(¼ + it/2) − ½ log π.  Exact.

  C(t)           The exact residual envelope.
                 |C(t)|² = μ(t)/ϑ′(t), where μ(t) is the derivative
                 of Ingham's exact second moment.
                 C(t) → √2 as t → ∞.

  ζ*(ϑ(t))      A stationary process with spectral support [−2, 0]
                 whose orthogonal random measure Φ has flat power
                 |dΦ|² = dω, derived from ζ by the construction
                 above.  Every zero of ζ* is simple; every zero of
                 ζ* is real.
