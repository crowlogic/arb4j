            CUMULATIVE PHASE, WHITE NOISE, AND CONVERGENCE

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                               ABSTRACT
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Riemann zeta function on the critical line is a complex-valued function
whose polar factorization ζ(½+it) = |ζ(½+it)|·e^{i·arg ζ(½+it)} encodes two
objects: the Ingham mean-square density μ(t) = |ζ(½+it)|² in the modulus, and
the full phase structure of ζ in the argument. A piecewise monotonic
modification ϑ★ of the Riemann–Siegel theta function ϑ(t) furnishes a global
time-change from cumulative time to cumulative phase. The pullback

             ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(T))
  X(T) = ─────────────────────────
           √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(T))|

is shown to be band-limited to [−2, 0] by a term-by-term analysis of the
Riemann–Siegel formula: each Dirichlet term n^{−it}/√n has instantaneous
frequency −log n / |ϑ′(t)| in the phase variable, confined to [−1, 0] by the
bound log n ≤ ½·log(t/2π); the functional equation factor χ(½+it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)}
shifts the second-sum frequencies to [−2, −1].

The analytic core of the construction is the convergence of the Fourier
inversion integral defining the orthogonal random measure dZ(ω) = X̂(ω) dω.
This integral converges because the phase variable S = ϑ(t) renders the zeros
of ζ equispaced at mean gap 2π, independent of height. In the original
t-variable, zero spacing shrinks as 2π/ϑ′(t) → 0, and no convergence argument
closes. In the phase variable, the sign alternation at each zero produces an
alternating series with slowly varying terms, convergent by Abel summation.

This establishes the existence of a stationary white-noise base process Z(ω)
on [−2, 0] underlying ζ. The oscillatory process representation

  ζ(½+it) = ∫₋₂⁰ Aₜ(ω)·e^{iωt} dZ(ω)

with gain Aₜ(ω) = √|ϑ′(t)|·e^{iω(ϑ★(t)−t)} and evolutionary spectral density
dFₜ(ω) = |ϑ′(t)|·dμ(ω) follows, where dμ(ω) is the spectral measure of the
stationary base process.

§1.  INTRODUCTION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

§1.1  The Polar Factorization
─────────────────────────────

Every nonzero complex number z admits a unique polar factorization
z = |z|·e^{i·arg z}. Applied pointwise to ζ(½+it) for t not a zero:

  ζ(½+it) = |ζ(½+it)| · e^{i·arg ζ(½+it)}                          ⋯ (1.1)

The modulus |ζ(½+it)| = √μ(t) carries the Ingham mean-square density
μ(t) = |ζ(½+it)|², whose cumulative integral

  I(t) = ∫₀ᵗ μ(u) du = ∫₀ᵗ |ζ(½+iu)|² du

measures the total L²-mass accumulated up to height t. The argument
arg ζ(½+it) carries the phase of ζ, encoding the locations and structure of
the zeros. The polar factorization (1.1) is the observation that these two
objects — the energy density and the phase — are the modulus and argument of
a single complex-valued function. The mean-square density is not an external
weight imposed on ζ; it is the squared modulus of ζ itself.


§1.2  The Central Problem: Existence of the Base Process
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Priestley's theory of oscillatory processes represents a non-stationary
process X(t) as

  X(t) = ∫ Aₜ(ω) · e^{iωt} dZ(ω)                                   ⋯ (1.2)

where Z(ω) is a stationary orthogonal random measure with 𝔼|dZ(ω)|² = dμ(ω),
and Aₜ(ω) is a slowly varying gain function. The evolutionary power spectral
density at time t is dFₜ(ω) = |Aₜ(ω)|² dμ(ω).

The representation (1.2) requires, as its input, a stationary base process
Z(ω) with a well-defined spectral measure dμ(ω). For ζ(½+it), the claim is
that such an object exists and is supported on the compact band [−2, 0]. This
requires proving that the Fourier inversion integral

              ∞
  X̂(ω) = ∫     ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))
          −∞  ─────────────────────── · e^{−iωS} dS                  ⋯ (1.3)
               √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|

converges. This is the integral that defines the orthogonal random measure
dZ(ω) = X̂(ω) dω, and its convergence is the analytic core of the entire
construction.

In the original time variable t, no analogous integral converges: the zero
spacing of ζ(½+it) shrinks as 2π/ϑ′(t) → 0, so oscillatory cancellation is
height-dependent and no uniform estimate controls the tail. The
reparameterization from cumulative time t to cumulative phase S = ϑ(t) renders
the zero spacing uniform at 2π, and this uniformity is what makes the
convergence proof work.

§2.  THE RIEMANN–SIEGEL THETA FUNCTION AND ITS MODIFICATION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

§2.1  Definition and Asymptotics
────────────────────────────────

𝐃𝐞𝐟𝐢𝐧𝐢𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝟐.𝟏. The Riemann–Siegel theta function is

  ϑ(t) = Im log Γ(¼ + it/2) − (t/2) log π                          ⋯ (2.1)

with the branch ϑ(0) = 0.                                                 ■

The derivative satisfies

  ϑ′(t) = ½ log(t/2π) − 1/(8t²) + 𝒪(t⁻⁴),   t → +∞               ⋯ (2.2)

so ϑ′(t) < 0 for t small (where log(t/2π) < 0) and ϑ′(t) → +∞. There is a
unique critical point t_c ≈ 6.290 with ϑ′(t_c) = 0.

  • On [0, t_c): ϑ′ < 0, ϑ strictly decreasing.
  • On (t_c, ∞): ϑ′ > 0, ϑ strictly increasing.

The minimum value is ϑ(t_c) ≈ −1.772. The second derivative at t_c is
ϑ″(t_c) = 1/(2t_c) + 𝒪(t_c⁻³) > 0, so the critical point is nondegenerate:
ϑ′(t) vanishes linearly at t_c.


§2.2  The Piecewise Monotonic Modification
──────────────────────────────────────────

𝐃𝐞𝐟𝐢𝐧𝐢𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝟐.𝟐. Define

             ⎧ 2ϑ(t_c) − ϑ(t),    t ∈ [0, t_c]
  ϑ★(t) =   ⎨                                                       ⋯ (2.3)
             ⎩ ϑ(t),               t ∈ [t_c, ∞)
                                                                           ■

𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐩𝐨𝐬𝐢𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝟐.𝟑. The function ϑ★ has the following properties:

  (i)   𝗖𝗼𝗻𝘁𝗶𝗻𝘂𝗶𝘁𝘆.  Both branches give ϑ(t_c) at t_c.

  (ii)  𝗝𝗮𝗰𝗼𝗯𝗶𝗮𝗻.    ϑ★′(t) = |ϑ′(t)| for t ≠ t_c, with ϑ★′(t_c) = 0.

  (iii) 𝗦𝘁𝗿𝗶𝗰𝘁 𝗺𝗼𝗻𝗼𝘁𝗼𝗻𝗶𝗰𝗶𝘁𝘆.  ϑ★ is strictly increasing on [0, ∞).

  (iv)  𝗥𝗮𝗻𝗴𝗲.  ϑ★([0,∞)) = [2ϑ(t_c), ∞).

  (v)   𝗜𝗻𝘃𝗲𝗿𝘀𝗲.  ϑ★⁻¹ : [2ϑ(t_c), ∞) → [0, ∞) is well-defined and satisfies

          ϑ★⁻¹(ϑ★(t)) = t      ∀ t ∈ [0, ∞)                        ⋯ (2.4)
          ϑ★(ϑ★⁻¹(T)) = T      ∀ T ∈ [2ϑ(t_c), ∞)                 ⋯ (2.5)

𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐨𝐟.

(i): 2ϑ(t_c) − ϑ(t_c) = ϑ(t_c). ✓

(ii): On (0, t_c), ϑ★′ = −ϑ′ = |ϑ′| > 0 since ϑ′ < 0.
On (t_c, ∞), ϑ★′ = ϑ′ = |ϑ′| > 0. ✓

(iii): Follows from (ii) and (i). ✓

(iv): ϑ★(0) = 2ϑ(t_c) − 0 = 2ϑ(t_c); ϑ★(t_c) = ϑ(t_c); ϑ★(t) → ∞.
Since 2ϑ(t_c) < ϑ(t_c) < 0, the reflected piece covers [2ϑ(t_c), ϑ(t_c)]
and the main piece covers [ϑ(t_c), ∞); these are contiguous. ✓

(v): Immediate from strict monotonicity and surjectivity. ✓                ∎

§3.  THE UNITARY PULLBACK
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

𝐃𝐞𝐟𝐢𝐧𝐢𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝟑.𝟏. The unitary pullback is

             ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(T))
  X(T) = ─────────────────────────,   T ∈ [2ϑ(t_c), ∞)              ⋯ (3.1)
             √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(T))|
                                                                           ■

𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐩𝐨𝐬𝐢𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝟑.𝟐 (𝗜𝘀𝗼𝗺𝗲𝘁𝗿𝘆). For any Borel set E ⊆ [2ϑ(t_c), ∞) with
ϑ★⁻¹(E) ⊆ (0, ∞) compact,

  ∫_E |X(T)|² dT  =  ∫_{ϑ★⁻¹(E)} |ζ(½+it)|² dt                   ⋯ (3.2)

𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐨𝐟. Write |X(T)|² = |ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(T))|² / |ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(T))|. Substitute
T = ϑ★(t), so dT = |ϑ′(t)| dt:

  ∫_E |X(T)|² dT = ∫_{ϑ★⁻¹(E)}  |ζ(½+it)|²      |ϑ′(t)| dt
                                 ─────────────  ·
                                   |ϑ′(t)|

                  = ∫_{ϑ★⁻¹(E)} μ(t) dt

The Jacobian |ϑ′(t)| in the numerator and the Jacobian |ϑ′(t)| dt from the
change of measure cancel exactly. This cancellation is the content of the word
"unitary": the 1/√|ϑ′| factor in the definition of X is precisely what makes
the map ζ(½+it) ↦ X(ϑ★(t)) an isometry from (L²([a,b], dt), ‖·‖) to
(L²([ϑ★(a), ϑ★(b)], dT), ‖·‖).                                           ∎

𝐑𝐞𝐦𝐚𝐫𝐤 𝟑.𝟑. The isometry (3.2) says that the total L²-energy of ζ on any
height interval [a,b] equals the total L²-energy of X on the corresponding
phase interval [ϑ★(a), ϑ★(b)]. No energy is created or destroyed by the
time-change.

§4.  BAND-LIMITATION VIA THE RIEMANN–SIEGEL FORMULA
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

§4.1  The Riemann–Siegel Formula
────────────────────────────────

For N(t) = ⌊√(t/2π)⌋:

                  N(t)  e^{−it log n}            N(t)  e^{it log n}
  ζ(½+it)  =  Σ  ───────────────────  +  e^{−2iϑ(t)} Σ  ──────────── + R(t)
               n=1       √n                      n=1       √n
               ╰──────────────────╯      ╰─────────────────────────╯
                    first sum                    second sum           ⋯ (4.1)

where |R⁽ʲ⁾(t)| ≤ Cⱼ · t^{−¼−j/2} for all j ≥ 0, and
χ(½+it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)} has modulus 1 (the functional equation on the critical
line).


§4.2  Instantaneous Frequencies of the First Sum
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

The n-th term e^{−it log n}/√n pulled back to the variable T = ϑ★(t) becomes
e^{−i(log n)·ϑ★⁻¹(T)}/√n. Its instantaneous frequency in the T-variable is

                d
  Φ′₋,ₙ(T) = − ── [(log n)·ϑ★⁻¹(T)]  =  − log n / |ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(T))|  ⋯ (4.2)
                dT

For n ≤ N(t) = ⌊√(t/2π)⌋ and t large:

     log n          ½ log(t/2π) + 𝒪(1)
  ────────────  ≤  ──────────────────────  =  1 + o(1)               ⋯ (4.3)
   |ϑ′(t)|        ½ log(t/2π) + 𝒪(t⁻²)

Therefore the instantaneous frequencies of all first-sum terms lie in
[−1−o(1), 0].


§4.3  Instantaneous Frequencies of the Second Sum
──────────────────────────────────────────────────

The n-th term of the second sum (including the χ-factor e^{−2iϑ(t)}) has phase
Ψₙ(t) = −2ϑ(t) + t log n. On the main piece (t ≥ t_c, ϑ★ = ϑ), the
instantaneous frequency in the T-variable is

                 −2ϑ′(t) + log n │
  Φ′₊,ₙ(T)  =  ────────────────  │           = −2 + log n / |ϑ′(t)| ⋯ (4.4)
                    ϑ′(t)         │t=ϑ★⁻¹(T)

By (4.3), log n / |ϑ′(t)| ∈ [0, 1+o(1)], so Φ′₊,ₙ(T) ∈ [−2, −1+o(1)].

On the reflected piece (t ≤ t_c ≈ 6.29), N(t) ≤ ⌊√(t_c/2π)⌋ = 1, so only
n = 1 with log 1 = 0 contributes; the instantaneous frequency is bounded.


§4.4  Assembly
──────────────

  • The first sum contributes frequencies in [−1, 0].
  • The second sum contributes frequencies in [−2, −1].
  • The remainder R(t) satisfies |R(t)| ≤ C·t⁻¹ᐟ⁴; its pullback is
    integrable and its Fourier transform is Schwartz-class.

Derivative bounds on the amplitudes, obtained via Faà di Bruno's formula
applied to the Dirichlet coefficients composed with ϑ★⁻¹, establish slow
amplitude variation. The Montgomery–Vaughan mean-value theorem, applied with
frozen coefficients on windows [t₀, 2t₀] in the phase variable, confirms

  supp X̂ ⊆ [−2, 0]                                                  ⋯ (4.5)

§5.  CONVERGENCE OF THE INVERSION INTEGRAL
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This section is the analytical heart of the construction. The object whose
existence must be established is

              ∞
  X̂(ω) = ∫     ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))
          −∞  ─────────────────────── · e^{−iωS} dS                  ⋯ (5.1)
               √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|

the Fourier transform of the pullback X, which defines the orthogonal random
measure dZ(ω) = X̂(ω) dω and thereby the stationary base process underlying ζ.
The convergence is established by splitting at S₀ = ϑ(t_c) into a reflected
piece and a main piece.


§5.1  The Reflected Piece: Absolute Convergence
───────────────────────────────────────────────

𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐩𝐨𝐬𝐢𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧 𝟓.𝟏. The integral

                    ϑ(t_c)
  I_ref(ω) =  ∫            ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))
               2ϑ(t_c)   ─────────────────────── · e^{−iωS} dS      ⋯ (5.2)
                            √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|

converges absolutely for all ω ∈ ℝ and defines an entire function of ω.

𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐨𝐟.

For S ∈ [2ϑ(t_c), ϑ(t_c)], we have ϑ★⁻¹(S) ∈ [0, t_c].

𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗻𝘂𝗺𝗲𝗿𝗮𝘁𝗼𝗿 𝗶𝘀 𝗯𝗼𝘂𝗻𝗱𝗲𝗱. The first nontrivial zero of ζ on the critical
line has imaginary part γ₁ ≈ 14.135. Since t_c ≈ 6.29 < 14.135, the function
ζ(½+it) has no zeros on [0, t_c] and is therefore continuous and bounded on
this compact interval: |ζ(½+it)| ≤ M for some M > 0 and all t ∈ [0, t_c].

𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗱𝗲𝗻𝗼𝗺𝗶𝗻𝗮𝘁𝗼𝗿 𝘀𝗶𝗻𝗴𝘂𝗹𝗮𝗿𝗶𝘁𝘆 𝗶𝘀 𝗶𝗻𝘁𝗲𝗴𝗿𝗮𝗯𝗹𝗲. The factor |ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|
vanishes only when ϑ★⁻¹(S) = t_c, i.e. at S = ϑ(t_c). Since ϑ″(t_c) ≠ 0:

  |ϑ′(t)| ∼ |ϑ″(t_c)| · |t − t_c|     as t → t_c

In the S-variable, the map t ↦ ϑ★(t) satisfies

  ϑ★(t) − ϑ(t_c) ∼ ½|ϑ″(t_c)|(t − t_c)²     near t_c

so |t − t_c| ∼ √(2|S − ϑ(t_c)| / |ϑ″(t_c)|). Therefore:

         1                         1                      C
  ──────────────────── ∼ ──────────────────────── ∼ ─────────────────
  √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|      √(|ϑ″(t_c)| · |t−t_c|)    |S − ϑ(t_c)|¹ᐟ⁴

and |S − ϑ(t_c)|⁻¹ᐟ⁴ is integrable near S = ϑ(t_c) since ¼ < 1.

The integrand of (5.2) is therefore bounded by M·C·|S − ϑ(t_c)|⁻¹ᐟ⁴, which is
in L¹([2ϑ(t_c), ϑ(t_c)]). Absolute convergence follows. By dominated
convergence and Morera's theorem, I_ref(ω) extends to an entire function of ω.
                                                                             ∎

§5.2  The Main Piece: Why Cumulative Phase Makes It Work
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

For S ∈ [ϑ(t_c), ∞), ϑ★⁻¹(S) = ϑ⁻¹(S) ∈ [t_c, ∞), and
ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S)) has infinitely many zeros at the points Sₖ = ϑ(γₖ), where
γ₁ < γ₂ < ⋯ are the positive imaginary parts of the nontrivial zeros.


§5.2.1  Zero Spacing in the Phase Variable

The Riemann–von Mangoldt formula gives the zero-counting function

  N(T) = #{k : γₖ ≤ T} = (T/2π) log(T/2πe) + 𝒪(log T)

The density of zeros at height T is N′(T) = ϑ′(T)/π (to leading order). The
mean spacing between consecutive zeros at height ∼T is Δγ ∼ 2π/ϑ′(T).

In the S-variable S = ϑ(T), the spacing becomes:

  Sₖ₊₁ − Sₖ  =  ϑ(γₖ₊₁) − ϑ(γₖ)
              ≈  ϑ′(γₖ) · Δγ
              ≈  ϑ′(γₖ) · 2π/ϑ′(γₖ)
              =  2π                                                  ⋯ (5.3)

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  The zeros of ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S)) in the phase variable S are         │
│  equispaced at mean gap 2π, independent of height.                  │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

This is the key fact. In the original t-variable, the zero spacing shrinks to
zero: Δγ ∼ 2π/ϑ′(γₖ) → 0 as k → ∞. Any Fourier integral of ζ(½+it) in the
t-variable faces oscillatory cancellation whose rate depends on height — the
cancellation accelerates without bound, and no uniform estimate controls the
tail. In the phase variable, the cancellation rate is uniform.


§5.2.2  Sign Alternation

Between consecutive zeros Sₖ and Sₖ₊₁ (in the S-variable),
ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S)) does not vanish, so it maintains a definite sign on each
inter-zero interval (Sₖ, Sₖ₊₁). At each zero, ζ changes sign (the zeros on
the critical line are simple — known unconditionally for the first 10¹³ zeros
and expected in general). Thus ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S)) alternates sign on consecutive
inter-zero intervals.


§5.2.3  The Slowly Varying Envelope

Define the envelope:

            |ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S))|
  E(S) = ──────────────────────                                      ⋯ (5.4)
           √|ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(S))|

The relative variation of E(S) over one inter-zero interval [Sₖ, Sₖ₊₁] (of
length ∼ 2π) is bounded as follows.

𝗩𝗮𝗿𝗶𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗼𝗳 |ϑ′(t)|. On the height interval [γₖ, γₖ₊₁] of length
∼ 2π/ϑ′(γₖ):

  Δ|ϑ′|     |ϑ″(γₖ)| · Δγ       (1/(2γₖ) + 𝒪(γₖ⁻³)) · 2π/ϑ′(γₖ)
  ─────  ∼  ────────────────  =  ──────────────────────────────────────
  |ϑ′|         |ϑ′(γₖ)|                    ϑ′(γₖ)

                                        𝒪(1)
                                =  ────────────────  =  o(1)
                                   γₖ · (log γₖ)²

So |ϑ′| varies by a fraction o(1) over one inter-zero interval, and √|ϑ′|
varies by half that.

𝗩𝗮𝗿𝗶𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗼𝗳 |ζ|. The maximum of |ζ(½+it)| on [γₖ, γₖ₊₁] is controlled by
the Lindelöf estimate: |ζ(½+it)| ≤ C_ε · t^ε for any ε > 0 (unconditional).
On the inter-zero interval, the average value of |ζ| is of order (log γₖ)⁻¹ᐟ²
(by the mean-value theorem applied in the phase variable). The relative
variation of |ζ| within one inter-zero interval is bounded by 𝒪(γₖ^{−1+ε})
for any ε > 0.

The envelope E(S) is therefore slowly varying on the scale 2π of one inter-zero
interval: it changes by a fraction o(1) between Sₖ and Sₖ₊₁.


§5.2.4  Abel Summation

Write the main piece as

                  ∞
  I_main(ω) =  Σ  bₖ(ω)                                            ⋯ (5.5)
                k=0

where
                     Sₖ₊₁
  bₖ(ω) = ∫          ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S))
            Sₖ      ─────────────────── · e^{−iωS} dS               ⋯ (5.6)
                     √|ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(S))|

On [Sₖ, Sₖ₊₁], ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S)) has constant sign (−1)ᵏ (fixing sign
conventions at k = 0). The integrand of bₖ is therefore a product of three
factors:

  (a) The sign (−1)ᵏ.

  (b) A positive, slowly varying envelope E(S), approximately constant at
      Eₖ ≡ E(½(Sₖ + Sₖ₊₁)) on [Sₖ, Sₖ₊₁].

  (c) A bounded oscillatory factor e^{−iωS}, which integrates to 𝒪(1/|ω|) over
      an interval of length ∼ 2π when ω ≠ 0, and to 𝒪(2π) when ω = 0.

Let Bₖ = ∫_{Sₖ}^{Sₖ₊₁} |ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S))| / √|ϑ′(ϑ⁻¹(S))| dS, so that
|bₖ| ≤ Bₖ.

𝘽ₖ → 𝟬. Each Bₖ equals

  ∫_{γₖ}^{γₖ₊₁} |ζ(½+it)| / √|ϑ′(t)| · |ϑ′(t)| dt
  = ∫_{γₖ}^{γₖ₊₁} |ζ(½+it)| · √|ϑ′(t)| dt

By the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality:

  Bₖ² ≤ (γₖ₊₁ − γₖ) · ∫_{γₖ}^{γₖ₊₁} |ζ(½+it)|² · |ϑ′(t)| dt
         ╰─────────╯    ╰───────────────────────────────────────╯
         ∼ 2π/ϑ′(γₖ)                      Jₖ

The first factor tends to zero. The second factor Jₖ is bounded: Jₖ equals
∫_{Sₖ}^{Sₖ₊₁} |ζ(½ + i·ϑ⁻¹(S))|² dS (by the Jacobian identity), and the mean
value of |ζ|² in the phase variable over one period 2π is bounded by the
Hardy–Littlewood–Ingham estimate. Therefore

  Bₖ² ≤ 𝒪(2π/ϑ′(γₖ)) · 𝒪(1) → 0

𝗘𝗳𝗳𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝘃𝗲 𝗮𝗹𝘁𝗲𝗿𝗻𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻. Fix ω ∈ [−2, 0]. The factor (−1)ᵏ · e^{−iωS}
integrated over [Sₖ, Sₖ₊₁] of length ∼ 2π produces an effective sign change
between consecutive k: since the interval has length ∼ 2π and ω ∈ [−2, 0], the
oscillation e^{−iωS} traverses at most 4π radians, so the integrated
contributions from consecutive intervals retain the (−1)ᵏ alternation (up to a
bounded phase rotation that does not destroy the alternating character).

Abel's test (Dirichlet's test for conditional convergence of series) now
applies: the partial sums Σₖ₌₀ᴷ (−1)ᵏ are bounded, the sequence Bₖ → 0, and
Bₖ is eventually decreasing. Therefore (5.5) converges for each ω ∈ [−2, 0].

The convergence is uniform on compact subsets of [−2, 0] because the estimates
on Bₖ are independent of ω.                                                 ∎


§5.3  Why This Fails in the t-Variable
───────────────────────────────────────

If one attempts the same factorization in the t-variable — splitting the
integral ∫₀^∞ ζ(½+it)·e^{−iωt} dt over inter-zero intervals [γₖ, γₖ₊₁] — the
analogous terms bₖ have |bₖ| ≤ Bₖ where now
Bₖ = ∫_{γₖ}^{γₖ₊₁} |ζ(½+it)| dt. The interval length
γₖ₊₁ − γₖ ∼ 2π/ϑ′(γₖ) → 0, so Bₖ → 0 as well.

However, the effective alternation is now 𝗻𝗼𝘁 𝗰𝗼𝗻𝘁𝗿𝗼𝗹𝗹𝗲𝗱: the phase e^{−iωt}
traverses ω·(γₖ₊₁ − γₖ) ∼ 2πω/ϑ′(γₖ) → 0 radians on consecutive inter-zero
intervals, so consecutive bₖ become 𝗰𝗼-𝘀𝗶𝗴𝗻𝗲𝗱 in the limit, and the series
reduces to a positive series that diverges (since Σ Bₖ = ∞ by the divergence
of the Ingham integral ∫₀^∞ |ζ(½+it)| dt = ∞).

The Abel summation argument requires the effective sign alternation per
inter-zero interval to be 𝒪(1), which holds in the phase variable (where each
interval has length ∼ 2π) but fails in the time variable (where each interval
shrinks to zero).

§6.  THE MAIN THEOREM
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

𝐓𝐡𝐞𝐨𝐫𝐞𝐦 𝟔.𝟏. The Riemann zeta function on the critical line satisfies

                     0
  ζ(½+it) = ∫      Aₜ(ω) · e^{iωt} dZ(ω)                           ⋯ (6.1)
              −2

where

  Aₜ(ω) = √|ϑ′(t)| · e^{iω(ϑ★(t)−t)}                              ⋯ (6.2)

  dZ(ω) = X̂(ω) dω                                                   ⋯ (6.3)

and X̂(ω) is given by the convergent integral (5.1). The base spectral measure
is dμ(ω) = |X̂(ω)|² dω, and the evolutionary power spectral density at time t
is

  dFₜ(ω) = |Aₜ(ω)|² dμ(ω) = |ϑ′(t)| · dμ(ω)                      ⋯ (6.4)


𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐨𝐟.

𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟭: 𝗙𝗼𝗿𝘄𝗮𝗿𝗱 𝗿𝗲𝗽𝗿𝗲𝘀𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻. §4 gives supp X̂ ⊆ [−2, 0].
Band-limitation yields

                    0
  X(T) = ∫       e^{iωT} X̂(ω) dω                                   ⋯ (6.5)
           −2

𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟮: 𝗜𝗻𝘃𝗲𝗿𝘀𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗳𝗼𝗿𝗺𝘂𝗹𝗮. §5 establishes convergence of

              ∞   ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))
  X̂(ω) = ∫     ─────────────────────── · e^{−iωS} dS               ⋯ (6.6)
          −∞     √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|

𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟯: 𝗗𝗼𝘂𝗯𝗹𝗲 𝗶𝗻𝘁𝗲𝗴𝗿𝗮𝗹. Insert (6.6) into (6.5):

              0                ∞   ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))
  X(T) = ∫      e^{iωT} · [ ∫     ─────────────────── · e^{−iωS} dS ] dω
           −2              −∞    √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|
                                                                     ⋯ (6.7)

𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟰: 𝗙𝘂𝗯𝗶𝗻𝗶. The outer integral is over the compact set [−2, 0]. The inner
integral converges: absolutely on the reflected piece (Proposition 5.1),
conditionally on the main piece (§5.2). The factor |e^{iωT}| = 1 is bounded.
The reflected piece contributes an L^∞ function of ω (since I_ref is entire).
The main piece contributes a bounded function of ω (by the Abel summation
estimate, which is uniform in ω). Fubini's theorem applies:

              ∞   ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))           0
  X(T) = ∫     ─────────────────────── · [ ∫      e^{iω(T−S)} dω ] dS
          −∞     √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|          −2
                                                                     ⋯ (6.8)

𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟱: 𝗦𝗶𝗻𝗰 𝗸𝗲𝗿𝗻𝗲𝗹. Set τ = T − S. Compute the inner integral:

   0
  ∫    e^{iωτ} dω  =  e^{iωτ}/(iτ) │         =  (1 − e^{−2iτ}) / (iτ)
  −2                                │ω=−2→0

                    =  e^{−iτ}(e^{iτ} − e^{−iτ}) / (iτ)

                    =  2i sin(τ) · e^{−iτ} / (iτ)

                    =  2 sin(τ)/τ · e^{−iτ}

                    =  K(τ)                                          ⋯ (6.9)

Therefore:

              ∞   ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))
  X(T) = ∫     ─────────────────────── · K(T−S) dS                  ⋯ (6.10)
          −∞     √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|

𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟲: 𝗥𝗲𝗰𝗼𝘃𝗲𝗿 ζ. By definition, X(T) = ζ(½+i·ϑ★⁻¹(T))/√|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(T))|.
Equating with (6.10) and multiplying both sides by √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(T))|:

                                           ∞   ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))
  ζ(½+i·ϑ★⁻¹(T)) = √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(T))| · ∫     ─────────────────── · K(T−S) dS
                                        −∞     √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|
                                                                     ⋯ (6.11)

Set T = ϑ★(t). On the left side, by ϑ★⁻¹(ϑ★(t)) = t:

  ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(ϑ★(t))) = ζ(½+it)                                   ⋯ (6.12)

The prefactor:

  √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(ϑ★(t)))| = √|ϑ′(t)|                                   ⋯ (6.13)

The kernel argument: K(ϑ★(t) − S).

The variable S in the integral is free; ϑ★⁻¹(S) is 𝐧𝐨𝐭 𝐬𝐢𝐦𝐩𝐥𝐢𝐟𝐢𝐞𝐝.

This is the reproducing identity:

                                  ∞   ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(S))
  ζ(½+it) = √|ϑ′(t)| · ∫          ─────────────────────── · K(ϑ★(t)−S) dS
                          −∞         √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(S))|
                                                                     ⋯ (6.14)

𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟳: 𝗢𝘀𝗰𝗶𝗹𝗹𝗮𝘁𝗼𝗿𝘆 𝗳𝗼𝗿𝗺. Reverse the Fubini step:

                              0
  ζ(½+it)  =  √|ϑ′(t)| · ∫      e^{iω·ϑ★(t)} · X̂(ω) dω
                            −2
                 0

              −2    ╰─────────────────────────────╯

                 0
           = ∫      Aₜ(ω) · e^{iωt} · dZ(ω)
              −2

§7.  ORTHOGONALITY AND RESOLUTION OF THE IDENTITY
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

𝐓𝐡𝐞𝐨𝐫𝐞𝐦 𝟕.𝟏. For ω, ω′ ∈ [−2, 0], in the distributional sense:

   ∞
  ∫   |ϑ′(t)| · e^{i(ω′−ω)·ϑ★(t)} dt  =  δ(ω′ − ω)              ⋯ (7.1)
   0

𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐨𝐟. Set λ = ω′ − ω. Split at t_c:

   ∞
  ∫   |ϑ′(t)| · e^{iλ·ϑ★(t)} dt  =  J_ref(λ) + J_main(λ)
   0

𝗥𝗲𝗳𝗹𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗲𝗱 𝗽𝗶𝗲𝗰𝗲. Under the pushforward t ↦ ϑ★(t)|_{[0,t_c]}, the measure
(−ϑ′(t)) dt becomes Lebesgue measure on [2ϑ(t_c), ϑ(t_c)]:

                    ϑ(t_c)
  J_ref(λ) =  ∫            e^{iλu} du
               2ϑ(t_c)

This is absolutely convergent, entire in λ, and J_ref(λ) → 0 as |λ| → ∞ by
the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma (it is the Fourier transform of a compactly
supported L¹ function).

𝗠𝗮𝗶𝗻 𝗽𝗶𝗲𝗰𝗲. Under the pushforward t ↦ ϑ(t)|_{[t_c,∞)}, the measure ϑ′(t) dt
becomes Lebesgue measure on [ϑ(t_c), ∞):

                       ∞
  J_main(λ) =  ∫         e^{iλu} du
                ϑ(t_c)

𝗔𝘀𝘀𝗲𝗺𝗯𝗹𝘆. The two intervals are contiguous:

                              ∞
  J_ref(λ) + J_main(λ) = ∫        e^{iλu} du
                           2ϑ(t_c)

Extending ϑ★ to negative t by odd symmetry contributes the half-line
(−∞, 2ϑ(t_c)]. The full integral is

   ∞
  ∫    e^{iλu} du  =  2π δ(λ)
  −∞

With appropriate normalization, (7.1) follows. The Riemann–Lebesgue lemma
controls the reflected piece: it is entire, vanishing at infinity, so it
contributes no distributional defect. The distributional singularity δ(λ)
arises entirely from the main piece (the half-line integral), and the reflected
piece fills in the finite gap [2ϑ(t_c), ϑ(t_c)] so that the full Lebesgue
measure on ℝ is recovered.                                                 ∎


𝐂𝐨𝐫𝐨𝐥𝐥𝐚𝐫𝐲 𝟕.𝟐 (𝗥𝗲𝘀𝗼𝗹𝘂𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗼𝗳 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗶𝗱𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗶𝘁𝘆). For X ∈ PW_{[−2,0]}:

   0               ∞
  ∫    e^{iωT} · [∫   X(S) · e^{−iωS} dS] dω  =  X(T)
  −2               −∞

𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐨𝐟. Exchange orders (Fubini) and compute the inner integral as the sinc
kernel K(T−S), then invoke the reproducing property of K for PW_{[−2,0]}.
Alternatively, apply Theorem 7.1: the δ(ω′−ω) identity means the composition
inversion ∘ forward is the identity on PW_{[−2,0]}.                       ∎

§8.  IDENTIFICATION WITH PRIESTLEY'S FRAMEWORK
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

§8.1  The Oscillatory Process Condition
───────────────────────────────────────

By Definition 3.1 of Priestley [1965], a function ϕₜ(ω) = Aₜ(ω)·e^{iωt} is
oscillatory if, for each fixed ω, the modulus of the generalized Fourier
transform of Aₜ(ω) in t achieves its absolute maximum at zero frequency.

Here Aₜ(ω) = √|ϑ′(t)| · e^{iω(ϑ★(t)−t)}. Its modulus |Aₜ(ω)| = √|ϑ′(t)|
grows as 𝒪((log t)¹ᐟ²) and is independent of ω. Its phase ω(ϑ★(t) − t) has
t-derivative ω(|ϑ′(t)| − 1) ∼ ω · ½ log t, which is sublinear relative to the
carrier e^{iωt}. A compactly supported test function w(t/T) with T → ∞ applied
to Aₜ(ω) produces a Fourier transform concentrated near ξ = 0 with bandwidth
𝒪(1/T). The gain condition of Definition 3.1 is satisfied.


§8.2  The Evolutionary Power Spectral Density
─────────────────────────────────────────────

By Definition 4.1 of Priestley [1965], the evolutionary power spectrum at time
t is dFₜ(ω) = |Aₜ(ω)|² dμ(ω). The base spectral measure dμ(ω) is the spectral
measure of the stationary orthogonal process Z(ω): 𝔼|dZ(ω)|² = dμ(ω). By
construction, dZ(ω) = X̂(ω) dω, so dμ(ω) = |X̂(ω)|² dω as a consequence of the
specific construction, not by definition. The evolutionary spectral density is

  dFₜ(ω) = |ϑ′(t)| · dμ(ω)

At each time t, the total energy is

                                0                        0
  var X(ϑ★(t)) = ∫           dFₜ(ω) = |ϑ′(t)| · ∫         dμ(ω)
                   −2                               −2


§8.3  The Process Is Not Uniformly Modulated
────────────────────────────────────────────

By Definition 5 of Priestley [1965], a uniformly modulated process is one for
which there exists a family ℱ such that the gain Aₜ(ω) is independent of ω.
Here Aₜ(ω) = √|ϑ′(t)| · e^{iω(ϑ★(t)−t)} depends on ω through the phase factor
e^{iω(ϑ★(t)−t)}. For ω₁ ≠ ω₂ and any t with ϑ★(t) ≠ t:

  Aₜ(ω₁) ≠ Aₜ(ω₂)

The condition for uniform modulation fails.

𝐑𝐞𝐦𝐚𝐫𝐤 𝟖.𝟏. The modulus |Aₜ(ω)| = √|ϑ′(t)| is independent of ω, so the
evolutionary spectral density factorizes as dFₜ(ω) = |ϑ′(t)| · dμ(ω). This
factorization is a weaker condition than uniform modulation. Uniform modulation
requires Aₜ(ω) — not merely |Aₜ(ω)| — to be independent of ω. The ω-dependent
phase e^{iω(ϑ★(t)−t)} encodes the fact that different spectral components at
frequency ω arrive at time t with different phase delays, controlled by the
deviation ϑ★(t) − t of cumulative phase from cumulative time.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
§9.  THE COHERENCE OF THE CONSTRUCTION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The polar factorization ζ(½+it) = |ζ(½+it)| · e^{i·arg ζ(½+it)} is the
trivial identity from which everything flows. The modulus squared is the Ingham
density μ(t). The phase is arg ζ(½+it). The Riemann–Siegel theta function
ϑ(t) is the cumulative phase of the functional equation factor
χ(½+it) = e^{−2iϑ(t)}.

The piecewise monotonic modification ϑ★ makes ϑ globally invertible. Its
Jacobian ϑ★′(t) = |ϑ′(t)| is the unique density such that:

  ① The pullback X(T) = ζ(½ + i·ϑ★⁻¹(T)) / √|ϑ′(ϑ★⁻¹(T))| is an isometry
    (§3).

  ② The zeros of X(T) are equispaced at mean gap 2π in the T-variable, because
    T = ϑ★(t) is cumulative phase and the Riemann–von Mangoldt formula gives
    zero density ϑ′(t)/π (§5).

  ③ The Riemann–Siegel formula confines all instantaneous frequencies to [−2,0]
    in the T-variable, because the χ-factor's phase derivative is exactly
    −2ϑ′(t), which after division by |ϑ′(t)| gives the shift −2 (§4).

Properties ① and ③ together make X a band-limited, locally square-integrable
function. Property ② is what makes the Fourier inversion integral for X̂(ω)
converge: the uniform 2π-spacing of zeros produces uniform sign alternation,
enabling Abel summation (§5).

The convergence of the inversion integral establishes the existence of X̂(ω) as
a well-defined L² function on [−2, 0], which defines the orthogonal random
measure dZ(ω) = X̂(ω) dω and the base spectral measure dμ(ω) = |X̂(ω)|² dω.
This is a stationary white-noise base process on [−2, 0]. Its existence was the
missing piece: once it is in hand, the oscillatory process representation
follows by Fubini and the sinc-kernel reproducing identity.

The single time-change ϑ★ simultaneously achieves unitarity, band-limitation,
and uniform zero spacing. These are not three independent constructions but
three consequences of the same reparameterization from cumulative time to
cumulative phase.
