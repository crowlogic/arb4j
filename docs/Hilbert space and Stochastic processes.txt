Hilbert Space Structure of Stationary Processes:
Stone's Theorem, Projection-Valued Measures, and Orthogonal Measures


1  The Cramér–Kolmogorov Framework

Let (X_t)_{t∈ℝ} be a second-order stationary process on a probability
space (Ω, 𝔉, P). The closed linear span

  Hₓ ≔ c̅l̅ span{X_t : t ∈ ℝ}  ⊂  L²(Ω)

is a Hilbert space on which the time-shift operator Uᵗ X_s ≔ X_{s+t}
acts as a strongly continuous unitary group. The element X₀ is a cyclic
vector for this group: its orbit {Uᵗ X₀ : t ∈ ℝ} spans all of Hₓ.


2  Stone's Theorem and the Spectral Resolution

Stone's theorem provides a projection-valued measure E on the Borel
σ-algebra of the frequency domain such that

  Uᵗ = ∫ eⁱᵗλ E(dλ).

Applying E to the cyclic vector X₀ defines the orthogonal measure

  Φ(B) ≔ E(B) X₀,

which is an L²(Ω)-valued, orthogonally scattered, countably additive
measure on the Borel sets, in the Lebesgue–Stieltjes sense. Disjoint
Borel sets receive orthogonal masses: if B₁ ∩ B₂ = ∅ then
⟨Φ(B₁), Φ(B₂)⟩ = 0.


3  The Spectral Representation

The process is recovered from Φ by

  X_t = Uᵗ X₀ = ∫ eⁱᵗλ E(dλ) X₀ = ∫ eⁱᵗλ Φ(dλ).

This is the Cramér spectral representation. The identification E ↔ Φ
is the core structural fact: E is the operator-valued spectral measure
of the unitary group; Φ is the same object seen from the cyclic vector
X₀. In subscripted notation, one writes Φₓ(B) ≔ E(B) X₀ to make
explicit which cyclic vector is in play.


4  The Kolmogorov Isomorphism

The Kolmogorov Isomorphism Theorem establishes a unitary map between
the spectral domain and the time domain by extending the assignment

  eⁱⁿᶿ  ↦  Xₙ

to a surjective isometry T : L²(μ) → Hₓ, where μ is the spectral
measure on the frequency domain. A function f ∈ L²(μ) corresponds
to the process element ∫ f(θ) Φ(dθ). This bijection between L²(μ) and
Hₓ is mediated entirely by the orthogonal measure Φ.


5  Sample-Path Representation

Fix ω ∈ Ω. The spectral representation evaluated at ω gives

  X_t(ω) = ∫ eⁱᵗλ Φ(dλ)(ω),

where B ↦ Φ(B)(ω) is a complex Borel measure for each ω. The sample
path t ↦ X_t(ω) is thus recovered by integrating the kernel eⁱᵗλ
against this ω-section of Φ — the same Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral
structure that appears at the L²(Ω) level, now instantiated pointwise.


6  The RKHS and the Loève Isometry

The RKHS ℋ_K of the covariance kernel K(s,t) = 𝔼[X_s X̄_t] is a
Hilbert space of functions on the index set. The Loève isometry maps
K(·, t) ↦ X_t and extends by linearity and closure to an isometric
identification ℋ_K ≅ Hₓ. In the Gaussian case, ℋ_K coincides with
the Cameron–Martin space: translation by h ∈ ℋ_K preserves equivalence
of the Gaussian law, while translation by any element outside ℋ_K
produces a singular law.

Sample paths t ↦ X_t(ω) are ω-sections living in a path space (e.g.
C[0,1] or L²[0,1]). For Gaussian processes with infinite-dimensional
RKHS, sample paths almost surely do not belong to ℋ_K. For Wiener
measure, ℋ_K is the Sobolev space of absolutely continuous functions
with square-integrable derivative vanishing at 0; Brownian paths are
almost surely nowhere differentiable and therefore not in ℋ_K.

The RKHS thus sits on the function side of the Loève isometry, encoding
the covariance geometry. The orthogonal measure Φ and its identification
with the projection-valued measure E sit on the L²(Ω) side, encoding
the spectral decomposition of the unitary group of shifts.
