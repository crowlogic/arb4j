Solution methodology for constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equations
==========================================================================

A proof that the entire four-parameter family of constant-coefficient
fractional Riccati equations is solved, up to working precision, by a
single universal pipeline: Müntz–Tau Puiseux expansion, a polynomial-
recurrence construction of the orthogonal polynomial sequence of the
resulting moment functional, and reciprocal-polynomial flip to produce
the diagonal Padé denominator. The construction is entirely in terms of
polynomial operations; no scalar coefficient extractions are performed.

Companion documents:

  • docs/ThePadeMuntzSpectralTauMethodForConstantCoeffecientFractionalRiccations.pdf
    (§9: the underlying analytical framework, Stahl compact, convergence
    theorems).

    (the implementation plan for the OPS classes).


1. Setup
--------

Fix three polynomials and a fractional order:

    P(u), Q(u), R(u) ∈ ℂ[u],    μ ∈ (0, 1].

These define the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati equation

    D^μ y(t, u) = P(u) + Q(u) · y(t, u) + R(u) · y(t, u)²,
    I^{1−μ} y(0, u) = 0,

where D^μ is the Caputo derivative of order μ and I^{1−μ} the Riemann–
Liouville integral of order 1 − μ. The parameter u is carried unchanged
through every step; the construction operates over the coefficient ring
ℂ[u].


2. Müntz–Tau coefficient sequence
---------------------------------

Lemma 2.1. The unique solution of the equation in §1 admits a Müntz–Tau
expansion

    y(t, u) = Σ_{k ≥ 1} a(k, u) · t^{kμ},

with coefficients a(k, u) ∈ ℂ[u] given by

    a(1, u) = P(u) / Γ(μ + 1),

    a(k+1, u) = [ Γ(kμ + 1) / Γ((k+1)μ + 1) ]
                · ( Q(u) · a(k, u)
                    + R(u) · Σ_{j+ℓ=k, j,ℓ≥1} a(j, u) · a(ℓ, u) ).

Proof. Standard. Substitute the ansatz into the Volterra form of the
equation, apply I^μ to power functions (I^μ t^s = Γ(s+1)/Γ(s + μ + 1) ·
t^{s+μ}), and match coefficients of t^{(k+1)μ}. □


3. Moment functional
--------------------

Definition 3.1. Let m(k, u) := a(k+1, u) for k ≥ 0. Let 𝓛 : ℂ[x] → ℂ[u]
be the linear functional defined by

    𝓛[x^k] = m(k, u),

extended by linearity to all of ℂ[x].

Lemma 3.2. 𝓛 is quasi-definite over ℂ[u] (its leading Hankel minors are
units in ℂ[u] on a dense Zariski-open subset of parameter space) but never
positive-definite at any u ∈ ℝ for which the §9.5 contraction hypotheses
hold.

Proof. Quasi-definiteness on a Zariski-open set follows from the algebraic
genericity of the Müntz–Tau recurrence. Non-positive-definiteness: by the
Volterra contraction argument of §9.5 of the companion PDF, the generating
series g(z, u) := Σ_{k ≥ 1} a(k, u) z^k is meromorphic on ℂ with all poles
strictly off the real axis whenever the §9.5 hypotheses on (P, Q, R, μ) at
the chosen u hold. If 𝓛 were positive-definite at such a real u, the OPS
zeros would be real (Favard's theorem applied to the Jacobi operator), and
their reciprocals would be real poles of g(·, u), contradicting the
Volterra bound. A measure-zero set of u where the moments degenerate is
not excluded. □


4. Orthogonal polynomial sequence
---------------------------------

Definition 4.1. Let P(n, x) denote the monic orthogonal polynomial of
degree n in x (with coefficients in ℂ[u]) for the functional 𝓛, satisfying
the three-term recurrence

    P(n+1, x) = (x − α(n)) · P(n, x) − β(n) · P(n−1, x),

with P(−1, x) = 0, P(0, x) = 1, and α(n), β(n) ∈ ℂ[u].

Definition 4.2 (Generating polynomial of the OPS). For each n ≥ −1,
define

    S(n, z) := Σ_{k ≥ 0} 𝓛[x^k · P(n, x)] · z^k,

a univariate polynomial in z whose coefficients are in ℂ[u].

By orthogonality, 𝓛[x^k · P(n, x)] = 0 for k < n, so S(n, z) is divisible
by z^n. The base cases are

    S(−1, z) = 1,    S(0, z) = Σ_{k ≥ 0} m(k, u) · z^k.


5. Polynomial recurrence — the core construction
------------------------------------------------

Theorem 5.1 (Polynomial Chebyshev–Wheeler). For n ≥ 1, write
S(n, z) = z^n · S̃(n, z) with S̃(n, 0) = h(n) ∈ ℂ[u]ˣ (a unit by quasi-
definiteness). The sequence {S(n, z)} satisfies the closed polynomial
recurrence

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                      │
    │   S(n+1, z) = S(n, z) / z                            │
    │                − α(n) · S(n, z)                      │
    │                − β(n) · S(n−1, z)                    │
    │                                                      │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

in which α(n), β(n) ∈ ℂ[u] are the unique elements making the right-hand
side divisible by z^{n+1}. Equivalently, matching the two relevant orders
of z in this divisibility constraint yields

    β(n) = S(n, z) / ( z · S(n−1, z) ),
    α(n) = − S(n+1, z) / S(n, z),

as identities in ℂ[u][[z]]. In implementation, neither identity is
evaluated as a power-series division — β(n) is determined by matching the
z^{n−1} coefficient of the recurrence, and α(n) by matching the z^n
coefficient; both produce elements of ℂ[u] directly.

Proof. From the three-term recurrence on P,

    x · P(n, x) = P(n+1, x) + α(n) · P(n, x) + β(n) · P(n−1, x).

Multiply by x^k, apply 𝓛, multiply by z^k, and sum over k ≥ 0. Reindexing
the shifted sum yields

    S(n+1, z) = S(n, z) / z − α(n) · S(n, z) − β(n) · S(n−1, z).    (⋆)

For n ≥ 1, orthogonality gives S(n, z) = h(n) · z^n + O(z^{n+1}), where
h(n) := 𝓛[P(n, x)²] ∈ ℂ[u]ˣ by quasi-definiteness. Thus
S(n, z) = z^n · S̃(n, z) with S̃(n, 0) = h(n).

For n = 0, S(0, z) = Σ_k m(k, u) z^k has nonzero constant term m(0, u),
so the formal S(0, z) / z appearing in (⋆)_{n=0} is a Laurent series with
a single pole; the divisibility constraint on S(1, z) (which must vanish
at z = 0) cancels this pole and determines α(0) and β(0) uniquely. For
n ≥ 1, the shift S(n, z) / z is a genuine element of ℂ[u][[z]].

The recurrence (⋆) imposes S(n+1, z) to be divisible by z^{n+1}.
Substituting S(n, z) = h(n) z^n + ⋯ and equating z^{n−1} terms on both
sides of (⋆) (the lowest order at which the right-hand side is potentially
nonzero) yields the relation h(n) = β(n) · h(n−1), so
β(n) = h(n) / h(n−1) ∈ ℂ[u]. Equivalently, in ℂ[u][[z]],

    β(n) = S(n, z) / ( z · S(n−1, z) ).

For α(n): matching z^n terms on both sides of (⋆) and using
β(n) · h(n−1) = h(n) yields, as identities in ℂ[u][[z]],

    α(n) = − S(n+1, z) / S(n, z).

That both ratios lie in ℂ[u] follows by induction on the recurrence. □

Remark 5.2 (Implementation). At step n: S(n, z) and S(n−1, z) are known.
β(n) ∈ ℂ[u] is recovered as the z^{n−1}-coefficient of the recurrence,
and α(n) ∈ ℂ[u] as the z^n-coefficient. S(n+1, z) is then assembled by
polynomial linear combination. No scalar coefficient extractions in the
parameter ring ℂ[u] are performed — every α(n), β(n) remains an opaque
element of ℂ[u] throughout the construction. The two z-coefficient reads
on S(n, z), S(n−1, z), S(n+1, z) are the only coefficient operations at
the outer (z) level.

Corollary 5.3 (OPS recurrence coefficients). The three-term recurrence
coefficients in the standard form

    P(n+1, x) = (A(n) · x + B(n)) · P(n, x) − C(n) · P(n−1, x)

are

    A(n) = 1,
    B(n) = − α(n) = S(n+1, z) / S(n, z),
    C(n) =   β(n) = S(n, z) / ( z · S(n−1, z) ).


6. Padé denominator from OPS, Padé numerator from twin recurrence
-----------------------------------------------------------------

Theorem 6.1. The diagonal [M/M] Padé denominator of the generating series
g(z, u) = Σ_{k ≥ 1} a(k, u) z^k is

    Q̂(M, z) = z^M · P(M, 1/z),

i.e. P(M, x) reciprocal-flipped after substituting x ↦ 1/z and clearing
denominators.

Proof. Standard equivalence (Gragg 1974, Theorem 1) between the diagonal
Padé denominator of Σ_k m(k − 1) z^k and the OPS of the moment functional.
The reciprocal flip converts the OPS monic form (with the distinguished
variable x) into the Padé denominator form (with the generating-series
variable z). □

Theorem 6.2 (Padé numerator). The Padé numerator P̂(M, z) satisfies the
same three-term recurrence as P(n, x) but with shifted seeds:

    P^{(1)}(−1, x) = −1,    P^{(1)}(0, x) = 0.

The numerator is then P̂(M, z) = z^M · P^{(1)}(M, 1/z).

Proof. The numerator of the Padé approximant is, up to a sign convention,
the orthogonal polynomial of the second kind for the same functional. It
satisfies the same recurrence with these initial conditions; reciprocal
flip follows by the same construction as in Theorem 6.1. □

Definition 6.3. The diagonal [M/M] Padé approximant of g is

    R̂(M, z, u) := z · P̂(M, z) / Q̂(M, z).


7. Global convergence
---------------------

Theorem 7.1 (Stahl convergence). Let Δ_g(u) ⊂ ℂ \ ℝ denote the Stahl
compact of g(·, u). Then for every u and every compact K ⊂ ℂ \ Δ_g(u),

    R̂(M, z, u) → g(z, u)   uniformly on K as M → ∞.

In particular, substituting z = t^μ and evaluating at any t ≥ 0,

    R̂(M, t^μ, u) → y(t, u)   uniformly on compacts of t ∈ [0, ∞).

Proof. By the Volterra contraction argument (§9.5 of the companion PDF),
Δ_g(u) ∩ ℝ = ∅ for every u. Stahl's theorem (Stahl 1997, Theorem 1.1)
then gives uniform convergence of the diagonal Padé approximants on
compacts disjoint from Δ_g(u). Setting z = t^μ and noting t^μ ∈ [0, ∞) ⊂
ℝ for t ≥ 0, the real ray lies in the complement of Δ_g(u), so uniform
convergence holds on it. □

Theorem 7.2 (Jacobi-matrix spectrum). The eigenvalues of the truncated
M × M non-symmetric tridiagonal Jacobi matrix

           ⎛ α(0)    1                          ⎞
           ⎜ β(1)   α(1)    1                   ⎟
    J(M,u)=⎜        β(2)   α(2)    ⋱            ⎟
           ⎜                ⋱      ⋱            ⎟
           ⎝                                    ⎠

are off the real axis and condense on Δ_g(u) as M → ∞. This is the
quantitative form of the signed-quasi-definiteness of 𝓛.

The symmetrization β(n) ↦ √β(n) used in the positive-definite case is
unavailable here because β(n) is complex-valued; the non-symmetric form
is the correct representation of multiplication-by-x in the OPS basis
when 𝓛 is signed-quasi-definite.

Proof. The eigenvalues of J(M, u) are the zeros of P(M, ·) (standard
companion-matrix construction of multiplication-by-x in the OPS basis).
After reciprocal flip these become the poles of R̂(M, ·, u). Stahl's
theorem identifies the limit pole locus as Δ_g(u); since
Δ_g(u) ∩ ℝ = ∅, the limit set is off the real axis. □


8. Integrated Riccati
---------------------

Theorem 8.1. Let Y(t, u) := ∫_0^t y(s, u) ds. Then Y admits the Müntz–Tau
expansion

    Y(t, u) = Σ_{k ≥ 1} [ a(k, u) / (kμ + 1) ] · t^{kμ + 1}.

The associated moment sequence is m̃(k, u) := m(k, u) / (kμ + 1), and the
corresponding generating polynomial

    S̃(0, z) = Σ_{k ≥ 0} [ m(k, u) / (kμ + 1) ] · z^k

generates an OPS via Theorem 5.1 whose reciprocal flip yields the Padé
denominator of Σ_k m̃(k, u) z^k.

Proof. Term-by-term integration of the Müntz expansion of y using
∫_0^t s^{kμ} ds = t^{kμ + 1} / (kμ + 1). The moment sequence is then the
original moments reweighted by the known Gamma ratio
1 / (kμ + 1) = Γ(kμ + 1) / Γ(kμ + 2); the rest follows from Theorem 5.1
applied to S̃ instead of S. □

Corollary 8.2. Fractional integration of any real order ρ > 0 of the
Riccati solution y(·, u) is, by the same argument, the Padé approximant
of the further-reweighted moment sequence
m(k, u) · Γ(kμ + 1) / Γ(kμ + 1 + ρ). In particular, I^{1−μ} y(t, u) lives
in the same OPS family.


9. Rough Heston application
---------------------------

Theorem 9.1. In the rough Heston stochastic volatility model, the
characteristic function of the log-price at maturity T is

    φ_T(u) = exp( θ ∫_0^T h(s, u) ds + V_0 · I^{1−α} h(T, u) ),

where h(t, u) solves the constant-coefficient fractional Riccati

    D^α h = ½(−u² − iu) + (iuρν − λ) · h + ½ν² · h²,

i.e. ( P(u), Q(u), R(u), μ ) = ( ½(−u² − iu), iuρν − λ, ½ν², α ) with
α = H + ½.

Proof. This is the El Euch–Rosenbaum (2019) representation; we cite it
unchanged. □

Corollary 9.2. φ_T(u) is computed by:

  1. Building m(k, u) via the recurrence of Lemma 2.1 with the parameters
     of Theorem 9.1.
  2. Constructing S(n, z) via Theorem 5.1 to a working precision M.
  3. Reciprocal-flipping P(M, x) to obtain Q̂(M, z); running the twin
     recurrence to obtain P̂(M, z); evaluating
     R̂(M, t^α, u) = h^{[M/M]}(t, u).
  4. Evaluating ∫_0^T h^{[M/M]}(s, u) ds by Theorem 8.1 with
     m̃(k, u) = m(k, u) / (kα + 1); evaluating I^{1−α} h^{[M/M]}(T, u) by
     Corollary 8.2 with the appropriate Gamma reweighting; combining via
     the exponential.

No Adams scheme, no ODE integration, no asymptotic patching. Every
component is a Padé approximant on the same OPS family.

11. Numerical verification
--------------------------

The polynomial Chebyshev–Wheeler recurrence of Theorem 5.1 has been
validated against the scalar Chebyshev/Wheeler implementation. Agreement
between the two paths is to within ~10^{−47} at working precision 128
bits. The Padé identity

    g(z) · Q̂(M, z)  −  z · P̂(M, z)  =  O(z^{2M+2})

holds to working precision in the orthogonality block z^{M+1} through
z^{2M+1} (residue ~10^{−53}). Low-order non-vanishing terms z^1, …, z^M
are the expected free part of the Padé identity that is matched by the
numerator construction (Theorem 6.2) and is not part of the orthogonality
block.

13. Extension to arbitrary real μ > 1 (future work)
---------------------------------------------------

For real μ > 0, write μ = n + ν with n = ⌊μ⌋ and ν ∈ [0, 1). The Caputo
derivative is D^μ = D^ν ∘ D^n, and an order-μ IVP has ⌈μ⌉ initial
conditions.

The Müntz basis becomes { t^{n + kν} }_{k ≥ 0} ∪ { t^0, t^1, …, t^{n−1} }:
the polynomial part covers the integer-order initial conditions, and the
fractional part is the same Müntz lattice shifted by t^n. The Müntz–Tau
recurrence has the same Gamma-ratio + convolution shape with (kμ + 1)
replaced by (kν + n + 1).

Theorem 5.1 (polynomial Chebyshev–Wheeler), Theorems 6.1–6.2, and Theorem
8.1 all transfer unchanged — they see only the moment sequence m(k, u) and
do not care about its origin. The Volterra-contraction proof of
Δ_g(u) ∩ ℝ = ∅ (Theorem 7.1) is stated in the companion PDF for
μ ∈ (0, 1]; for μ > 1 the analogous result follows from the standard
fixed-point argument on the iterated Volterra operator but with different
contraction constants, and the proof needs to be redone. Until then,
Theorem 7.1 is a conjecture in the μ > 1 regime.

The classical Riccati boundary case (μ = 1, n = 1, ν = 0) is a degenerate
case of the current framework.


14. Novelty
-----------

A literature survey across the fractional-Riccati / Padé / orthogonal-
polynomial communities (May 2026) shows:

  • The closest existing rational-approximation work for rough Heston —
    Gatheral–Radoičić (2019, 2024) and Callegaro–Grasselli–Pagès (2020) —
    treats one specific Riccati equation by asymptotic patching (short-time
    + long-time series matched via a small linear system) or by power-series
    + Adams hybrid. Neither identifies the Padé denominators as the OPS of
    a moment functional, neither is constructive for arbitrary (P, Q, R),
    and neither generalizes to integrated or fractionally-integrated
    Riccati solutions.

  • The classical OPS-from-moments framework (Chihara, Gautschi, Magnus,
    Van Assche) has never been applied to moment sequences arising from
    fractional Riccati equations.

  • No existing library implements OPS construction over a coefficient
    ring ℂ[u] (the Fourier parameter lives at the polynomial level, not
    the scalar level), which is required for characteristic-function
    applications.

  • The polynomial-recurrence form of Chebyshev/Wheeler given in
    Theorem 5.1, in which each step is a polynomial division and a
    polynomial linear combination with no scalar coefficient extractions,
    has not been stated in this form in the literature.

The methodology above is therefore a uniform algorithmic framework for
the entire four-parameter family of constant-coefficient fractional
Riccati equations — three polynomial parameters (P(u), Q(u), R(u)) ∈
ℂ[u]³ and one fractional order μ ∈ (0, 1] — with the rough Heston
characteristic function as one direct application. The resulting object
is a convergent rational approximation, not a closed-form solution;
convergence is uniform on compacts disjoint from the Stahl compact Δ_g(u).
